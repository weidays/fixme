## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single Rheem fault code — it's a symptom. Your Rheem or Ruud air conditioner may be running, blowing air, and showing no error at all, yet the house won't get cold. On EcoNet-equipped systems you may see a communicating fault on the thermostat, but most classic (24V) Rheem systems give no display code for this at all.

The underlying problem is almost always one of three categories: **restricted airflow**, **an electrical failure** (capacitor, contactor, motor), or a **refrigerant/compressor issue**. This page helps you separate the cheap DIY causes from the ones that need a licensed HVAC tech.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked return** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops output and can freeze the coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll get weak, warm air and possibly ice on the copper line at the furnace/air handler.
3. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common part failure. The compressor or condenser fan may hum, struggle, or not start.
4. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — a mud- or cottonwood-clogged outdoor unit can't reject heat, so cooling drops on hot days.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, longer runtimes, and coil freeze. Requires EPA-certified repair.
6. **Bad contactor** — pitted or stuck contacts prevent the outdoor unit from energizing.
7. **Condensate safety switch tripped** — a full drain pan can shut cooling off entirely on many installs.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor or blower motor** — no airflow across a coil means no useful cooling.
9. **TXV / metering device failure** — restricts refrigerant flow; a diagnostic-heavy fault.
10. **Failing compressor** — the worst case, often the most expensive repair on the unit.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. If cooling doesn't return after them, call a pro.

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Set it to **Cool**, temperature several degrees below room temp. If it's battery-powered and blank or glitchy, install fresh batteries.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a tech.
- **Vents & returns:** Make sure supply registers are open and furniture/rugs aren't blocking return grilles.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear grass, leaves, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside fins with a garden hose (unit off) if they're caked with dirt.
- **Condensate:** If cooling stopped and you see a full drain pan or water near the indoor unit, a safety switch may have tripped. Clearing the visible drain line/pan can restore operation.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant line or coil, turn the system to **Off** and the fan to **On** for 1–2 hours to thaw, then run cooling again with a clean filter.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps shutting off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical path, so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirms the complaint** — measures supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is usually ~15–20°F).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects filter, coil, and blower operation.
3. **Tests electrical** — reads the run capacitor with a meter, checks the contactor, and verifies the outdoor unit is getting 24V and line voltage.
4. **Inspects the coils** — for dirt and for ice, which points to airflow or charge problems.
5. **Connects gauges** — reads system pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge refrigerant charge. Low charge means a **leak search**, not just a top-off.
6. **Checks the metering device / TXV and compressor** — measures amp draw and windings if the compressor is suspect.

Ask for the measured pressures, temperature split, and capacitor reading. A legitimate diagnosis produces numbers, not guesses.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter / blocked return | Replace filter, open vents | Check blower, clean coil |
| Ice on line or coil | Low airflow or low charge | Off + fan-on to thaw, new filter | Find cause; leak search / charge |
| Outdoor fan hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None (electrical) | Test & replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit never runs | Contactor / breaker / control | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, 24V, board |
| Cooling stopped, water near unit | Tripped condensate switch | Clear drain line/pan | Clear blockage, test switch |
| Long runtimes, small temp split | Low refrigerant leak | None | Leak repair + recharge |
| Outdoor coil caked with dirt | Dirty condenser | Rinse fins (unit off) | Deep coil clean |
| Air handler runs, no cooling at all | Compressor / TXV failure | None | Diagnose & replace |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and unit age):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser or evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Blower motor:** $400–$900
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,600 (depends on leak location)
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $500–$1,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,300–$2,200+ (on older units, often worth weighing against a full system replacement)

If your Rheem system is still under its parts warranty, the failed part may be covered — you'd typically pay only labor and refrigerant.
