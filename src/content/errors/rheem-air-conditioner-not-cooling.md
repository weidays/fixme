## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific Rheem fault code — it's a symptom. Your Rheem air conditioner may be running (outdoor fan spinning, indoor blower moving air) but the air coming from your vents isn't cold, or the house simply never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

On Rheem systems with **EcoNet controls**, you may see an alert or status message at the thermostat or in the app. On standard 24V systems, there's no display — you diagnose by symptom and by reading the diagnostic LED on the indoor control board (flash codes vary by board model, so check the label inside the air-handler access panel).

Because "not cooling" covers everything from a $20 filter to a failed compressor, this page walks you through the likely causes in order, the safe checks you can do yourself, and how a technician confirms the real problem so you can sanity-check any quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling output, and can freeze the coil. Most common and cheapest fix.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow (dirty filter/coil) or low refrigerant. Ice blocks heat transfer, so air feels warm.
3. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a programming/schedule error keeping the system off.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit or air handler has lost power on one circuit.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common electrical failure; the compressor or fan motor hums or won't start.
6. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity and can freeze the coil. Requires a licensed tech.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
9. **Clogged condensate drain / float switch tripped** — a safety float can shut down cooling to prevent overflow.
10. **Failed compressor or blower motor** — the most expensive outcomes, usually last to suspect.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. If a check requires opening a sealed system, handling refrigerant, or bypassing a switch — stop and call a pro.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL, the setpoint is below room temp, and the fan is on AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Air filter:** Pull it out and look at it against a light. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling and frozen coils.
- **Breakers:** Check the breaker panel for the air handler and the outdoor unit (often a separate 240V breaker). If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — don't keep resetting it.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many rooms can freeze the coil.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for tall grass, leaves, or debris choking the coil fins. Clear obstructions and trim back vegetation at least 2 feet on all sides.
- **Frozen coil / ice:** If you see ice on the copper line or coil, switch the thermostat to **OFF**, then set the fan to **ON** for 1–2 hours to thaw it. Replace the filter before restarting in COOL mode.
- **Condensate drain:** Check for standing water in the drain pan. A clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Pour the line clear if accessible; drain tablets help prevent clogs.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Rheem tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you spot a shortcut or an overcharged invoice:

1. **Verify the complaint and check airflow** — measures temperature split (return vs. supply air, typically 15–20°F when healthy) and inspects the filter and coils.
2. **Reads the control board LED / EcoNet alerts** — flash codes differ by board, so they confirm against the model-specific legend on the panel.
3. **Checks electrical** — tests the capacitor with a meter, inspects the contactor for pitting, and confirms voltage at the compressor and fan.
4. **Inspects the evaporator and condenser coils** — for ice, dirt, or restricted airflow.
5. **Connects gauges and checks refrigerant pressures** — compares suction/liquid pressures and superheat/subcooling to Rheem's charging chart. Low charge points to a leak, which they'll then locate with electronic detection or dye.
6. **Tests the compressor and blower motor** — amp draw and winding resistance, last because they're the costliest components.

A trustworthy quote names the specific failed part and the test result behind it — not just "needs a recharge" with no leak search.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect coil & duct if persists |
| Ice on coil/lines | Low airflow or low charge | Fan ON to thaw, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or leak |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Nothing runs at all | Tripped breaker / no power | Reset breaker once | Trace short, replace contactor/fuse |
| Indoor blower runs, outdoor dead | Contactor or capacitor | Check outdoor breaker | Test & replace contactor/cap |
| Cools weakly all day | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Dirty outdoor coil, runs hot | Condenser fouling | Clear debris, rinse fins gently | Deep coil clean, check pressures |
| Water near air handler, no cooling | Clogged drain / float tripped | Clear drain, use tablets | Clear line, test float switch |
| Loud hum then shutdown | Failing compressor | None — call pro | Compressor amp/winding test |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (regional rates vary):

- **Air filter:** $0–$30 (DIY)
- **Service call / diagnostic:** $75–$200
- **Run or dual capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$300
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 (depends on leak location and refrigerant type)
- **Blower motor (ECM):** $450–$900
- **Condenser fan motor:** $350–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500 (often worth comparing against a new unit if out of warranty)

Rheem parts may be covered under the standard 10-year limited parts warranty if registered — confirm your model and registration before paying for parts.
