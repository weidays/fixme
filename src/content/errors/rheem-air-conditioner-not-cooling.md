## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific Rheem fault code. Your air conditioner may be running — fan spinning, thermostat calling for cool — but the air coming from the vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the set temperature.

Rheem and Ruud air conditioners generally don't display lettered error codes on the unit itself the way some furnaces do. On EcoNet-enabled systems, faults appear in the EcoNet app or thermostat as alerts. On standard units, the only "diagnostics" you get are the unit's behavior: short cycling, ice on the lines, a humming-but-not-starting compressor, or a tripped breaker.

This page covers a system that powers on but fails to cool. If your unit is completely dead or showing a flashing light, see the related Rheem heat pump article.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter** — Restricts airflow across the evaporator coil, reducing cooling and eventually freezing the coil. The single most common and cheapest cause.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — Caused by low airflow (dirty filter/blocked vents) or low refrigerant. A frozen coil blows warm air and can drip water.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — Caked grass clippings, dust, or debris stop the unit from rejecting heat, so the house won't cool on hot days.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — A weak or dead capacitor stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting; you may hear a hum or clicking outside.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Causes poor cooling, ice on the lines, and longer run times. Requires a licensed tech — never a DIY top-off.
6. **Failed contactor** — The electrical relay that powers the outdoor unit can pit or fail, leaving the condenser dead.
7. **Clogged condensate drain / float switch lockout** — A full drain pan trips a safety switch that shuts the system down to prevent water damage.
8. **Thermostat or low-voltage wiring fault** — Bad batteries, wrong settings, or a broken 24V wire stops the call for cooling.
9. **Compressor failure** — The most expensive cause; often the diagnosis after other components check out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. If none resolve it, call a licensed HVAC technician.

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and a temperature several degrees below the room reading. Confirm fan is on AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and inspect. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of "not cooling."
- **Breakers:** Check the breaker panel for a tripped breaker for the air handler and the outdoor unit. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated resets are dangerous.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for obvious debris, leaves, or tall grass choking the condenser. Gently clear the area (with power off at the disconnect).
- **Condensate drain:** If you see water around the indoor unit or it shut off after rain/humidity, the drain may be clogged. A wet/dry vac on the drain line outlet can clear it. Drain tablets help prevent recurrence.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to OFF and run the fan only for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter. If it refreezes, you need a tech.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass the float switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence — use this to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** at the thermostat and confirm 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. **Check airflow** — filter, blower operation, and coil condition.
3. **Test the capacitor** with a meter; a reading well below its rated microfarads means replacement.
4. **Inspect the contactor** for pitting and confirm it pulls in when energized.
5. **Measure refrigerant pressures** with gauges and check superheat/subcooling. Low pressure indicates a leak, which they'll trace with a leak detector or dye.
6. **Check the compressor** — windings, amp draw, and whether it's drawing locked-rotor amps (a sign of failure).
7. **Inspect the condensate drain and float switch** if the unit is locking out.

Expect a diagnostic fee of roughly $80–$200, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air from vents, fan running | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw coil with fan-only | Check refrigerant, clean coil |
| Ice on refrigerant lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Replace filter, thaw, open vents | Find and repair refrigerant leak |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace run capacitor |
| Outdoor unit completely dead | Tripped breaker / blown fuse / contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace fuse, contactor, or wiring |
| System shut off, water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain with wet/dry vac | Clear line, replace float switch |
| House never reaches set temp on hot days | Dirty condenser coil / low charge | Clear debris around unit | Clean coil, check charge |
| No response to thermostat | Dead batteries / wiring fault | Replace batteries, check settings | Trace 24V control wiring |
| Outdoor unit short cycles | Low refrigerant / failing compressor | None — call a pro | Diagnose charge and compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $75–$250
- **Float switch replacement:** $75–$150
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Coil cleaning (indoor or outdoor):** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs older R-22 is far costlier)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500 (often makes replacing the whole condenser worth considering on older units)

Prices vary by region, refrigerant type, and whether the part is under Rheem's parts warranty (typically 10 years if registered — labor is usually not covered).
