## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numeric fault code — it's the most common complaint Rheem and Ruud AC owners report. The system powers on, the indoor blower may run, but the air coming from the vents is warm, lukewarm, or only slightly cool. On variable-speed inverter models (Prestige/Endeavor), the unit may also display a fault on the control board or flash a status light if it has detected a low-pressure or sensor fault.

Because "not cooling" can stem from anything from a $0 thermostat setting to a $2,000 compressor, this page walks you from the cheapest, safest checks up to what a technician must diagnose. The goal is to fix what you safely can and to sanity-check any quote you receive.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, freezes the indoor coil, and kills cooling capacity. Cheapest and most common cause.
2. **Dirty condenser coil (outdoor unit)** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat. Very common after winter or in dusty yards.
3. **Failed dual run capacitor** — the compressor and/or fan won't start or runs weakly. A frequent, affordable failure, especially after a heat wave.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, ice on the lines, longer run times. Requires EPA-certified repair, not a top-off.
5. **Tripped breaker or contactor failure** — outdoor unit gets no power, so only the indoor fan runs and blows warm air.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low charge; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
7. **Condenser fan motor failure** — outdoor fan stops, compressor overheats and trips on internal overload.
8. **Thermostat misconfiguration or failure** — set to "fan" instead of "cool," wrong programming, or dead batteries.
9. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; the unit runs but produces no cooling at all. Least common but most expensive.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** (not just FAN), and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone can stop cooling and freeze the coil.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker (often a double-pole). Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals a real fault.
- **Outdoor unit:** Make sure it's running. Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around it, and gently rinse the coil fins from the outside with a garden hose (power off). Keep at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Supply and return vents:** Confirm they're open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain:** A full drain pan can trigger a float switch that shuts the system down. Check for standing water near the indoor unit and clear an obvious clog.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to **OFF** and run the FAN only for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter before restarting.

Do not open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you spot a guesser:

1. **Verify the complaint and airflow** — measures temperature split (return vs. supply air); a healthy system shows roughly 16–22°F drop.
2. **Inspect filter and coils** — checks for restriction and a frozen evaporator.
3. **Check electrical** — tests the run capacitor with a meter, inspects the contactor, and confirms the compressor and condenser fan are drawing correct amperage.
4. **Gauge the refrigerant** — connects manifold gauges to read suction and head pressures and calculates superheat/subcooling. Low charge points to a leak.
5. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test to locate the leak before recharging.
6. **Compressor test** — if the unit runs with no cooling and pressures are equalized, tests compressor windings and start components.

Be wary of any tech who recommends "just adding refrigerant" without finding a leak — that's a band-aid, not a repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, indoor fan runs, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker or bad contactor | Reset breaker once; check outdoor power | Replace contactor, test circuit |
| Weak cooling, ice on lines | Dirty filter or low refrigerant | Replace filter, thaw coil | Find/repair leak, recharge |
| Outdoor unit humming, fan not spinning | Failed capacitor or fan motor | Replace filter, kill power | Replace capacitor or fan motor |
| Long run times, slowly losing cool | Refrigerant leak | None — call pro | Leak search and EPA-certified recharge |
| No cooling at all, unit runs | Failed compressor | None — call pro | Compressor replacement or system quote |
| Lukewarm air, thermostat blank | Dead batteries / wiring | Replace batteries, set to COOL | Replace or rewire thermostat |
| Dirty outdoor coil, hot exhaust | Restricted condenser airflow | Rinse coil, clear debris | Deep coil cleaning |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, including parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$30 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Professional coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is far pricier)
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,800–$2,200 (often makes full system replacement worth considering on older units)

If your unit is more than 12–15 years old and faces a compressor or coil failure, ask for a replacement quote alongside the repair — the economics often favor a new system.
