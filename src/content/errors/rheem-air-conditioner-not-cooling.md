## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's the symptom you notice when your Rheem air conditioner is running (or trying to run) but the air coming from your vents is warm, weak, or barely moving. On EcoNet-enabled Rheem systems you may also see an app alert or a flashing light on the control board, but many older single-stage units give no code at all — they just stop cooling.

Because "not cooling" can stem from anything from a $20 filter to a $2,500 compressor, this page is rated **pro**: a few causes are safe to check yourself, but most require a licensed HVAC technician with gauges and refrigerant certification.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, can freeze the evaporator coil and stop cooling entirely. The single most common cause.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer, so air comes out warm or not at all.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common electrical failure. The compressor or condenser fan hums or won't start, so no cold air is produced.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — refrigerant never depletes normally; if it's low, there's a leak. Causes weak cooling and coil freezing.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat and cooling drops off, especially on hot days.
6. **Tripped breaker or blown control fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power while the indoor blower still runs.
7. **Contactor failure** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit sticks or burns out; common on aging Rheem condensers.
8. **Condensate safety switch tripped** — a full drain pan trips the float switch and shuts down cooling to prevent water damage.
9. **Failed condenser fan or blower motor** — without airflow over a coil, the system can't transfer heat.
10. **Bad compressor** — the least common but most expensive; the heart of the system has failed.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, handle refrigerant, or repeatedly reset a locked-out unit.

- **Air filter:** Pull it. If it's gray, furry, or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. If it's battery-powered, replace the batteries.
- **Vents and registers:** Walk the house. Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Breaker:** Check the panel for a tripped breaker for the AC. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — that's an electrical fault.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside fins with a garden hose (low pressure, top-down) if visibly dirty.
- **Condensate drain:** If you see water around the indoor unit or the AC shut off, the float switch may have tripped. Clearing the drain line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination can help; otherwise call a pro.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** and run the **FAN ON** to thaw it (can take hours). Once thawed, replace the filter before restarting. If it freezes again, you have a refrigerant or airflow problem needing a tech.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Rheem tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** and check the thermostat call and EcoNet/board status lights.
2. **Measure temperature split** across the indoor coil (return vs. supply air) — a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F.
3. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower wheel, coil cleanliness, duct restrictions.
4. **Electrical checks** — test the capacitor with a meter, inspect the contactor, check voltage at the outdoor unit, and verify the blower and condenser fan run.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** — read suction and head pressures and superheat/subcooling to determine if charge is correct. Low readings point to a leak.
6. **Leak search** if refrigerant is low — electronic detector, dye, or bubble test to find the source before any recharge.
7. **Compressor test** — windings, amp draw, and start performance if everything upstream checks out.

Be cautious of any tech who tops off refrigerant without searching for a leak — that's a temporary, EPA-frowned-upon fix.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower/duct if persists |
| Ice on copper lines or coil | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Turn off, run fan to thaw, swap filter | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor or contactor | None — electrical | Test and replace capacitor/contactor |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker, contactor, or fuse | Reset breaker once | Diagnose power path, replace parts |
| Water near indoor unit, AC off | Tripped condensate float switch | Clear drain line with wet/dry vac | Clear/repair drain, test switch |
| Hot days only, weak cooling | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse outdoor fins gently | Deep coil clean, check charge |
| Loud or no condenser fan | Failed fan motor/capacitor | None | Replace motor/capacitor |
| Cooling slowly degraded over months | Refrigerant leak | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Nothing runs, breaker fine | Control board or transformer | None | Diagnose board/low-voltage |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges, including parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $0 DIY ($15–$40 for a multi-pack)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $200 pro
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150 – $400
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400 – $700
- **Blower motor:** $450 – $900 (more for ECM/variable-speed)
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300 – $1,500 (depends on leak location and refrigerant type)
- **TXV / metering device:** $500 – $900
- **Control board (EcoNet or standard):** $400 – $900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500 – $2,500+ (often worth comparing to full system replacement on older units)

Prices vary by region, refrigerant type (R-410A vs. R-32 on newer Rheem models), and whether your unit is still under Rheem's parts warranty — registered systems often carry a 10-year parts limited warranty, so confirm coverage before authorizing parts.
