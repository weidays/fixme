## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a single Rheem fault code — it's a symptom. Your Rheem or Ruud air conditioner may be running (the outdoor unit hums, the indoor blower moves air) but the air at the registers isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

On communicating **EcoNet** systems you may instead see a specific alert or fault on the thermostat (such as a low-pressure, high-pressure, or comms fault). On standard single- and two-stage units there's no display — you just feel warm air. This page covers the underlying causes when the unit is energized but failing to cool. If you instead have a flashing diagnostic LED on a heat pump board, see the related page below.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil into a block of ice.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so warm air comes out.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — packed with grass, cottonwood, or dirt, it can't reject heat, so the system runs but barely cools.
4. **Failed run/dual capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan won't start; you may hear a hum or clicking outside with no fan spin.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Rheem systems are sealed; if charge is low there is a leak. Cooling drops and the coil may ice over.
6. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse on the outdoor disconnect** — indoor blower runs, but the condenser is dead.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit doesn't pull in, so the compressor and fan never start.
8. **Thermostat issue or wiring fault** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a broken thermostat wire that never calls for cooling correctly.
9. **Clogged condensate drain triggering a float switch** — a full drain pan safety switch can shut down cooling on many setups.
10. **Failing compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit may try to start, trip on overload, or run without cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

- **Filter:** Pull it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it with the correct size. This is the #1 fix.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is several degrees below room temp. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check the indoor and outdoor breakers. If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — that's a real fault.
- **Vents and returns:** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Visually clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Don't remove panels.
- **Condensate drain:** If you see standing water in the drain pan or near the air handler, the float switch may have cut cooling. Clearing the drain line is reasonable DIY.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or copper lines, switch the system to **fan-only** to thaw it (can take a few hours) before running cooling again.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Rheem/Ruud tech should:

1. Verify the thermostat call and confirm 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. Check the run capacitor's microfarad rating with a meter against the nameplate.
3. Test the contactor for proper pull-in and clean/burned contacts.
4. Inspect both coils for dirt and ice, and confirm adequate airflow.
5. Attach gauges to read suction and head pressures and calculate **superheat/subcooling** against Rheem's charging chart for that model.
6. If charge is low, perform a leak search (electronic sniffer, dye, or nitrogen pressure test) — refrigerant should never just be "topped off" without finding the leak.
7. Check compressor windings (ohms) and amp draw against the nameplate.

Sanity check: any quote to "add refrigerant" without mentioning a leak search is a red flag. By law and good practice, the leak gets found and repaired.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Check duct/blower if it persists |
| Ice on indoor coil or lines | Low airflow or low charge | Set to fan-only to thaw; change filter | Leak search, recharge, airflow check |
| Outdoor fan hums, won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None (high voltage) | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit totally dead | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Unit short-cycles, trips breaker | Compressor or electrical fault | Stop resetting; call pro | Diagnose compressor/electrical |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Dirty condenser or low charge | Clear debris outside | Clean coil, check pressures |
| No cooling, water near air handler | Clogged condensate float switch | Clear drain line | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries / power | Replace batteries | Check 24V transformer/wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor), varying by region and contractor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$450
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **TXV / metering device:** $400–$900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,300–$2,500 (often worth comparing against full-system replacement on older units)

Note: R-410A and newer refrigerants vary in price, and labor to access a leak can dominate the bill. Get an itemized quote.
