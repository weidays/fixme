## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific Rheem fault code — it's the symptom homeowners report when the system runs but the house stays warm, or the air from the vents isn't cold. On modern Rheem units with a communicating control board (Prestige series and some Classic Plus models), the board may store a related fault or flash an LED pattern at the outdoor unit. On older single-stage units (RA13/RA14), there's often no code at all — just warm air.

Because "not cooling" covers everything from a clogged filter to a dead compressor, the goal here is to narrow it down: confirm airflow, confirm the outdoor unit is actually running, and identify whether you have a safe DIY fix or a job that needs a licensed tech with gauges and an EPA cert.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter** — the single most common cause. A clogged filter starves airflow, can freeze the indoor coil, and kills cooling capacity.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode (set to Fan instead of Cool), wrong setpoint, dead batteries, or a loose wire.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power, so only the indoor fan runs and blows room-temperature air.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit ices over and stops moving heat.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common Rheem outdoor failure. The fan may spin but the compressor hums and won't start, or nothing runs.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — a coil packed with grass, dust, or cottonwood can't reject heat, so the house never cools.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — warm or only slightly cool air, long run times, possible ice or hissing. Requires a pro.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the compressor pits or sticks, so the outdoor unit won't engage.
9. **Failed compressor** — least common but most expensive; the heart of the system has died.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Air filter** — pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. Run the system 30–60 minutes and recheck cooling.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace the batteries if it's a battery model and the screen is dim or blank.
- **Breaker** — check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers in your panel, plus the outdoor disconnect box near the condenser. If a breaker is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Supply and return vents** — make sure no registers are closed, blocked by furniture, or covered. Confirm the return grille is clear.
- **Condensate drain** — if the indoor unit has standing water or the float switch has tripped, a clogged drain can shut down cooling. Clearing a visible clog or emptying a full pan is fine.
- **Outdoor coil** — if the fins are buried in leaves or grass clippings, gently rinse from the outside with a garden hose (power off). Don't bend the fins or use a pressure washer.

If the coil is iced over, turn the system to **Off**, set the fan to **On** to help it thaw, and call a technician — running it frozen can damage the compressor.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Rheem tech follows a logical path so you can sanity-check the visit:

1. **Confirms the complaint** — measures the temperature split between return and supply air (typically 16–22°F on a healthy system).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Verifies power and controls** — tests voltage at the contactor, checks the thermostat call, and reads any stored fault on communicating boards.
4. **Tests the capacitor and contactor** — uses a meter to confirm the run capacitor is within rated microfarads and the contactor pulls in cleanly.
5. **Reads refrigerant pressures** — connects gauges to check suction/liquid pressures and superheat/subcooling. Low readings point to a leak.
6. **Leak search** — if low on charge, uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak before adding refrigerant.
7. **Checks the compressor** — measures amp draw and windings if it won't start or trips.

Be cautious of any tech who tops off refrigerant without searching for a leak, or quotes a compressor without testing the capacitor and contactor first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor fan runs, air not cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw coil with fan On | Restore airflow, check refrigerant |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Tripped breaker / disconnect off | Reset breaker once; check disconnect | Diagnose repeated trips, electrical fault |
| Compressor hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Warm air, long run times | Low refrigerant (leak) | Verify airflow only | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| House warm, coil iced over | Low airflow or low charge | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Find root cause, fix airflow/charge |
| Outdoor coil packed with debris | Dirty condenser | Rinse fins with hose, power off | Deep coil clean, verify pressures |
| Nothing runs at all | Contactor / control fault | Check thermostat and breakers | Test contactor, board, low-voltage |
| Cooling fades after years | Failing compressor | None — call a pro | Amp/winding test, replace compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges for a Rheem AC that isn't cooling (parts plus labor; regional and access differences apply):

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries** — $5 (DIY)
- **Service call / diagnostic** — $90–$200
- **Run/start capacitor** — $150–$400
- **Contactor** — $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning** — $150–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair** — $300–$1,500+ depending on location (coil leaks cost most)
- **Refrigerant recharge** — $150–$600+ depending on type and amount (R-410A vs. older R-22 is far pricier)
- **Evaporator coil replacement** — $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement** — $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth weighing against a new system if out of warranty)

Many Rheem units carry a 10-year parts warranty if registered, which can cut part costs significantly — labor is usually still your expense.
