## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Your Rheem air conditioner may be running (the outdoor fan spins, the indoor blower moves air) but the air coming from your vents is warm, lukewarm, or only slightly cool. On EcoNet-equipped systems you may see a wrench icon or a generic "service" alert rather than a numeric code.

This page covers cooling failures that are **not** explained by a flashing fault light (see Related codes). The underlying problem is almost always one of: restricted airflow, a frozen coil, low refrigerant from a leak, or an electrical/compressor failure.

Because diagnosing low cooling capacity usually requires refrigerant gauges, a multimeter, and EPA certification, this is rated a **pro** repair — but there are several safe checks you can do first that resolve the issue surprisingly often.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the coil. The #1 cause and the easiest to fix.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — ice on the indoor coil blocks heat transfer. Caused by low airflow or low refrigerant. The unit blows mild air and may "sweat" near the air handler.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Rheem systems are sealed; if charge is low, there's a leak. Symptoms include warm air, hissing, ice on the lineset, and longer run times.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a weak or dead capacitor keeps the compressor or fan from starting properly. Common after hot spells and on older units.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat outdoors.
6. **Compressor not running / failing** — outdoor fan spins but the compressor is silent or hums and trips. Often the most expensive outcome.
7. **Thermostat or control fault** — wrong mode, dead batteries, miswired or failed thermostat, or a tripped contactor not energizing the outdoor unit.
8. **Clogged condensate drain triggering a safety float switch** — shuts the system down to prevent overflow; common in humid climates.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks you should attempt yourself. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, remove electrical panels on the outdoor unit, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps shutting off.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Set the fan to AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone causes many "not cooling" calls.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel. Central AC usually has two breakers (indoor air handler + outdoor condenser). Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many rooms can freeze the coil.
- **Ice check:** Look at the refrigerant lines and indoor coil area. If you see ice or frost, **turn the system off at the thermostat and let it thaw fully (often 1–4 hours)** before running again. Running iced is the fast track to compressor damage.
- **Condensate drain:** If there's standing water in the pan or a wet floor near the air handler, a clogged drain may have tripped a float switch. Clearing the drain line is reasonable DIY; drain tablets help prevent recurrence.
- **Outdoor unit:** With the unit off, look for obvious leaves, grass clippings, or debris choking the condenser coil. Gentle rinsing with a garden hose (water only, low pressure) is acceptable.

If cooling doesn't return after a clean filter, a thawed coil, and a clear drain, the remaining causes need a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent tech should work through this sequence — use it to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Verify airflow** — checks filter, blower operation, and static pressure. Confirms the coil isn't frozen.
2. **Measure temperature split** — compares return-air and supply-air temps. A healthy split is roughly 14–22°F. A low split points to charge or airflow problems.
3. **Check capacitor and contactor** — tests the run capacitor's microfarad rating and confirms the contactor pulls in to energize the compressor.
4. **Connect refrigerant gauges** — reads suction and head pressures, calculates superheat/subcooling against Rheem's charging chart for that model. This reveals undercharge (leak) or overcharge.
5. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses electronic detector or dye to locate the leak (coil, lineset, valve, or Schrader).
6. **Compressor test** — checks windings, amp draw, and start behavior if the compressor won't run or trips.

Be cautious of any tech who tops off refrigerant without searching for a leak — that's a temporary fix that vents regulated refrigerant and you'll be paying again next season.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, dirty filter | Restricted airflow | Replace filter, run again | None if resolved |
| Mild air, ice on indoor coil/lines | Frozen coil (low airflow or low charge) | Turn off, let thaw fully, replace filter | Find airflow or refrigerant cause |
| Warm air, hissing, ice on lineset | Low refrigerant / leak | Turn off, call pro | Locate & repair leak, recharge |
| Outdoor fan runs, compressor silent/humming | Failed run capacitor | Note symptom, call pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit caked in debris | Dirty condenser coil | Gentle hose rinse (power off) | Deep coil clean if needed |
| Nothing happens outdoors | Tripped breaker / bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, find trip cause |
| System off, water near air handler | Clogged drain / float switch | Clear drain line, add tablets | Clear blockage, test switch |
| Outdoor fan spins, no cooling, trips breaker | Compressor failing | Stop resetting, call pro | Compressor test / replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Regional rates and EcoNet diagnostics vary.

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 pro
- **Coil cleaning (condenser or evaporator):** $100–$400
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair (lineset/valve):** $300–$1,500 depending on location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,000+ (often worth comparing against a full system replacement on older units)
- **Diagnostic / service call fee:** $80–$200 (often credited toward repair)

If your unit is more than 12–15 years old and the compressor or coil has failed, ask for a replacement quote alongside the repair — a major component on aged equipment is often not cost-effective.
