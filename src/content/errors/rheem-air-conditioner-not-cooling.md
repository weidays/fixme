## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a single fault code on Rheem and Ruud air conditioners — it is a symptom. Your system may be running, blowing air, and drawing power, yet the air coming from your vents is not cold. On some Rheem systems with communicating thermostats (EcoNet) or modern air handlers, a related fault may appear, but most single-stage condensers have no display, so you diagnose by behavior.

The job of an air conditioner is to move heat from inside your home to outside. When it can't cool, the breakdown is in one of four places: **airflow** (the indoor side can't move enough air), **the refrigerant circuit** (low charge or a blockage), **the electrical components** (capacitor, contactor, compressor), or **the controls** (thermostat or low-voltage wiring). This page helps you sort which one you're facing.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter or blocked indoor coil.** Restricted airflow is the number-one cause. It reduces cooling and can ice the evaporator coil, which then blows warm air.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil.** Grass, dust, and cottonwood seed clog the outdoor coil so it can't reject heat. The compressor runs but cooling drops sharply.
3. **Failed run capacitor.** A weak or dead capacitor stops the compressor and/or fan motor from starting. You may hear humming from the outdoor unit but the fan won't spin.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak.** Causes weak cooling, longer run times, ice on the suction line, and rising energy bills. Always a leak — never a normal "top-off."
5. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse / failed contactor.** No power to the condenser means the outdoor unit never starts even though the indoor blower runs.
6. **Thermostat fault or dead batteries.** Wrong settings, dead batteries, or a wiring issue can stop the cooling call from reaching the equipment.
7. **Frozen evaporator coil.** A downstream result of low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
8. **Failed compressor.** Least common but most expensive. The system runs but produces no cooling, or trips the breaker on start.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should perform. If they don't fix it, stop and call a licensed HVAC technician.

- **Air filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray and clogged, replace it with the correct size. A dirty filter is the cheapest possible cause.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Try lowering the setpoint a few degrees and wait for the system to respond.
- **Breaker:** Check the breakers for both the indoor air handler/furnace and the outdoor condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, leave it off and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure registers are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs, and that return grilles aren't covered.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for grass clippings, leaves, or debris packed against the condenser. With the unit off, you can gently clear loose debris and trim back plants 18–24 inches around it.
- **Condensate drain:** If there's water pooling near the indoor unit, a clogged drain may have tripped a safety float switch that stops cooling. You can clear a visible blockage, but don't disable any switch.
- **Ice:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to **OFF** and run the fan only to thaw it (this can take a few hours). Running it iced up can damage the compressor.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the cooling call** at the thermostat and confirm 24V is reaching the condenser contactor.
2. **Check airflow** — filter, blower operation, and coil cleanliness — before touching refrigerant.
3. **Measure electrical components** — capacitor microfarads against the rating, contactor function, and compressor/fan amp draw.
4. **Connect refrigerant gauges** and take temperature readings to calculate **superheat and subcooling**. This tells them whether the charge is correct, low (leak), or overcharged.
5. **Leak search** if the charge is low — using electronic detectors or dye. Reputable techs find and fix the leak rather than just adding refrigerant.
6. **Compressor test** — if everything else checks out and the compressor won't start or is grounded, they'll confirm with a meter before quoting a replacement.

Be wary of any quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" or "replace the compressor" without checking airflow and electrical components first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air from vents not cold, weak airflow | Clogged filter / dirty coil | Replace filter, clear outdoor unit | Clean evaporator & condenser coils |
| Outdoor unit hums, fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Long run times, ice on lines, high bills | Low refrigerant (leak) | Turn off to thaw ice | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, wiring, breaker |
| Nothing responds | Thermostat / dead batteries | Replace batteries, check settings | Test low-voltage circuit & wiring |
| Water near indoor unit, cooling stopped | Clogged condensate, float tripped | Clear visible clog | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Coil iced over | Low airflow or low charge | Run fan to thaw | Diagnose airflow vs. refrigerant |
| Unit trips breaker on start | Failing compressor / electrical | Don't keep resetting | Test compressor, quote replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Your price varies by region and model (two-stage and inverter units cost more).

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY).
- **Coil cleaning (condenser + evaporator):** $100–$400.
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$400.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350.
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$300.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and amount of refrigerant.
- **TXV (metering valve) replacement:** $400–$900.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ (often worth comparing against a full system replacement on older units).

If your Rheem system is under 10 years old, check whether the compressor or parts are covered under Rheem's standard limited warranty — you may owe only labor.
