## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a stored fault code — Rheem air conditioners don't display a numeric error for it. It means the system is powered and may even be running, but the air at your supply registers isn't cold enough to satisfy the thermostat. Sometimes the unit short-cycles; sometimes it runs continuously without ever reaching setpoint.

The root cause falls into one of three buckets: **airflow problems** (filter, coil, blower), **heat-rejection problems** (dirty condenser, failed fan or capacitor), or **refrigerant problems** (leak, undercharge). Several of these have safe homeowner checks; the rest require a licensed technician.

This page applies broadly to Rheem split-system and packaged AC units across the RA13–RA20, Endeavor, and Classic lines. Exact protection features, refrigerant type (R-410A vs. newer A2L blends on 2025+ models), and control behavior vary by model and install year — where it matters, we say so.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops cooling output and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator (indoor) coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so air blows warm.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — leaves, grass, and grime trap heat the unit is trying to reject, killing capacity on hot days.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a weak or bulged capacitor stops the compressor or outdoor fan from starting; you may hear a hum or clicking.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, ice on the line, or longer run times. Always a leak — never a normal "low" condition.
6. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (HEAT/FAN ON vs. AUTO), or a failed thermostat.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power while the indoor blower still runs.
8. **Failed contactor or compressor** — compressor won't engage or has failed internally; this is the most expensive outcome.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Stop if anything is unclear.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone can stop cooling and freeze the coil.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and **AUTO** (not FAN ON), and that setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor AC breakers. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure registers are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain:** A full drain pan can trip a float switch and shut down cooling. Look for standing water near the indoor unit; clear obvious clogs at the drain line opening.
- **Frozen coil / iced line:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** and run the fan on **ON** for a few hours to thaw it, then restart in COOL. If it ices again, you have an airflow or refrigerant problem that needs a tech.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass the float switch or safety controls, or repeatedly reset a unit that won't stay running.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical path so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measures supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and indoor coil for ice or dirt.
3. **Inspects the condenser** — coil cleanliness, fan operation, and clearance.
4. **Tests electricals** — measures the run capacitor with a meter (not by eye), checks the contactor, and confirms voltage at the disconnect.
5. **Connects gauges** — reads suction and head pressures and superheat/subcooling to determine if the charge is correct. Low readings point to a leak.
6. **Leak search if undercharged** — electronic detector, soap bubbles, or dye to find the leak before adding refrigerant.
7. **Compressor test** — checks amp draw and windings if the compressor won't run or trips on overload.

Be wary of any quote that adds refrigerant without finding a leak, or replaces a compressor without confirming it's actually failed.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Verify airflow, check blower |
| Ice on indoor coil or copper line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw with fan ON | Find cause: airflow or leak |
| Outdoor unit runs, house won't cool | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse coil gently with hose (power off) | Deep coil clean, charge check |
| Outdoor fan/compressor hums but won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Gradual loss of cooling over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None — call pro | Leak detection and repair, recharge |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Find why it tripped; replace fuse |
| No cooling, thermostat blank | Dead batteries / thermostat fault | Replace batteries | Replace or rewire thermostat |
| Unit clicks but compressor won't engage | Failed contactor or compressor | None — call pro | Test contactor and compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Diagnostic/service-call fees ($75–$200) usually apply and may be credited toward the repair.

- **Air filter:** $5–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5–$15 (DIY)
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $75–$250
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate float switch / drain clearing:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500+ (depends on leak location and refrigerant type; R-410A and newer A2L blends cost more)
- **Outdoor fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often near the cost of a new condenser — get a replacement-vs-repair opinion)
