## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code — it's the symptom most Rheem and Ruud air-conditioner owners describe when the system runs but the house stays warm. Your thermostat is calling for cool, the indoor blower may be moving air, but either the air isn't cold or there isn't enough of it.

On communicating **EcoNet** systems you may see a fault or status message on the thermostat or control board; on standard single- and two-stage units there's no display, so you diagnose by symptom. Because "not cooling" can be anything from a $20 filter to a $1,800 compressor, this page helps you rule out the easy stuff and understand what a tech should be checking.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked return** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow makes the indoor coil ice over, which kills cooling capacity.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse at the outdoor unit** — the indoor blower runs on a separate circuit, so the fan can blow warm air while the condenser is dead.
3. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll often see ice on the copper line or water around the indoor unit.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a common failure that prevents the compressor or condenser fan from starting; you may hear a hum or clicking outside.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system can't absorb heat. This always means a leak somewhere, not normal "use up."
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil can't reject heat when it's caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood.
7. **Bad contactor** — the electrical relay that powers the outdoor unit can stick or burn out.
8. **Thermostat or wiring fault** — dead batteries, a miswired or failed thermostat, or a broken low-voltage wire stops the cool call.
9. **Failed compressor** — least common but most expensive; the heart of the system won't pump.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no tools beyond a flashlight:

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and at least 3°F below room temperature. If it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank, replace the batteries.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it against a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of no-cool.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure none are closed, blocked by furniture, or covered by rugs.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser so air can flow freely. Gently rinse heavy dirt off the outer fins with a garden hose (power off first).
- **Condensate drain:** If there's water pooling near the indoor unit, a clogged drain may have triggered a safety float switch that shuts cooling off. Clear visible clogs and check the pan.
- **Ice:** If you see ice on the coil or refrigerant line, turn the system to **OFF** but leave the fan **ON** to thaw it (can take a few hours), then replace the filter before restarting.

Do **not** add refrigerant, open the sealed system, or bypass any safety switch. Those are pro-only jobs.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Rheem tech follows a logical path so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirms the call** — verifies the thermostat is sending 24V to cool and that the indoor blower energizes.
2. **Checks airflow and the coil** — inspects the filter and evaporator coil for ice or dirt; measures temperature drop across the coil (a healthy split is usually about 16–22°F).
3. **Tests the outdoor electrical** — measures voltage at the contactor, tests the run/start capacitor with a meter, and checks the contactor for pitting.
4. **Reads refrigerant pressures** — connects gauges to read suction and head pressure and calculates superheat/subcooling. Abnormal readings point to undercharge (leak), overcharge, or a restriction.
5. **Performs a leak search** — if refrigerant is low, uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak rather than just adding gas.
6. **Tests the compressor** — checks amp draw, windings, and whether it starts and pumps.

Ask for the measured numbers (temperature split, capacitor microfarads, refrigerant pressures). A tech who can show you readings is diagnosing, not guessing.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air from vents is weak or warm | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents | Check blower, ductwork, coil |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker or bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor, find trip cause |
| Ice on coil or copper line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off (fan on) to thaw, swap filter | Measure charge, find leak, check airflow |
| Outdoor unit hums or clicks, won't start | Failed run/start capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Vents barely cool, runs constantly | Low refrigerant (leak) or dirty condenser | Rinse outdoor coil | Leak search, seal & recharge |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries or wiring fault | Replace batteries | Test 24V signal, repair wiring/stat |
| Water near indoor unit, cooling stopped | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear visible clog | Clear drain line, test float switch |
| Unit runs but never cools, high amp draw | Failed compressor | None — call pro | Test compressor, quote replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and access):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Coil cleaning (condenser or evaporator):** $150–$450
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $600–$1,500 (more if the coil itself must be replaced)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth weighing against a new unit if the system is 10+ years old)

If your AC is out of warranty and needs a compressor or coil, get a second quote and compare against full replacement cost.
