## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code on a Rheem air conditioner — it's a symptom. Your system may run, blow air, and show no flashing lights, yet the air coming from the vents is room temperature or barely cool. On EcoNet-equipped systems you may see a status message in the app, but most standard Rheem units give no specific code for poor cooling.

The job of the AC is to move heat out of your home using refrigerant, a compressor, and two coils (indoor evaporator, outdoor condenser). When any link in that chain is restricted, dirty, low on charge, or electrically weak, you lose cooling capacity. Because diagnosing it correctly often requires gauges and refrigerant certification, this is rated a **pro** repair — though several of the most common causes are things you can rule out yourself in minutes.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow over the evaporator coil, reducing cooling and sometimes freezing the coil solid.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, leaves, and dust blanket the fins so heat can't escape; cooling drops sharply on hot days.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system can't absorb enough heat. Often shows ice on the copper lines or indoor coil.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a weak or bulged capacitor stops the compressor or outdoor fan from starting; you may hear humming.
5. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck, so the condenser never runs.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low charge; ice blocks airflow and kills cooling entirely.
7. **Thermostat or wiring fault** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a broken low-voltage wire prevents a cooling call.
8. **Failing compressor** — the costliest cause; the unit hums or trips the breaker but won't pump refrigerant.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening sealed refrigerant lines or electrical panels beyond the breaker.

- **Filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 DIY-fixable cause.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Breaker:** Check the breaker panel for a tripped AC or air-handler breaker. Reset **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look (don't touch) for the fan spinning and clear away leaves, grass clippings, or debris within a foot of the unit. Gently rinse loose surface debris off the exterior fins with a garden hose — never a pressure washer.
- **Condensate drain:** If the indoor unit has a clogged drain, a float switch may shut cooling off. Check the drain line for standing water and add cleaning tablets to the pan.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, switch the system to **FAN ONLY** for a few hours to thaw it, then call a technician — ice means low airflow or low refrigerant.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Rheem tech follows a logical path, and knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirms the cooling call** — verifies the thermostat is sending 24V to the contactor.
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects the filter, blower, and evaporator coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Measures electrical components** — tests the capacitor's microfarad rating against spec and inspects the contactor for pitting.
4. **Attaches refrigerant gauges** — reads suction and head pressures and calculates superheat/subcooling to judge the charge.
5. **Checks temperature split** — a healthy system drops supply air 16-22°F below return air; a small split points to charge or airflow problems.
6. **Leak search** — if charge is low, a tech should locate the leak with an electronic detector or dye, not just "top it off."
7. **Compressor test** — measures windings and amp draw if the compressor won't start or trips the breaker.

Be wary of any quote that adds refrigerant without finding the leak first — that's a temporary fix you'll pay for again.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower & coil if persists |
| Outdoor unit running, indoor coil iced | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Set to FAN to thaw; replace filter | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor fan off, compressor hums | Failed capacitor | None — power off unit | Test & replace capacitor |
| Condenser never starts, no hum | Failed contactor | Check breaker once | Replace contactor |
| Cooling poor on hot days only | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse loose debris with hose | Professional coil cleaning |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or wiring fault | Stop resetting; power off | Diagnose compressor/electrical |
| No cooling at all, thermostat blank | Dead batteries / no power | Replace batteries, check breaker | Trace low-voltage wiring |
| Water around indoor unit, AC shuts off | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear line, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10-$30 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0-$150
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150-$350
- **Contactor:** $150-$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500-$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs R-32 on newer Rheem units)
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,000-$2,200
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200-$2,500 (often worth comparing against full system replacement on older units)
- **Diagnostic/service call:** $80-$200, frequently credited toward the repair

Refrigerant cost varies widely by type — older R-410A systems and newer R-32 EcoNet models price differently, so confirm which your unit uses.
