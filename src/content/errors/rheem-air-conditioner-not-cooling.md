## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's the symptom you see when your Rheem (or Ruud) air conditioner is powered and possibly running, but the air coming from your vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint. Unlike a heat pump with a flashing diagnostic light, most straight-cool Rheem split systems give you no numeric code; you diagnose by behavior, airflow, and (on EcoNet/communicating models) any alerts in the app.

The underlying cause falls into one of three buckets:

- **Airflow problems** — the system makes cold refrigerant but can't move air across it (dirty filter, blocked coil, blower issue).
- **Refrigeration problems** — low refrigerant, a failed compressor, or a stuck reversing component, so no real cooling happens.
- **Electrical/control problems** — a bad capacitor, contactor, fan motor, or thermostat that stops a component from starting.

This page is rated **pro** because the most damaging causes (refrigerant and compressor) require certified tools and can't be safely DIYed.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow can ice the indoor coil and kill cooling output.
2. **Dirty condenser or evaporator coil** — caked-on dirt insulates the coil so heat can't transfer. Very common after a season or two without service.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a swollen or dead dual capacitor stops the compressor and/or condenser fan from starting. The outdoor unit may hum but not run.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, warm vents, possible ice on the copper line. Always indicates a leak; needs repair plus recharge.
5. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit dead while indoor blower still runs.
6. **Failed condenser fan motor** — outdoor fan won't spin, unit overheats and shuts off on high-pressure or thermal limit.
7. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit fails to pull in.
8. **Thermostat fault or wrong setting** — dead batteries, set to "fan" not "cool," or a miswired/failed smart stat.
9. **Frozen evaporator coil** — usually downstream of low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks air entirely.
10. **Failed compressor** — least common but most expensive; unit may try to start, trip, or run with no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should perform. Do them in order.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for a tripped breaker (often a dedicated double breaker for the outdoor unit). Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — a repeat trip means a fault.
- **Vents and returns:** Make sure supply registers and return grilles aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for obvious debris, tall grass, or leaves choking the condenser coil. Clear anything within about two feet. Don't open panels or remove the top.
- **Condensate:** If you see standing water or a full drain pan, a clogged drain or a safety float switch may be shutting the system off. Clearing a visible clog is fine; opening sealed components is not.
- **Ice:** If the indoor coil or the large copper line is frosted, turn the system to **OFF** and run the fan only to thaw it (can take a few hours) before calling. Don't run cooling on a frozen coil.

Do **not** add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, open the sealed system, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech will work through a logical sequence — knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint and airflow** — check filter, blower operation, and measure temperature drop (supply vs. return) across the indoor coil. A healthy split system shows roughly an 18–22°F split.
2. **Electrical checks** — measure capacitor microfarads against the rating stamped on it, test the contactor, and confirm voltage at the compressor and fan terminals.
3. **Refrigerant pressures** — connect gauges to read suction and liquid pressures, then calculate superheat and subcooling to confirm whether the charge is correct, low (leak), or overcharged.
4. **Leak search** — if charge is low, use an electronic detector or dye to locate the leak before any recharge. A reputable shop won't just top off without addressing the leak.
5. **Component test** — check the condenser fan motor, compressor windings (amp draw and resistance), and reversing/control components on EcoNet models via the app or board diagnostics.
6. **Quote** — they should tell you the specific failed part, not just "it needs refrigerant."

Ask to see the capacitor reading or the subcooling/superheat numbers — a tech who measured them can show you.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vents blow but air isn't cold | Dirty filter or low refrigerant | Replace filter; check thermostat | Measure pressures, find/repair leak |
| Outdoor unit hums but fan/compressor won't start | Failed capacitor | None — verify breaker only | Test & replace dual capacitor |
| Outdoor unit completely dead | Tripped breaker, bad contactor, blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor/fuse, find cause |
| Ice on coil or copper line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Diagnose airflow vs. charge |
| Outdoor fan not spinning, unit shuts off | Failed condenser fan motor | Clear debris around unit | Replace fan motor & capacitor |
| House never reaches setpoint on hot days | Dirty condenser coil, undersized charge | Rinse exterior debris (power off) | Deep coil clean, verify charge |
| Unit runs constantly, weak cooling | Failed/weak compressor | None | Test compressor, recommend repair/replace |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries, blown 24V fuse | Replace batteries | Replace fuse, check wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor; your region and contractor vary.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650.
- **Low-voltage fuse / minor wiring:** $80–$200.
- **Coil cleaning (condenser or evaporator):** $150–$450.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A units cost more as supply tightens).
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+; on an older unit, replacing the whole system often makes more sense.

If your Rheem AC is out of warranty and the compressor or coil has failed, get a quote for a full system replacement too — repair often isn't worth it past 12–15 years.
