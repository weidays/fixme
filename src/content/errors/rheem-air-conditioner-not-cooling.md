## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Your Rheem air conditioner may be running normally at the thermostat, the outdoor condenser may be humming, yet the air from the vents stays warm or the house never reaches setpoint.

On EcoNet-equipped Rheem systems, you may also see a fault or status code on the thermostat or control board that points to a specific cause (low pressure, high pressure, locked rotor, etc.). On older or standard models with no smart board, there's no code at all — you diagnose by symptom. **Display behavior varies by model**, so don't assume a missing code means nothing is wrong.

This is rated a **pro-level** issue because the most expensive causes — refrigerant leaks, compressor failure, and sealed-system problems — legally and safely require a licensed HVAC technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops coil temperature, and can freeze the evaporator. The single most common and cheapest cause.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so the unit blows warm.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass clippings, dust, and cottonwood fluff insulate the coil so it can't reject heat.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — cooling fades over weeks; you may see ice on the lineset or hear hissing. Requires EPA-certified repair.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan won't start; you may hear a hum or clicking outside.
6. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser is pitted or stuck, so the outdoor unit never energizes.
7. **Tripped breaker or float switch** — a full condensate pan can trip a safety switch that kills cooling.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a loose C-wire.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the compressor overheats and shuts down on its high-pressure or overload protection.
10. **Compressor failure** — the most serious and expensive cause; the unit runs but produces no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps shutting off.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it uses them.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of no cooling.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser disconnect) breakers. If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris at least two feet around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside of the coil with a garden hose (power off).
- **Condensate drain/pan:** If you see standing water or a full pan, the float switch may have shut the system down. Empty the pan and check that the drain line isn't clogged.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or the copper lines, turn the system to FAN ONLY (cooling OFF) for a few hours to let it thaw, then call a technician — ice means a deeper problem.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent tech follows a logical sequence — use this to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** and check thermostat call, mode, and temperature split (return vs. supply air, typically 15–20°F when healthy).
2. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower operation, and coil condition.
3. **Check electrical** — incoming voltage, capacitor microfarad reading, contactor condition, and fan/compressor amp draw against the nameplate.
4. **Attach gauges** to read suction and head pressure (this is where refrigerant level and superheat/subcooling are evaluated).
5. **Leak search** if refrigerant is low — electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test. A proper diagnosis finds *and* fixes the leak.
6. **Read EcoNet/board codes** if equipped, to confirm low-pressure, high-pressure, or locked-rotor faults.

Be wary of any tech who wants to "just add a pound of Freon" without searching for the leak — that's a temporary fix that violates EPA best practice.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vents blow warm, unit runs | Dirty filter / low airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Check airflow, coil, blower |
| Ice on indoor coil or lines | Frozen evaporator | Set to FAN ONLY to thaw | Find cause: airflow or low charge |
| Cooling fades over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None — call a pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call a pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit completely dead | Contactor / breaker / disconnect | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Indoor blower runs, outdoor off | Contactor or control fault | Check breaker | Test low-voltage circuit |
| System stops, water near furnace | Full condensate pan / float switch | Empty pan, clear drain | Clear line, replace pump/switch |
| Unit runs constantly, no cooling | Compressor failure | None — call a pro | Compressor test/replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Your area, model, and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22) affect the total.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate pump or float switch:** $150–$450
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$700
- **Coil cleaning (deep):** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 (more if the coil must be replaced)
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $900–$2,500+
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,800+ (often the point where replacing the whole condenser or system makes more sense)

If your Rheem is under 10 years old, check warranty — Rheem's standard parts warranty (typically 10 years with registration) may cover the part while you pay labor.
