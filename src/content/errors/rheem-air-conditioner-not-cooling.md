## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific Rheem fault code. Your air conditioner may be running — the outdoor fan spinning, the indoor blower moving air — but the air at your registers is warm, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Rheem split systems and packaged units don't display a single "not cooling" code on a screen. Instead you diagnose it by working through the system: airflow, electrical components, and the refrigerant circuit. If you have a **Rheem EcoNet communicating system**, the EcoNet app or smart thermostat may show a specific fault (low pressure, compressor lockout, etc.) — read that first, because it narrows the search dramatically. Behavior here varies by control board and model, so treat the app message as the most authoritative starting point if you have one.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops capacity and can freeze the coil.
2. **Dirty condenser or evaporator coil** — caked dirt insulates the coil so it can't reject or absorb heat.
3. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a cheap, common failure that stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system runs but can't cool. This is always a leak, never "normal use."
6. **Thermostat problem or wrong setting** — dead batteries, set to "fan" instead of "cool," or a wiring/calibration fault.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** to the outdoor unit, so the condenser never runs.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor or contactor** — the compressor can't stay running without the fan.
9. **Failed compressor** — the least common but most expensive. The unit hums but won't cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening the sealed system or electrical panels.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. This fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker to the indoor and outdoor units. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting it.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside coil fins with a garden hose (water only) if they look dirty.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut down cooling. Check for standing water in the drain pan and clear the visible line if you can.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, switch the system to **OFF**, set the fan to **ON** to thaw it, and let it fully melt (a few hours) before restarting.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician follows a logical sequence — this is what you're paying for:

1. **Confirm the complaint** and read any EcoNet/board fault messages.
2. **Check airflow:** filter, blower operation, return/supply temperature split (a healthy system drops air about 16–22°F across the coil).
3. **Inspect coils** indoor and outdoor for fouling and ice.
4. **Electrical tests:** measure capacitor microfarads, verify the contactor pulls in, confirm the compressor and fan motor draw correct amperage.
5. **Refrigerant gauges:** connect to read suction and head pressures and calculate superheat/subcooling. This tells them if the charge is correct, low (leak), or overcharged.
6. **Leak search** if charge is low — electronic detector, bubbles, or dye. A reputable tech repairs the leak; they don't just "top off" and leave.

Ask for the measured temperature split and pressure readings. A quote that jumps straight to "you need refrigerant" or "you need a compressor" without these numbers deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blower runs, air is warm | Dirty filter or low refrigerant | Replace filter | Test charge, find/repair leak |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Correct airflow or charge |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Failed capacitor or fan motor | Check breaker only | Replace capacitor/motor |
| Unit hums but won't start | Failed capacitor or compressor | None | Test capacitor, then compressor |
| Outdoor unit completely dead | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, trace fault |
| Water around indoor unit / shutdown | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible drain line | Clear line, test float switch |
| House won't reach setpoint | Dirty coils or undercharge | Rinse outdoor coil, new filter | Clean coils, verify charge |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries / no power | Replace batteries | Trace control wiring/transformer |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Your area, model, and refrigerant type (R-410A vs newer R-454B in 2025+ units) all affect price.

- **Air filter:** $5–$40 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$200
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$450
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Coil cleaning (in-depth):** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth comparing against full system replacement if out of warranty)

Many Rheem units carry a 10-year parts warranty if registered — confirm your serial number's coverage before approving major parts, as it can cut the bill significantly.
