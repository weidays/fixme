## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom rather than a stored fault code on most Rheem residential air conditioners. Your system may be running, blowing air, or even showing no error at all, yet the air coming out of the vents is not cold or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Rheem split systems cool by moving heat outdoors through the refrigerant cycle. For that to work, you need three things at once: adequate airflow across both the indoor coil and outdoor coil, a charged and leak-free refrigerant loop, and a working compressor with its electrical components. A failure in any one of those breaks cooling.

Some Prestige and Endeavor units with communicating controls (EcoNet) will log specific diagnostic codes you can read at the thermostat. If you have a fault light or code, check our related Rheem fault-light guide first.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops cooling capacity and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuse** at the outdoor unit — the indoor blower runs but the condenser never starts.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan motor hums but won't start, or the fan spins slowly.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; you may see ice on the lineset or no airflow.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — debris, grass clippings, or cottonwood blocking heat rejection.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling over a season, often with a hissing sound or oil residue at fittings.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser is pitted or stuck.
8. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a wiring/wifi fault not calling for cooling.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit may trip the breaker or hum without starting.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are all things a homeowner can safely do:

- **Filter:** Pull the air filter. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. Run the system 30 minutes and recheck cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL, the fan is on AUTO, and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it uses them.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor air handler/furnace breaker and the outdoor condenser breaker. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor unit:** Make sure the condenser fan is spinning and the coil fins are clear of leaves, grass, and debris. Gently hose off the exterior fins with the power off if they're caked.
- **Vents and registers:** Confirm supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate:** Check the drain pan and line. A full pan or a tripped float switch will shut the system down. Empty standing water and clear visible clogs.
- **Ice:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant lines, turn the system to OFF and the fan to ON to thaw it (can take several hours), then call a technician — ice means an airflow or refrigerant problem.

Do not open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass the float/condensate switch, or repeatedly reset a tripping breaker.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence so you can sanity-check the bill:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** at the thermostat and confirm 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. **Check airflow** — inspect filter, blower, and coil; measure temperature split across the indoor coil (should be roughly 16-22°F).
3. **Inspect electrical components** — test the capacitor with a meter, check the contactor for pitting, and measure compressor and fan motor amp draw.
4. **Read refrigerant pressures** with gauges on the service ports and compare to the charging chart for your model and outdoor temperature.
5. **Leak search** if charge is low — using electronic detectors, soap bubbles, or dye, because a recharge without a repair is a temporary fix.
6. **Test the compressor** for windings continuity and start behavior if it won't run.

Ask the tech to show you the capacitor reading or pressure numbers. A legitimate diagnosis includes measured values, not just "it needs refrigerant."

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil with fan ON | Confirm airflow, check charge |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker or fuse | Reset breaker once | Replace fuse, find why it tripped |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on lines or coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Thaw with fan ON, replace filter | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Gradual loss of cooling over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None | Locate and seal leak, recharge |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Short or failing compressor | Stop resetting, call pro | Diagnose electrical fault |
| No cold air, thermostat blank | Dead batteries / control fault | Replace batteries | Test thermostat and wiring |
| Outdoor coil caked with debris | Dirty condenser | Hose off fins, power off | Deep coil cleaning |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10-$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150-$400 installed
- **Contactor:** $150-$350 installed
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300-$650 installed
- **Coil cleaning (deep):** $150-$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400-$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs older systems)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000-$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500-$2,500+ (often worth comparing to full system replacement on older units)

A standard diagnostic service call typically runs $80-$180 and is often credited toward the repair.
