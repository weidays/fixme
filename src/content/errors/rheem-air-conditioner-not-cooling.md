## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code — it's the symptom homeowners search for when a Rheem or Ruud air conditioner runs but the house won't get cold, or the outdoor unit won't start at all. Rheem split systems don't usually display a lettered/numbered error on the AC itself the way furnaces and heat pumps do; you'll notice warm air from the vents, a system that runs constantly, or an outdoor condenser that's silent or just humming.

This page covers the well-documented causes for that behavior across Rheem/Ruud split-system condensers and matched air handlers. Because a communicating thermostat or an Endeavor-line control board *can* log related codes, always check your thermostat and unit for any displayed fault first — behavior varies by model and board year.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil so no cold air moves.
2. **Frozen evaporator (indoor) coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer and vents blow warm.
3. **Failed dual run capacitor** — very common; the outdoor fan and/or compressor hum, buzz, or won't start.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse at the disconnect** — outdoor unit gets no power and stays silent.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil caked with dirt/cottonwood can't reject heat; unit runs but barely cools.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — long run times, warm air, ice on the copper line; requires a licensed tech.
7. **Failed condenser fan motor** — fan doesn't spin, unit overheats and trips out.
8. **Contactor failure** — the relay that powers the condenser is pitted or stuck, so the compressor never energizes.
9. **Thermostat or control wiring fault** — no cooling call reaches the outdoor unit; varies by thermostat model.
10. **Failing compressor** — worst case; unit hums, draws high current, trips out, or won't build cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. If none fix it, stop and call a pro.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and inspect. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. This is the single most common cause.
- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and 5°F below room temp. Confirm it's on AUTO or that the fan runs. If it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank, install fresh batteries.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers in the main panel. Flip a tripped breaker fully OFF, then ON — **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a tech; repeated resets can damage equipment.
- **Vents & registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or the copper line at the outdoor unit, turn the system to OFF and set the thermostat fan to ON to help it thaw (can take a few hours). Do not run cooling on an iced unit.
- **Condensate:** A full drain pan or a tripped float switch can shut cooling down. Check for standing water near the air handler and clear a visibly clogged drain line if you can reach it safely.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around and off the condenser. Never remove panels or touch internal parts.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent tech should:

1. Verify a cooling call is reaching the outdoor unit (thermostat and low-voltage check at the contactor).
2. Confirm power at the disconnect and inspect the contactor for pitting or stuck contacts.
3. **Test the dual run capacitor** with a meter — a bulged or out-of-spec cap is a frequent, cheap find.
4. Check condenser fan and compressor amp draw against the nameplate.
5. Inspect indoor and outdoor coils for dirt and the evaporator for icing.
6. **Attach gauges and read superheat/subcooling** to judge refrigerant charge — and if it's low, leak-search rather than just topping off.
7. Confirm airflow at the air handler (filter, blower speed, ductwork).

If a quote jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without any mention of finding the leak, ask why. Topping off a leaking system is a temporary and non-compliant fix.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vents blow warm, house won't cool | Dirty filter / airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Check blower, ducts, coil |
| Ice on indoor coil or copper line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Find cause; leak-search, recharge |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None (electrical) | Test & replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker / fuse | Reset breaker once | Find cause of trip, replace fuse |
| Fan not spinning outside | Fan motor or capacitor | Turn off to prevent overheat | Test & replace motor/cap |
| Runs constantly, weak cooling | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse gently with hose | Deep-clean coil, verify charge |
| Long runs, warm air, high bills | Low refrigerant (leak) | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Compressor hums, trips breaker | Failing compressor / contactor | None | Amp test; replace part |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Breaker reset / minor fix:** $0 DIY, or ~$90–$150 service call
- **Dual run capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Coil cleaning (evap or condenser):** $150–$500
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500+ depending on leak location; evap coil replacement runs higher
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500 (often worth comparing to a new condenser if the unit is old or out of warranty)

Rheem compressors and coils may carry a 5–10 year parts warranty if the unit was registered — check your model and registration before paying for parts.
