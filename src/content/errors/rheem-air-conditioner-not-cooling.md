## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a single Rheem fault code — it's a symptom. Your Rheem condenser and indoor blower may both be running, yet the air from your vents is room temperature or only slightly cool. On units with a control board and diagnostic LED, you may see a flashing fault light pointing to a specific issue (see Related codes). On older single-stage units, there's often no code at all — just warm air.

The root problem is almost always one of three things: **airflow** (the system can't move enough air across the coils), **refrigerant** (the charge is low because of a leak), or an **electrical/component failure** (capacitor, contactor, or compressor). This page helps you tell them apart and sanity-check any quote you receive.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter or blocked return** — restricts airflow, freezes the evaporator coil, and kills cooling capacity.
2. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — leaves, grass, and cottonwood block heat rejection so the system can't dump heat outside.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan hums but won't start, or the fan spins but the compressor doesn't.
4. **Tripped breaker or condenser disconnect** — the indoor blower runs (warm air) but the outdoor unit is dead.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the indoor coil or the suction line.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, ice formation, hissing, or long run times.
7. **Failed contactor** — pitted or stuck contacts prevent the outdoor unit from energizing.
8. **Thermostat or control-board fault** — bad wiring, dead batteries, or a board not calling for cooling.
9. **Failed compressor** — the unit tries to start, trips the breaker, or runs without cooling. Most expensive outcome.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or risk:

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This is the #1 cause and is free to fix.
- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and the temperature several degrees below room temp. Confirm it's not in "fan only" or a schedule hold. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check the breaker panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop — do not keep resetting it, and call a pro.
- **Condenser disconnect:** The outdoor box near the unit should have its pull-out block fully seated.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate:** Check the drain pan and float switch. A full pan or tripped safety float can shut cooling down. Clear standing water and add drain tablets.
- **Outdoor coil:** Visually clear leaves and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or copper lines, turn the system to OFF (fan ON) and let it thaw fully — several hours — before running again.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch. Those are pro jobs.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Rheem tech will work in roughly this order, and you can use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** — confirms the thermostat is sending 24V to the contactor.
2. **Check airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and evaporator coil; measures temperature split (return vs. supply, typically 16–22°F on a healthy system).
3. **Inspect electrical components** — tests the capacitor with a meter, checks the contactor for pitting, and verifies voltage at the compressor.
4. **Read superheat and subcooling** — attaches gauges to assess the refrigerant charge. Low subcooling points to undercharge/leak.
5. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak. A reputable tech repairs the leak rather than just "topping off."
6. **Compressor test** — measures amp draw and resistance to confirm whether the compressor is mechanically failed or electrically shorted.

Be wary of any quote that adds refrigerant without locating a leak, or recommends a full system replacement before completing these steps.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, both units running | Dirty filter / low airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Clean coils, check blower speed |
| Indoor blower runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / disconnect | Reset breaker once, seat disconnect | Test contactor, capacitor, wiring |
| Outdoor fan or compressor hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on coil or copper lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw fully, swap filter | Find cause; repair leak or airflow |
| Gradual loss of cooling, long run times | Low refrigerant from leak | None — call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Shorted compressor or wiring | Stop resetting; call pro | Megohm test, compressor diagnosis |
| Vents weak, water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate / float trip | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear blockage, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 pro.
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Coil cleaning (indoor or outdoor):** $150–$450.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. newer R-454B on recent Rheem units).
- **TXV / metering device:** $400–$800.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,400–$2,200, or more. Often the point where replacing the whole condenser makes more sense, especially out of warranty.

Many Rheem units carry a 10-year parts warranty if registered, which can drastically cut compressor and coil costs — verify your serial number and registration before approving major parts.
