## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code on Rheem air conditioners — it's the symptom you notice when the system runs but the house won't reach the thermostat setpoint, or the air from the vents isn't cold. Unlike a heat pump that flashes a diagnostic light, most Rheem straight-cool condensers (RA13 through RA20 series) have no LED display on the outdoor unit, so there's no number to read.

Instead, the cause shows up as airflow problems, electrical failures, or a refrigerant charge that's off. Some matched Rheem air handlers and EcoNet-enabled systems do log communication or sensor faults indoors, so check your thermostat or EcoNet app for any alerts before assuming a hardware failure. Behavior varies by model: two-stage units may run on low stage and feel weak, while single-stage units are simply on or off.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked return** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops capacity and can ice the indoor coil.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass clippings, dust, and cottonwood block heat rejection, so the system runs but barely cools.
3. **Failed run capacitor** — a weak or blown dual capacitor stops the compressor and/or fan from starting. Very common after a few seasons of heat.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — causes weak cooling, longer run times, and sometimes a frozen coil or hissing.
5. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a miswired/failing smart thermostat.
6. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power.
7. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low charge; blocks cooling until it thaws.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks over time.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit may hum, trip the breaker, or do nothing.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — none involve refrigerant, capacitors, or bypassing safety switches.

- **Filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. Run the system 30 minutes and re-check cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it uses them. For EcoNet/smart thermostats, check the app for alerts.
- **Breaker:** Look at the electrical panel. If the AC breaker is tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop — do not keep resetting; call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs, and that return grilles aren't covered.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from at least 18 inches around the condenser. Gently rinse visible dirt off the outside of the coil with a garden hose (low pressure, not a pressure washer).
- **Condensate:** Check the drain pan and line. A clogged drain can trip a float switch that shuts off cooling. Clearing a visibly full pan is fine; deeper drain work is a pro task.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant lines, turn the system to OFF and the fan to ON for an hour to thaw it, then run cooling again. Repeated freezing means call a tech.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician follows a logical path — this is what you're paying for:

1. **Verifies the complaint** and checks thermostat call, mode, and indoor airflow (filter, blower speed).
2. **Measures temperature split** across the indoor coil (return vs. supply air, typically 16–22°F when cooling properly).
3. **Checks electrical:** voltage at the contactor, capacitor microfarad reading with a meter, and compressor/fan amp draw.
4. **Inspects the contactor and wiring** for pitting or burnt connections.
5. **Connects gauges and measures refrigerant pressures** (superheat/subcooling) to determine if the charge is correct, low, or overcharged.
6. **Performs a leak search** if charge is low — refrigerant doesn't get "used up," so low charge means a leak.
7. **Evaluates the compressor** with electrical tests if the unit won't start or trips.

Ask to see the capacitor reading and the temperature split numbers — they make it easy to sanity-check whether a recommended repair matches the actual fault.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter / blocked return | Replace filter, open vents | Check blower, ductwork |
| Outdoor fan runs, no cold air | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse exterior of coil | Deep coil clean, charge check |
| Unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — replace battery in stat | Test & replace capacitor |
| Long run times, ice on lines | Low refrigerant (leak) | Thaw coil, replace filter | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| House warm, stat blank | Dead thermostat batteries | Replace batteries | Replace/rewire thermostat |
| Nothing runs outdoors | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Find cause of trip, replace fuse |
| Ice on indoor coil | Low airflow or low charge | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Diagnose airflow/charge |
| Buzzing, intermittent cooling | Failed contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or short | Stop resetting; call pro | Electrical test, compressor eval |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 (more for high-end smart/EcoNet models)
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500 (often near the cost of a new condenser — get a replacement quote too)

R-410A and newer refrigerant pricing varies; older units using phased-out refrigerant can cost more per pound. If the compressor or coil has failed on a system over 10–12 years old, ask whether full replacement is the better value.
