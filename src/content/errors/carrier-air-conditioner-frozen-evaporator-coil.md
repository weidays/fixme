## What this code means

A "frozen evaporator coil" isn't a numeric fault code on most Carrier equipment — it's a physical condition. The indoor evaporator coil (inside your air handler or above your furnace) drops below 32°F, and moisture from the air freezes into a block of ice on and around the coil.

Once ice forms, airflow collapses, cooling stops, and you'll often see water pooling around the indoor unit as the ice melts. On **Carrier Infinity** systems the SYSTXCC/Infinity control may flag a low-airflow or system-performance message, but there is no dedicated "frozen coil" code — the display behavior varies by control version, so don't expect a specific number.

The root problem is almost always one of two things: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Both make the coil too cold.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow lets the coil get too cold and freeze.
2. **Blocked return/supply vents or closed dampers** — starves the coil of warm return air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the fins insulate the coil and choke airflow.
4. **Low refrigerant charge (leak)** — low pressure lowers the coil's boiling temperature below freezing. This is a pro repair.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / ECM fault** — reduced CFM across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — operating cooling when outdoor temps are below ~60°F can freeze the coil.
7. **Stuck or failing metering device (TXV/piston)** — meters refrigerant incorrectly, chilling the coil. Pro-level diagnosis.
8. **Oversized system / short cycling** — the coil never reaches steady state; more common on mismatched installs.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Turn off cooling first.** Set the thermostat to OFF (cooling), and switch the fan to ON. Running the blower with the compressor off is the safe way to thaw the ice and protect the compressor.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A dirty filter is the #1 cause. Match the exact size printed on the frame.
- **Confirm the thermostat setup** — make sure it's set to COOL at a reasonable temperature and that fan mode works.
- **Walk your vents.** Open all supply registers and clear return grilles of furniture, rugs, or blankets. Aim for as many open vents as possible.
- **Look at the condensate drain and pan.** A frozen coil dumps a lot of water when it melts. Make sure the drain isn't clogged and the pan/overflow isn't backing up. If you have a condensate pump, verify it runs.
- **Check the breaker** for the air handler/furnace so the blower can run and thaw the coil.
- **Wait for a full thaw** (up to 24 hours) before restarting cooling. Never chip, scrape, or blow-dry the ice off.

If it refreezes after a full thaw and a fresh filter, stop and call a pro — the cause is deeper.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will:

1. **Confirm the coil is thawed** before taking any pressure readings (a frozen coil gives false data).
2. **Measure airflow** — check blower speed/CFM, inspect the coil for dirt, and verify duct static pressure.
3. **Attach gauges and read superheat/subcooling** to determine if the charge is correct, low, or if the metering device is misbehaving.
4. **Leak-search** if the charge is low — with electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test. Adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a red flag.
5. **Inspect the blower motor and control** — on ECM/Infinity systems they'll check for airflow-related faults in the control.

Sanity check: if a tech proposes "just adding a pound of refrigerant" without a leak search or airflow check, ask why. Refrigerant doesn't get used up — if it's low, there's a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil/lines, weak airflow | Dirty filter or blocked vents | Turn off cooling, fan ON, replace filter, open vents | Confirm airflow, clean coil |
| Refreezes after new filter | Dirty coil or low charge | Thaw fully, keep cooling off | Coil cleaning or leak search + recharge |
| Water around indoor unit | Melting ice overflowing pan | Clear condensate drain, check pump | Clear drain line, service pump/pan |
| Freezes only in mild/cool weather | Low refrigerant | Stop cooling, call pro | Leak test, repair, recharge |
| Blower barely moving air | Failing blower/ECM motor | Check breaker | Test/replace motor or module |
| Warm air, hissing near indoor coil | Metering device (TXV) fault | None — pro job | Diagnose and replace TXV/piston |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (labor + parts, varies by region and system):

- **Filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Coil thaw:** $0 (just time and the blower)
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Condensate drain clearing / pump replacement:** $75–$300
- **Blower motor (PSC):** $400–$700
- **ECM blower motor/module:** $600–$1,200
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ (depends on leak location; a leaking evaporator coil replacement can run $1,500–$2,900+)
- **TXV/metering device replacement:** $500–$900

Refrigerant work must be done by an EPA-certified tech — homeowners cannot legally purchase or handle it.
