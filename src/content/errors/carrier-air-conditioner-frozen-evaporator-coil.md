## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" is not a Carrier fault code — it's a physical condition you'll see or feel, not a number on a board. Your indoor evaporator coil (inside the air handler or furnace cabinet) gets so cold that condensation freezes into a layer of ice. Carrier systems don't have a dedicated "frozen coil" code, but the result shows up as warm air from the vents, ice on the copper refrigerant lines, or water pooling near the indoor unit once it thaws.

The coil freezes when one of two things goes wrong: **not enough warm air moves across the coil**, or **the refrigerant pressure is too low**. Both let the coil temperature drop below 32°F, so moisture in the air freezes onto it. Once iced over, the coil can't absorb heat, cooling stops, and — worst case — liquid refrigerant can flood back to the compressor and damage it.

Because the root cause is often refrigerant-related (a sealed-system repair that requires EPA certification), this is rated **pro**. You can safely thaw the unit and rule out airflow problems, but diagnosing and repairing leaks or low charge is a licensed technician's job.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the most common cause by far. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Blocked return or supply vents** — closed registers, blocked returns, or furniture over vents starve the coil of airflow.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and grime insulate the coil and reduce heat transfer, leading to icing.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge drops coil pressure and temperature. This is a sealed-system repair, not a "top-off."
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / ECM module** — weak airflow has the same effect as a dirty filter.
6. **Running AC in cold weather** — operating the system below ~60°F outdoor temp can freeze the coil.
7. **Clogged condensate drain or dirty blower wheel** — restricts airflow or causes water/ice backup.
8. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — improper refrigerant flow, a professional diagnosis.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Do these only after you've **turned cooling OFF and let the coil fully thaw** (run the fan-only setting to speed it up). Never chip at the ice.

- **Air filter** — pull it and look. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This fixes a huge share of frozen-coil cases.
- **Thermostat** — set the fan to ON (continuous) while the system is off to help thaw, then return to AUTO. Confirm it's set to COOL and the temperature isn't set unusually low.
- **Supply and return vents** — open every register and clear furniture, rugs, or boxes blocking airflow. Don't run the system with vents closed.
- **Return grilles** — make sure they aren't blocked by furniture or coated in dust.
- **Breaker** — if the system tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Thermostat batteries** — replace if your thermostat uses them and the display is dim or blank.
- **Condensate drain / pan** — check for standing water or overflow near the indoor unit; a clogged drain can signal or worsen icing.

After all that, run cooling again. If the coil refreezes within a day or two, you likely have a refrigerant or blower problem — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier technician should work the airflow side first, then the refrigerant side:

1. **Confirm the coil is fully thawed** before any pressure readings — ice makes gauges meaningless.
2. **Measure airflow / static pressure** across the coil and check the blower motor amperage and speed (especially ECM motors on Infinity/Performance models).
3. **Inspect the evaporator coil and blower wheel** for dirt buildup.
4. **Attach refrigerant gauges** and check suction pressure, superheat, and subcooling against Carrier's spec for your model.
5. **Leak-search** with an electronic detector or dye if the charge is low — adding refrigerant without finding the leak is bad practice.
6. **Test the metering device (TXV or fixed orifice)** for proper operation.

A quote that jumps straight to "you need a recharge" without checking airflow or finding the leak is a red flag. Ask whether they confirmed airflow and located a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on copper lines, warm vent air | Dirty filter / low airflow | Thaw unit, replace filter, open vents | Verify airflow, check blower speed |
| Coil refreezes within days of clean filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop running AC, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Weak airflow even with clean filter | Failing blower / ECM module | Thaw and inspect, then call pro | Test/replace blower motor or module |
| Water pooling near air handler | Thawed ice or clogged drain | Clear/flush condensate drain | Inspect pan, drain, and float switch |
| Dirty visible coil through access panel | Coil grime | Leave to pro (refrigerant lines) | Professional coil cleaning |
| Freezing when it's cool outside | Low ambient operation | Don't run AC below ~60°F outdoor | Confirm no other fault |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Carrier parts and labor vary by region and model tier (Infinity parts cost more).

- **New air filter (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Service call / diagnostic:** $90–$200
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Blower motor (PSC):** $400–$700 installed
- **ECM blower module/motor (Infinity/Performance):** $600–$1,200 installed
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ installed

If your system uses older R-22 refrigerant, recharge costs run much higher and coil replacement may be the smarter long-term choice.
