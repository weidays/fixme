## What this code means

A "frozen evaporator coil" is not a Carrier diagnostic code — it's a physical condition. The indoor coil (inside your air handler or furnace cabinet) gets so cold that moisture in the air freezes onto it, building a layer of ice. Once iced, the coil can't absorb heat, so your house stops cooling even though the system runs.

Carrier split systems and most packaged units don't flash a specific "frozen coil" fault. On **Infinity** systems with a wall control, you may see a low-airflow or low-suction-related message, but the freeze itself is something you diagnose by sight and symptom, not by a code. If your AC is running hard but blowing warm or weak air and you find ice or sweating around the indoor unit or the copper line, that's a frozen coil.

The root cause is always one of two things: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Both drop the coil temperature below freezing.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the most common cause. Restricted airflow lets the coil get too cold. (Easy DIY fix.)
2. **Closed, blocked, or too many shut supply/return vents** — starves the coil of return air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust caked on the fins acts like insulation and reduces airflow.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge drops coil pressure and temperature, freezing it. This is a pro repair and the leak must be found.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / bad capacitor** — weak airflow across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** (below ~60°F outdoors) — outdoor temps too low for a standard AC cause freezing.
7. **Stuck or blocked condensate drain** restricting the area, or a thermostat set extremely low for long runtimes.
8. **Failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — overfeeds or starves the coil. Pro diagnosis.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Turn cooling OFF, set the fan to ON.** This is step one — it stops more ice forming and starts the thaw using room-temperature air.
- **Replace the air filter** if it's dirty or you can't remember the last change. Use the size printed on the old one.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles aren't closed, covered by furniture, or blocked by rugs. Don't run with more than a couple of vents closed.
- **Check the thermostat** — confirm it's calling for normal cooling, not set to an unrealistically low temperature.
- **Look at the condensate drain and pan.** A frozen coil drips a lot as it thaws; make sure the pan isn't overflowing and the drain line isn't clogged. A wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain stub can clear a simple clog.
- **Check the breaker** for the air handler/furnace — the blower must run during thaw.
- **Let it thaw fully (1–3 hours, sometimes longer)** before restarting cooling. Never chip, scrape, or use a sharp tool on the ice — you can puncture the coil.

If it freezes again within a day or two after a clean filter and open vents, stop and call a technician — that points to refrigerant or blower issues.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will:

- Confirm the coil is fully thawed before testing (running on ice gives false readings).
- Measure **airflow** — checking the blower speed, capacitor, and total static pressure across the system to find duct or motor restrictions.
- Inspect the **evaporator coil** for dirt buildup.
- Connect **manifold gauges** and read suction/head pressures, then calculate **superheat and subcooling** to determine if the charge is correct or low.
- If charge is low, perform a **leak search** (electronic detector, UV dye, or nitrogen pressure test) — adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a temporary, code-violating fix.
- Test the **metering device (TXV/orifice)** for proper operation.

Be wary of any quote that adds refrigerant without mentioning a leak search. Low refrigerant means a leak — it doesn't get "used up."

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on copper line / coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter or blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw | Verify airflow & static pressure |
| Warm air, hissing, coil refreezes fast | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop running it, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water overflowing from indoor unit | Coil thawing + clogged drain | Vac drain line, add tablets | Clear/replace drain, check pan switch |
| Coil dirty even with clean filter | Dirty evaporator coil | None (coil is enclosed) | Pull and clean coil |
| Blower runs weak or noisy | Failing motor / bad capacitor | Confirm filter is clean | Replace capacitor or blower motor |
| Freezes only in cool weather | Outdoor temp too low to run AC | Don't run AC below ~60°F | Confirm no other fault |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and system size):

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain cleaning** — $0 DIY, or $75–$200 for a pro clearing.
- **Blower capacitor** — $150–$400.
- **Blower motor (ECM/PSC)** — $400–$900 depending on type.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning** — $200–$500.
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis** — $150–$450.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge** — $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is much pricier).
- **Evaporator coil replacement** — $1,200–$2,800 if the coil itself is leaking.
- **TXV / metering device replacement** — $400–$900.

A simple filter-and-thaw fix can cost nothing. A refrigerant leak in the coil is where bills climb fast.
