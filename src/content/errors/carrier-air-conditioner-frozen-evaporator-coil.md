## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" isn't a numeric fault code — it's a physical condition where ice forms on the indoor evaporator coil of your Carrier air conditioner. Carrier systems don't usually flash a dedicated "frozen coil" code, so you'll typically notice it as weak airflow, poor cooling, ice on the copper refrigerant lines, or water pooling around the indoor unit after the ice melts.

The coil freezes when it gets too cold. That happens for two broad reasons: **not enough warm indoor air moving across the coil** (an airflow problem) or **refrigerant pressure/charge that's too low** (a refrigerant problem). Both drop the coil surface below freezing, and condensation turns to ice instead of draining away.

This is rated **pro** because while some causes are simple DIY fixes, a frozen coil that returns is usually a sign of a refrigerant leak or a deeper airflow fault that needs a technician — and running the system frozen risks compressor damage.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A restricted filter starves the coil of airflow.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers or blocked returns reduces airflow across the coil.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and reduce heat exchange.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — drops evaporator pressure and temperature below freezing. Common on older systems.
5. **Failing or weak blower motor / blower wheel buildup** — reduced airflow from a slowing or dirty blower.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below roughly 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil.
7. **Clogged condensate drain causing a shutdown/imbalance** — less common as a direct cause, but a full drain pan signals melting ice.
8. **Thermostat set fan to "on" while cooling issues persist, or a stuck metering device (TXV)** — a pro-level failure.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do them before booking service.

- **Turn cooling OFF first.** Set the thermostat to **fan ONLY** (or off) so the coil can thaw. Never keep running cooling with visible ice.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A dirty filter is the number-one cause. Swap in a clean one — this alone fixes many cases once the coil thaws.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs. Keep at least 80% of registers open.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to Cool with a reasonable setpoint, and replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Look at the breaker.** If the indoor blower breaker tripped, that would explain no airflow. Reset it once; if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Inspect the condensate drain and pan.** A full pan or water on the floor means ice is melting. Clear a visibly clogged drain line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor drain exit if accessible.
- **Let it fully thaw** (1–24 hours in fan-only). Then run cooling and watch whether ice returns.

If the coil freezes again after a fresh filter and open vents, that points to refrigerant or a mechanical fault — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will:

- **Confirm the coil has fully thawed** before testing — measurements are meaningless while iced.
- **Measure airflow / static pressure** across the coil and blower to rule out airflow restriction.
- **Inspect the evaporator coil and blower wheel** for dirt and buildup.
- **Attach gauges and take superheat/subcooling readings** to determine if the refrigerant charge is correct or low.
- **Leak-search** with electronic detectors or dye if the charge is low, rather than just "topping off."
- **Check the metering device (TXV/piston)** for a stuck or restricted valve.
- **Verify blower motor operation and speed** on ECM/variable-speed Carrier air handlers.

Be wary of any tech who adds refrigerant without finding a leak — that's a temporary patch, not a repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, ice on lines, poor cooling | Dirty filter | Replace filter, set fan-only to thaw | Confirm airflow after thaw |
| Some rooms not cooling, coil freezes | Closed/blocked vents | Open registers and returns | Balance airflow, check duct sizing |
| Coil re-freezes with clean filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop running cooling; call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water pooling at indoor unit | Melting ice / clogged drain | Clear drain line, replace filter | Verify pan, drain, and cause of ice |
| Little airflow even with clean filter | Weak/dirty blower motor | Fan-only to thaw; call pro | Clean wheel, test/replace ECM motor |
| Coil freezes in cool weather | Running AC below ~60°F outside | Turn off cooling until warmer | Confirm no other fault |
| Ice returns despite good airflow | Stuck TXV / metering device | None — pro required | Test and replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your Carrier model and local labor rates affect the final number.

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0 DIY – $150–$300 service call
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $100–$400
- **Blower motor cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower/ECM motor replacement:** $450–$1,000 (variable-speed Carrier motors run higher)
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (older R-410A vs. legacy systems)
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$700
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ if the coil itself is leaking

A simple filter or airflow fix can cost you nothing but a filter. Refrigerant and coil repairs are where costs climb — and on aging systems, replacement math starts to matter.
