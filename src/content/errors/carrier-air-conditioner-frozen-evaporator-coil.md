## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil isn't a fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a physical condition you'll see or feel. The evaporator coil (the indoor coil inside your air handler or furnace cabinet) gets so cold that condensation on it freezes into ice. Once ice forms, it blocks airflow, which makes the coil even colder, which builds more ice. The system spirals quickly from "weak cooling" to "no cooling at all."

On **Carrier Infinity systems**, the wall control may report low airflow, a long runtime, or a related fault — but it usually won't say "frozen coil" in plain language. On **Comfort and Performance series** units there's typically no code at all; you diagnose by what you see: ice on the copper lines, water around the air handler, and warm air from the vents.

The root issue is always one of two things: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Everything below traces back to one of those two.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, dropping coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — furniture over returns or many closed registers reduces airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and restrict the air passing through its fins.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge drops coil pressure and temperature below freezing. This is the most common *repeat*-freeze cause.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wheel** — a weak or dirty blower can't push enough air; ECM motor faults on some models reduce output.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating cooling when it's below ~62°F outside can freeze the coil on many systems.
7. **Clogged condensate drain causing a safety/float trip** — less common as a freeze cause, but related, since water management and a wet coil go together.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do them with the system off.

- **Turn the AC off, fan to On.** At the thermostat set mode to **Off** and fan to **On**. This thaws the coil safely. Wait until no more ice is visible and the lines are dry before doing anything else.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of frozen coils.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't covered by rugs, furniture, or filters stacked behind them.
- **Verify thermostat settings.** Confirm it's set to Cool at a reasonable temperature (not, say, 60°F) and that it isn't calling for cooling on a cold day.
- **Check the breaker.** A tripped breaker for the air handler or condenser is worth a look, though it's not usually the freeze cause.
- **Inspect the condensate drain and pan.** Look for standing water or overflow near the air handler. You can clear a visible clog with a wet/dry vac on the drain line outside, or add drain tablets. Don't disassemble the system.

If the coil refreezes within a day of thawing and replacing the filter, stop and call a technician — that points to refrigerant or blower issues you shouldn't tackle yourself.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech follows airflow-first, refrigerant-second logic. Use this to sanity-check a quote:

- **Confirms the coil is fully thawed** before testing — readings on a frozen system are meaningless.
- **Measures airflow / static pressure** and inspects the filter, coil cleanliness, and blower wheel for buildup.
- **Checks the blower motor operation** — on ECM/variable-speed Infinity systems, they'll verify the motor is hitting commanded airflow.
- **Connects gauges and takes superheat/subcooling readings** to determine if refrigerant charge is correct. Low subcooling with high superheat suggests undercharge or a leak.
- **Performs a leak search** (electronic detector, dye, or bubble test) if charge is low — adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a temporary, costly fix.
- **Inspects the metering device (TXV)** if airflow and charge are fine but the coil still freezes.

Be cautious of any quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without checking the filter, airflow, and looking for a leak. Topping off a leaking system just delays the real repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air from vents, ice on copper line | Frozen coil from dirty filter | Turn off, fan On, replace filter | Verify airflow after thaw |
| Coil refreezes a day after thawing | Low refrigerant / leak | Stop running unit, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Weak airflow from all vents | Dirty coil or blower | Replace filter, open vents | Clean coil, service blower |
| Water pooling at air handler | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain with wet/dry vac | Clear line, check float switch |
| Freezing only on cool days | Running AC below ~62°F outside | Avoid cooling in cold weather | Confirm no other fault |
| Infinity control shows low airflow | Blower or duct restriction | Check filter and vents | Test ECM motor, static pressure |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges. Your area and contractor will vary.

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Service call / diagnostic:** $80–$200, often credited toward repair
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450
- **Leak repair + recharge (R-410A):** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and amount
- **Blower motor (PSC):** $400–$700 installed
- **ECM / variable-speed blower motor:** $600–$1,200 installed
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ if the coil itself is the leak source

A simple filter-and-thaw is genuinely $0–$40. The big numbers come from refrigerant leaks and coil replacement — which is why diagnosing the *why* matters before authorizing parts.
