## What this code means

A "frozen evaporator coil" is not a Carrier fault code displayed on a board — it is a physical condition you'll either see or feel. The indoor evaporator coil (inside your air handler or above your furnace) drops below freezing and ice forms on it. Once iced over, airflow stalls, cooling collapses, and you may see water pooling around the indoor unit when the ice melts.

The coil freezes for one of two root reasons: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant pressure to keep coil temperature above freezing**. Everything below traces back to one of those two buckets.

Because icing is a system condition rather than a stored fault, your Carrier thermostat or control board usually shows no error at all — just weak or no cooling. Some Infinity systems may log a low-suction or airflow-related alert, but that varies by configuration.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Blocked return or supply airflow** — closed registers, crushed flex duct, or a dirty evaporator coil itself.
3. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — lower pressure means a colder coil surface that ices over.
4. **Failing or dirty blower wheel / blower motor** — moving too little air across the coil.
5. **Stuck or failed blower relay / ECM module** — blower not running at the right speed (Carrier Infinity/Performance variable-speed setups).
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — operating below roughly 60°F outdoors can ice the coil.
7. **Plugged condensate drain backing water onto the coil** — less common, but possible on packaged units.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do **not** open the refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or remove the coil access panel beyond a quick look.

- **Turn the thermostat to OFF (or FAN ONLY) first.** This stops the compressor and starts thawing the ice. Running it frozen can damage the compressor.
- **Check the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of freeze-ups.
- **Open all supply registers and clear return grilles.** Closing too many vents starves the coil of airflow.
- **Look at the indoor unit.** Visible ice on the coil or the copper line confirms a freeze. Let it thaw completely — 1 to 24 hours.
- **Check the condensate drain and pan.** Standing water suggests a clogged drain; a shop vac on the outdoor drain line termination often clears it.
- **Confirm the breaker is on** and the thermostat is set to COOL with a temperature below room temp.
- **Note the outdoor temperature.** If it's below ~60°F, the system may simply be too cold to run.

After the coil fully thaws, restart in COOL and watch. If it freezes again within a day, you need a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Carrier tech should:

1. **Confirm the coil thawed** and inspect for ice patterns that hint at the cause.
2. **Measure airflow** — check filter, blower wheel cleanliness, blower motor RPM, and duct static pressure.
3. **Connect gauges and read superheat/subcooling** to determine if the charge is correct or low.
4. **Leak-search if charge is low** — electronic detector, dye, or soap bubbles. Adding refrigerant without finding the leak is bad practice.
5. **Inspect the evaporator coil** for dirt buildup that insulates it.
6. **Test blower controls** — relay, capacitor, or ECM/variable-speed module on Infinity/Performance systems.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search or airflow check is a red flag. Always ask which root cause they found.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked airflow | Replace filter, open vents, thaw | Clean coil, check blower & static pressure |
| Freezes again days after filter change | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop and call a pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Coil ices, blower barely moving air | Failing blower motor / ECM | Confirm fan runs on FAN ONLY | Test/replace blower motor or module |
| Water around indoor unit | Thawing ice or clogged drain | Clear drain with shop vac | Clear drain, verify pan slope |
| Freezes only on cool days | Running AC below ~60°F outdoor | Don't run AC in cold weather | Verify low-ambient limits |
| Hissing or oily residue near coil | Refrigerant leak | Do not touch; call a pro | Locate and repair leak |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your area, system size, and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22) shift these significantly.

- **New air filter (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150–$250 professional
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Blower motor capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor (PSC):** $400–$700
- **ECM / variable-speed blower module (Infinity/Performance):** $600–$1,200
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $150–$450
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant cost
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ (higher if matched to a new outdoor unit)

A diagnostic/service-call fee of **$90–$200** is typical and often credited toward the repair.
