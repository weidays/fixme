## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" isn't a numeric fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a condition you can see and feel. The indoor evaporator coil (inside your air handler or above the furnace) drops below 32°F and the moisture condensing on it turns to ice instead of draining away. You may notice ice on the copper refrigerant lines, water pooling near the indoor unit, weak or warm airflow, and rising indoor temperatures even though the system is running.

On Carrier Infinity systems, a frozen coil can sometimes trigger a related low-suction or low-pressure fault on the wall control, but the freeze itself is a mechanical symptom, not a self-diagnosing error code. The root cause is almost always one of two things: **not enough airflow across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**.

A frozen coil is marked **pro severity** here because the most common serious cause — low refrigerant from a leak — legally and safely requires a licensed technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow lets the coil get too cold and freeze.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — reduces airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and choke airflow.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — drops coil temperature below freezing. This requires a licensed tech; it never "uses up" normally.
5. **Failing or dirty blower wheel / weak blower motor** — moves too little air across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** (below ~60°F outdoors) — outdoor temps too low can cause freezing.
7. **Stuck-closed metering device (TXV or fixed orifice)** — restricts refrigerant flow and freezes the coil.
8. **Blocked condensate drain backing up** — less common, but a flooded pan plus a cold coil makes ice worse.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do them with the system **off**.

- **Turn the system OFF to thaw it.** Set the thermostat to OFF and the fan switch to ON. Let the coil thaw fully (1–3 hours, sometimes longer). Never chip ice off the coil or fins.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted, or 3+ month-old filter is the #1 cause. Swap it for a clean one of the correct size.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles aren't closed, covered by furniture, or blocked by rugs.
- **Check the breaker.** If the indoor blower tripped, the coil can freeze. Reset a tripped breaker once.
- **Check the condensate drain.** Look for water in or around the drain pan. A wet/dry shop vac on the outdoor drain line outlet can clear a clog. Drain tablets help prevent regrowth.
- **Inspect the line insulation.** Confirm the foam on the large (suction) copper line outdoors isn't missing or crushed.
- **Don't run cooling below ~60°F outdoors.**

If the coil refreezes after a clean filter and open vents, stop and call a pro — that points to refrigerant or a mechanical fault.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech confirms the freeze is gone, then works through airflow and refrigerant in order:

- **Measures static pressure and airflow** across the coil to find restrictions (filter, coil, blower, ductwork).
- **Inspects the evaporator coil and blower wheel** for dirt; cleans if needed.
- **Connects refrigerant gauges** and checks suction pressure, superheat, and subcooling against Carrier specs for your model.
- **Identifies low refrigerant** — if charge is low, they perform a **leak search** (electronic detector, UV dye, or bubble solution). A reputable tech will find and repair the leak, not just "top it off."
- **Tests the metering device (TXV/orifice)** for a restriction.
- **Verifies blower motor amperage and speed tap** settings.

Sanity check on a quote: anyone proposing to add refrigerant without searching for a leak is cutting corners. Anyone quoting a full coil or compressor replacement should be able to show you the leak location or the failed component.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on copper lines, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Turn off, thaw, replace filter, open vents | Measure static pressure, check duct restriction |
| Coil refreezes after clean filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call a pro | Leak search, repair, recharge to spec |
| Water around indoor unit | Thawing coil + clogged drain | Clear drain line, add tablets | Clear drain, inspect pan and float switch |
| Warm air, long run times | Dirty evaporator coil | Replace filter; schedule cleaning | Chemically clean coil, verify airflow |
| Freezing only in cool weather | Outdoor temp too low | Don't run AC under ~60°F outdoors | Verify low-ambient operation if needed |
| Ice + system short cycles | Failing blower or TXV | Confirm blower runs | Test blower amps/speed, inspect metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Diagnostic/service-call fees often apply to the total.

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 DIY.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $200 pro.
- **Service call / diagnostic:** $80–$200.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450.
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$800 (ECM motors run higher).
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$750.
- **Refrigerant leak repair (varies widely):** $200–$1,500+ depending on location and access.
- **Refrigerant recharge:** $150–$600 depending on type (R-410A) and amount.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,500+ depending on model and labor.

Costs vary by region, system size, refrigerant type, and whether parts are under Carrier warranty. Get an itemized estimate before approving work.
