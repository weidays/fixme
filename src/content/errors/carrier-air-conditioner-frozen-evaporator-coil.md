## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil isn't a fault code on Carrier units — it's a physical symptom. The evaporator coil sits in your indoor air handler or above the furnace, and it's where refrigerant absorbs heat from your home's air. When something drops the coil temperature below freezing **and** moisture is present, condensation turns to frost, then a solid block of ice.

Once ice forms, it insulates the coil and blocks airflow, which makes the problem snowball: less airflow means a colder coil, which means more ice. You'll notice weak or no cold air, higher humidity, water pooling near the furnace as it melts, and sometimes a hissing or bubbling sound.

The two root issues are almost always **restricted airflow** or **low refrigerant charge**. The fix is straightforward for airflow problems and a pro job for refrigerant problems — and you can't tell which it is by looking at the ice.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the #1 cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, so it runs cold enough to ice up.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too-few supply/return vents** — furniture over registers, closed dampers, or undersized return ductwork reduce airflow.
3. **Low refrigerant charge / refrigerant leak** — under-charged systems run abnormally cold at the coil. Requires a technician with gauges to confirm.
4. **Failing blower motor or wheel** — a weak, dirty, or failing indoor blower can't move enough air across the coil.
5. **Dirty evaporator coil** — years of dust on the coil fins act like the dirty filter, restricting airflow.
6. **Running the AC when it's too cold outside** — cooling below roughly 62°F outdoor temp commonly causes freeze-ups.
7. **Clogged condensate drain backing up** — less common, but a flooded pan and high humidity around the coil can contribute.
8. **Thermostat or fan setting issues** — fan set to AUTO with very short cycles can let the coil over-cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are all safe and may save a service call:

- **Turn the system off first.** Set the thermostat to **OFF** for cooling, then set the **fan to ON**. Running the blower with cooling off speeds up thawing and prevents more ice.
- **Check the air filter.** Pull it out. If you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the most common cause.
- **Walk the house and open vents.** Make sure supply registers aren't closed or blocked by rugs, furniture, or curtains, and that return grilles are clear.
- **Check the breaker.** If the indoor blower isn't running at all, confirm the air-handler/furnace breaker is on.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to a reasonable temperature and the fan can run. Replace thermostat batteries if the screen is dim.
- **Look at the condensate drain and pan.** If you see standing water, clear the visible drain line with a wet/dry vacuum at the outdoor termination and add drain tablets.

After the coil is **fully thawed** (no visible ice — often several hours), put in a clean filter, then switch cooling back on. If it freezes again within a day, stop and call a pro: that points to refrigerant or blower problems you should not chase yourself.

**Do not** scrape ice off the coil, add refrigerant, or repeatedly turn the unit on and off while iced.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier technician will:

1. **Confirm and thaw** the ice, and inspect the filter, vents, and ductwork for restrictions.
2. **Measure airflow** across the coil and check blower amperage and wheel cleanliness.
3. **Inspect the evaporator coil** for dirt buildup between the fins.
4. **Attach refrigerant gauges** and read suction/discharge pressures, then calculate superheat and subcooling to determine if the charge is correct.
5. **Leak-check** with electronic detector or UV dye if the charge is low — adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a temporary fix, not a repair.
6. **Verify thermostat and control settings**, including low-ambient lockout if applicable.

If a quote jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search or airflow check, ask why. Refrigerant doesn't get "used up" — if it's low, there's a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on copper lines/coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Off + fan ON, replace filter, open vents | Confirm airflow, inspect duct restrictions |
| Refreezes within a day of thawing | Low refrigerant / leak | Stop running it; call pro | Gauge readings, leak search, repair & recharge |
| Blower barely moves air | Failing blower motor/wheel | None safe | Test motor, clean/replace wheel or motor |
| Coil iced even with clean filter | Dirty evaporator coil | None safe | Pull and clean coil |
| Water around furnace, musty smell | Condensate backup + ice melt | Vacuum drain line, add tablets | Clear/replace drain, check pan & safety switch |
| Freezes only on cool nights | Running AC below ~62°F outside | Switch to fan or off when cool | Confirm low-ambient limits |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **New air filter (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Thaw + recharge after airflow fix (no leak):** $0 if DIY airflow; pro thaw/inspection $100–$250
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $100–$330
- **Refrigerant recharge (R-410A):** $200–$700 depending on amount; older R-22 systems cost much more
- **Refrigerant leak repair (varies by location):** $200–$1,000+
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$1,600+ (more for cased coils and Infinity-series matched systems)
- **Blower motor replacement:** $300–$750 (ECM motors on Performance/Infinity cost more than PSC motors)

Costs vary by region, refrigerant type, and whether your system is still under Carrier's parts warranty — check your registration, as the coil is often covered for parts even when labor isn't.
