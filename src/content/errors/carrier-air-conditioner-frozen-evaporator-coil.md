## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" is not a fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a physical condition you'll see or feel: ice or frost forming on the indoor evaporator coil (inside the air handler or furnace cabinet) and often on the copper refrigerant lines.

The evaporator coil is supposed to stay cold but above freezing. When something disrupts the balance between airflow and refrigerant flow, the coil surface drops below 32°F, condensation freezes, and the ice steadily insulates the coil. The result: weak or no cold air, high indoor humidity, and — if you keep running it — water leaks when the ice finally melts and possible compressor damage.

On basic Carrier Comfort and Performance units there is no error code; you diagnose by observation. On **Infinity** systems with a wall control, you may see related faults or reduced-capacity messages, but the wall control does not have a dedicated "frozen coil" code.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Restricted airflow from a dirty filter** — the single most common cause. Not enough warm air across the coil lets it freeze.
2. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil so it can't transfer heat properly.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge drops coil pressure and temperature below freezing. This is a sealed-system repair.
4. **Blower problems** — a failing blower motor, slipping belt (older units), or wrong fan speed setting reduces airflow.
5. **Closed or blocked ducts/registers** — too many closed vents or crushed/disconnected ductwork starves the coil of return air.
6. **Running the AC when it's too cold outside** — operating below roughly 60°F outdoor temperature can freeze the coil.
7. **Faulty metering device (TXV/orifice)** — overfeeds or underfeeds refrigerant, dropping coil temperature.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Turn the system off first.** Set the thermostat to OFF, or to **FAN ONLY**, and let the coil thaw completely before doing anything else. Never run cooling with a frozen coil.
- **Check the air filter.** A clogged filter is the #1 cause. Replace it if it's gray or you can't see light through it.
- **Confirm the thermostat fan setting.** Setting the fan to ON (not AUTO) during thaw circulates warm air and speeds melting.
- **Open all supply registers** and confirm at least 80% of vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Check the return-air grille** for dust, drapes, or anything blocking it.
- **Look at the condensate drain.** When ice melts, a lot of water comes out. Make sure the drain pan and line aren't overflowing; clear the line with a wet/dry vacuum at the outdoor drain stub if needed.
- **Check the breaker** for the air handler/furnace — the blower must be running for the coil to thaw and to prevent refreezing.

If, after a full thaw and a fresh filter, the coil freezes again within a day or two, the cause is internal (refrigerant, coil, blower, or metering device) and needs a licensed technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will not just "add freon." Expect them to:

- Confirm the coil is fully thawed before testing, then run the system and measure **superheat and subcooling** at the service ports.
- Measure **temperature split** (return vs. supply air) and **static pressure** to judge airflow.
- Inspect the evaporator coil and blower wheel for dirt.
- Check blower motor amp draw and verify the correct fan speed/CFM for the system.
- If refrigerant is low, perform a **leak search** (electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test) — adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a temporary fix that violates good practice.
- Inspect the TXV/metering device operation if charge and airflow check out.

A quote that recommends a refrigerant top-off with no leak search, or a coil/compressor replacement without airflow and charge measurements, deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on coil/lines, weak airflow | Dirty filter | Replace filter, thaw fully | Verify airflow, static pressure |
| Refreezes after filter change | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — shut down | Leak search, repair, evacuate, recharge |
| Water leaking near indoor unit | Melting ice overflowing pan | Clear drain, vacuum line | Confirm coil thawed, check pan/float switch |
| Some rooms cold, coil freezes | Closed vents / duct restriction | Open all registers | Inspect ductwork, measure CFM |
| Low airflow, hot blower smell | Failing blower motor | Shut down | Test/replace blower motor or module |
| Coil freezes only on cool nights | Running AC below ~60°F outside | Use fan only when cool | Verify operating range |
| Charge & airflow OK, still freezes | Faulty TXV/orifice | None | Replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $15–$40
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $75–$250
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $200–$500
- **Blower motor (ECM/PSC) replacement:** $400–$900
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500+ depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,500 (more if it's matched to an Infinity system)
- **Compressor damage from running frozen:** $1,500–$3,500+ — the reason you shut down at the first sign of ice

Prices vary by region, refrigerant type (R-410A systems cost more to recharge), and whether your Carrier equipment is still under its 10-year parts warranty.
