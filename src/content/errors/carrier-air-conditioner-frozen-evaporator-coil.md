## What this code means

A "frozen evaporator coil" isn't a numbered fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a physical condition you'll usually spot as ice or frost on the indoor coil or on the copper refrigerant line near the indoor unit. Some Carrier Infinity systems may log a related airflow or low-suction fault, but base Comfort and many Performance models simply keep running and cool poorly.

The evaporator coil is supposed to stay cold but **above freezing**. When something drops the coil temperature below 32°F or restricts the heat it can absorb, condensation on the coil turns to ice. That ice blocks airflow, which makes the coil even colder — a runaway loop that ends with a solid block of frost and little or no cooling.

The two root families of causes are **not enough airflow over the coil** and **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Both are common, and telling them apart is the heart of the diagnosis.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Starves the coil of warm return air, so it freezes.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers or furniture over returns reduces airflow.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil insulate it and restrict airflow.
4. **Failing or slow blower motor** — a weak blower (or wrong fan speed setting) under-moves air.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — lowers coil pressure and temperature below freezing. Always a leak, never normal consumption.
6. **Clogged condensate drain backing up onto the coil** — less common, but standing water can ice over.
7. **Running the AC in cold outdoor weather** — operating below ~60°F outdoors can freeze the coil.
8. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — a pro-level diagnosis.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only steps a homeowner should do — no refrigerant, no opening the sealed system.

- **Turn cooling OFF, fan ON.** Set the thermostat to *Off* for cooling and the fan to *On*. This thaws the coil safely. Do not run cooling on a frozen coil.
- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or older than 1–3 months, swap it. This fixes a large share of frozen-coil cases.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by rugs or furniture.
- **Check the breaker.** A tripped AC breaker can leave the blower off while other parts run — reset once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Inspect the condensate drain.** If the pan is full or water is backing up, clear the drain line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination, or add drain tablets.
- **Let it fully thaw.** Wait until all ice is gone (1–24 hrs) before restarting cooling. Place towels to catch melt water.

After thawing and a new filter, restart cooling. If it refreezes within a day, the cause is deeper — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will:

- Confirm the coil is fully thawed before testing — readings on an iced coil are useless.
- Measure airflow and inspect the blower wheel, motor, and fan speed taps for correct CFM.
- Inspect and, if needed, clean the evaporator coil.
- Attach gauges to read **suction and head pressures**, and measure **superheat and subcooling** to judge the refrigerant charge.
- Check the metering device (TXV or fixed orifice) for proper operation.
- If charge is low, perform a **leak search** (electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test) — they should find the leak, not just top off.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without checking airflow or hunting the leak is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil/line, weak cooling | Dirty filter / airflow | Replace filter, thaw, restart | Confirm CFM, clean coil |
| Refreezes within a day of new filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop running, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Warm air, high energy bills | Dirty coil insulating heat exchange | Thaw, replace filter | Deep-clean evaporator coil |
| Blower barely moving air | Failing blower motor/wheel | None safe | Test/replace motor or capacitor |
| Water around indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear line with wet/dry vac | Clear/replace drain, pan |
| Freezes only on cool nights | Low charge or running below 60°F | Avoid running in cold weather | Verify charge, check metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and model variation applies):

- **Air filter replacement:** $0 DIY (under $25 for the filter).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250 if a pro does it.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450.
- **Blower motor capacitor:** $150–$400.
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900 (ECM motors on higher-end models cost more).
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+, depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A systems).
- **Evaporator coil replacement (if corroded/leaking):** $1,000–$2,500+.

Diagnostic/service-call fees typically run $90–$200 and are often credited toward the repair.
