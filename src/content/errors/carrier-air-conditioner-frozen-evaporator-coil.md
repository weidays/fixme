## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil is not a fault code Carrier displays — it is a physical condition you'll see or feel: a sheet of ice on the indoor coil (inside the air handler or furnace cabinet) and often on the copper refrigerant line near the outdoor unit. Newer Carrier Infinity systems may report related symptoms (poor cooling, low suction) on the control, but most homeowners discover this by finding warm air at the vents, water pooling around the indoor unit, or visible frost.

The coil freezes when the refrigerant inside it gets too cold relative to the air flowing across it. That happens for two broad reasons: **not enough warm air moving over the coil** (an airflow problem) or **too little refrigerant pressure** (a charge or metering problem). Either way, condensation on the coil freezes instead of draining, and the ice insulates the coil so it cools even worse — a snowball effect.

This is rated **pro** because the two leading non-DIY causes — refrigerant leaks and metering-device faults — require EPA-certified refrigerant handling. You can safely diagnose and fix the airflow side yourself.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Blocked or closed return/supply vents, or a collapsed/disconnected duct** — starves the coil of warm air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust caked on the fins acts like the filter restriction even with a clean filter.
4. **Weak or failing blower motor / wrong fan speed** — common on aging ECM motors or after a board/setting change.
5. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — lower pressure means a colder coil. Requires a certified tech.
6. **Faulty metering device (TXV or piston)** — meters too little refrigerant into the coil.
7. **Running the AC in cool weather (below ~62°F outdoors)** — low outdoor temps can ice the coil; uncommon but seen with bad thermostat scheduling.
8. **Stuck-closed or failing blower relay / control board issue** — fan doesn't run with the compressor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no refrigerant, no opening sealed components.

- **First, stop the freeze.** Set the thermostat to **Off** for cooling, and switch the fan to **On**. This thaws the coil safely. Do not run cooling until all ice is gone.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted, or sucked-in filter is the #1 cause. Replace it.
- **Walk the house and open all supply vents** and confirm return grilles aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or filters stacked behind them.
- **Check the thermostat** — make sure it's set to Cool, the fan to Auto (after thawing), and replace the batteries if it's a battery model and the display is dim.
- **Check the breaker** for both the indoor air handler/furnace and outdoor unit; a tripped indoor breaker stops the blower while the outdoor unit keeps trying.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A frozen coil dumps a lot of water when it thaws — clear the drain line and pan so it doesn't overflow. Drain tablets help prevent algae clogs.
- **Look for obvious frost** on the larger copper line at the outdoor unit. Note it for the tech.

If the coil refreezes within a day of a clean filter and open vents, the cause is likely refrigerant or the coil itself — call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Carrier tech should:

1. Confirm the coil is fully thawed before testing — diagnosing a frozen system gives false readings.
2. **Measure airflow** across the coil (temperature split / static pressure) and inspect the blower wheel and motor speed.
3. Inspect the evaporator coil and filter for restriction.
4. **Attach gauges and check superheat/subcooling** against Carrier's charging chart for your specific model and refrigerant (R-410A or newer R-454B). This tells them charge vs. metering issues apart.
5. If charge is low, **leak-search** (electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test) rather than just "topping off" — a recharge without leak repair is a temporary band-aid.
6. Test the TXV/metering device and blower control if airflow and charge check out.

Be wary of any quote to "add refrigerant" without a leak search — that's not a real repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, warm vent air | Dirty filter / blocked airflow | Turn off cooling, fan On, replace filter, open vents | Measure airflow, inspect coil |
| Frost on outdoor copper line | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Thaw, check filter; if it recurs, call pro | Gauge readings, leak search, recharge |
| Coil refreezes after clean filter | Low charge / leak / bad TXV | Stop running cooling, call pro | Subcooling test, leak detection, repair |
| Water leaking around indoor unit | Thawed coil overflowing pan | Clear condensate drain and pan | Inspect drain, confirm slope |
| Cooling weak only on hot days | Marginal charge or weak blower | Note pattern for tech | Charge and blower-speed verification |
| Fan not running with AC on | Blower motor, relay, or board | Check indoor breaker | Test blower, relay, control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your area and unit size affect price.

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40.
- **Thaw + restart (DIY):** $0, just time.
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450.
- **Blower motor (ECM) replacement:** $400–$900+ on Carrier ECM systems.
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $100–$350.
- **Leak repair + recharge (R-410A):** $300–$1,000 depending on leak location and refrigerant amount. **R-454B** systems can run higher as that refrigerant rolls out.
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,000+ (parts, labor, refrigerant); higher if the coil is matched to a newer high-efficiency Carrier system.

A frozen coil caused by a dirty filter is essentially free to fix. A frozen coil caused by a leak is where the cost climbs.
