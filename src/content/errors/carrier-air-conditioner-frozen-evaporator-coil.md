## What this code means

A "frozen evaporator coil" isn't a fault code on Carrier equipment — it's a physical condition you'll usually spot as ice or frost on the indoor coil, the copper line set near the air handler, or water pooling around the unit as ice melts. On Infinity systems with a wall control, you may instead see weak airflow, a unit that runs constantly without cooling, or a generic "system malfunction" message.

The evaporator coil freezes when its surface temperature drops below 32°F. That happens for two broad reasons: **not enough warm air moving across the coil** (an airflow problem) or **not enough refrigerant pressure** (a refrigerant problem). Both lower the coil temperature until condensation turns to ice, which then blocks airflow further — a snowball effect that ends with a solid block of ice.

This is rated **pro** because the two most damaging causes — low refrigerant from a leak and blower failure — require gauges, leak detection, and EPA-certified refrigerant handling. You can safely thaw the coil and rule out simple airflow issues, but a coil that refreezes needs a technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — restricts airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and block air, even with a clean filter.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — drops coil pressure and temperature. Common on older R-410A systems; requires leak repair, not just a "top-off."
5. **Failing or slow blower motor (ECM or PSC)** — moves too little air. Carrier variable-speed ECM blowers can run in a degraded state before failing outright.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below about 62°F outdoor temperature can freeze the coil; not a fault, just a misuse.
7. **Clogged condensate drain backing up onto the coil** — less common but can contribute to icing and water damage.
8. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — feeds the wrong amount of refrigerant, a true pro diagnosis.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop after these — don't open the sealed refrigerant system or remove the blower.

- **Turn cooling OFF, fan ON.** At the thermostat or Infinity control, set mode to off and fan to ON. This thaws the coil safely. Let it run 1–3 hours (longer if heavily iced).
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone fixes many frozen coils.
- **Open and unblock all vents.** Make sure supply registers are open and not covered by rugs or furniture, and return grilles are clear. Closing too many vents starves the coil.
- **Check the thermostat fan setting.** AUTO is normal, but ON during thaw helps. Confirm you're not running cooling on an unusually cool day.
- **Check the breaker.** If the indoor blower lost power but the outdoor unit ran, the coil will freeze fast. Reset a tripped breaker once.
- **Check the condensate drain.** Look for standing water in the pan or a clogged drain line. Clearing a visible clog and pouring drain tablets is fine.
- **After thawing, run cooling and watch.** If it cools normally with a clean filter, you've solved it. **If it refreezes within a day, stop and call a technician** — that points to refrigerant or blower issues.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Carrier tech follows a logical path — use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirms the coil is fully thawed** before testing. Diagnosing a frozen coil gives false readings.
2. **Measures airflow and static pressure** to confirm the blower and ductwork move enough air across the coil.
3. **Inspects the evaporator coil and filter** for blockage and biofilm.
4. **Tests the blower motor** — ECM module health on variable-speed Carrier units, capacitor and amp draw on PSC motors.
5. **Connects gauges and takes superheat/subcooling readings** to determine if the charge is correct. Low readings indicate a leak.
6. **Performs an electronic or dye leak search** if charge is low. A reputable shop finds and repairs the leak rather than just adding refrigerant.
7. **Checks the metering device (TXV/orifice)** if airflow and charge look right but freezing persists.

Be cautious of any tech who adds refrigerant without performing a leak search — repeatedly recharging a leaking system is a recurring expense and an environmental violation.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, dirty filter | Restricted airflow | Replace filter, thaw with fan ON | Verify airflow, clean coil if needed |
| Coil refreezes after clean filter | Low refrigerant / leak | Thaw, then stop and call | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Weak airflow, no obvious cause | Failing blower / ECM | Confirm filter & vents clear | Test motor, capacitor or ECM module |
| Ice plus water around unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible clog, add tablets | Flush drain line, check pan switch |
| Freezes only on cool days | Running AC in low temps | Don't run cooling below ~62°F | Confirm no other fault |
| Cools poorly, all vents open | Dirty evaporator coil | Replace filter and recheck | Professional coil cleaning |
| One or few rooms iced/no air | Closed vents / duct issue | Open all registers | Inspect ductwork and dampers |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your area, system size, and refrigerant type affect the total.

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40.
- **Condensate drain clearing (DIY):** $0–$25 for tablets and a brush; **$100–$250** for a pro flush.
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450.
- **Blower capacitor (PSC motors):** $150–$400 installed.
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$700 (PSC) or **$600–$1,200** (variable-speed ECM module/motor on Performance/Infinity units).
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450.
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant amount. Newer R-454B systems may cost more as the refrigerant ramps up in availability.
- **TXV/metering device replacement:** $500–$900.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ — and worth weighing against system age if your unit is 12+ years old.
