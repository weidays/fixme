## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil is not a numeric Carrier fault code — it is a physical symptom. The evaporator (indoor A-coil) gets so cold that moisture in the air freezes into a layer of ice on the fins and copper. Most Carrier residential ACs do not have a dedicated "frozen coil" code; you'll usually notice the problem as weak cooling, ice on the copper lines at the indoor unit, or water on the floor after the ice melts.

The root issue is almost always one of two things: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Both drop the coil temperature below freezing. On Infinity systems, you may see airflow-related notices at the wall control, but the freeze itself is diagnosed by inspection, not a stored code.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow starves the coil of warm return air and it ices over.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers or blocked returns reduce airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust caked on the fins insulates the coil and chokes airflow.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge lowers coil pressure and temperature below freezing. This is a pro repair and the leak must be found, not just topped off.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor** — a weak ECM or PSC blower can't move enough air. Some Infinity systems may flag airflow problems.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below roughly 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil.
7. **Stuck TXV / metering device** — a faulty expansion valve over-feeds or under-feeds the coil.
8. **Plugged condensate drain causing a tripped float** — less a freeze cause, more a co-symptom you'll see at the same time.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Turn the system OFF, fan ON.** Set the thermostat to OFF and the fan to ON to thaw the coil with room-temperature air. Never chip at the ice.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A clogged filter is the #1 cause. Swap it for a clean one of the correct size and a reasonable MERV rating (MERV 8–11 for most homes).
- **Open closed vents.** Make sure supply registers are open and return-air grilles aren't blocked by furniture, rugs or curtains.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the AC and air-handler breakers are on; a tripped breaker after refreezing can cause confusion.
- **Inspect the condensate line and pan.** Look for standing water or overflow. Clearing a clogged drain with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination is a safe DIY task.
- **Confirm thawing is complete.** Wait until all ice is gone (can take hours) before restarting. Restarting on ice can damage the compressor.

After thawing, restart and watch. If it refreezes within a day, stop running it and call a technician — the cause is no longer something you can safely fix.

## How a technician will diagnose it

- **Confirms airflow first** by measuring static pressure and inspecting the filter, coil and blower wheel for buildup.
- **Cleans the coil and blower** if dirt is the cause — no refrigerant work needed.
- **Connects gauges and checks superheat/subcooling** to determine if the charge is low. Low charge points to a leak.
- **Performs a leak search** with an electronic detector, dye or nitrogen pressure test if refrigerant is low. Reputable techs **find and repair the leak** rather than just adding refrigerant.
- **Tests the metering device (TXV/orifice)** and blower motor speed if airflow and charge both look correct.
- **Checks the outdoor temperature** against operating conditions.

A quote that says "just add refrigerant" without mentioning a leak search is a red flag — refrigerant doesn't get "used up," so a low charge means there's a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on copper line at indoor unit, weak cooling | Dirty filter / low airflow | Replace filter, set fan ON to thaw | Verify airflow, measure static pressure |
| Refreezes within a day of a new filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop running it, call pro | Leak search, repair, evacuate & recharge |
| Water pooling under air handler | Melting ice / clogged drain | Thaw, clear drain with wet/dry vac | Clear drain, confirm float switch works |
| Coil iced despite clean filter & open vents | Dirty coil or weak blower | Inspect for visible dust | Clean coil/blower, test motor speed |
| Freezes only in mild weather | Running AC below ~60°F outdoor | Switch to fan-only on cool days | Confirm charge and metering device |
| Some rooms warm, coil freezes | Too many closed registers | Open vents and returns | Balance airflow, check ductwork |

## Repair costs

| Repair | Typical US cost |
|---|---|
| Filter replacement (DIY) | $10 – $40 |
| Professional coil & blower cleaning | $150 – $400 |
| Condensate drain clearing | $75 – $250 |
| Blower motor (ECM) replacement | $400 – $900 |
| TXV / metering device replacement | $300 – $700 |
| Refrigerant leak search | $100 – $350 |
| Leak repair + recharge | $400 – $1,200 |
| Evaporator coil replacement | $900 – $1,800+ |

Costs vary by region, refrigerant type (older R-22 systems are far more expensive to recharge), and whether the coil is still under Carrier's parts warranty — labor is usually not covered.
