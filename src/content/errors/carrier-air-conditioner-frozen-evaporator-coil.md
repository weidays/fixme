## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil isn't a digital fault code on most Carrier units — it's a physical condition. The indoor evaporator coil (inside your air handler or furnace cabinet) gets so cold that condensation freezes into a layer of ice instead of draining away. On Carrier **Infinity** systems with a communicating control, you may see an airflow or low-suction-related fault on the thermostat; on **Comfort** and **Performance** series and older units, there's no code at all — you simply notice weak airflow, water on the floor, or visible ice on the copper lines and coil.

The root problem is almost always one of two things: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Both drop the coil temperature below freezing.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow starves the coil of warm air and it freezes.
2. **Blocked return or supply vents / closed dampers** — reduces airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil insulate it and choke airflow.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — low pressure drops coil temperature below freezing. This is a repair, not a "top-off."
5. **Failing or weak blower motor / wheel** — not enough air volume moving across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** (below ~62°F outdoor) — low ambient temps can ice the coil.
7. **Stuck or failing metering device (TXV / piston)** — overfeeds refrigerant and freezes the coil.
8. **Clogged condensate drain** — backed-up water can refreeze and worsen icing.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Stop here and call a pro if they don't solve it.

- **Turn the cooling OFF, set the fan to ON.** This is step one. Let warm indoor air thaw the coil — never chip at ice with anything sharp.
- **Check and replace the air filter** if it's gray, matted, or more than 30–90 days old.
- **Walk the house and open every supply vent**, and make sure return grilles aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or drapes.
- **Check the thermostat** — set it to a sensible cooling setpoint (not below ~68°F) and confirm it's calling for cooling, not stuck in a continuous run.
- **Look at the condensate drain and pan.** If you see standing water, clear the drain line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination point.
- **Check the breaker** for the air handler/furnace and condenser; flip any tripped breaker once.
- **Let it fully thaw** (several hours to a day) before restarting cooling. Once thawed, run it and watch — if it ices again within a day, the cause is internal and needs a technician.

Do **not** add refrigerant, open the sealed system, or repeatedly cycle a unit that keeps freezing.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will confirm the coil is fully thawed, then:

- **Measure airflow / static pressure** to confirm the blower and ductwork move enough air across the coil.
- **Inspect the evaporator coil and blower wheel** for dirt buildup.
- **Connect gauges and read suction/head pressures**, then calculate **superheat and subcooling** to determine if the charge is correct or low.
- **Leak-search** with electronic detector or dye if pressures point to a low charge — a low charge is always a leak, not normal loss.
- **Check the metering device (TXV/piston)** operation and the blower motor amperage/capacitor.
- **Verify the condensate system** drains freely.

If a tech wants to "just add a pound of refrigerant" without searching for a leak, push back — that's a temporary patch on a sealed system.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow + warm air, gray filter | Dirty filter | Replace filter, thaw coil | None if resolved |
| Ice only on suction line near condenser | Low refrigerant / leak | Shut off, thaw | Leak search + repair, recharge |
| Coil refreezes within a day of clean filter | Low charge or weak blower | Shut off, call pro | Test charge, blower amps, capacitor |
| Visible dirt/dust mat on coil | Dirty evaporator coil | None — leave coil to pro | Professional coil cleaning |
| Water on floor + ice | Clogged condensate + icing | Clear drain with shop vac | Clear drain, verify slope |
| Freezing in cool/spring weather | Low outdoor temp running AC | Stop using AC below ~62°F | Confirm no other fault |
| Coil ices with clean filter + open vents | Failing blower or stuck TXV | Shut off, call pro | Replace blower motor or TXV |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor, regional variation applies):

- **Air filter replacement:** $0 DIY (about $10–$30 for the filter)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY with a shop vac, or $75–$200 for a service call
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Blower motor capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900 (ECM motors on Infinity systems run higher)
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $100–$350
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ (R-410A/R-454B coil, labor-intensive)

A diagnostic/service-call fee of $90–$180 is typical and often credited toward the repair.
