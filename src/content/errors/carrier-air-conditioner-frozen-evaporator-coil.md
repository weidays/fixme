## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil isn't a single Carrier fault code — it's a physical condition where ice builds up on the indoor coil (the A-coil above your furnace or inside your air handler). On Infinity systems, the control board may flag a related airflow or charge fault, but most Carrier ACs simply stop cooling and you discover ice when you open the cabinet or see water on the floor as it thaws.

The coil freezes when its surface temperature drops below 32°F. That happens when **not enough warm indoor air is moving across the coil** (airflow problem) or when **there isn't enough refrigerant to absorb heat properly** (charge problem). Either way, moisture from the air condenses on the cold coil and freezes into a block of ice, which blocks airflow even further — a runaway cycle that quickly leaves you with no cooling at all.

This is rated **pro** because the two leading causes after a dirty filter — low refrigerant and a failing blower — require gauges, certification, or a meter to diagnose correctly.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Starves the coil of airflow.
2. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — reduces coil pressure and temperature below freezing.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — years of dust insulate the coil and restrict airflow.
4. **Blower problems** — failing blower motor, wrong fan speed, or a slipping belt (older units) moving too little air.
5. **Closed, blocked, or undersized return/supply ducts** — too few open vents or crushed ductwork.
6. **Thermostat set too low / running in extreme cold** — running AC when it's below ~62°F outside.
7. **Clogged condensate drain backing up onto the coil** (less common as a freeze cause, but pairs with the others).
8. **Failed metering device (TXV or piston)** — overfeeds or starves the coil; a true pro repair.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Turn the AC off, set the fan to ON.** Running the blower with cooling off helps the ice thaw and protects the compressor. Do this first.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone fixes many freeze-ups.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers aren't closed and returns aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or filters.
- **Check the thermostat.** Make sure it's set to COOL and to a reasonable temperature (don't run AC below ~68°F indoors or in cold weather). Replace thermostat batteries if it's the battery-powered type.
- **Check the breaker** for the indoor air handler/furnace — if the blower lost power but the condenser didn't, that can freeze the coil.
- **Clear the condensate drain.** If you see standing water in the drain pan, vacuum the drain line at the cleanout with a wet/dry vac and add drain tablets.
- **Let it fully thaw before restarting.** Restart in COOL only after all ice is gone and the pan is clear, and watch whether it freezes again.

Do **not** open sealed refrigerant lines, add refrigerant, or run the unit repeatedly while iced — those steps need a licensed tech.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will:

- Confirm the coil has fully thawed before testing.
- **Measure airflow** — check filter, coil cleanliness, blower speed taps, and static pressure to confirm the blower moves the rated CFM.
- **Attach gauges** and check superheat/subcooling to determine whether the charge is low (leak) or the metering device is faulty.
- **Leak-search** with an electronic detector or dye if the charge is low — refrigerant doesn't get "used up," so low charge means a leak that must be found.
- Inspect the **blower motor, capacitor, and wheel** for failure or buildup.
- Check the **TXV/piston** operation and the condensate drain.
- On Infinity/Performance systems, **read board fault history** for airflow or charge codes.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search is a red flag — ask where the leak is.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on coil or copper lines, weak airflow | Dirty filter / restricted airflow | Replace filter, open vents, run fan to thaw | Measure static pressure, clean coil |
| Coil freezes again days after thawing | Low refrigerant from a leak | Turn off, switch to fan, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water on floor near furnace/air handler | Coil thawing or clogged drain | Vacuum drain line, add tablets | Clear blockage, inspect pan/float switch |
| Weak airflow even with clean filter | Failing blower motor or wrong speed | Listen for blower running | Test motor, capacitor, set fan speed |
| Freezes only in cool weather/at night | Running AC below ~62°F outdoors | Avoid running AC in cold weather | Verify charge and low-ambient suitability |
| Outdoor unit runs, indoor blower silent | Blower power loss or motor failure | Check indoor breaker | Diagnose blower circuit/motor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and model):

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0 DIY – $150–$250 pro
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900 (ECM/variable-speed on Infinity units cost more)
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **TXV/metering device replacement:** $400–$800
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ (more if the coil is no longer in production)

Note: R-410A and especially older R-22 refrigerant costs vary widely — confirm which your system uses, as R-22 systems are expensive to recharge and often better replaced.
