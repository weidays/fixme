## What this code means

A "frozen evaporator coil" isn't a numbered fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a physical condition you'll see or feel: ice or frost on the indoor coil (inside the air handler or furnace cabinet) and often on the copper refrigerant line running to the outdoor unit.

The evaporator coil gets cold by absorbing heat from your indoor air. When something stops enough warm air from flowing across it — or when refrigerant pressure drops too low — the coil surface falls below freezing, and moisture in the air turns to ice. Ice insulates the coil, cooling collapses, and in bad cases liquid refrigerant returns to the compressor and damages it.

On **Infinity** systems, the control board may flag reduced airflow or an efficiency/communication message, but it does not have a dedicated "frozen coil" code. **Comfort and Performance** series units typically give no electronic indication at all — you just lose cooling.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the #1 cause. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — same airflow-starvation effect.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and reduce heat transfer.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — lower pressure lowers the coil's boiling point and freezes it. Requires a pro.
5. **Weak or failing blower motor / wrong fan speed** — not enough air moved across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** (below ~60°F outside) — common in spring/fall.
7. **Plugged condensate drain** that backs up against the coil (less common, but worsens icing).
8. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — overfeeds or starves the coil. Pro diagnosis.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no tools beyond a screwdriver and no refrigerant handling:

- **Turn the system OFF, fan ON.** Set the thermostat mode to OFF for cooling and the fan to ON. This thaws the coil safely. Wait until all ice is gone (1–24 hours) before doing anything else.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted, or clogged filter is the most common cause. Swap in a clean one of the correct size.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Walk the house and make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and not blocked by furniture, rugs, or drapes.
- **Verify the thermostat setting.** Don't set cooling far below room temperature or run the AC when it's cold outside.
- **Check the condensate drain.** If water is pooling near the indoor unit, clear the drain line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination, and add drain tablets to the pan.
- **Reset the breaker once** if the unit tripped. Do **not** repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping — call a pro.

If the coil refreezes after a clean filter, open vents, and a full thaw, stop running it and book a technician. Continued operation risks the compressor.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will work the airflow side first, then the refrigerant side:

1. **Confirm the coil is fully thawed** before testing — a frozen system gives false readings.
2. **Measure airflow / temperature split** across the coil and check blower speed taps or ECM settings.
3. **Inspect the evaporator coil** for dirt buildup and the metering device.
4. **Connect gauges and read su!ction/head pressures and superheat/subcooling** — this is how they distinguish low refrigerant from an airflow problem.
5. **Leak-search** with electronic detector or dye if refrigerant is low (the leak must be fixed, not just topped off — EPA rules and good practice).
6. **Check the condensate drain and pan** for backup.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without checking your filter, airflow, and leak-searching is a red flag. Topping off a leaking system just refreezes it later.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow from vents | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw with fan ON | Confirm airflow, check blower speed |
| Coil refreezes within hours of cleaning filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop running unit | Leak search, repair, evacuate & recharge |
| Ice on outdoor copper line set | Low refrigerant or airflow loss | Thaw, check filter | Pressure & superheat test, leak repair |
| Water around furnace/air handler | Coil thaw runoff or clogged drain | Clear drain line, add tablets | Clear/repair drain, check pan & float switch |
| Cooling fine then quits each afternoon | Gradual ice buildup | Thaw, replace filter | Diagnose airflow vs. refrigerant cause |
| Coil freezes only in cool weather | Running AC below ~60°F outdoors | Don't cool when it's cold out | Verify metering device if it persists |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and Infinity-system premiums vary):

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 DIY. The cheapest possible fix.
- **Coil thaw + diagnostic service call** — $90–$200.
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning** — $150–$450.
- **Condensate drain clearing** — $100–$250.
- **Blower motor (ECM/PSC) replacement** — $400–$900.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge** — $500–$1,500+, depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A pricing varies).
- **Evaporator coil replacement** — $1,000–$2,500+; higher if the coil is under warranty but labor is not.
- **Compressor replacement** (if a long-running frozen unit damaged it) — $1,500–$3,000+.

The takeaway: a $20 filter prevents most of these. Letting a frozen unit run is what turns a cheap problem into a four-figure one.
