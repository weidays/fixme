## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil isn't a single fault code on most Carrier systems — it's a physical condition where the indoor coil (inside your air handler or furnace) drops below 32°F and the moisture on it turns to ice. As ice builds, airflow chokes off, cooling drops to nothly, and eventually you get warm air, water leaks, or no cooling at all.

On **Carrier Infinity systems**, the control board may log a related fault (low airflow, low suction pressure, or a communication/sensor code) rather than naming "frozen coil" directly. **Older single-stage Comfort and Performance units** usually show no code at all — you discover the problem by seeing ice on the copper lineset or water on the floor near the air handler.

The root issue is almost always one of two things: **not enough air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Both lower the coil temperature below freezing.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, so it ices over.
2. **Blocked return vents or closed supply registers** — too many closed doors/vents reduces total airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — years of dust film insulates the coil and restricts airflow between the fins.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — low pressure means a colder coil. This is a pro repair (sealed system, EPA-regulated refrigerant).
5. **Failing or wrong-speed blower motor / ECM module** — if the indoor fan runs slow or not at all, airflow collapses.
6. **Clogged condensate drain causing a float switch trip** — on some setups the blower behaves oddly; standing water can also re-freeze.
7. **Running the AC when it's too cold outside** (below ~60°F) — common with marginal systems in shoulder season or at night.
8. **Stuck/failed expansion valve (TXV) or metering device** — overfeeds or underfeeds the coil. Pro diagnosis required.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks you should do yourself. **First, switch the thermostat to OFF for cooling and the fan to ON** — this thaws the coil without making more ice.

- **Air filter:** Pull it. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This fixes a large share of frozen-coil calls.
- **Supply registers and return grilles:** Open all supply vents and make sure return grilles aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or filters caked in dust.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's not set unusually low and that fan is set to ON (not AUTO) during thawing.
- **Breaker:** If the indoor blower never runs, check that the air handler/furnace breaker hasn't tripped. Reset once only.
- **Condensate drain & pan:** Look for standing water or overflow near the air handler. You can clear a visible clog at the drain line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination, or drop in drain tablets.
- **Let it fully thaw** (1–24 hrs) before running cooling again. Place towels to catch melt water.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or bend coil fins. If the coil re-freezes after a filter change and full thaw, it's time for a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech should:

1. **Confirm airflow** — measure static pressure, inspect the filter, blower wheel, and coil cleanliness, and verify ECM blower speed/programming.
2. **Read the board** — on Infinity systems they'll pull fault history from the user interface or service tool for airflow or pressure faults.
3. **Connect gauges and measure superheat/subcooling** — this tells them whether the charge is low (likely leak), the metering device is misbehaving, or airflow is the real culprit.
4. **Leak-search if charge is low** — electronic detector, UV dye, or nitrogen pressure test. Adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a band-aid, not a fix.
5. **Check the blower motor amp draw and TXV operation.**

If a quote jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search or airflow check, ask why — undercharge has a cause that needs finding.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on copper lineset / coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter or blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw with fan ON | Verify airflow, clean coil |
| Water pooling near air handler | Coil thawed / clogged drain | Clear drain, dry water, replace filter | Clear/replace drain, check pan & float switch |
| Coil re-freezes after filter change | Low refrigerant (leak) or dirty coil | Stop running cool mode | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Indoor blower runs weak or not at all | Failing ECM/blower motor | Check breaker once | Test motor amps, replace motor/module |
| Freezes only at night / cool weather | Marginal charge or running below 60°F | Avoid cooling when cool out | Verify charge, metering device |
| Warm air despite running, no obvious ice | Coil fully iced internally | Switch to fan-only, thaw fully | Diagnose airflow vs. charge |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and Infinity systems trend higher):

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Service call / diagnostic:** $90–$200
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A/R-454B)
- **Blower motor or ECM module replacement:** $400–$900
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $350–$700
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ (matched Carrier coil, labor, refrigerant)

A simple filter or airflow fix is often $0–$40. The big numbers come from sealed-system work — refrigerant is EPA-regulated and not a DIY job.
