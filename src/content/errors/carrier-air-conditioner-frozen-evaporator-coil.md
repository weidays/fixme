## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil isn't a Carrier fault code — it's a physical condition. The indoor coil (inside your air handler or furnace cabinet) gets so cold that the moisture in the air condensing on it freezes into a block of ice instead of dripping away as water.

When the coil freezes, two things happen: airflow through the coil chokes off, so you get little or no cold air, and liquid refrigerant can return to the compressor instead of vapor. That second problem is why this is rated **pro** — running a frozen system risks **slugging the compressor**, the single most expensive part in your AC.

The root cause is almost always one of two categories: **not enough warm air moving across the coil** (an airflow problem) or **not enough refrigerant** (a leak). Both make the coil colder than it should be.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the number-one cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, dropping its temperature below freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked or too-few open supply/return vents** — reduces airflow the same way a dirty filter does. Common after furniture rearranging or closing rooms.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — lowers coil pressure and temperature, causing ice. This is a sealed-system repair, not a "top off."
4. **Dirty evaporator coil** — years of dust insulate the coil and disrupt airflow.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / ECM module** — Carrier variable-speed blowers that ramp down or fail move too little air.
6. **Clogged condensate drain** — on some systems a full drain pan or float switch shuts cooling, but standing water and poor drainage can also contribute to icing conditions.
7. **Running AC in cold weather** — operating the AC when outdoor temps are below about 60°F can freeze the coil.
8. **Thermostat set too low / fan set to AUTO with extreme settings** — prolonged low-temperature operation.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **First, turn the system OFF at the thermostat and let the coil thaw.** Set the fan to **FAN ONLY** (cooling off) to melt the ice faster.

- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone fixes a large share of frozen-coil calls.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and not covered by rugs, furniture or closed doors. Don't close off more than a couple of rooms.
- **Check the thermostat.** Set it to a reasonable temperature (around 72–75°F) and don't run cooling when it's cold outside (below ~60°F).
- **Check the breaker.** If the system tripped, leave it off until the coil is fully thawed and you've found the airflow cause.
- **Check the condensate drain.** Look for standing water in the pan or a tripped float switch. You can clear a clogged drain line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination and add drain tablets.
- **Look for visible ice** on the copper lines outside (the larger insulated line). Heavy frost there points to a refrigerant or airflow issue for a pro.

**Do not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or repeatedly restart a unit that keeps freezing. If it re-freezes after a thaw and new filter, stop and call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will work systematically — you can sanity-check their quote against this:

1. **Confirm the coil is thawed** before testing anything; readings on a frozen system are meaningless.
2. **Measure airflow and static pressure** — checking filter, blower speed/operation, and duct restrictions. On variable-speed Carrier ECM blowers they'll verify the motor is ramping correctly.
3. **Inspect the evaporator coil** for dirt and the drain for clogs.
4. **Attach gauges and take temperatures** to measure **superheat and subcooling** — this tells them whether refrigerant charge is correct.
5. **If charge is low, find the leak** — electronic leak detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test. A reputable tech locates and repairs the leak rather than just adding refrigerant.
6. **Check blower motor amp draw and capacitor** if airflow is weak.

Be cautious of any tech who just adds refrigerant without finding the leak — that's a temporary fix you'll pay for again.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak/warm airflow, ice on indoor coil | Dirty filter or blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw in FAN ONLY | Verify airflow, check blower speed |
| Ice keeps returning after thaw + new filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Stop running, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Standing water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear line with wet/dry vac, add tablets | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Little airflow even with clean filter | Failing/slow blower motor | None — call pro | Test ECM module, motor, capacitor |
| Coil dirty, gray, dusty | Dirty evaporator coil | None — call pro | Professional coil cleaning |
| Freezes only in cool/mild weather | Running AC when too cold out | Don't run cooling below ~60°F | Confirm no other fault |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and Infinity/Performance/Comfort variation applies):

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150–$300 pro
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90–$200
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Blower motor capacitor:** $150–$350
- **ECM blower motor / module (variable-speed):** $400–$900
- **Refrigerant leak detection:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,800 (more if the coil is under warranty but labor isn't)

If your system is older and the coil leak is in the coil itself, ask for a repair-vs-replace comparison — a major leak repair on an aging unit may not be worth it.
