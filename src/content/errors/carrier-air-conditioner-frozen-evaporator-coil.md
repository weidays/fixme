## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" isn't a numeric fault code — it's a physical condition where ice forms on the indoor coil (the A-coil inside or above your furnace/air handler). Carrier thermostats and Infinity/Greenspeed control boards generally don't have a dedicated "frozen coil" code; instead you notice the symptom directly: weak or no cool air, ice on the copper lines, water around the indoor unit, or the system short-cycling.

The underlying problem is almost always **not enough heat reaching the coil**. The coil is designed to run cold, but it relies on a steady stream of warm indoor air flowing across it. When airflow drops or refrigerant pressure falls too low, the coil surface drops below freezing and condensation turns to ice — which then blocks airflow even more, making the freeze worse.

This is a **pro-level** condition because the two most common deep causes (low refrigerant from a leak, or a failing blower motor) require licensed diagnosis and EPA-certified refrigerant handling.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm air. (DIY-fixable.)
2. **Low airflow from a dirty blower wheel, closed vents, or collapsed/blocked ductwork** — same effect as a dirty filter, just deeper in the system.
3. **Low refrigerant charge due to a leak** — undercharge drops coil pressure and temperature below freezing. Requires a tech; refrigerant is not a DIY item.
4. **Dirty evaporator coil** — accumulated dust insulates the coil and disrupts airflow.
5. **Failing blower motor or ECM module** — the blower runs slow or intermittently, reducing airflow.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** (below ~62°F outside) — outdoor temps too low for normal operation can cause freezing.
7. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — feeds the wrong amount of refrigerant to the coil. Pro diagnosis.
8. **Blocked condensate drain backing up** — a related symptom; a flooded pan with ice on the coil often appears together.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **Stop here and call a pro for anything involving refrigerant, the sealed system, or opening the blower compartment while powered.**

- **Turn cooling OFF, set the fan to ON.** This is your first move — it begins thawing the coil safely and protects the compressor. Leave it for several hours until all ice is gone.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone resolves many freeze-ups.
- **Walk the house and open all supply and return vents.** Closing too many vents starves the coil. Don't block returns with furniture.
- **Check the thermostat.** Make sure it's set to a reasonable temperature (not, say, 60°F on a mild day) and the fan mode is correct. Replace batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Check the breaker.** A tripped breaker for the air handler or furnace stops the blower. Reset once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Look at the condensate drain and pan.** If you see standing water, clear the drain line with a wet/dry vac at the exterior outlet and add drain tablets. A backed-up drain often accompanies freezing.
- **Once fully thawed and dry**, restart cooling and watch it. If it freezes again within a day, you have a root cause that needs a technician — don't keep running it.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will confirm the cause before quoting parts. Expect them to:

1. **Confirm the coil is fully thawed** — they can't read pressures accurately on iced equipment.
2. **Measure airflow and static pressure** across the coil and blower, checking filter, coil cleanliness, and duct condition.
3. **Check blower motor operation** — verifying RPM, amp draw, and (on ECM/Infinity systems) any control faults.
4. **Connect gauges and measure superheat/subcooling** to determine if the refrigerant charge is correct. Low readings point to a leak.
5. **Perform a leak search** (electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test) if charge is low — they should find and repair the leak, not just "top off."
6. **Inspect the metering device (TXV/orifice)** for proper operation.
7. **Inspect the condensate drain and pan** if water was present.

Sanity check: if a tech quotes "add refrigerant" without ever mentioning a leak search, push back. Refrigerant doesn't get "used up" — if it's low, it's leaking somewhere.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil/lines, weak airflow | Dirty filter or closed vents | Replace filter, open all vents, run fan to thaw | Measure airflow, clean coil/blower |
| Freezes again days after thawing | Low refrigerant from a leak | Thaw, then stop running cooling | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water pooling around indoor unit | Backed-up condensate drain | Clear drain with wet/dry vac, add tablets | Clear blockage, check pan/float switch |
| Blower runs weak or cuts in and out | Failing blower motor / ECM | None — call a pro | Test and replace motor or module |
| Freezes only in cool weather | Outdoor temp too low for AC | Avoid running AC below ~62°F | Verify low-ambient suitability |
| Cool-but-not-cold air, no ice yet | Dirty coil or early undercharge | Replace filter, monitor | Clean coil, check charge/metering |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor. Your area and system size will vary these.

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40.
- **Thaw + restart (DIY):** $0 — just time.
- **Condensate drain clearing (pro):** $100–$250.
- **Blower wheel / coil cleaning (pro):** $150–$450.
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450.
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older systems).
- **TXV/metering device replacement:** $400–$900.
- **Blower motor / ECM module replacement:** $450–$1,200.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+.
- **Compressor failure (if the coil ran frozen too long):** $1,500–$2,800+ — the costly outcome you're trying to avoid by shutting cooling off early.
