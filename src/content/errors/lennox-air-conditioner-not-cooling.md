## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single Lennox error code — it's a symptom. Your Lennox air conditioner may be running (fan and compressor on) but failing to lower the indoor temperature, or it may not be starting at all. On systems with an **iComfort S30/E30 thermostat** or a control board that logs faults, you may also see a specific alert code; behavior and the exact codes shown **vary by model and board generation**, so treat any displayed code as additional detail, not the whole story.

The underlying problem falls into one of three buckets: **airflow** (filter, coils, blower), **refrigerant** (low charge from a leak), or **electrical/control** (capacitor, contactor, thermostat, or board). This page walks you through the safe checks you can do yourself and how a technician will narrow it down — so you can sanity-check any quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter** — Restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — Grass, dust, and cottonwood block heat rejection, so the system runs but barely cools.
3. **Frozen evaporator (indoor) coil** — Caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit ices over and cooling stops.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Reduced charge cuts capacity and can cause icing. Always a leak, never "used up."
5. **Failed run capacitor** — A cheap part that prevents the compressor or fan motor from starting; very common in hot climates.
6. **Bad contactor** — Worn or pitted contacts stop the outdoor unit from energizing.
7. **Thermostat or wiring issue** — Dead batteries, wrong mode, miswired or failed thermostat.
8. **Tripped breaker or blown control fuse** — The outdoor unit or air handler loses power.
9. **Failed compressor** — The expensive worst case; the unit hums or trips but won't cool.
10. **Control board fault** — Less common; varies by Lennox model and may log a code.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening sealed electrical panels or touching refrigerant lines.

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. Run the system for a few hours afterward.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser/disconnect) breakers. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor unit:** Make sure the condenser isn't blocked by plants, fencing, or debris. Gently rinse visible dirt from the coil fins with a garden hose (power off) — don't use a pressure washer.
- **Vents and registers:** Confirm supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain:** If the indoor unit has a clogged drain or a full safety float switch, it may shut off cooling. Clear a visibly clogged drain line and check for standing water in the pan.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or the outdoor line, turn the AC **OFF** and run only the **FAN** to thaw it (can take a few hours). Persistent icing means you need a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified Lennox tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you judge whether a quote is fair:

1. **Verify the complaint and check the thermostat/board** — reads any logged fault codes (codes vary by model/board).
2. **Confirm power and call for cooling** — verifies 24V signal reaching the contactor and the outdoor unit energizing.
3. **Measure electrical components** — tests the capacitor (microfarads), contactor, and compressor/fan amp draw.
4. **Inspect airflow** — checks filter, blower operation, and coil cleanliness.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** — measures suction/liquid pressures and calculates superheat/subcooling to confirm proper charge.
6. **Leak search if charge is low** — uses an electronic detector or dye; refrigerant is never simply "topped off" without finding the leak.
7. **Check the metering device and compressor** — if pressures and electricals point there.

Expect a diagnostic fee of **$75–$200**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unit runs, weak/no cool air | Clogged filter or dirty coils | Replace filter, rinse outdoor coil | Deep coil cleaning, airflow check |
| Ice on indoor coil or lineset | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw with fan only | Find/repair leak, recharge, clean coil |
| Outdoor fan spins, no cooling | Failed run capacitor or low charge | None — call pro | Test/replace capacitor, check charge |
| Outdoor unit dead, indoor blows warm | Tripped breaker or bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Compressor hums then stops/trips | Failed capacitor or compressor | None — call pro | Test compressor, replace part |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries / settings | Replace batteries, set to COOL | Replace/rewire thermostat |
| Water near indoor unit, AC off | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear line, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (regional pricing varies):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries / reset:** $0–$10 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Outdoor coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 (more if a coil must be replaced)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500 (sometimes a full condenser replacement is more economical)

If your system is **out of warranty and over ~12 years old**, weigh a major repair (compressor or coil) against full replacement.
