## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Some Lennox units with **iComfort** thermostats will display a specific error code on the wall unit, but most Lennox air conditioners (especially older Merit, ML and pre-iComfort models) have no display at all. The thermostat simply calls for cooling, but the air coming out of your vents stays warm, or the temperature never drops to setpoint.

Behind that symptom, the system has broken somewhere in the chain: airflow, electrical, refrigerant, or the controls that tie them together. The good news is that several common causes are safe for a homeowner to rule out before paying for a service call. The rest — anything involving refrigerant, the compressor, or live high-voltage components — is firmly in **pro** territory.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — chokes airflow across the indoor coil, which can freeze the coil into a block of ice and stop cooling entirely.
2. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — years of dust, grass clippings and cottonwood block heat rejection. The unit runs but can't dump heat.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure. A bad capacitor can stop the compressor, the outdoor fan, or both. You may hear humming or clicking.
4. **Tripped breaker or outdoor disconnect** — the indoor blower may run on furnace power while the outdoor unit sits dead.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, ice on the copper lines, or hissing. Always a leak — refrigerant is not "used up."
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit gets pitted or stuck and won't energize the compressor.
7. **Frozen evaporator coil** — downstream of restricted airflow or low refrigerant; ice on the indoor coil blocks airflow.
8. **Bad condenser fan motor** — if the outdoor fan won't spin, the compressor overheats and trips on its internal protector.
9. **Thermostat fault or wiring issue** — no cooling call ever reaches the equipment.
10. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; the unit's heart no longer pumps refrigerant.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not** open the electrical panel on the outdoor unit, touch the capacitor, or add refrigerant.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's a battery model. For iComfort, check the screen for an error code or message.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it. If you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the single most common cause of freeze-ups.
- **Breaker / disconnect:** Check the breaker panel for a tripped AC breaker (reset once). Look at the outdoor disconnect box near the condenser — it should be switched on. **If the breaker trips again immediately, stop and call a pro** — that's a fault, not a fluke.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit airflow:** Visually clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Do not remove panels.
- **Ice check:** Look at the copper lines and indoor coil area. If you see ice, **turn the system off and run the fan only** for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter. If it ices again, you need a tech.
- **Condensate:** A clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut cooling down. Check for standing water near the indoor unit.

If all of the above are clear and the unit still won't cool, it's time for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the workflow helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the call:** Confirm the thermostat is sending a Y (cooling) signal and 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. **Check airflow and the coil:** Inspect filter, blower, and evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
3. **Test the capacitor:** With a meter, measure microfarad value against the rating printed on the cap. A weak cap is replaced.
4. **Test the contactor and fan motor:** Check for pitting, proper pull-in, and that the condenser fan spins freely.
5. **Take refrigerant readings:** Connect gauges, read suction/liquid pressures and superheat/subcool. Low readings point to a leak.
6. **Leak search:** If refrigerant is low, an electronic detector or dye locates the leak — topping off without finding it is bad practice.
7. **Compressor test:** Check windings, amp draw, and start behavior if everything upstream is good.

A good tech tests components and shows you readings — they don't just swap parts blindly or quote a recharge without a leak search.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, indoor fan runs | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; run fan to thaw | Find airflow restriction, check charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, dead | Tripped breaker/disconnect | Reset breaker once; check disconnect | Test contactor, capacitor, wiring |
| Humming outdoor, fan not spinning | Failed capacitor or fan motor | None — high voltage | Test/replace capacitor or motor |
| Ice on copper lines | Low refrigerant or restricted airflow | Replace filter, thaw with fan | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Weak cooling, never reaches setpoint | Dirty condenser coil or low charge | Clear debris around unit | Clean coil, verify refrigerant |
| Outdoor unit clicks but won't start | Pitted contactor or weak start cap | None — high voltage | Replace contactor/capacitor |
| No cooling call at all | Thermostat / wiring fault | Replace batteries, check settings | Test 24V signal, repair wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$2,000 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,800–$2,500+ — often the point where replacing the whole unit makes more sense

Prices vary by region, unit size, and refrigerant type (older R-22 systems cost far more to recharge). On a unit over 12–15 years old with a failed compressor or major leak, ask your tech to compare repair vs. replacement.
