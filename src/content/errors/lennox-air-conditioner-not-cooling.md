## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code — it's a symptom. Your Lennox air conditioner may be running (fan spinning, compressor humming) but failing to lower the indoor temperature, or it may not be turning on at all when the thermostat calls for cooling.

Lennox systems with **iComfort** thermostats or communicating control boards may log actual error codes (like coil temperature faults or communicating errors) that point to the root cause. Older Lennox units with conventional thermostats give you no code at all — you're diagnosing purely by behavior. This page covers both.

If you see a specific numbered code on your thermostat, note it — a technician can cross-reference it to a precise fault.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common cause and the easiest to rule out.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — ice on the indoor coil blocks airflow entirely. Caused by low airflow (dirty filter/blower) or low refrigerant.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling and can freeze the coil. Requires a licensed tech; never a simple "top-off."
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a weak or dead capacitor stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting. Common in Lennox units 8+ years old.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood can't reject heat, killing efficiency.
6. **Failed contactor** — the outdoor unit's relay is pitted or stuck, so the compressor never energizes.
7. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a wiring/communication fault (especially iComfort setups).
8. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser or air handler has lost power.
9. **Compressor failure** — the worst case; the unit runs but produces no cooling, or trips on overload.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening a sealed panel or touching electrical components.

- **Air filter** — Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of poor cooling and frozen coils.
- **Thermostat** — Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on AUTO. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers** — Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker to the AC (usually a double-pole 30–50A) and the indoor air handler/furnace. Reset once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers** — Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit** — Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Frozen coil check** — If you see ice on the copper lines or the indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** and set the fan to **ON** to thaw it (can take 2–4 hours). Do not run cooling on a frozen coil.
- **Condensate drain** — A clogged drain can trip a float switch and shut cooling off. Check for standing water in the drain pan.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Lennox tech should work through this sequence — use it to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirm the call for cooling** — verifies the thermostat is sending 24V to the system and reads any stored iComfort/board fault codes.
2. **Check airflow** — inspects filter, blower operation, and coil for ice.
3. **Measure electrical** — tests the capacitor's microfarad rating, the contactor, and voltage at the condenser.
4. **Take refrigerant readings** — connects gauges to measure suction/liquid pressures and superheat/subcooling. Abnormal readings indicate low charge (a leak) or a restriction.
5. **Leak search** — if refrigerant is low, they use electronic detectors or dye to find the leak. A responsible tech won't just add refrigerant and leave.
6. **Compressor test** — checks amp draw and windings if the compressor won't start or run.

Be wary of any tech who adds refrigerant without diagnosing *why* it's low.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| AC runs, weak airflow | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Find leak, recharge, fix airflow |
| Outdoor fan won't spin | Failed capacitor | None (electrical) | Test and replace capacitor |
| Warm air from vents | Low refrigerant / compressor | Check filter first | Leak search, recharge, compressor test |
| Nothing turns on | Breaker or thermostat | Reset breaker once, check t-stat | Diagnose control circuit / board |
| Water around indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Outdoor coil dirty | Poor heat rejection | Gently rinse fins with hose | Chemical coil cleaning |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges for Lennox AC cooling repairs. Prices vary by region and unit age.

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 DIY
- **Condensate drain cleaning** — $0 DIY – $150 pro
- **Run/dual capacitor** — $150–$400 installed
- **Contactor** — $150–$350 installed
- **Condenser coil cleaning** — $150–$300
- **Refrigerant recharge (with leak repair)** — $600–$1,600 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is far pricier)
- **TXV / metering device** — $500–$900
- **Blower motor** — $450–$900
- **Compressor replacement** — $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth weighing against a new system on units 12+ years old)

Note: units still using **R-22 refrigerant** cost dramatically more to recharge, since it's phased out. If yours is R-22 and leaking, ask about replacement economics.
