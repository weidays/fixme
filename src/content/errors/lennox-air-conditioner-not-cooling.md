## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific Lennox fault code — it's a symptom. Your Lennox air conditioner may be running (the indoor blower and/or outdoor unit are on) but the air coming from your vents is warm, lukewarm, or not cold enough to hold the thermostat setpoint. On Lennox systems with an **iComfort** thermostat, you may instead see a specific alert or code on the display; on **legacy 24V thermostats** you'll just notice the house warming up.

A central AC cools by moving heat from inside your home to the outdoors using refrigerant. "Not cooling" means that heat-transfer loop is broken somewhere — airflow, refrigerant charge, electrical components, or controls. The goal of this page is to help you rule out the easy stuff safely, then understand what a technician should check before you approve a repair.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow across the indoor coil, reducing cooling and often freezing the coil solid.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely. You'll feel weak or no airflow.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, and cottonwood block the unit from rejecting heat, so cooling fades on hot days.
4. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (Heat instead of Cool), or a wiring/configuration fault, especially after a new install.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan won't start; the outdoor unit hums but doesn't spin.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck, so the condenser never energizes.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling capacity; never a "top-off" fix — the leak must be found and repaired.
8. **Failed compressor** — the heart of the system; expensive and usually the last suspect after others are ruled out.
9. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit dead while indoor blower runs.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — no panels off, no refrigerant, no electrical components touched.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at the light. If it's gray or clogged, replace it. This is the #1 cause and may be all you need.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's a battery model. Lower the temp 3–4 degrees and listen for the system to start.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker (both the indoor air handler/furnace circuit and the outdoor condenser circuit). Reset once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers. Blocked airflow can freeze the coil.
- **Outdoor unit:** Visually clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Don't remove panels.
- **Frozen coil / ice:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **Off** and set the fan to **On** for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter. If it refreezes, you have an airflow or refrigerant problem for a pro.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a float switch that shuts cooling down. If you can see standing water in the drain pan, that's a sign to call.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Lennox tech should work the problem in order rather than jumping to "you need refrigerant." Expect them to:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measure supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Check airflow** — inspect the filter, blower, and coil for ice or dirt.
3. **Inspect the outdoor unit** — measure incoming voltage, test the capacitor with a meter, and check the contactor for pitting.
4. **Read controls** — on iComfort systems, pull stored alerts/codes from the thermostat or control board; on legacy systems, confirm the 24V call for cooling reaches the contactor.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** — measure pressures and superheat/subcooling to determine if the charge is correct. Low charge triggers a **leak search** (electronic detector, dye, or bubble test), not a top-off.
6. **Test the compressor** — check windings, amp draw, and start components.

A good quote names the failed part and explains why. Be cautious of any tech who adds refrigerant without mentioning a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow, ice on lines | Frozen coil / dirty filter | Replace filter, run fan to thaw | Find airflow/refrigerant root cause |
| House won't cool, dirty filter | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Verify no coil damage |
| Outdoor unit hums but fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit completely dead | Tripped breaker, contactor, or fuse | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, low-voltage fuse, wiring |
| Cooling fades on hottest days | Dirty condenser coil | Clear debris around unit | Professional coil cleaning |
| Gradual loss of cooling over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| No cooling, blower runs fine, iComfort alert shown | Control/thermostat fault | Note the code, check Cool mode | Diagnose board/thermostat config |
| No cold air, AC short-cycles | Failing compressor | Shut off, don't cycle | Compressor test and replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Your price varies by region and contractor.

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 installed; more for smart/iComfort models.
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250.
- **Low-voltage fuse / minor wiring:** $100–$250.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500, depending on leak location and refrigerant type (older R-22 systems cost far more).
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,000.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,200+. On an older system, replacing the whole unit often makes more financial sense.
