## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numeric fault code — it's the symptom homeowners report most often with Lennox air conditioners. Your system may be running, blowing air, or appearing to operate normally, yet the indoor temperature won't drop to the thermostat setpoint.

On newer Lennox systems with an **iComfort** thermostat or a communicating control board, you may see an actual alert or numbered code stored in the thermostat menu or on the outdoor control board's LED. On older conventional (non-communicating) Merit and many Elite systems, there's no code at all — you just have warm air. Because reporting differs by model and board generation, treat any displayed code as the more specific guide, and use this page to narrow down a no-cooling complaint.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat set wrong or batteries dead** — wrong mode, schedule override, or a dead conventional thermostat.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power, so only the indoor fan runs.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — the most common electrical failure; compressor and/or fan motor won't start.
5. **Burned or stuck contactor** — the 24V relay that powers the outdoor unit fails to close.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — outdoor coil packed with dirt, grass, or cottonwood can't reject heat.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling and can ice the indoor coil; always a leak, never normal "use."
8. **Frozen evaporator coil** — usually downstream of low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks cooling entirely.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — compressor runs but overheats and shuts down on its internal protector.
10. **Failed compressor** — least common but most expensive; unit hums or trips the breaker.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop at the first one that fixes it.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at the light. If it's gray or clogged, replace it. A restricted filter is the single most common cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. If it's a battery model and the screen is dim or blank, install fresh batteries.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor disconnect breakers. Reset one that's tripped **once**. If it trips again, stop — that's a fault for a pro.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for grass, leaves, or cottonwood matted against the coil. You may gently rinse the *exterior* fins with a garden hose (power off) — do not bend the fins or open the cabinet.
- **Condensate drain/pan:** A full pan or clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut off cooling. Check for standing water near the indoor unit.
- **Ice:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **Off** and the fan to **On** to thaw it (can take hours), then call a pro — icing means low airflow or low refrigerant.

Do **not** open the outdoor cabinet, touch the capacitor, or attempt to add refrigerant.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Lennox tech follows a repeatable path. Knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — confirms thermostat call for cooling and measures supply/return air temperature split (typically 15–20°F when healthy).
2. **Check power and controls** — confirms 24V at the contactor, line voltage at the disconnect, and reads any stored codes on the iComfort thermostat or board LED.
3. **Test electrical components** — measures the run capacitor with a meter (not by guessing), inspects the contactor for pitting, and checks the fan and compressor windings.
4. **Inspect airflow and coils** — examines the filter, blower, and both coils; looks for a frozen evaporator.
5. **Read refrigerant pressures** — connects gauges to check suction and liquid pressures and superheat/subcooling. Abnormal readings point to a leak or charge problem.
6. **Leak search if needed** — uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak before any recharge.

Be cautious of any tech who "tops off" refrigerant without finding the leak — that's a temporary fix you'll pay for again.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor fan runs, warm air | Dirty filter or frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw coil with fan on | Clear airflow restriction, check charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Tripped breaker, blown fuse, bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace fuse/contactor, test 24V circuit |
| Outdoor fan spins, no cold air | Failed run capacitor or compressor | None — call a pro | Test/replace capacitor; check compressor |
| Loud hum from outdoor unit, no start | Failed capacitor or seized compressor | Shut off, call a pro | Replace capacitor or compressor |
| Ice on copper lines / indoor coil | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Set to Off, fan On to thaw | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Breaker trips every time | Shorted compressor or wiring | Stop resetting; call a pro | Diagnose short, replace component |
| Weak cooling, high indoor humidity | Dirty condenser coil or low charge | Rinse exterior coil, replace filter | Clean coil, verify refrigerant charge |
| Water near indoor unit, no cooling | Clogged condensate / float switch tripped | Check drain for standing water | Clear drain line, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US prices including parts and labor. Your area, model, and warranty status will shift these.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY, near $0 in labor)
- **Thermostat batteries:** under $10 (DIY)
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$700
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 (more if the leak is in the coil)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth weighing against a full system replacement if out of warranty)

Lennox compressors and coils often carry 5–10 year parts warranties if registered — confirm coverage before approving major work, since you may only owe labor.
