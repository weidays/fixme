## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a specific error code on Lennox air conditioners — it's a symptom. Unlike furnaces, which flash diagnostic codes (see Lennox Furnace Code 200), most residential Lennox AC condensers don't display fault codes at the unit. Higher-end communicating systems with an iComfort thermostat may show a status or alert on the screen, but the majority of Merit and Elite split systems give you no code at all.

So "not cooling" covers any situation where the system runs but the house won't reach setpoint, the air from the vents is warm or weak, or the outdoor unit won't start. The job here is to narrow that broad symptom down to a root cause — which ranges from a $0 filter swap to a failed compressor.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — Restricted airflow is the single most common cause of poor cooling and frozen coils. Easy to check, easy to fix.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — Caused by low airflow (dirty filter) or low refrigerant. Ice on the indoor coil or the copper line set blocks heat transfer, so vents blow warm.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — A blown dual run capacitor stops the compressor and/or condenser fan from starting. You'll often hear a hum from the outdoor unit but see no fan spinning.
4. **Tripped breaker or pulled disconnect** — The outdoor unit has its own breaker and a disconnect box near the condenser. If either is off, the compressor can't run.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Refrigerant doesn't get "used up." Low charge always means a leak. Symptoms include long run times, weak cooling, and ice on the line set.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — A coil packed with grass, dust, or cottonwood can't reject heat, so the system runs constantly without cooling well.
7. **Failed contactor** — The relay that sends power to the compressor can pit or stick, leaving the outdoor unit dead or chattering.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring issue** — Dead thermostat batteries, a loose wire, or a misconfigured iComfort can prevent a cooling call.
9. **Failed compressor** — The most expensive cause. The compressor won't start (or trips the breaker) even with a good capacitor and proper voltage.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Air filter** — Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone can stop cooling and freeze the coil.
- **Thermostat** — Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered or the screen is dim.
- **Breakers** — Check both the indoor air-handler/furnace breaker and the separate outdoor AC breaker in your main panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect** — There's a pull-out disconnect in a box on the wall near the condenser. Make sure it's fully seated.
- **Supply and return vents** — Confirm registers are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many vents starves airflow.
- **Condensate drain** — If the system shut off and the drain pan is full, a clogged condensate line may have tripped a float switch. You can clear a visible clog and empty the pan; pour drain tablets to prevent regrowth.
- **Look for ice** — If you see ice on the indoor coil or the insulated copper line outside, turn the system to FAN ONLY (not COOL) and let it thaw fully — this can take a few hours. Running a frozen unit damages the compressor.

If the coil keeps freezing after a filter change and full thaw, that points to low refrigerant — call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence, and knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the cooling call** — Confirms the thermostat is sending 24V to the contactor (Y signal). Rules out thermostat and low-voltage wiring.
2. **Check power at the disconnect and contactor** — Confirms 240V is reaching the unit.
3. **Test the capacitor** — Uses a meter to read microfarads against the rated value printed on the cap. A weak cap reads low and gets replaced.
4. **Measure compressor and fan amp draw** — Confirms whether motors are starting and pulling correct current.
5. **Connect gauges and take temperatures** — Reads suction/liquid pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge refrigerant charge. This is how a leak or undercharge is confirmed — not by guessing.
6. **Inspect coils and airflow** — Checks the condenser and evaporator for dirt and the blower for proper speed.
7. **Leak search if low** — Uses electronic detectors or dye to find the leak before adding refrigerant.

Be wary of any tech who "just adds a couple pounds of refrigerant" without finding a leak — that's a temporary fix you'll pay for again.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vents blow warm, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker, pulled disconnect, dead contactor | Check/reset breaker once, seat disconnect | Test contactor, voltage, capacitor |
| Outdoor unit hums but fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None safely | Test and replace capacitor |
| Weak cooling, long run times | Dirty filter or coil, low refrigerant | Replace filter, rinse condenser fins gently | Measure charge, find/repair leak |
| Ice on coil or copper line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Set to FAN, thaw fully, replace filter | Diagnose airflow vs charge |
| System short-cycles, vents warm | Float switch tripped by clogged drain | Clear drain, empty pan, add tablets | Clear line, check pan/pump |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries, low-voltage fault | Replace batteries | Trace 24V wiring, test transformer |
| Breaker trips immediately on cooling | Shorted compressor or wiring fault | Stop — do not keep resetting | Test compressor windings, wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor; your area and system size affect totals:

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries** — $5 (DIY)
- **Run/dual capacitor** — $150–$400 installed
- **Contactor** — $150–$350 installed
- **Condenser coil cleaning** — $100–$250
- **Condensate drain clearing** — $75–$200
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge** — $600–$1,500+ depending on leak location; coil leaks cost more
- **Evaporator coil replacement** — $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement** — $1,800–$2,500+ (often worth comparing against full system replacement on older units)
- **Diagnostic/service call** — $90–$180, often credited toward the repair

If your Lennox system is over 12–15 years old and uses R-22 refrigerant, a major repair like a compressor or coil may not be cost-effective versus replacement — get the tech's honest read.
