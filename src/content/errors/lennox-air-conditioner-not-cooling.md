## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a fault code. Many Lennox air conditioners — especially Merit and Elite single-stage units — do not display an error code for general cooling complaints. Higher-end **Signature Collection units with iComfort thermostats and communicating boards** may log diagnostic codes you can read at the thermostat, but a basic "house won't get cold" problem often shows no code at all.

The phrase covers several distinct conditions:

- The system runs but blows warm or weakly cool air.
- The outdoor unit (condenser) hums or buzzes but the fan doesn't spin.
- The indoor blower runs but the outdoor unit never starts.
- The system short-cycles (turns on and off quickly) without lowering the temperature.

Because the cause can range from a $0 filter swap to a failed compressor, this page helps you triage what's safe to check yourself and what needs a licensed technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat exchange so air comes out warm.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — debris, grass clippings or cottonwood block heat rejection, killing efficiency.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — undercharged systems cannot absorb heat. This is a sealed-system repair, never a DIY top-off.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a common, relatively cheap part failure that stops the compressor or fan motor from starting.
6. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse / disconnect** — the outdoor unit gets no power.
7. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode, loss of communication on iComfort systems.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser doesn't pull in.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — outdoor unit overheats and shuts down on a thermal limit.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive failure; the system runs but produces no real cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is hot, smells burnt, or requires opening a sealed refrigerant system.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check the electrical panel for a tripped breaker for both the air handler and the outdoor unit. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor unit airflow:** Make sure grass, leaves, and debris are clear of the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance on all sides.
- **Supply and return vents:** Confirm vents inside the house are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain:** A full drain pan or clogged line can trip a safety float switch and stop cooling. You can clear visible standing water and confirm the line isn't blocked at its exit point.
- **Check for ice:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or the refrigerant lines, **turn the system to OFF and the fan to ON** to thaw it. Do not run cooling on a frozen coil.

**Do not** add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping or locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech follows a logical sequence so you can sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — confirms thermostat settings, indoor/outdoor airflow, and measures supply vs. return temperature (a healthy split is roughly 15–20°F).
2. **Check power** — tests voltage at the disconnect, contactor, and capacitor.
3. **Test the capacitor** — measures microfarads against the rating; a weak capacitor is a quick, cheap find.
4. **Inspect coils and airflow** — looks for a frozen evaporator, dirty condenser, or failing blower.
5. **Take refrigerant readings** — connects gauges and checks superheat/subcooling. Low charge points to a leak, which they'll locate with an electronic detector or dye.
6. **Read iComfort codes** (Signature units) — pulls stored fault data from the communicating board to pinpoint sensor, communication, or compressor faults.
7. **Test the compressor** — checks amperage draw and windings if everything upstream is good.

Be wary of any tech who recommends "just adding refrigerant" without finding the leak — undercharge always has a cause.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Check blower & duct static pressure |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or refrigerant leak |
| Outdoor fan won't spin, humming | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit dead, no hum | Tripped breaker / bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Low refrigerant / failed compressor | None — call pro | Leak search, recharge, or compressor replacement |
| Short cycling | Dirty condenser, electrical fault | Clear debris around unit | Diagnose pressures and controls |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries or power loss | Replace batteries | Check transformer / wiring |
| iComfort error on display | Communication or sensor fault | Note the code, call pro | Read board codes, replace sensor/board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Regional rates and unit tier (Merit vs. Signature) shift these.

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $75–$250
- **Coil cleaning (evaporator or condenser):** $150–$500
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often near the cost of a new condenser)

If the unit is out of warranty and the compressor has failed, ask whether replacing the outdoor unit — or the whole system — is more economical than a major repair.
