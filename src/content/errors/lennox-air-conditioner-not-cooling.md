## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's the symptom you see when your Lennox air conditioner runs but the house never reaches setpoint, or blows air that isn't cold. On older Lennox units (Merit and many Elite models) there's no fault display at all, so you diagnose by behavior. On newer iComfort-enabled systems (S30/S40 thermostats with compatible outdoor units), the thermostat or app may log a specific alert or equipment code that points to airflow, refrigerant pressure, or a compressor problem.

Because Lennox covers everything from single-stage 13ACX units to variable-capacity XC25 systems, the exact diagnostics differ. This page covers the causes common to all of them and flags where behavior varies by model.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops coil temperature, and can freeze the evaporator. The single most common cause of weak or no cooling.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant. Ice on the coil or the copper line at the indoor unit blocks heat transfer entirely.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a bulging or dead capacitor stops the compressor and/or condenser fan from starting. Very common in hot-climate units after 5–8 years.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Lennox systems are sealed; "low charge" means a leak. Symptoms include warm air, hissing, ice, and long run times.
5. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked grass, dust or cottonwood blocks heat rejection, so the system can't cool effectively.
6. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — outdoor disconnect fuses or a tripped breaker leave the indoor blower running while the compressor stays off.
7. **Contactor failure** — pitted or stuck contacts prevent the outdoor unit from energizing.
8. **Thermostat or wiring fault** — dead thermostat batteries, a miswired or failed thermostat, or a broken low-voltage wire.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive outcome, often the end result of an ignored capacitor or charge problem.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Replace the air filter.** A clogged filter is the cheapest fix and the most common cause. Use the correct size and check the airflow-direction arrow.
- **Check the thermostat.** Set it to COOL, lower the setpoint at least 5°F, and confirm the fan is set to AUTO. On battery thermostats, install fresh AA batteries.
- **Look for ice.** If you see frost on the indoor coil or the copper line, turn the system OFF (set thermostat fan to ON to help it thaw) and let it melt fully before running again. Running a frozen unit can damage the compressor.
- **Check the breaker.** Find both the indoor air-handler breaker and the outdoor AC breaker. If one is tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Inspect supply and return vents.** Make sure registers aren't closed, blocked by furniture, or covered, and that the return grille is clear.
- **Clear the outdoor unit.** Turn off power at the disconnect, then gently rinse caked debris off the condenser fins with a garden hose (never a pressure washer) and clear weeds within two feet.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A full drain pan or clogged line can trip a float switch that shuts the system down. Clear standing water and add drain tablets if you have access.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a breaker or contactor that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech will typically:

1. **Verify the complaint** and read any iComfort alerts on S30/S40 systems if equipped.
2. **Measure airflow and inspect the coil** for ice or dirt, confirming the filter and blower operation.
3. **Check capacitor microfarads** with a meter against the rating printed on the part.
4. **Test the contactor** for proper pull-in and check incoming/outgoing voltage.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** and measure superheat and subcooling to determine if the charge is correct — and if low, perform a leak search (electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen).
6. **Measure compressor amperage** against the nameplate rated load amps to judge compressor health.
7. **Inspect low-voltage wiring** and thermostat signals (Y, C, R) at the board.

If a quote jumps straight to "recharge the refrigerant" without mentioning a leak search, ask why — a sealed system that's low has a leak that needs to be found.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Ice on coil or copper line | Low airflow or low charge | Turn off, thaw fully, replace filter | Check charge, find leak |
| Outdoor fan runs, no cold air | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Indoor blower runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker/fuse or contactor | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, fuses, wiring |
| Long run times, never cools | Low refrigerant (leak) | None | Leak search, seal, recharge |
| Hot, dirty outdoor coil | Blocked condenser | Rinse fins, clear weeds | Deep coil clean |
| System shuts off, pan full of water | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear line, check pump |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries / wiring | Replace batteries | Diagnose thermostat/wiring |
| Loud hum, no start, hot compressor | Compressor or overload failure | None | Test amps, replace compressor |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US prices including parts and labor; your area and Lennox model affect the total.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 service call
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Outdoor coil deep cleaning:** $150–$350
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often near the cost of a new condenser; weigh against replacement if out of warranty)

Lennox parts may be covered under a 5- or 10-year limited warranty if the unit was registered — check before paying for compressor or coil parts.
