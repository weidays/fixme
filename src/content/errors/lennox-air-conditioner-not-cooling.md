## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numbered fault code on most Lennox air conditioners — it's a symptom. Your indoor blower may run and the thermostat calls for cooling, but the air coming from the vents is warm, lukewarm, or the house never reaches setpoint.

On Lennox systems with communicating controls (iComfort / S30 / S40 thermostats paired with EL or XC variable-capacity outdoor units), you may instead see a specific alert or error number on the thermostat screen. If you have a numbered alert, note it — it narrows the diagnosis. This page covers the general "warm air / won't cool" complaint that applies across Merit, Elite and Signature lines.

Lennox AC behavior varies by model. Single-stage units simply run or don't; two-stage and variable units modulate, so "weak cooling" can also mean the unit is correctly running at low capacity. When in doubt, check the thermostat's reported mode.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow reduces cooling and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode (fan "on" vs "auto"), dead batteries, schedule override, or a setpoint that isn't actually calling for cool.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — the outdoor condenser has power but isn't running, so you get blower-only airflow.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure that prevents the compressor and/or condenser fan from starting; you may hear humming or clicking.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — clogged with grass, dust or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat and cooling drops off.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer and only warm or no air flows.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Lennox systems are sealed; if charge is low, there's a leak. Causes weak cooling and coil freezing.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks and stops the compressor from energizing.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit runs the fan but produces no cold refrigerant.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here and call a pro for anything involving refrigerant or electrical components.

- **Check the air filter.** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Verify the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check the breakers.** Find the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers. If one is tripped, switch it fully off, then on. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Look at the vents and returns.** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A clogged drain or full pan can trip a float switch and shut the system down. Clear visible standing water if you can reach the pan safely.
- **Look at the outdoor unit.** Clear leaves, grass and debris from around it. If you see thick ice on the lines or coil, **turn the system to fan-only** and let it thaw fully before running cooling again — never chip ice off.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, remove the electrical panel on the condenser, or top off refrigerant. Those require an EPA-certified technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Lennox tech follows a logical path. Knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirms the complaint** — measures supply vs return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and evaporator coil for icing or dirt.
3. **Tests electrical** — measures capacitor microfarads against the rating stamped on it, checks the contactor, and verifies voltage to the compressor and fan.
4. **Reads pressures** — connects gauges to the service ports to check refrigerant charge and superheat/subcooling. Low readings indicate a leak.
5. **Leak search** — if refrigerant is low, uses electronic detectors or dye to find the leak (recharging without finding the leak is bad practice).
6. **Checks communicating data** — on iComfort/S30/S40 systems, pulls alert history and live data from the control board.

Ask the tech to show you the capacitor reading or pressure numbers — reputable techs will.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blower runs, warm air from vents | Tripped condenser breaker or bad capacitor | Reset breaker once; check filter | Test/replace capacitor or contactor |
| Weak airflow, not enough cooling | Dirty filter or restricted airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Clean coil, check blower |
| Ice on lines or coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Fan-only to thaw, replace filter | Find leak, recharge, clear coil |
| Outdoor fan runs, no cold air | Failed compressor or low charge | None — call a pro | Test compressor, check pressures |
| Unit hums but won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Replace capacitor |
| System shuts off shortly after starting | Clogged condensate / float switch | Clear drain, empty pan | Clear line, test switch |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries or blown low-voltage fuse | Replace batteries | Replace fuse, check transformer |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, including parts and labor. Prices vary by region and contractor.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $100–$400 depending on model (iComfort thermostats cost more)
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$450
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Evaporator coil thaw + diagnosis:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth weighing against full system replacement on older units)
- **Service call / diagnostic fee:** $75–$200, often credited toward repair

A note on refrigerant: if your system uses R-22 (older units), recharge costs are much higher and a major leak may make replacement more economical. Newer Lennox units use R-410A or R-454B.
