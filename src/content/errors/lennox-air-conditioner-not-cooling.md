## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single Lennox fault code — it's a symptom. Your Lennox air conditioner may be running, blowing air, and showing no error on the thermostat, yet the air coming from your vents is warm or only mildly cool. On units with a communicating control board (many Signature SL and Elite XC series with iComfort thermostats), a related diagnostic code may appear; on basic Merit-series units there's often no code at all, just poor performance.

The root issue is almost always one of three things: **airflow** (the indoor side can't move enough air across the coil), **refrigerant charge** (a leak has dropped the system's ability to absorb heat), or an **electrical/component failure** (a capacitor, contactor, or compressor that won't run). This page walks through what you can safely check and what genuinely requires a licensed HVAC technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, freezes the evaporator coil, and kills cooling output. The single most common cause.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice on the indoor coil blocks heat transfer entirely.
3. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — grass, cottonwood, and dust insulate the coil so it can't dump heat.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a bad capacitor stops the compressor or fan motor from starting; you may hear a hum or clicking.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity; often shows as ice on the lines or weak airflow. Requires EPA-certified repair.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks, so the compressor never engages.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a broken signal wire never calls for cooling.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive failure; the unit runs but produces no cold refrigerant.
10. **Failed blower or fan motor / control board fault** — on communicating Lennox systems, a board error can disable the call for cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening the sealed refrigerant system or electrical panels beyond the breaker.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. On battery-powered thermostats, replace the batteries.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it against a light. If it's gray or clogged, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling and frozen coils.
- **Breaker:** Check the breakers for both the indoor air handler/furnace and the outdoor condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers. Closing too many rooms starves the system.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Do **not** spray a pressure washer into the coil fins.
- **Condensate drain:** If the system shut down, a clogged condensate drain may have tripped a float switch. Look for a full drain pan; a dropped pan tablet can help, but a fully clogged line needs clearing.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to FAN-only (cooling OFF) for a few hours to let it thaw, then replace the filter before restarting.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Lennox tech follows a logical sequence, and knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirms the symptom** — measures supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects filter, blower wheel, and coil for ice or dirt before touching refrigerant.
3. **Reads control board codes** — on communicating Elite/Signature units they'll pull stored fault codes from the iComfort thermostat or board LED.
4. **Tests electrical components** — measures capacitor microfarads against the nameplate rating, checks the contactor, and verifies compressor/fan motor draw with a clamp meter.
5. **Gauges the refrigerant** — connects manifold gauges to read suction and head pressures and calculate superheat/subcooling. Low pressures point to a leak.
6. **Locates leaks** — uses an electronic detector or nitrogen pressure test if charge is low; a proper repair finds and fixes the leak, not just a top-off.
7. **Verifies the fix** — re-measures the temperature split and amp draw after repair.

If a tech wants to "just add a pound of Freon" without looking for a leak, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, dirty filter | Clogged filter / low airflow | Replace filter, clear vents | Inspect blower and coil if it persists |
| Ice on indoor coil or lines | Frozen evaporator | Turn off cooling, run fan to thaw | Find low airflow or low charge cause |
| Outdoor unit silent, no fan | Tripped breaker, contactor, or capacitor | Reset breaker once | Test capacitor/contactor, replace |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed start/run capacitor | None — call a pro | Replace capacitor, verify compressor |
| Weak cooling, lines iced over | Low refrigerant (leak) | Replace filter, then call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge to spec |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Failed compressor or severe undercharge | None — call a pro | Pressure test, replace compressor |
| System won't turn on at all | Thermostat, batteries, or low-voltage fuse | Replace batteries, check mode | Test transformer, fuse, board |
| Cooling drops then unit shuts off | Clogged condensate / float switch | Clear drain, add pan tablet | Clear line, check pump/switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Your price varies by region and unit accessibility.

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250.
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$450.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300.
- **Low-voltage transformer or fuse:** $75–$250.
- **Refrigerant leak repair:** $200–$1,500 depending on leak location (a coil leak is far more than a fitting).
- **Refrigerant recharge:** $100–$600+ depending on system and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is much costlier).
- **Blower or condenser fan motor:** $400–$700.
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ — at this price, weigh it against replacing the whole condenser, especially if out of warranty.

Many Lennox units carry a 5- or 10-year parts warranty (registration required), which can cover the compressor or coil part cost — you'd still pay labor and refrigerant.
