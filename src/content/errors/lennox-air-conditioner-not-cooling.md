## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a specific fault code — it's the symptom you notice when your Lennox air conditioner runs (or tries to run) but the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint. On communicating **iComfort** systems you may see a related alert or setback message on the thermostat, but most single-stage Merit and Elite units give you no code at all — just warm air.

Cooling depends on three things working together: airflow across the indoor evaporator coil, a running outdoor condenser, and a properly charged sealed refrigerant loop. If any one of those fails, the result looks the same to a homeowner: the AC is "on" but the house is hot. Because several of the likely causes involve refrigerant, high-voltage components, or the sealed system, this is rated **pro** — a few checks are safe, but most repairs are not DIY.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a common failure that stops the compressor or fan motor from starting; you'll often hear a hum or clicking.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces capacity and can cause icing; requires a licensed tech to find and seal.
5. **Failed or pitted contactor** — the outdoor relay won't pull in, so the condenser never energizes.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor unit can't reject heat, so cooling drops on hot days.
7. **Thermostat problem or wiring fault** — bad setpoint, dead batteries, or a broken low-voltage wire.
8. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — kills power to the condenser or control board.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor or blower motor** — no airflow over one of the coils.
10. **Failed compressor** — the least common but most expensive; the unit hums but won't cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or opening sealed/electrical panels:

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers in your panel. If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure furniture, rugs, or closed dampers aren't blocking registers. All supply vents should be open.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser and make sure the fan is spinning when the system calls for cooling. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Condensate:** Check for standing water or a full drain pan at the indoor unit. A clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and stop cooling. Clearing a visible clog with a wet/dry vac at the drain line outlet is fine.
- **Ice:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** and set the fan to **ON** to thaw it (can take a few hours), then run cooling again. Repeated freezing means a real problem — call a pro.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, discharge capacitors, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Lennox technician follows a logical sequence — use this to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Confirm the call:** Verify the thermostat is calling for cooling and that 24V is reaching the condenser contactor.
2. **Electrical checks:** Test the contactor, capacitor, and motor windings with a multimeter. A weak capacitor is a cheap, fast find.
3. **Airflow inspection:** Check filter, blower operation, and the evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
4. **Refrigerant pressures:** Connect gauges to read suction and liquid-line pressures and measure superheat/subcooling. This tells them if the charge is correct — and low pressure points to a leak.
5. **Leak search:** If undercharged, they'll use electronic detectors or dye to locate the leak before adding refrigerant. Recharging without finding the leak is a red flag.
6. **Condenser check:** Inspect the outdoor coil for dirt and test the condenser fan motor.
7. **Compressor test:** If everything else checks out but the unit still won't cool, they'll test compressor amp draw and windings.

On **iComfort communicating systems**, the tech may also pull fault history from the thermostat and check for communication faults between the indoor and outdoor control boards, which can mimic a cooling failure.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air from vents, unit running | Dirty filter or low refrigerant | Replace filter; check for ice | Measure charge, find/repair leak |
| Ice on indoor coil or copper lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw, swap filter | Diagnose airflow vs. charge, repair |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor blower runs | Contactor, capacitor, or breaker | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor or capacitor |
| Humming outdoor unit, fan not spinning | Failed capacitor or fan motor | None — power components | Replace capacitor or fan motor |
| Weak cooling on hot days only | Dirty condenser coil | Clear debris, rinse coil gently | Deep-clean coil, verify charge |
| Nothing runs at all | Tripped breaker or blown 24V fuse | Check/reset breaker once | Replace fuse, find short |
| Water near indoor unit, AC stops | Clogged condensate drain, float switch | Clear visible clog, vacuum line | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Unit short-cycles, never cools | Thermostat, control board, or low charge | Replace tstat batteries | Diagnose board/charge/tstat |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included where noted:

- **Air filter:** $10 – $40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries / reset:** $0 – $15 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75 – $250 professionally, or DIY with a wet/dry vac.
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150 – $400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350 installed.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300 – $650 installed.
- **Blower motor:** $400 – $900 installed (ECM motors cost more).
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100 – $300.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500 – $1,600 depending on leak location and system charge; a coil replacement pushes this higher.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900 – $2,000 installed.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,800 – $2,800+ installed — often the point where replacing the whole condenser or system makes more financial sense, especially on units over 10 years old.

Prices vary by region, unit tonnage, and whether your Lennox is still under its parts warranty (typically 5–10 years registered). Always get an itemized quote.
