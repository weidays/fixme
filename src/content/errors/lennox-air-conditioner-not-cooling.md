## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numeric fault code — it's the symptom you'll describe to a technician when your Lennox air conditioner runs but the house won't reach the thermostat setpoint, or the air from the vents feels lukewarm. On Lennox units with an **iComfort communicating thermostat**, you may instead see a specific alert code on the display; on standard 24V systems there's usually no code at all, just warm air.

Because "not cooling" covers everything from a $20 filter to a failed compressor, the goal of this page is to help you narrow it down, do the safe checks yourself, and recognize a fair repair quote. The **pro** severity reflects that the most common *unfixed* causes — refrigerant leaks, electrical faults, and compressor issues — require a licensed HVAC technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, freezes the evaporator coil, and kills cooling output. Cheapest and most common.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat. Very common in summer.
3. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "fan" not "cool"), or a miswired/communication fault on iComfort systems.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a swollen or dead capacitor stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting. Common and inexpensive.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, ice on the lines, or hissing. Always indicates a leak; never just a "low charge."
6. **Tripped breaker or blown contactor** — the outdoor unit gets no power, so the fan/compressor never run.
7. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by airflow restriction or low refrigerant; blocks airflow until it thaws.
8. **Clogged condensate drain / tripped float switch** — a safety float can shut down cooling to prevent water damage.
9. **Failed compressor** — least common but most expensive; the unit hums or trips but won't cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Air filter:** Pull it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of "not cooling."
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers. Flip a tripped one **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for grass clippings, leaves, or debris blocking the condenser fins. Clear debris and trim plants back 2 ft. Don't bend the fins or spray with a pressure washer.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain / float switch:** If you see standing water in the drain pan or near the indoor unit, the float switch may have tripped. Clearing a visibly clogged drain line is okay; don't bypass the switch.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** but leave the **FAN ON** for a few hours to thaw, then restart. If it freezes again, call a pro — that means low refrigerant or an airflow problem.

If none of these restore cooling, the remaining causes are refrigerant, electrical, or mechanical — and those need a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Lennox technician will typically:

1. **Confirm the symptom** and check thermostat settings, mode, and (on iComfort) pull any stored alert codes from the controller.
2. **Measure the temperature split** (return vs. supply air). A healthy system shows roughly a 16–22°F drop; a small split points to refrigerant or airflow issues.
3. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower, evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
4. **Check electrical components** — capacitor microfarad reading, contactor, and incoming voltage at the condenser.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** and read suction/liquid pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge the charge. If low, they'll **leak-search** rather than just top off.
6. **Test the compressor** for amp draw, hard starts, or grounded windings.

A trustworthy quote names the specific failed part and what was measured (e.g., "capacitor reading 18 µF on a 45 µF rating"). Be cautious of a quote that just says "add refrigerant" with no mention of finding the leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow | Clogged filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw coil with fan on | Inspect coil, restore airflow, check charge |
| Outdoor fan runs, compressor hums then stops | Failed capacitor or compressor | None | Test/replace capacitor; test compressor |
| Outdoor unit completely dead | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Ice on refrigerant lines | Low refrigerant or airflow restriction | Thaw with fan; replace filter | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Hissing or bubbling near lines | Refrigerant leak | None | Locate and repair leak, recharge |
| System shut off, water in drain pan | Tripped condensate float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Clear line, test/replace float switch |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries or control fault | Replace batteries | Diagnose 24V/iComfort wiring or board |
| Cools weakly even when clean | Dirty condenser coil or low charge | Rinse debris off outdoor unit gently | Deep coil clean, verify charge |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condensate float switch + drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Outdoor coil deep cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 (depends on leak location and refrigerant type; R-410A vs. older R-22 raises cost sharply)
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing against a new condenser or full system if the unit is older or out of warranty)

Many Lennox units carry a **5- or 10-year parts warranty** (compressor often covered longer if registered) — ask the technician to check your serial number, as it can dramatically cut part costs while you still pay labor.
