## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numeric fault code — it's the symptom you'll most often search when a Lennox air conditioner runs but the house stays warm, or the outdoor unit won't start at all. Lennox split systems don't always flash a code for poor cooling; basic Merit and Elite units may give you no indication beyond warm air, while variable-capacity iComfort-equipped models (like the SL28XCV) log specific fault codes on the control board and thermostat.

Because "not cooling" covers everything from a $20 filter to a $1,800 compressor, the goal of this page is to help you tell the difference — and to sanity-check any quote before you pay it.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or restricted airflow** — the single most common cause. A clogged filter starves the evaporator coil, drops capacity, and can freeze the coil solid.
2. **Dirty or blocked outdoor condenser coil** — grass clippings, cottonwood, and dust insulate the coil so it can't reject heat. Cooling drops sharply on hot days.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a bulged or dead dual capacitor stops the compressor and/or fan from starting. Very common, and a frequent legitimate repair.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — undercharged systems cool weakly, ice up, or trip on high/low pressure. This is a leak that needs a licensed tech — refrigerant never "uses up" normally.
5. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit. Pitted or stuck contacts mean the compressor never engages.
6. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit dead, indoor blower may still run and blow warm air.
7. **Thermostat or wiring fault** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a broken C-wire on iComfort systems.
8. **Frozen evaporator coil** — a downstream symptom of items 1 and 4; the coil ices over and airflow nearly stops.
9. **Condensate safety switch tripped** — a full drain pan switch cuts cooling to prevent overflow.
10. **Failed condenser fan motor** — fan stops, head pressure climbs, compressor overheats and shuts down.
11. **Failed compressor** — least common but most expensive. Hums, trips the breaker, or won't start even with a good capacitor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Stop here if the fix isn't in this list.

- **Air filter** — replace it if it's gray or you can't see light through it. A fresh filter is the #1 cheapest fix.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries on battery-powered models. On iComfort touchscreens, note any fault message shown.
- **Breaker** — check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor disconnect breakers. Reset a tripped one **once**. If it trips again immediately, stop and call a tech — repeated trips signal an electrical fault.
- **Supply and return vents** — make sure they're open and not blocked by furniture or rugs. Closed registers can cause coil freezing.
- **Outdoor unit** — clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. With power off at the disconnect, gently rinse the outer coil fins with a garden hose from the inside out (low pressure only — never a pressure washer).
- **Condensate drain** — if cooling stopped and you see water near the indoor unit, the float/safety switch may have tripped. Clearing a clogged drain line can restore operation.
- **Frozen coil** — if you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, switch the system to **FAN ONLY** for 1-3 hours to thaw it, then replace the filter before running cooling again.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, handle refrigerant, replace a capacitor unless you're trained to discharge it, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Lennox tech follows a logical path — use this to judge whether a quote makes sense:

1. **Confirms the complaint** — measures supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16-22°F).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and evaporator coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Reads control data** — on iComfort/variable-capacity systems, pulls fault codes and runtime data from the board and thermostat. On basic units, there may be no code, so they work by measurement.
4. **Tests electrical components** — measures the capacitor with a meter (against its rated µF), checks the contactor for pitting and proper pull-in, and verifies 24V control voltage.
5. **Takes pressure and temperature readings** — gauges on the service ports plus superheat/subcooling tell them if the charge is correct or there's a leak. Low charge prompts a leak search (electronic detector or dye).
6. **Checks the compressor and fan motor** — amp draw, winding resistance, and start behavior.

Ask any tech who claims "low on refrigerant" where the leak is — a properly sealed system doesn't lose refrigerant without a leak, and topping off without finding it is a temporary fix.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, indoor fan runs, outdoor unit silent | Failed capacitor, contactor, or tripped breaker | Reset breaker once; check thermostat mode | Test/replace capacitor or contactor; check wiring |
| Weak cooling on hot days | Dirty condenser coil or low refrigerant | Rinse outdoor coil; replace filter | Measure pressures; find and repair leak; recharge |
| Ice on indoor coil or lines | Dirty filter, low airflow, or low charge | Run fan-only to thaw; replace filter | Verify charge, airflow, and blower operation |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Bad capacitor or seized compressor | None — call a tech | Test capacitor; check compressor windings/amps |
| Cooling stops, water near indoor unit | Tripped condensate float switch | Clear drain line if accessible | Clear drain, test float switch, check pan slope |
| Outdoor fan not spinning, unit hot | Failed condenser fan motor or capacitor | Shut unit off to prevent damage | Replace fan motor and/or capacitor |
| Breaker trips immediately on cooling call | Electrical short or failing compressor | Don't keep resetting — call a tech | Isolate fault; test compressor and wiring |
| iComfort thermostat shows fault code | Board/sensor/communication fault | Note the code; check C-wire | Read code, test sensors and communicating wiring |

## Repair costs

US ranges including parts and labor. Variable-capacity Lennox units (SL/XC-series) cost more for board and sensor work.

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY).
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Condenser coil cleaning (professional):** $100–$250.
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$250.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22).
- **iComfort control board / sensor:** $400–$900 (model-dependent).
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+; on an older unit, often worth comparing against full system replacement.

Diagnostic/service-call fee is typically $90–$190 and is often credited toward the repair.
