## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a fault code — it's a symptom. Your Lennox air conditioner may be running, partially running, or not starting at all, but the indoor temperature is not dropping. Lennox single-stage and two-stage condensers (Merit, Elite, Signature series) generally don't display a numeric error for low cooling; instead you diagnose by behavior.

On **iComfort-equipped Signature systems (XC/SL/XP with a communicating thermostat)**, the thermostat may show a fault message or a yellow alert. On standard 24V Merit and Elite units there is no display, so you read the symptoms directly. Because root causes range from a $20 filter to a $2,800 compressor, this is rated **pro** — many fixes require gauges, electrical testing, or EPA refrigerant certification.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the condenser or air handler has lost power while the thermostat still calls for cooling.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the fan or compressor hums but won't start; very common in heat and on aging units.
4. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil is matted with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, so it can't reject heat.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — long run times, ice on the lines, weak airflow at vents. Always indicates a leak, never normal "topping off."
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — ice from low airflow or low refrigerant blocks the indoor coil; warm air results.
7. **Failed contactor or relay** — the outdoor unit gets no signal to energize.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a broken 24V wire stops the cooling call.
9. **Clogged condensate drain** — a tripped float switch shuts cooling off to prevent overflow.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive failure; unit clicks or hums but never cools.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **Do not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Lower it 5°F and listen for the outdoor unit to start.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for a tripped breaker labeled for the AC/condenser and the air handler. Reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — that's an electrical fault.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure interior vents and the return grille are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Make sure nothing is leaning against the fins.
- **Condensate drain:** If you have a float switch and a full drain pan, the unit may have shut off intentionally. Empty standing water and clear the drain line.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or copper lines, switch the system to FAN ONLY for 1–3 hours to thaw, replace the filter, then try cooling again.

If cooling doesn't return after these checks, it's a pro repair.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech follows a logical sequence — you can use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** — confirms 24V is reaching the contactor when the thermostat calls.
2. **Check power and the contactor** — measures line voltage and tests whether the contactor pulls in.
3. **Test the capacitor** — measures microfarads against the rating printed on the cap; a weak cap is a quick, cheap fix.
4. **Inspect coils and airflow** — checks the condenser and evaporator coils, filter, and blower operation.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** — reads suction/head pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge the charge. Low readings point to a leak.
6. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses electronic detector or dye to find the leak before adding refrigerant.
7. **Compressor test** — checks amp draw and windings if the compressor won't start or trips on overload.
8. **iComfort diagnostics** — on Signature/communicating systems, pulls fault history from the thermostat.

Be cautious of any tech who adds refrigerant without searching for a leak — that's a temporary patch you'll pay for again.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, room won't cool | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, coil, ductwork |
| Outdoor unit silent, thermostat calls cool | Tripped breaker / dead thermostat | Reset breaker once, change batteries | Test 24V signal, contactor, wiring |
| Outdoor fan hums but won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None (electrical) | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on copper lines / indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Fan-only to thaw, new filter | Gauge pressures, find leak |
| Long run times, warm vents | Low refrigerant (leak) | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor coil caked with dirt | Dirty condenser coil | Gently rinse fins with hose | Deep coil clean, capacity check |
| Unit off, water in drain pan | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear drain, empty pan | Flush line, replace float switch |
| Loud hum or clicking, no cooling at all | Failed contactor or compressor | None | Test contactor, then compressor amps |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Regional rates and warranty status (Lennox compressors often carry a 5–10 year parts warranty) change these significantly.

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY)
- **Breaker reset / battery change:** $0 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Condensate drain clear / new float switch:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 (more for iComfort)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,800 (often partially covered under Lennox warranty)

If the unit is over 12–15 years old and facing a compressor or coil failure, ask your tech whether full replacement is the better value.
