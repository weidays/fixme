## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a fault code on the control board — it's the symptom Lennox homeowners most often search for. Your air conditioner runs (or tries to), but the air coming from your vents is warm, weak, or the house just never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Unlike a furnace lockout code, your Lennox AC won't usually flash a number on a panel for this. Newer Lennox systems paired with an **iComfort** or **S30/S40 smart thermostat** may display a communication or equipment alert, but most split systems (Merit, Elite, Signature series) give you no code at all — just a hot house. That makes systematic troubleshooting important.

The cause ranges from a $0 fix you can do in five minutes (a clogged filter) to a major repair (compressor or refrigerant leak). This page helps you find the safe-to-check items first and sanity-check any quote before you pay.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops efficiency, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Thermostat settings or batteries** — wrong mode (FAN vs COOL), wrong setpoint, dead batteries, or a scheduling override.
3. **Frozen evaporator (indoor) coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so air blows cool but never cold.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Lennox systems don't "use up" refrigerant; if it's low, there's a leak. Causes weak cooling and coil freezing. Pro only.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — leaves, grass clippings, or dirt blanket the outdoor coil so it can't reject heat.
6. **Failed run capacitor** — a weak or bulged capacitor stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting; you may hear humming.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown outdoor fuses** — the outdoor unit gets no power while the indoor blower runs, giving warm air.
8. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks, preventing the compressor from running.
9. **Clogged condensate drain / float switch tripped** — a full drain pan can trip a safety switch and shut down cooling.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit may hum, trip breakers, or run without cooling. Usually last on the list.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks you should do yourself. Stop here if the problem isn't obvious — refrigerant, capacitors, and compressors are pro territory.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. Recheck cooling after.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on AUTO. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for tripped breakers — there are usually two (indoor air handler/furnace and outdoor condenser). Reset once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Confirm the pull-out disconnect box near the outdoor unit is fully seated.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit airflow:** Clear leaves, weeds, and debris at least 2 feet around the outdoor condenser. Don't pressure-wash the coil fins.
- **Frozen coil check:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** (fan ON to help thaw) and let it melt for several hours before running again.
- **Condensate drain:** If you have a visible drain line or a full pan, a clogged drain can trip a float switch. Drain-pan tablets help prevent buildup.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, remove the capacitor cover, or repeatedly reset a breaker that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Lennox tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you spot an inflated quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measures supply and return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Check airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and coil for ice or restriction.
3. **Electrical checks** — tests the capacitor with a meter, inspects the contactor, and verifies voltage at the outdoor unit.
4. **Refrigerant pressures** — connects gauges to read suction and liquid pressures, then calculates superheat/subcooling. Low charge points to a leak.
5. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak (a top-off without finding the leak is a red flag).
6. **Compressor test** — checks amp draw, windings, and start behavior if everything else passes.
7. **Component reading** — on iComfort/S30 systems, pulls equipment alerts and sensor data from the thermostat.

Ask for the measured temperature split, pressures, and capacitor reading. A quote with no measurements is worth questioning.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; turn off to thaw ice | Diagnose airflow, check charge |
| Indoor blower runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / blown fuse / contactor | Reset breaker once, check disconnect | Replace fuses/contactor, test power |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — pro only | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on copper lines or indoor coil | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Cooling stops mid-cycle, water near unit | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear visible drain, add tablets | Clear drain, test float switch |
| House never cools, long run times | Dirty condenser coil / low charge | Clear debris around outdoor unit | Wash coil, check refrigerant |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Electrical fault / failing compressor | Stop resetting; call a pro | Test compressor, wiring, capacitor |
| No air at all + thermostat blank | Dead batteries / control power loss | Replace batteries | Diagnose transformer/control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Regional rates and R-454B vs R-410A models affect pricing.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Outdoor fuses / disconnect repair:** $75–$250
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $150–$450
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ (depends on leak location and refrigerant type; R-454B can cost more)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,800+ (often worth comparing to full unit replacement on older systems)
- **Full system replacement:** $5,000–$12,000+ if the unit is old and the compressor fails

If your Lennox AC is under 10 years old, check whether the **compressor and parts are still under Lennox's limited warranty** — you may only pay labor.
