## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numbered fault code on most Lennox air conditioners — it's a symptom. Your thermostat is calling for cooling and the equipment may even be running, but the air coming from your vents isn't getting cold enough to satisfy the setpoint.

On systems with an **iComfort S30/E30/M30 thermostat**, you may see a specific alert or equipment code stored in the thermostat's notification log (for example, alerts related to compressor, outdoor unit communication, or charge). On older Lennox units with a standard 24V thermostat, there is no code at all — only the behavior. Because diagnosing a true "no cooling" condition often requires gauges and electrical testing, we've rated this **pro** severity. There are still several safe checks you can do first.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow reduces cooling and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator (indoor) coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so warm air blows from the vents.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, and cottonwood fuzz block heat rejection, so the system runs but can't cool.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power, so only the indoor blower runs.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a cheap part that prevents the compressor and/or condenser fan motor from starting. Common in hot weather.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks, leaving the compressor off.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, long run times, and icing. Always indicates a leak, never normal "topping off."
8. **Failed condenser fan motor** — outdoor fan doesn't spin, compressor overheats and shuts down on internal protection.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit hums or trips but produces no cooling.
10. **Thermostat or control-board fault** — miswiring, dead batteries, or a communication error on iComfort systems.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Air filter** — pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A dirty filter alone can stop cooling.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace batteries if it's a battery model and the screen is dim or blank.
- **Breakers** — check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor AC breakers in your panel. If one tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect** — make sure the pull-out disconnect box by the condenser is fully seated (some get bumped loose). Don't open it or touch internal parts.
- **Vents and registers** — confirm supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain** — if the unit shut off and there's water around the indoor unit, the safety float switch may have tripped from a clogged drain. You can clear a visible standing-water clog and pour drain tablets in, but don't disable the float switch.
- **Frozen coil** — if you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, set the thermostat to **Off** but the fan to **On** for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter before restarting.

Do **not** open electrical panels, add refrigerant, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Lennox tech follows a logical order so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirms the call** — verifies the thermostat is calling for cool and checks for iComfort alerts/codes if equipped.
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects the filter, blower, and indoor coil for ice or dirt; measures temperature split (return vs. supply air, typically 16–22°F when working right).
3. **Tests power and controls** — measures voltage at the disconnect, contactor, and capacitor; reads the run capacitor's microfarads against the rating printed on it.
4. **Inspects the condenser** — checks coil cleanliness and confirms the fan motor spins freely.
5. **Connects refrigerant gauges** — reads suction and head pressures and calculates superheat/subcooling to determine if charge is correct. Low charge prompts a **leak search** (electronic detector or dye), not a blind recharge.
6. **Tests the compressor** — checks amp draw, windings, and start components if it won't run.

Ask the tech to show you the capacitor reading or pressure numbers — legitimate diagnoses are backed by measurements.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor fan runs, warm air | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil with fan-only | Confirm airflow, check charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor runs | Tripped breaker / disconnect | Reset breaker once, reseat disconnect | Test contactor, capacitor, wiring |
| Outdoor fan off, unit humming | Failed capacitor or fan motor | None — call pro | Replace capacitor / condenser fan motor |
| Ice on lines, weak cooling | Low refrigerant (leak) | Thaw, replace filter | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Long run times, never cools | Dirty condenser coil | Gently rinse exterior fins (power off) | Deep coil clean, charge check |
| Unit clicks/trips repeatedly | Compressor or contactor fault | Stop resetting, call pro | Test compressor windings, amps |
| No response at all | Thermostat / control board | Replace batteries, check setpoint | Diagnose board / iComfort comms |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and contractor):

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Condenser coil cleaning** — $100–$250.
- **Run capacitor** — $150–$350 installed.
- **Contactor** — $150–$300 installed.
- **Condensate float switch / drain clearing** — $100–$250.
- **Condenser fan motor** — $400–$700 installed.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge** — $600–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is far pricier).
- **Compressor replacement** — $1,500–$2,500+; on an older unit, often worth comparing to full system replacement.
- **Thermostat / control board** — $200–$600 depending on iComfort vs. standard.

If your unit is under the Lennox limited warranty, many parts may be covered — labor usually is not unless you bought an extended labor plan.
