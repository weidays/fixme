## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code — it's the symptom you'll see when something interrupts the cooling cycle. Lennox air conditioners don't display a flashing error code the way furnaces do (a Lennox furnace shows codes like 200 ignition lockout). Instead, the symptom shows up as warm air from the vents, an outdoor unit that won't start, ice on the lines, or short-cycling.

Because cooling depends on the indoor blower, the outdoor compressor and condenser, the refrigerant charge, and a clean airflow path all working together, "not cooling" can have a dozen root causes ranging from a $15 filter to a failed compressor. This page walks you through them in order of likelihood so you can rule out the cheap stuff before paying for a service call.

On communicating Lennox systems (iComfort thermostats paired with Signature-series equipment), the thermostat or controller may show a system fault or "service" message. The specific text varies by thermostat firmware, so treat any on-screen message as a clue rather than a definitive diagnosis.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow across the evaporator coil, reducing cooling and often causing the coil to freeze.
2. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode (Heat instead of Cool), wrong fan setting, or a setpoint that's too high.
3. **Tripped breaker or open disconnect** — the outdoor unit has its own breaker and a disconnect switch near the condenser.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit runs but blows warm or weak air, sometimes with visible ice on the copper line.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common outdoor failure; the fan or compressor hums but won't start, or only the fan runs.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood can't reject heat, so cooling drops in hot weather.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradually worsening cooling, ice on lines, hissing; requires leak detection and recharge by a certified tech.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the compressor pits or sticks, so the outdoor unit won't energize.
9. **Bad capacitor on the indoor blower or a failed blower motor** — no air movement at the vents.
10. **Compressor failure** — the most expensive cause; the compressor won't start, overheats, or trips repeatedly. Diagnosed by a tech.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are genuinely safe for a homeowner. Do not open the sealed refrigerant system, remove the compressor cover with power on, or bypass any safety switch.

- **Air filter** — pull it and look at the light through it. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling and frozen coils.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered and the display is dim or blank.
- **Breakers** — check the indoor (air handler/furnace) breaker and the separate outdoor AC breaker in your main panel. Reset a tripped breaker once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect** — there's a pull-out or switch disconnect in a box near the condenser. Make sure it's fully seated/on.
- **Supply and return vents** — confirm vents aren't closed, blocked by furniture, or covered. Closing too many vents starves airflow.
- **Condensate drain** — many systems have a float switch that shuts off cooling when the condensate pan backs up. Check for standing water near the indoor unit; clearing a clogged drain line can restore operation.
- **Check for ice** — if you see frost or ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to **Off** but leave the **Fan On** for a few hours to thaw it. Running a frozen unit makes things worse. If it refreezes after thawing, you have an airflow or refrigerant problem for a pro.

If none of these restores cooling, the remaining causes involve refrigerant, electrical components, or the compressor — call a licensed HVAC technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence, and knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

- **Verifies the call for cooling** — checks 24V signal at the thermostat and contactor to confirm the system is being told to run.
- **Checks airflow** — inspects the filter, blower operation, and evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
- **Measures electrical components** — tests the run/start capacitor with a meter (microfarad reading vs. the rating stamped on it), checks the contactor for pitted contacts, and verifies voltage to the compressor.
- **Reads refrigerant pressures** — connects gauges to measure suction and liquid pressures, then calculates superheat and subcooling to determine if the charge is correct. Low readings point to a leak.
- **Leak search** — if the charge is low, uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak rather than just recharging.
- **Tests the compressor** — checks windings, amp draw, and whether it starts. A locked or shorted compressor is the worst-case finding.

On two-stage and variable-capacity models (XC20, XC21), the tech may also connect to the system controller to read fault history. Ask any tech to show you the capacitor reading or the pressure numbers — a legitimate diagnosis is based on measurements, not guesses.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm vents | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower & coil if it persists |
| Ice on copper lines / coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or refrigerant leak |
| Outdoor fan & compressor silent | Tripped breaker / open disconnect | Reset breaker once, seat disconnect | Test contactor, capacitor, wiring |
| Humming outdoor unit, won't start | Failed run/start capacitor | None (capacitor holds charge) | Test and replace capacitor |
| Cooling drops on hottest days | Dirty condenser coil | Gently rinse coil with hose, power off | Deep clean, check charge |
| Gradual loss of cooling, hissing | Refrigerant leak | None | Leak detection, repair, recharge |
| No air at all from vents | Blower motor or its capacitor failed | Check filter & breaker | Test/replace blower motor or capacitor |
| Indoor unit floods / shuts off | Clogged condensate drain & float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Clear line, test float switch, treat pan |
| Unit trips breaker repeatedly | Short, failing compressor, or wiring fault | Stop resetting | Electrical diagnosis, possible compressor work |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US prices including parts and labor. Your area, model, and warranty status affect the final bill.

- **Air filter:** $15–$60 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 installed (more for iComfort communicating models)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Blower motor:** $400–$700
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A is pricier; older R-22 units are very expensive to recharge)
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,800+ — at this point, replacing the whole condenser or system is often the better value, especially on units 10+ years old

A note on refrigerant: if a tech says you just need a "top-off" every season, that's a leak being ignored, not a fix. Insist on leak detection.
