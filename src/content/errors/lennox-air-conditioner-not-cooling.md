## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numeric fault code — it's the symptom most Lennox homeowners report. Your system may be running but blowing warm air, short-cycling, or not turning on at all. Lennox air conditioners depend on three things working together: airflow (blower and filter), the refrigerant circuit (compressor, coils, refrigerant charge), and electrical controls (capacitor, contactor, control board).

On variable-speed Signature-series units (XC21, SL28XCV) paired with an iComfort thermostat, an actual fault code may appear at the thermostat or on the outdoor control board. On Merit and most Elite-series units, there's no display — diagnosis is done by measurement. Where your unit shows a code, write it down and give it to your technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, freezes the evaporator coil, and kills cooling. The #1 cause and the easiest to fix.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (HEAT instead of COOL), or a setpoint above room temperature.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuse** — the outdoor condenser loses power while the indoor blower keeps running, so you get airflow but no cold air.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a common failure that prevents the compressor or condenser fan from starting. You may hear a hum or clicking outside.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer. You may see ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant lines.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity and can freeze the coil. Requires a licensed tech; never a "top-off" without finding the leak.
7. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — blocked by dirt, grass, or debris, the unit can't reject heat.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit; pitted or stuck contacts stop the compressor.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive failure; the unit runs but produces no cooling, or trips the breaker.
10. **Clogged condensate drain (with safety float switch)** — a backed-up drain can trip a float switch that shuts down cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — no panels removed, no electrical or refrigerant work.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. Run the system again — a frozen coil can take 1–3 hours to thaw with the fan on FAN/ON.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered or the screen is dim/blank.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers in your panel. Reset a tripped breaker once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Confirm the pull-out disconnect box near the condenser is fully seated.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit clearance:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside of the coil fins with a garden hose (unit off) if visibly dirty.
- **Condensate drain/float switch:** If your indoor unit has a small float switch on the drain pan and it's tripped, a clogged drain may be the cause — but unclogging it beyond a basic vacuum should be left to a pro if you're unsure.

If the system still won't cool after the coil has fully thawed and these checks pass, it's a pro job.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Lennox technician should:

- Read any thermostat or control-board fault codes (on iComfort/Signature units).
- Measure supply and return air temperature split (typically 16–22°F on a healthy system).
- Check capacitor microfarad (µF) readings against the rating stamped on the part.
- Test the contactor and verify the compressor and condenser fan draw correct amps.
- Connect gauges to read suction and head pressures and calculate superheat/subcooling — this reveals refrigerant charge problems and leaks.
- Inspect the evaporator and condenser coils for dirt, ice, or restriction.
- Leak-search (electronic detector or dye) if the charge is low — a proper repair finds the leak, not just adds refrigerant.

Sanity check: if a tech quotes a "refrigerant recharge" without mentioning a leak search, ask where the refrigerant went. Adding refrigerant to a leaking system is a temporary, EPA-discouraged fix.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Airflow fine but air is warm | Outdoor unit not running (breaker/disconnect) | Reset breaker once; reseat disconnect | Diagnose capacitor, contactor, or compressor |
| Indoor coil or lines iced over | Dirty filter or low refrigerant | Replace filter; run fan to thaw | Find airflow restriction or refrigerant leak |
| Outdoor fan off, humming/clicking | Failed run capacitor | None — electrical | Test and replace capacitor |
| Weak cooling, long run times | Dirty condenser coil or low charge | Rinse outdoor coil; replace filter | Clean coil, check charge, leak search |
| Unit completely dead | Tripped breaker, blown fuse, dead thermostat | Reset breaker once; replace batteries | Trace power, replace fuse/board |
| Blower won't start, no air | Control board, blower motor, or float switch | Check filter and drain pan float | Test board, motor, and safety switch |
| Cools then trips breaker | Failing compressor or hard-start condition | Stop resetting; call a pro | Amp-test compressor; quote replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries / settings:** $0–$15 (DIY).
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300.
- **Refrigerant leak repair (find + fix + recharge):** $600–$1,800, depending on leak location and refrigerant type. R-410A and older R-22 systems differ widely in refrigerant cost.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,300–$2,200+. On older units, replacing the whole condenser or system is often the better value.

Costs vary by region, refrigerant type, and whether your unit is under Lennox warranty. Many Lennox units carry a parts warranty (often 5–10 years if registered), so ask about coverage before paying for parts.
