## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single Lennox fault code — it's a symptom. On variable-capacity Lennox units (Signature **XC20, XC21, XC25** and some Elite models) the control board or iComfort thermostat may post a specific numbered fault that explains *why* cooling stopped. On single-stage Merit and most Elite condensers, there's no display — you only see the result: the system runs but air from the registers isn't cold, or the unit won't start at all.

In every case, "not cooling" means one link in the cooling chain has broken: airflow (filter, blower, ducts), the refrigerant loop (charge, leaks, coils), or the electrical controls that drive the compressor and fans. This page covers all three so you can sanity-check what's happening before and after a service call.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or evaporator coil** — restricted airflow makes the coil freeze into a block of ice; warm air results once it ices over. Most common and often DIY-preventable.
2. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode (HEAT instead of COOL), wrong programming, or a failed thermostat that never calls for cooling.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown control fuse** — the condenser or air handler loses power. Common after storms or with a struggling motor drawing high current.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common AC failure. The compressor or condenser fan won't start, often with a humming sound. Affordable part, but pro-level testing.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — warm air, hissing, ice on the lines, or short-cycling. Always a leak (never "used up"). Requires an EPA-certified tech.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the compressor burns or pits, so the outdoor unit won't engage.
7. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood can't shed heat, so cooling drops off in the heat of the day.
8. **Condensate float switch tripped** — a full or clogged drain pan trips a safety switch that shuts cooling down to prevent water damage.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor or compressor** — the most expensive failures. The unit hums, trips the breaker, or runs without cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no panels removed, no electrical or refrigerant work:

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the target temperature is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the **batteries** if it's a battery-powered model.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for a tripped breaker for the **air handler/furnace** and the **outdoor condenser**. Flip it fully off, then on — **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance around it. Gently rinse the outside fins with a garden hose (power off) if they look dirty.
- **Condensate drain:** Check for standing water near the indoor unit. A full drain pan can trip a safety float switch. Clear an obvious clog at the drain line if you can reach it safely.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or the copper refrigerant lines, **turn the system off** and run the fan only to thaw it. Running an iced-up system can damage the compressor.

If none of this restores cooling, the next steps require a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Lennox tech follows a logical path. Knowing it helps you judge a quote:

1. **Reads any fault codes** on variable-capacity (XC20/21/25) boards or the iComfort thermostat before touching anything.
2. **Verifies airflow** — checks the filter, blower operation, and looks for a frozen evaporator coil.
3. **Confirms the call for cooling** — measures 24V at the contactor to verify the thermostat is actually commanding the outdoor unit on.
4. **Checks electrical components** — tests the capacitor with a meter, inspects the contactor for pitting, and measures compressor and fan amp draw.
5. **Takes refrigerant readings** — connects gauges and measures superheat/subcooling to determine if the charge is correct, low (leak), or overcharged.
6. **Leak search** if charge is low — electronic detector, UV dye, or bubble test. A proper repair finds and fixes the leak, not just a "top-off."
7. **Compressor diagnosis** — if everything upstream is good but the compressor won't run or cool, they test windings and start components before condemning it.

Be wary of any tech who recommends a recharge without checking for a leak, or condemns the compressor without verifying the capacitor, contactor, and electrical supply first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter, low refrigerant, dirty coil | Replace filter, rinse condenser | Measure charge, leak search, recharge |
| Ice on indoor coil or copper lines | Airflow restriction or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw, change filter | Diagnose airflow vs. charge, fix root cause |
| Outdoor unit silent / won't start | Tripped breaker, failed capacitor or contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace capacitor or contactor |
| Outdoor unit hums but fan won't spin | Failed capacitor or fan motor | None — call a pro | Replace capacitor or condenser fan motor |
| Unit short-cycles (on/off rapidly) | Low refrigerant, dirty coil, control fault | Check filter and coil cleanliness | Measure charge, check controls and pressures |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or wiring fault, electrical short | Stop resetting — call a pro | Amp-draw test, inspect wiring/compressor |
| Water near indoor unit, no cooling | Clogged condensate drain, tripped float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Clear drain, test float switch, service pan |
| Weak cooling on hottest days only | Dirty condenser coil, marginal charge | Rinse outdoor coil | Deep coil clean, verify charge |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Lennox parts can run higher than budget brands, and labor varies by region:

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** under $10 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$700
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Thermostat replacement (incl. iComfort):** $200–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing against a new condenser, especially on older units)
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80–$200, often credited toward the repair

If your Lennox is under the standard **5-year parts warranty** (10 years if registered), parts like the compressor may be covered — you'd pay labor and refrigerant only. Have your model and serial numbers ready to confirm coverage.
