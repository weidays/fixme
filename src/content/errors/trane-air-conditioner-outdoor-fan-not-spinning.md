## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is not a numbered Trane fault code — it's a physical symptom you can see and hear. The outdoor unit (condenser) has a large fan on top that pulls air across the coil to dump heat outside. When the compressor runs but that fan stays still, your AC quickly builds dangerous pressure and either trips off or risks compressor damage.

On most Trane single- and two-stage condensers (XR, XL series and 4TTR/4TTX models), the fan is a standard PSC motor driven by a run capacitor and switched by a contactor. On variable-speed XV units (e.g. XV20i, 4TTV), the fan is an ECM motor controlled by an onboard module — diagnosis there leans on board/communication faults rather than capacitors.

Because this often involves high-voltage components and refrigerant pressures, this is a **pro-level** repair once you've ruled out the basic items below.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run/start capacitor** — by far the most common. The fan hums or won't start without a manual spin. A bulged or leaking capacitor is the tell.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — bearings lock up or windings fail. May smell burnt or feel hot.
3. **Contactor not pulling in** — pitted, stuck, or coil failure means power never reaches the fan and compressor.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — no power to the outdoor unit at all.
5. **Debris jamming the fan blade** — twigs, ice, or a bent blade physically blocking rotation.
6. **High-pressure or float safety lockout** — a safety opened the circuit; common with a dirty coil or refrigerant issues.
7. **Control board / ECM module fault** — mainly on variable-speed XV units, where the board commands fan speed.
8. **Wiring or relay failure** — chewed wires, loose terminals, or a failed fan relay.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Stick to these — never open the electrical panel on the condenser yourself.

- **Thermostat:** Set to COOL and lower the setpoint below room temp so the system actually calls for cooling. Replace dead batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and pressure trips. Replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the dedicated outdoor AC breaker. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Disconnect:** Many Trane condensers have a pull-out disconnect box on the wall nearby. Confirm it's fully seated.
- **Visible debris and ice:** With the system OFF and power cut at the disconnect, look (don't reach into) for leaves, sticks, or ice on the fan. If you see ice, turn the AC off and run the fan-only mode at the thermostat to thaw, then call for service.
- **Condensate:** A full drain pan can trip a float switch and stop the system; empty/clear an obvious overflow.

If the fan still won't spin after these, turn the system off and call a technician. Running it risks your compressor.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will work in this order, and your quote should reflect it:

1. **Confirm the call:** Verify 24V is reaching the contactor when the thermostat calls for cooling.
2. **Check the contactor:** Test that it pulls in and passes line voltage to the fan and compressor; inspect for pitted contacts.
3. **Test the capacitor:** Measure microfarads against the rating printed on the cap. A weak or dead cap is the most common find. (They safely discharge it first.)
4. **Test the fan motor:** Check windings for resistance/shorts and spin the shaft by hand for bearing drag.
5. **Inspect safeties:** Verify the high-pressure switch and any float switch aren't open, which points to a coil or refrigerant problem.
6. **Variable-speed units:** Read board fault codes and confirm the ECM motor is being commanded and powered correctly.

Ask which specific part tested bad — a legitimate diagnosis names the failed component, not just "the fan."

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't start; spins if nudged | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Discharge and replace capacitor with matching uF rating |
| Compressor runs, fan dead, no hum | Burned-out fan motor or open winding | Shut system off | Test windings, replace condenser fan motor |
| Whole outdoor unit dead, silent | Contactor not engaging or no power | Check breaker/disconnect once | Test 24V coil, replace contactor |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Short in motor or wiring | Stop resetting; call a pro | Locate short, replace faulty component |
| Fan blade physically stuck | Debris or bent blade | Cut power, clear visible debris | Inspect blade, motor shaft, balance |
| Unit shuts off after a few minutes | High-pressure safety / dirty coil | Rinse outdoor coil gently with hose | Check pressures, clean coil, find root cause |
| Fan dead on XV variable-speed unit | ECM module or control board fault | None — call a pro | Read board codes, test/replace module or board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges, parts and labor:

- **DIY safe checks (filter, breaker, debris):** $0
- **Run/start capacitor replacement:** $150 – $300
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor replacement:** $400 – $650 (more for OEM variable-speed motors)
- **ECM fan module / control board (XV units):** $500 – $900+
- **Diagnostic / service call fee:** $80 – $180 (often credited toward the repair)
- **Coil cleaning to clear pressure trips:** $150 – $400

Variable-speed XV/4TTV systems cost more because their motors and boards are proprietary. Get a second quote before approving a board or motor replacement over $700.
