## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a blink code — it's a symptom. On a Trane split-system air conditioner, the outdoor unit (condenser) contains a fan that pulls air across the coil to reject heat. When that fan stops turning while the system is calling for cooling, the unit can't dump heat, pressures spike, and the compressor will eventually overheat or trip on a safety.

Some newer Trane systems with communicating controls or variable-speed (XV) fans may log a related fault on the indoor board or thermostat, but most XR and XL condensers give you no code at all — just a fan that hums, buzzes, or sits dead while the compressor runs or clicks.

This is a **pro-level** repair. The most common causes involve high-voltage capacitors and motors that store a dangerous charge even when power is off.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor.** The single most common cause. The fan (and often the compressor) shares a dual run capacitor. When the fan side weakens, the motor can't start — you'll hear a hum and may see it spin if nudged.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor.** Bearings fail or windings burn open. The motor may be hot, smell scorched, or not respond even with a good capacitor.
3. **Failed or pitted contactor.** The contactor is the relay that sends power to the outdoor unit. Burned or stuck contacts can starve the fan of voltage.
4. **Tripped high-pressure or compressor safety.** A dirty coil or low airflow can trip a safety that cuts the outdoor unit.
5. **Control board or relay fault.** On communicating/variable-speed Trane units, a board or fan-control module can fail to command the motor.
6. **Low-voltage / wiring problem.** A broken 24V signal, rodent-chewed wire, or loose connection can stop the unit from energizing.
7. **Debris jamming the fan blade.** Leaves, sticks, or ice can physically block the blade.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks you should do yourself. **Do not open the electrical panel of the condenser** — capacitors hold a lethal charge.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Indoor air filter:** A clogged filter restricts airflow and can trip safeties. Replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the **outdoor disconnect/breaker**. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked.
- **Condensate:** A full drain pan or clogged condensate line can trigger a float switch that shuts the system down. Clear visible standing water if you can reach it safely.
- **Look (don't touch) at the outdoor unit:** From outside the cabinet, check for obvious leaves or debris on top of the fan, ice on the coil, or a tripped disconnect. Do not reach inside.

If the fan still won't spin after these, shut the system off and call a licensed HVAC technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will work in roughly this order, and knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirm the call for cooling** and verify 24V is reaching the outdoor unit.
2. **Discharge and test the capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarad (µF) value. A weak fan-side reading is a smoking gun.
3. **Check the contactor** for pulled-in coil, voltage on both sides, and burned/pitted contacts.
4. **Test the fan motor** for proper resistance, open windings, and whether it spins freely by hand (with power off).
5. **Verify motor amperage** against the nameplate once running.
6. **Inspect safeties and pressures** — high-pressure switch, dirty coil, refrigerant charge.
7. **On communicating/XV units,** read fault history from the board or thermostat to rule out a control module.

Expect a diagnostic fee of roughly **$90–$200**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't turn; starts if nudged | Weak run capacitor | Turn unit off; don't run it | Test and replace dual run capacitor |
| Fan dead, no hum; compressor also off | Contactor or 24V signal | Reset breaker once; check thermostat | Test contactor, low-voltage wiring |
| Fan hot, scorched smell, won't move | Burned-out fan motor | Shut off immediately | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Whole outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker/disconnect | Reset breaker once | Trace electrical fault, repair |
| Fan blade physically blocked | Debris or ice | Power off; clear visible debris from outside | Inspect blade, coil, defrost |
| Unit short-cycles then fan stops | Dirty coil / high-pressure trip | Replace filter; rinse coil gently | Clean coil, check charge & pressures |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (regional and model variation applies):

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC):** $350–$650
- **Condenser fan motor (ECM/variable-speed on XV units):** $500–$900+
- **Control or fan module (communicating units):** $400–$800+
- **Low-voltage wiring repair:** $150–$400
- **Coil cleaning / safety reset (if that was the trip):** $100–$250

A failed capacitor is the cheapest and most common fix; a variable-speed motor on a premium XV unit is the most expensive. If a tech recommends a full condenser replacement, get a second opinion unless the compressor itself has also failed.
