## What this code means

On most Trane air conditioners, "outdoor fan not spinning" is not a logged error code — it is a physical symptom you observe at the outdoor condenser unit. The compressor and fan share the same outdoor cabinet, and the fan's job is to pull air across the condenser coil to reject heat.

On **communicating Trane XV systems**, a fan or motor fault may appear as a message on the ComfortLink II thermostat. On **older single-stage XR and XL units**, there is no diagnostic display at all — you simply notice the top fan is still while the unit is calling for cooling.

When the fan stops but the compressor keeps running, pressures and temperatures climb fast. This is why this problem is rated **pro**: continuing to run the system risks an expensive compressor failure.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — The most common cause by far. The dual run capacitor (or a separate fan capacitor) provides the starting torque. When it weakens, the fan hums, spins slowly, or doesn't move.
2. **Seized or failed fan motor** — Worn bearings or burned windings stop the motor entirely. Often accompanied by a burnt smell or a motor that's hot to the touch.
3. **Bad contactor** — The relay that sends power to the fan and compressor can have pitted or stuck contacts, interrupting power to the fan.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — Partial power loss can run the compressor circuit but not the fan, or vice versa.
5. **Obstruction or debris** — A stick, ice, or accumulated debris physically jamming the fan blade.
6. **Control board or relay fault** — Less common; on communicating units the board may withhold the fan command after detecting a fault.
7. **Failed fan motor wiring or connector** — Corroded or loose terminals at the motor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks you should do yourself. **Do not open the electrical panel of the condenser** — it contains capacitors that hold a lethal charge even with power off.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** A clogged indoor filter can cause pressure faults; replace it if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check the indoor and the dedicated outdoor AC breaker. Reset it once if tripped. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Visible debris:** With the unit fully off at the breaker, look down through the top grille for an obvious stick, leaves, or ice on the blade. Remove loose debris only — do not reach into the cabinet.
- **Indoor vents:** Make sure supply registers and the return grille are open and unblocked.
- **Condensate:** Check that the condensate drain isn't backed up (some systems shut down on a full float switch).

If the fan still won't spin after these, turn the system off and call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech will:

1. **Discharge and test the capacitor** with a multimeter, comparing measured microfarads to the rating printed on the cap. A reading well below spec confirms the fault.
2. **Check the contactor** for voltage in and out, and inspect contacts for pitting or welding.
3. **Test the fan motor** — measure winding resistance, check for an open or shorted winding, and verify it isn't seized by hand once de-energized.
4. **Verify control voltage** (24V) reaching the fan circuit, and on communicating units, read any logged faults at the thermostat or board.
5. **Inspect wiring and terminals** for corrosion, loose connections, or rodent damage.

This sequence — capacitor first, then contactor, then motor — should match a fair quote. Be cautious if a tech jumps straight to a full motor replacement without testing the capacitor.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but doesn't spin | Failed run capacitor | Turn unit off; don't spin by hand | Discharge & replace capacitor |
| Fan dead, compressor hums | Failed capacitor or seized motor | Shut off to protect compressor | Test cap, then motor |
| No power to fan or compressor | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Replace low-voltage fuse, find cause |
| Fan blade physically stuck | Debris or ice jam | Remove loose debris (power off) | Clear obstruction, inspect bearings |
| Burnt smell, hot motor | Burned-out fan motor | Turn off immediately | Replace fan motor |
| Intermittent fan, clicking | Pitted/stuck contactor | None — call pro | Replace contactor |
| Fault on XV thermostat | Control board / motor fault | Note the message | Read code, test board/motor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Run capacitor (dual or fan):** $150 – $300
- **Contactor:** $150 – $300
- **Low-voltage fuse / minor wiring:** $80 – $200
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400 – $650 (more on communicating/variable-speed motors)
- **Control board (communicating XV):** $400 – $900
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80 – $180, often credited toward the repair

A capacitor is the cheapest and most common fix. If the same capacitor fails again within months, the technician should also check the fan motor, since a failing motor can overload and kill a fresh capacitor.
