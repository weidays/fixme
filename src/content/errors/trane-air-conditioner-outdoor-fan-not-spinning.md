## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a blink code — it's a physical symptom. On a Trane split-system air conditioner, the outdoor unit (condenser) houses a fan that pulls air across the coil to reject heat. When that fan stops turning, the system can't shed heat, pressures climb, and the unit either stops cooling or trips on a high-pressure or compressor overload condition.

Some Trane units with communicating boards (XV20i, XV18 and similar variable-speed models) may log a related fault or show a code at the indoor control or thermostat. Single-stage XR and XL units typically have no diagnostic display for this — you just notice warm air inside and a still fan outside. Behavior varies by model, so don't assume a code exists for your unit.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed dual run capacitor** — The most common cause by far. The capacitor gives the fan motor its starting torque. When it weakens or fails, the fan won't start (you may hear a hum).
2. **Worn or seized fan motor** — Bearings dry out and bind, or the motor windings fail. Often shows as a fan that only spins with a nudge, or won't move at all.
3. **Failed contactor** — The relay that sends power to the outdoor unit can pit, stick, or fail to close, cutting power to both fan and compressor.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect/fuse** — No power reaching the condenser. Sometimes also kills the compressor.
5. **Failed fan relay or control board** — On some Trane models, a relay or board controls fan operation; failures here are less common but possible.
6. **Obstructed or damaged fan blade** — Debris, a bent blade, or ice can physically block rotation.
7. **High-pressure/safety lockout** — A safety switch has cut the unit, often as a downstream effect of one of the issues above.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should perform without tools or training:

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace dead batteries if it's a battery-powered model.
- **Air filter:** A badly clogged filter can cause icing and shutdowns. Replace it if it's dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor AC breakers in your panel. Reset a tripped breaker once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** The pull-out box near the condenser should be fully seated.
- **Visible obstructions:** Look for leaves, sticks, or debris in the fan that you can see and remove with the power off at the disconnect.
- **Vents:** Make sure indoor supply and return vents aren't blocked.
- **Condensate:** Check that the condensate drain/pan isn't full and triggering a float switch shutoff.

Do **not** open the electrical panel of the condenser, touch the capacitor, or reach into the unit while powered. Capacitors store a dangerous charge even when power is off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech will:

1. Confirm 24V call for cooling is reaching the contactor and that the contactor is closing.
2. Verify line voltage (typically 240V) is present at the condenser and contactor.
3. **Test the dual run capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarads — the single most common find.
4. Check the fan motor windings for continuity and resistance, and spin the blade by hand (power off) to feel for seized bearings.
5. Inspect the fan relay or control board on models that use one.
6. Check refrigerant pressures and safety switches to confirm the unit isn't in a protective lockout.

This sequence — power, contactor, capacitor, motor — is what a fair quote should reflect. Be wary of a tech who recommends a full motor or compressor replacement without first testing the capacitor.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan dead, compressor hums | Failed run capacitor | Turn unit off to protect compressor | Test & replace capacitor |
| Fan only spins after a nudge | Weak capacitor or worn bearings | Shut off, don't run | Replace capacitor or motor |
| Nothing runs outside, breaker OK | Failed contactor or no power | Check disconnect & breaker | Test/replace contactor, trace power |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Shorted motor or wiring fault | Reset once only, then stop | Diagnose short, replace component |
| Fan blocked by debris/ice | Obstruction or coil icing | Power off, clear visible debris | Inspect coil, airflow, refrigerant |
| Warm air, fan still, no codes | Multiple possible | Run safe checks above | Full electrical diagnosis |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Diagnostic/service call:** $80 – $180
- **Dual run capacitor:** $150 – $350 installed (part is cheap; labor and trip drive cost)
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350 installed
- **Condenser fan motor (standard PSC):** $300 – $550 installed
- **Variable-speed/ECM fan motor (XV-series):** $450 – $650+ installed
- **Fan blade:** $80 – $200 installed
- **Control board (communicating models):** $400 – $700+

A simple capacitor swap is the best-case outcome and is common. A motor or board replacement is the expensive case. Getting a capacitor tested first can save you hundreds.
