## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numeric Trane fault code — it's a physical symptom. The condenser (outdoor) unit has a fan that pulls air across the coil to release the heat your system pulled out of the house. When that fan stops, the compressor may still be trying to run, heat builds up fast, and the unit will eventually trip on a high-pressure or overload safety.

On most Trane split systems the outdoor fan and compressor share a **dual run capacitor** and are switched by a **contactor** energized by the indoor control or thermostat. A handful of newer communicating Trane systems (with variable-speed condenser fans) report a related fault on the thermostat or board instead of just sitting silent. Behavior varies by model, so use your installed unit's literature to confirm specifics.

The most common real-world cause by far is a failed run capacitor — cheap part, but live high-voltage work that we treat as pro for safety.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed or weak dual run capacitor** — the #1 cause. The fan won't start, may hum, or starts only if nudged. Capacitors can fail with no visible damage or may be visibly bulged/leaking.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect / control fuse** — no power to the outdoor unit at all (compressor also won't run).
3. **Pitted or stuck contactor** — the relay that sends power to fan and compressor isn't closing, or is welded.
4. **Seized or worn fan motor bearings** — motor won't turn even with good capacitor; may smell hot or feel rough by hand (power off).
5. **Burned-out condenser fan motor** — open windings, often after running undersized on a dying capacitor.
6. **Debris jamming the fan blade** — leaves, branches, or ice locking the blade.
7. **Low-voltage / control fault** — no 24V call reaching the contactor (thermostat, transformer, or board issue). More likely to also stop the compressor.
8. **Communicating control or board fault** (newer variable-speed Trane units only) — reports a fan or motor error rather than running silent.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not** open the electrical panel on the condenser, touch the capacitor, or pull the disconnect to "test" — capacitors hold a dangerous charge even with power off.

- **Thermostat:** Set to COOL and several degrees below room temperature so the system is actually calling. If it's a battery thermostat, replace the batteries.
- **Indoor air filter:** A badly clogged filter can cause freeze-ups that ice the outdoor coil/fan. Replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check the breaker for the outdoor unit (and the indoor air handler). If it's tripped, reset it once. If it trips again immediately, stop — call a pro.
- **Visible obstructions:** With the system **off at the thermostat**, look through the fan grille for leaves, sticks, or ice locking the blade. Clear loose debris by hand only — don't reach toward the motor or wiring.
- **Vents and condensate:** Make sure supply/return vents are open and the indoor condensate drain isn't backed up (a full pan can shut the system off on some setups).
- **Listen:** If the outdoor unit hums but the fan doesn't spin, that strongly points to a capacitor. Turn the system off and call a tech — don't keep it running.

If the fan still won't spin, turn the system off at the thermostat to protect the compressor and call an HVAC pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence — use this to sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the call and power:** Confirms 24V at the contactor and 240V line voltage to the unit; checks the disconnect and any control fuse.
2. **Test the capacitor:** Safely discharges and measures the capacitor's microfarads against the rating printed on it. A reading well below spec confirms a bad cap.
3. **Check the contactor:** Looks for pitted, burned, or stuck contacts and confirms it pulls in on a call.
4. **Test the fan motor:** With a known-good capacitor, checks whether the motor runs. Spins the blade by hand to feel for seized bearings; checks windings for an open/short and amp draw.
5. **Inspect the blade and wiring:** Confirms nothing is jamming the blade and that motor leads aren't burned or loose.
6. **Communicating units:** Pulls fault history from the control/thermostat and verifies motor module communication on variable-speed models.

A capacitor replacement is a quick, inexpensive visit. If they want to replace the motor, they should show you the failed cap reading or seized/open-winding evidence first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unit hums, fan won't spin, starts if nudged | Weak/dead run capacitor | Turn system off; don't keep running | Test and replace dual run capacitor |
| Outdoor unit completely dead, no hum | Tripped breaker / blown fuse / disconnect | Reset breaker once | Find cause of trip; replace fuse, repair fault |
| Fan and compressor both won't start | Bad contactor or no 24V call | Check thermostat & breaker | Test contactor, transformer, control board |
| Fan blade won't turn by hand (power off) | Seized motor bearings or jam | Clear loose debris only | Replace fan motor or remove obstruction |
| Burning smell, hot motor, no spin | Burned-out fan motor | Shut off immediately | Replace condenser fan motor + capacitor |
| Ice on outdoor coil/fan | Airflow/refrigerant issue (often dirty filter) | Replace filter; let it thaw | Diagnose airflow or refrigerant charge |
| Thermostat shows fan/motor fault | Communicating board/motor fault (newer units) | Note the message | Pull fault codes; test motor module/board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor, typical for residential service):

- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80 – $180 (often credited toward the repair).
- **Dual run capacitor:** $150 – $400 installed. The part is cheap (~$15–$40); you're paying for the visit and safe high-voltage work.
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350 installed.
- **Condenser fan motor (standard PSC):** $300 – $700 installed, depending on HP and region. Usually replaced with a new capacitor.
- **Variable-speed / communicating fan motor or module:** $600 – $1,200+ installed.
- **Control board (communicating units):** $400 – $900+ installed.
- **Clearing debris / blade adjustment:** often covered by the service call alone.

DIY note: a homeowner with proper training can replace a capacitor for the cost of the part, but the live high-voltage and stored charge make this a pro job for most people — hence the "pro" rating.
