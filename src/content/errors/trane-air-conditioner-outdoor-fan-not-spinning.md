## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is a symptom, not a Trane fault code — most Trane air conditioners (XR13, XR14, XR16, XL18i, XV20i and similar) don't flash a blink code for this on the outdoor unit. The condenser fan is the large fan on top of the outdoor unit that pulls air across the coil to dump heat outside.

When the compressor is running (or trying to) but the fan blade sits still, the system loses its ability to reject heat. This is a **pro-level** problem: running a compressor without condenser airflow quickly drives up head pressure and temperature, and can permanently damage the compressor — the single most expensive part in the system.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed dual-run capacitor.** By far the most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan (and compressor) the jolt needed to start. A bulged, leaking, or out-of-spec capacitor leaves the fan humming or dead.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor.** Bearings fail, windings burn, or the motor overheats. You may hear a hum with no rotation, or nothing at all.
3. **Failed contactor.** The relay that sends power to the outdoor unit can have pitted or stuck contacts, so the fan and compressor never get full voltage.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuse.** No power reaching the outdoor unit at all.
5. **Debris jamming the fan blade.** Sticks, ice, or a bent blade physically blocking rotation.
6. **Control board or thermostat issue (variable-speed/ECM units).** On XV-series units with ECM fan motors, a failed module or board can stop the fan even when everything else looks fine.
7. **Wiring or low-voltage fault.** Loose, corroded, or chewed wires interrupting the 24V control signal.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — **with the system off**, not while the unit is energized.

- **Thermostat:** Set it to "Cool" and lower the setpoint several degrees. Confirm it's calling for cooling. Replace batteries if it has them.
- **Air filter:** A filthy filter can cause freeze-ups and odd behavior. Replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor air handler breaker and the outdoor AC breaker in your panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Visible debris:** With the AC **off at the breaker**, look through the top grille for sticks, leaves, or ice on the blade. Don't reach in unless power is off and the blade is fully stopped.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut the system down — clear visible standing water at the indoor drain pan if accessible.
- **Indoor vents:** Make sure supply registers aren't all closed, which can cause freeze-ups that mimic this.

Do **not** open the outdoor electrical panel, test the capacitor, or spin the blade by hand on a live unit. Capacitors store a dangerous charge even with power off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech will work top-down so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirm the call for cooling** and verify 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. **Test the dual-run capacitor** with a meter — checking the FAN and HERM (compressor) terminals against the rated microfarads. This is usually the first thing they check.
3. **Check the contactor** for pulled-in coil and clean contacts; measure voltage in and out.
4. **Test the fan motor** for proper resistance/windings and check the bearings for a seize.
5. **Inspect wiring** for loose, burned, or corroded connections.
6. **On ECM/variable-speed units (XV-series),** they'll check the motor module and control board diagnostics rather than a simple capacitor test.

A capacitor test takes minutes. If a tech quotes a fan motor or compressor without first ruling out the capacitor and contactor, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan still | Failed dual-run capacitor | Turn AC off; don't spin the blade | Test & replace capacitor |
| Fan hums, won't spin, may smell hot | Seized/burned fan motor | Shut off to protect compressor | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Nothing runs outdoors at all | Tripped breaker / blown fuse / contactor | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, fuses, wiring |
| Fan blade visibly blocked or bent | Debris or bent blade | Power off, remove visible debris | Straighten/replace blade |
| Fan dead on XV variable-speed unit | ECM module / control board | Power-cycle at breaker once | Diagnose ECM module/board |
| Fan starts then stops, breaker trips | Failing motor or short | Don't keep resetting breaker | Locate short, replace part |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor, vary by region and contractor:

- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80 – $180 (often credited toward the repair)
- **Dual-run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC, single-stage):** $350 – $650
- **ECM fan motor / module (variable-speed XV units):** $600 – $1,200+
- **Bent/replacement fan blade:** $100 – $300
- **DIY capacitor (if you're qualified and trained to discharge it):** $25 – $60 part only — but this is genuinely risky and not recommended for most homeowners.

If a tech finds compressor damage from running without airflow, costs climb steeply ($1,500–$2,800+), which is exactly why you turn the system off the moment you notice the fan isn't spinning.
