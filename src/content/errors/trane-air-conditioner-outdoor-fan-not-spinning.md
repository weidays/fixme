## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numbered fault code on most Trane systems — it's a symptom you'll see (or hear) at the condenser, the large unit outside your home. The condenser fan pulls air across the coil to reject the heat your AC removes from inside. When the compressor runs but that top-mounted fan sits still, the system can't shed heat.

On single-stage Trane units (XR13, XR14, XR16) there is usually no error display — the fan simply doesn't turn. On variable-speed and communicating models (XL16i, XV18, XV20i), the indoor control or thermostat may log a fault or show a comfort-control alert tied to the outdoor inverter or ECM fan motor. Behavior varies by board, so don't assume your unit will report it.

This is rated **pro** because the most common fixes involve a charged capacitor and 240-volt wiring inside the condenser — both genuinely dangerous to untrained hands.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run/dual capacitor** — The single most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan motor its starting torque. When it weakens or fails, the fan won't start (often you'll hear a hum). A bulged or leaking top on the capacitor is a giveaway.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — Bearings fail, or the windings burn out. The fan won't turn even with a good capacitor, and the motor may smell hot.
3. **Failed contactor** — The relay that sends 240V to the fan and compressor can have pitted or stuck contacts, so the outdoor unit gets no power or partial power.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — No power to the condenser at all; the fan and compressor are both dead.
5. **Debris jamming the fan blade** — Sticks, ice, or a wasp nest physically blocking rotation.
6. **Bad control/inverter board (variable-speed models)** — On XV/XL communicating units, the ECM fan is driven by an inverter board; a board or motor module fault stops the fan.
7. **Failed thermostat or low-voltage wiring** — No call for cooling reaching the outdoor unit.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — stay out of the condenser cabinet:

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and at least 3–5°F below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause the indoor system to ice up and trip safeties that stop the outdoor unit. Replace it if it's dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor AC breaker (and the outdoor disconnect box on the wall near the condenser). Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — don't keep resetting.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked so airflow isn't restricted.
- **Condensate:** Check that the condensate drain isn't full or backed up — a tripped float switch will shut down the system.
- **Visible debris (power OFF):** With the outdoor disconnect pulled, look through the top grille for leaves, sticks, or ice on the fan. Do **not** reach inside, and do **not** open the access panel.

If the fan still won't spin after these, it's a service call.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech will work through this sequence — knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirm the call for cooling** and verify 24V is reaching the outdoor contactor coil.
2. **Pull the disconnect and discharge the capacitor** before touching anything (this is why it's a pro job).
3. **Test the capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarads (µF). An out-of-spec reading means replacement.
4. **Check the contactor** for pitted contacts and proper pull-in when 24V is applied.
5. **Test the fan motor** for continuity, shorts, and proper amp draw; spin it by hand to feel for seized bearings.
6. **On variable-speed units,** read fault data from the communicating thermostat/board and test the ECM motor module and inverter board.
7. **Verify proper rotation and amp draw** after repair, then confirm head pressure and refrigerant pressures are back in range.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan still | Failed run capacitor | Turn unit off to protect compressor | Discharge & replace capacitor, verify µF |
| Fan and compressor both dead | Tripped breaker / bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring & fuse |
| Fan won't turn, motor smells hot | Burned-out fan motor | Shut down, don't run it | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Fan blade visibly blocked | Debris or ice | Pull disconnect, clear visible debris | Inspect for damage, restart |
| Fan starts if pushed by hand | Weak capacitor or tired motor | Note it, then shut down | Replace capacitor and/or motor |
| No fan, thermostat blank | Dead batteries / no call | Replace batteries, set to COOL | Trace 24V control circuit |
| Fan stutters, board alert (XV/XL) | ECM motor or inverter board | Note alert code, call pro | Test/replace ECM module or inverter board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$300 installed. The part is cheap; you're paying for the diagnosis and safe handling.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condenser fan motor (single-stage):** $400–$700, depending on horsepower and whether a matching blade is needed.
- **Low-voltage fuse:** $75–$150.
- **ECM fan motor / inverter board (variable-speed XV/XL):** $600–$1,200+ — these modules are expensive and model-specific.
- **Diagnostic/service-call fee:** $90–$180, often credited toward the repair.

If a tech recommends replacing the compressor at the same time, get a second opinion — a stalled fan rarely means the compressor itself is bad unless it ran hot for a long time.
