## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a blink code — it's a physical symptom. On a Trane split-system air conditioner, the outdoor unit (condenser) has a fan that pulls air across the coil to reject the heat your system pulled out of the house. When that fan stops, the unit can no longer get rid of heat, so pressures and temperatures spike.

Most Trane condensers (XR, XL, and many XV series) use a single-phase fan motor driven by a **dual run capacitor** and switched on by a **contactor**. Variable-speed XV models may use an ECM motor controlled by a board, which fails and is diagnosed differently. Because behavior varies by motor type, the exact fix depends on which your unit uses — your installer's data plate or the motor label tells you.

This is rated **pro** because the most common fixes live inside an energized cabinet next to a capacitor that stores a dangerous charge even with power off.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — by far the most common. The fan section of the dual capacitor weakens, so the motor hums or sits still until nudged, then spins.
2. **Seized or worn fan motor bearings** — the motor won't turn freely, draws high current, and may trip the breaker or overheat.
3. **Burned-out fan motor windings** — no movement, sometimes a burnt smell; common after running with a weak capacitor.
4. **Contactor not pulling in** — pitted, stuck, or bad 24V coil means the outdoor unit gets no power at all (compressor also silent).
5. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse / control board issue** — no 24V signal reaching the contactor.
6. **Debris jamming the fan blade** — sticks, ice, or a bent blade physically blocking rotation.
7. **ECM/board fault (variable-speed XV units)** — control module or motor module failure.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Keep your hands out of the outdoor cabinet — do these only:

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and several degrees below room temperature. Confirm it's calling for cooling and (if battery-powered) swap fresh batteries.
- **Indoor air filter:** A clogged filter chokes airflow and can ice the coil, mimicking outdoor problems. Replace if dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor AC breaker (and the outdoor disconnect by the unit). Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Visible vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked.
- **Look (don't reach):** From a safe distance, check whether leaves, ice, or debris are visibly jamming the fan blade. Turn the system **off** before removing anything you can reach from outside the cabinet.
- **Condensate:** A full drain pan or float switch can shut down cooling on some setups — clear visible standing water at the indoor unit.

Do **not** open the outdoor electrical panel, touch the capacitor, push-start the fan with power on, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical order, and you can sanity-check the quote against it:

1. **Confirms the call:** Verifies 24V is reaching the contactor and that the contactor pulls in.
2. **Tests the capacitor** with a meter, comparing the fan section's reading to the rated microfarads on the label. A low/dead reading is the smoking gun.
3. **Checks the motor:** Spins the blade by hand (power off) to feel for seized bearings, and tests winding resistance and amp draw against the motor's nameplate.
4. **Inspects the contactor** for pitted or welded contacts and confirms coil operation.
5. **For ECM/XV units:** Reads any module fault codes and tests the motor and control module separately.
6. **Confirms the repair** by measuring head pressure and supply temps once the fan runs again.

If a quote jumps straight to a new motor or a whole condenser without testing the capacitor first, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums or won't start, spins if nudged | Failed run capacitor | Turn unit off; don't push-start with power on | Test and replace dual run capacitor |
| Compressor runs, fan dead | Bad capacitor or seized motor | Shut system off to protect compressor | Test capacitor, then motor bearings/windings |
| No outdoor noise at all | Contactor, breaker, or low-voltage fuse | Reset breaker once; check disconnect | Test 24V, contactor, control fuse/board |
| Fan blade physically stuck | Debris or bent blade | Power off; clear visible debris only | Remove debris, straighten/balance or replace blade |
| Breaker trips on cooling call | Shorted motor or compressor | Stop resetting; call a pro | Amp-test, isolate failed component |
| Burnt smell from condenser | Burned motor windings | Shut off; call same day | Replace condenser fan motor |
| XV unit, fan dead, error in app/board | ECM or module fault | Note any code; power off | Diagnose motor module vs. control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor unless noted:

- **Run capacitor replacement:** **$120 – $300.** The most common and cheapest fix.
- **Contactor replacement:** **$150 – $350.**
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC):** **$350 – $650**, depending on horsepower and access.
- **ECM fan motor / control module (XV variable-speed):** **$450 – $900+** — modules are pricier and model-specific.
- **Fan blade replacement/balancing:** **$150 – $400.**
- **Breaker or low-voltage fuse:** **$100 – $250.**
- **DIY capacitor (experienced, power off, capacitor safely discharged):** **$15 – $45** for the part — but this is energized work next to a charged capacitor, so most homeowners should hire out.

Prices vary by region, unit age, and whether your model is still in warranty (many Trane parts carry a 10-year registered limited warranty — labor usually isn't covered).
