## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is not a Trane blink code — it's a physical symptom. The fan inside your outdoor condenser unit (the box outside your home) isn't turning while the system calls for cooling. On most Trane single-stage units there is no fault display for this; you simply hear the compressor hum or notice warm air indoors and a still fan outside.

The outdoor fan's job is to pull air across the condenser coil to reject the heat your AC removed from inside. If it stops, the compressor quickly overheats and refrigerant pressure spikes. Running the system in this state risks permanent compressor damage, so this is a **pro-level** repair you should not run the unit through.

On variable-speed Trane XV units with an inverter/ECM-driven fan, a stopped fan is more likely a board or motor electronics fault and may set an actual error — diagnosis there differs from older capacitor-start units, so we flag that throughout.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — by far the most common cause on single-stage units. The capacitor that gives the fan motor its starting torque weakens or fails. Classic sign: fan won't start on its own but spins if nudged.
2. **Failed condenser fan motor** — worn bearings (seized), burned windings, or an open thermal overload. Often the motor is hot and won't restart even with a good capacitor.
3. **Contactor not pulling in or burned contacts** — if the contactor isn't passing power, neither the fan nor compressor get energized; pitted contacts cause intermittent operation.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse / disconnect pulled** — no power reaching the condenser at all.
5. **Debris or ice jamming the fan blade** — leaves, a stick, or a bent blade physically blocking rotation.
6. **Low-voltage/control problem** — bad thermostat call, failed transformer, or wiring fault means the unit never gets the signal to run.
7. **Inverter/control board fault (XV variable-speed units)** — on these models the ECM fan is driven by electronics, so a board failure can stop the fan; this is dealer-level diagnosis.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **Turn the thermostat to "off"** first if you find the fan stopped while the compressor is running — that protects the compressor.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to "cool" and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and odd behavior — replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check the AC breaker in your panel. If tripped, switch fully off then on once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting.
- **Indoor vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked.
- **Visible debris:** With the unit powered off at the breaker, look through the top grille for obvious leaves, sticks, or a bird's nest blocking the blade. Remove only what you can reach safely; do not stick fingers or tools into the fan.
- **Condensate:** A backed-up condensate float switch can shut the system down — check that the drain pan isn't overflowing.

Do **not** open the electrical access panel on the condenser. The run capacitor stores a lethal charge even with power off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence — use this to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Verify the call and power:** Confirm the thermostat is calling for cooling and check 240V at the disconnect and 24V control voltage.
2. **Watch the startup:** They'll see whether the fan attempts to start, hums, or does nothing.
3. **Test the capacitor:** With the unit powered down and the capacitor safely discharged, they measure microfarads against the rating printed on it. A reading well below spec confirms a bad cap.
4. **Test the motor:** They check windings for continuity/resistance, spin the blade by hand for bearing drag, and feel for an overheated motor on thermal overload.
5. **Inspect the contactor:** Look for pitted/welded contacts and confirm it pulls in when energized.
6. **Check the blade and wiring** for physical or electrical faults.
7. **On XV/variable-speed units:** They'll pull error codes and test the inverter/ECM drive — a different and more involved process.

A capacitor test takes minutes; if they're quoting a motor without testing the capacitor first, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan won't start but spins if nudged | Weak/failed run capacitor | Shut unit off; do not run it | Test and replace capacitor |
| Compressor hums, fan dead silent | Failed capacitor or motor | Turn thermostat off to protect compressor | Test cap, then motor windings |
| Fan hot, won't restart even after rest | Seized/overloaded fan motor | None — power off and wait for pro | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Nothing runs outside at all | Tripped breaker, contactor, or control | Reset breaker once; check thermostat | Test contactor, transformer, wiring |
| Buzzing at contactor, no fan | Stuck/pitted contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| Blade visibly blocked or bent | Debris or bent blade | Remove visible debris (power off) | Replace blade, check balance |
| Fan stopped, XV unit shows error | Inverter/ECM board fault | Note the code; call Trane dealer | Diagnose inverter/control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$300
- **Condenser fan motor (single-stage):** $300–$650 depending on horsepower and unit access
- **Fan blade replacement:** $100–$250
- **Control transformer / wiring repair:** $150–$400
- **Inverter/control board (XV variable-speed):** $600–$1,500+ — these are model-specific and often dealer-only
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90–$200, often credited toward the repair

A confident DIYer can buy a matching capacitor for $15–$40, but only attempt it if you can safely discharge the old one. When in doubt, the capacitor's stored charge makes this a pro job.
