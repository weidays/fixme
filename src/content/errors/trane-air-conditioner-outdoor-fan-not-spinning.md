## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numbered fault code — it's a symptom on the outdoor condenser unit of your Trane split-system AC. The large fan on top of the outdoor cabinet pulls air across the condenser coil to reject the heat your system removed from the house.

When that fan stops, the compressor may still run for a short time, but head pressure spikes and the unit overheats. Many Trane condensers will trip on a high-pressure switch or thermal overload and lock out — which is why this commonly shows up as **weak cooling, warm air, or short cycling** indoors.

This is rated **pro** because the most likely fixes live inside an energized cabinet with a capacitor that holds a dangerous charge.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — The single most common cause. The fan motor needs the capacitor for starting torque. A bulged, leaking, or out-of-spec capacitor leaves the fan dead or spinning only after a manual push.
2. **Burned or stuck contactor** — The relay that sends line voltage to the fan and compressor. Pitted contacts or a failed 24V coil can cut power to the fan.
3. **Failed condenser fan motor** — Worn bearings, an open winding, or a tripped internal thermal overload. Often smells burnt or feels seized.
4. **Debris or ice jamming the blade** — A stick, plastic bag, or ice buildup physically blocking the fan blade.
5. **Tripped high-pressure or thermal lockout** — A protective switch has opened because the unit overheated, stopping the fan and compressor.
6. **Control board or relay fault (variable-speed/ECM models)** — On XV and some XL units with ECM motors and a variable-speed board, a board or motor module failure prevents the fan from commanding on.
7. **Loose, corroded, or chewed wiring** — Rodent damage or a loose spade connector at the motor or contactor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no panel removal required.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it has them.
- **Indoor air filter:** A clogged filter causes coil/pressure problems that can lock out the outdoor unit. Replace if dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor AC breaker/disconnect. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Visible debris:** With the system OFF at the thermostat, look through the top grille for leaves, sticks, or a bag against the blade. Clear anything obvious without putting fingers or tools into the cabinet.
- **Condensate:** A full condensate drain or float switch can shut the system down. Check the drain line for clogs and the pan for standing water.
- **Don't repeatedly reset** a unit that keeps shutting off — that risks compressor damage. One reset attempt, then call a tech.

Do **not** open the condenser electrical panel, touch the capacitor, or remove the fan grille screws — that's pro territory.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a quick, logical sequence. Knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirms 24V call and line voltage** at the contactor with the system calling for cooling.
2. **Tests the run capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarads (µF). A reading well below spec confirms it.
3. **Checks the contactor** — pull-in of the coil, contact condition, and voltage on the load side.
4. **Tests the fan motor** — resistance/continuity of windings and whether it's seized or thermally tripped.
5. **Inspects safety switches** — high-pressure switch and any lockout the board is reporting.
6. **On ECM/variable-speed units**, scans the variable-speed board for faults and checks the motor module.

Expect a diagnostic fee of **$80–$180**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan dead, hums, spins if nudged | Failed run capacitor | Shut off at thermostat | Test & replace capacitor |
| Fan and compressor both off, clicking | Burned/stuck contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor |
| Fan tries to start then stops, burnt smell | Failed fan motor | Turn unit off | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Blade visibly blocked or icy | Debris / ice jam | Clear obvious debris, let ice melt | Inspect for root cause |
| Unit ran, then quit, warm air inside | High-pressure / thermal lockout | One reset only | Find overheat cause, restore |
| ECM fan never commands on | Board or motor module fault | None safe | Scan board, replace module/board |
| Intermittent, melted wire insulation | Loose/rodent-damaged wiring | Note where it failed | Repair/replace wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$300
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor + capacitor:** $350–$650 (PSC motors); ECM/variable-speed motors can run **$600–$1,100+**
- **High-pressure switch:** $150–$350
- **Variable-speed control board (XV models):** $400–$900+
- **Wiring repair:** $120–$300
- **Diagnostic fee:** $80–$180 (often credited toward repair)

DIY capacitor swaps are possible for experienced people but risky — capacitors store a lethal charge. If you're not certain, the $150–$300 pro price buys safety.
