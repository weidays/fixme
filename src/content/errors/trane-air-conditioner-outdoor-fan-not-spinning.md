## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a flashing fault code — it's a physical symptom on your Trane condenser (the outdoor unit). The fan on top of the condenser pulls air across the coil to release the heat your system removed from the house. When it stops, the compressor quickly overheats, refrigerant pressure climbs, and the unit will either trip an internal safety or risk permanent compressor damage.

On most Trane single-stage and two-stage condensers (XR and XL series), the fan is a simple PSC motor driven by a run capacitor and switched by a contactor. On variable-speed units (XV20i, XV18), the fan is an ECM motor controlled by a circuit board, so the failure behaves differently and is diagnosed differently. This page focuses mainly on the common PSC setup and flags the ECM differences where they matter.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — By far the most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan motor the jolt it needs to start. When it weakens or dies, the fan won't start (you may hear a hum) while the compressor may or may not run.
2. **Burned or stuck contactor** — The relay that sends power to the outdoor unit. Pitted or welded contacts can interrupt power to the fan.
3. **Failed condenser fan motor** — Bearings seize or windings burn out. Often the motor smells hot or the blade won't spin freely by hand (with power off).
4. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuse** — No power reaching the condenser at all; usually the compressor is silent too.
5. **Obstructed or seized fan blade** — Debris, a bent blade, or rust binding the shaft.
6. **Control board or ECM fault (variable-speed models)** — On XV-series units, a board or ECM motor fault can stop the fan; these throw diagnostic codes on the indoor board or app.
7. **Low-voltage / thermostat or wiring issue** — Less common, but a broken 24V signal can prevent the unit from energizing.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the condenser's electrical panel, discharge a capacitor, or push the fan blade with power on.

- **Thermostat:** Set it to Cool and lower the setpoint 3–5°F below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Indoor air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and shutdowns. Replace if dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor AC breakers in your main panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Make sure the pull-out disconnect box near the condenser is fully seated.
- **Vents and airflow:** Confirm supply and return vents inside aren't blocked.
- **Condensate:** Check that the condensate drain/float switch hasn't shut the system down — a full pan can stop cooling.
- **Visible obstructions:** With the system **off at the breaker**, look (don't reach in) for leaves or debris jammed in the fan.

If the fan still won't spin after these, it's a pro repair.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified HVAC tech will:

1. Confirm 240V is reaching the condenser and 24V is energizing the contactor.
2. **Test the run capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarad value — a reading well below spec confirms a bad cap.
3. Inspect the **contactor** for pitting, welding, or a chattering coil.
4. Spin the fan blade by hand (power off) to check for **seized bearings**.
5. Check motor windings for continuity and ground faults; measure amp draw against the nameplate.
6. On variable-speed XV units, **pull diagnostic codes** from the indoor board or Trane app and test the ECM motor and control board.
7. Verify refrigerant pressures only after the fan is restored, to rule out related compressor strain.

A trustworthy quote names the specific failed part and its measured value — not just "the fan's bad."

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan won't start | Failed run capacitor | Shut off at thermostat; don't push blade | Test & replace capacitor |
| No sound at all from outdoor unit | Tripped breaker / blown disconnect fuse | Reset breaker once; check disconnect | Trace power loss, replace fuse |
| Fan tries to start, hums, stops | Weak capacitor or failing motor | Turn unit off to prevent overheat | Test cap & motor amp draw |
| Buzzing/clicking at outdoor unit | Pitted or stuck contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| Fan blade won't spin freely (power off) | Seized motor bearings | None | Replace fan motor |
| Debris jamming blade | Obstruction | Clear visible debris, power off | Inspect blade & shaft |
| Fan dead, app/board shows fault | ECM or control board (XV models) | Note the code | Diagnose board/ECM, replace part |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Run capacitor:** $150–$300 — the most common and least expensive fix.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350.
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC):** $350–$650, depending on HP and access.
- **ECM fan motor (variable-speed XV models):** $600–$1,200+ — proprietary and pricier.
- **Control board (XV models):** $500–$1,000+.
- **Disconnect fuses / breaker reset:** $0 DIY to ~$150 for a service call.
- **Diagnostic service call:** typically $90–$180, often credited toward the repair.

Prices vary by region, after-hours rates, and parts availability. Get the failed part identified before approving anything.
