## What this code means

Most Trane air conditioners do **not** flash a specific "outdoor fan not spinning" code — this is a physical symptom you observe, not a numeric fault. When the thermostat calls for cooling, the outdoor unit (condenser) should power up and the top-mounted fan should spin steadily while the compressor hums.

If the fan sits still while the compressor runs, or the whole condenser is dead, you have a real problem. The outdoor fan's job is to pull air across the condenser coil to dump the heat your home rejected. Without it, refrigerant pressure and temperature climb fast and the compressor can be destroyed.

**Turn the system off at the thermostat now** if the compressor is humming but the fan isn't turning. This is a **pro-level** repair involving line-voltage electrical components and a capacitor that can hold a dangerous charge even with power off.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — The single most common cause. The dual capacitor supplies the starting/running boost to the fan motor (and compressor). A bulged, leaking, or out-of-spec capacitor leaves the fan unable to start.
2. **Condenser fan motor failure** — Worn bearings, a burned-out winding, or a seized motor. Often accompanied by a burning smell, grinding, or a hot motor housing.
3. **Failed or pitted contactor** — The relay that sends power to the outdoor unit can weld, corrode, or fail to pull in, cutting power to the fan.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuses** — A tripped outdoor disconnect or breaker cuts power entirely. If only the fan circuit is affected, low-voltage or motor fuses may be involved.
5. **Obstructed or seized fan blade** — Debris, a bird's nest, or ice can physically jam the blade.
6. **Bad control board or wiring (variable-speed / XV models)** — On communicating Trane systems, a fault in the outdoor control module or a loose connection can stop the fan.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser or touch the capacitor.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank.
- **Air filter:** A clogged indoor filter can cause freeze-ups that ice over the outdoor coil and stall the fan. Replace a dirty filter and let any ice melt fully.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor air-handler/furnace breaker and the outdoor AC breaker in your main panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** The pull-out box near the condenser may have been left out after service. Make sure it's fully seated (this is a plug, not an electrical repair).
- **Visible obstructions:** Look through the top grille for leaves, sticks, or a bird's nest jamming the blade. Clear debris **only with the disconnect pulled and power off**.
- **Condensate:** Confirm the indoor drain isn't overflowing, which can trip a safety float switch and stop the whole system.

If the fan still won't spin after these checks, call a licensed HVAC technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a repeatable sequence — use this to sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirms the call & voltage:** Verifies the thermostat is calling for cooling and that 240V line voltage and 24V control voltage are present at the condenser.
2. **Tests the capacitor:** Discharges it safely, then measures microfarads (uF) with a meter against the nameplate rating. An out-of-spec reading is a clear failure.
3. **Checks the contactor:** Inspects for pitted/welded contacts and confirms the coil pulls in when energized.
4. **Tests the motor:** Checks windings for continuity and shorts to ground, spins the blade by hand for bearing drag, and confirms the motor gets power.
5. **Inspects the blade & control board:** Looks for physical binding, and on XV/communicating units, pulls fault data from the outdoor module.

A thorough diagnosis takes 30–60 minutes. Be cautious of a tech who replaces the whole motor without first proving the capacitor is good.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan sits still | Failed run capacitor | Shut system off; don't run it | Discharge & test capacitor, replace to spec |
| Fan starts when nudged with a stick | Weak capacitor (or failing motor) | Turn off, don't keep running it | Confirm uF, replace capacitor; check motor |
| Whole condenser dead, no hum | Tripped breaker, disconnect out, bad contactor | Reset breaker once; seat disconnect | Test contactor & line voltage |
| Grinding/screeching then stops | Seized motor bearings | Turn off to prevent overheating | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Burning smell from unit | Burned motor winding or contactor | Shut off immediately at breaker | Replace failed component, inspect wiring |
| Fan blade physically jammed | Debris or ice | Clear debris with power off; melt ice | Inspect blade, motor shaft, coil |
| Communicating unit shows no fan | Control board / module fault (XV/XL) | Note any fault codes, then call | Pull codes, test outdoor module |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US installed prices including parts and labor. Your Trane model, tonnage, and local rates cause variation.

- **Capacitor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $300
- **Condenser fan motor (with new blade if needed):** $350 – $650
- **Breaker reset / seating disconnect:** $0 DIY, or a diagnostic fee ($75–$150) if a tech is already on-site
- **Clearing debris / seized blade:** $75 – $200
- **Outdoor control board/module (XV communicating units):** $400 – $900+
- **Diagnostic / service call fee:** $75 – $150 (often applied toward the repair)

If a technician finds the compressor was damaged from running without the fan, repair costs escalate dramatically — another reason to shut the system down at the first sign of trouble.
