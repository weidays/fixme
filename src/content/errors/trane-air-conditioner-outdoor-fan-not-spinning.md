## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numbered Trane fault code — it's a physical symptom. On most Trane split systems the outdoor (condenser) unit contains the compressor and a fan that pulls air across the coil to dump heat. When the thermostat calls for cooling, the contactor closes, sending high voltage to both the compressor and the fan motor.

If that fan blade sits still while the unit is humming or buzzing, heat has nowhere to go. The compressor will quickly overheat and trip on its internal protector or a high-pressure switch. On variable-speed Trane units (XV18, XV20i), the inverter board may instead log a fault and shut the system down rather than buzz.

**Bottom line:** turn the system off at the thermostat to protect the compressor, then work through the checks below or call a pro.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run/start capacitor.** The single most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan motor the jolt it needs to start. A bulged, leaking, or weak capacitor means the motor hums but won't spin (or spins only if nudged).
2. **Burned-out condenser fan motor.** Bearings seize or windings fail, usually after years of heat and weather. The motor stays silent or trips a breaker.
3. **Failed contactor.** The relay that sends power to the outdoor unit may have pitted/welded contacts or a dead 24V coil, so the fan never gets voltage.
4. **Seized or obstructed fan blade.** Debris, a bent blade, or a rusted bearing physically blocks rotation.
5. **Tripped high-pressure or motor overload.** A protective device has shut things down after overheating — often a downstream symptom of one of the causes above.
6. **Wiring or capacitor terminal failure.** Loose, corroded, or chewed wires (often by rodents) interrupt power to the fan.
7. **Control board / inverter fault (variable-speed models).** On XV-series units the outdoor board controls fan speed; a board fault can stop the fan. This is model-specific — confirm with Trane diagnostics.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Never open the condenser's electrical panel** — capacitors hold a lethal charge even with power off.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature so the system is actually calling.
- **Air filter:** A clogged indoor filter can cause overheating and nuisance shutdowns. Replace if dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor (AC) breaker, plus any outdoor disconnect. Reset a tripped breaker **once** — if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Visible obstructions:** With the unit OFF at the breaker, look (don't reach in) for leaves, sticks, or a cottonwood mat blocking the blade. Clear loose debris from outside the unit only.
- **Vents and airflow:** Make sure indoor supply/return vents are open and unblocked.
- **Listen:** A humming or buzzing outdoor unit with a still blade strongly points to a capacitor or motor — note this for your technician.

Do **not** push the blade by hand to start it. That's a documented shock and amputation hazard.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech will work in a logical order so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the call & voltage:** Confirm the thermostat is calling and that 24V reaches the contactor coil.
2. **Discharge and test the capacitor:** They safely discharge the capacitor, then measure its microfarad (µF) rating against the nameplate. Out-of-spec means replacement.
3. **Test the contactor:** Check that contacts close and pass line voltage to the fan when energized.
4. **Check the motor:** Test windings for continuity/short, spin the blade for bearing drag, and confirm voltage is reaching the motor.
5. **Inspect wiring & overloads:** Look for burned terminals, rodent damage, or a tripped high-pressure switch.
6. **Inverter models:** On XV18/XV20i units, they'll pull fault codes from the outdoor board with Trane service tools.

A good diagnosis takes 20–45 minutes. Be wary of a quote that jumps straight to a new motor without testing the capacitor first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unit hums, blade still; spins if nudged | Failed capacitor | Turn off; don't touch blade | Discharge & replace capacitor, verify µF |
| Outdoor unit silent, no fan or compressor | Bad contactor or no 24V signal | Check breakers/disconnect once | Test contactor & control voltage, replace as needed |
| Fan won't turn, motor hot or smells burnt | Seized/failed fan motor | Shut system off | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Blade physically stuck | Debris or bent blade/bearing | Clear loose debris (power off) | Free or replace blade/motor |
| Breaker trips when AC runs | Shorted motor or wiring fault | Reset once; if it retrips, stop | Find short, replace faulty component |
| Compressor runs, fan stops after minutes | Overheating / high-pressure trip | Turn off to protect compressor | Diagnose root cause (often capacitor/motor) |
| XV-series shuts down with board fault | Inverter/board fault | Note any displayed code | Read Trane diagnostics, repair board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included. Your area, unit age, and parts availability shift these:

- **Run/start capacitor:** $150 – $350
- **Contactor:** $150 – $300
- **Condenser fan motor (with new capacitor):** $400 – $700
- **Fan blade replacement:** $150 – $300
- **Wiring/terminal repair:** $100 – $300
- **Inverter control board (XV-series):** $600 – $1,500+ — model-specific; get a second opinion before approving
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80 – $200, often credited toward the repair

If your unit is older than ~12–15 years and needs a motor *plus* other parts, ask whether replacement is more economical — but a single capacitor fix is almost always worth doing.
