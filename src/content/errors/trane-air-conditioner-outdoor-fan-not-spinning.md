## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is not a specific Trane blink code — it's a physical symptom. The large fan on top of your outdoor condenser unit should spin whenever the compressor is running, pulling air across the coil to dump heat outside.

When that fan stops turning while the system is calling for cooling, the unit can't reject heat. The compressor may keep running, get hot, and trip on its internal overload — or in worst cases, be permanently damaged. That's why we rate this **pro-level**: the diagnosis involves live electrical testing and, often, a run capacitor that stores a dangerous charge even with power off.

On most Trane split systems (XR, XL and XV series condensers), the fan and compressor share a **dual run capacitor**. A single failed capacitor can stop the fan while leaving the compressor humming — a classic sign.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — By far the most common cause. The fan may hum but not spin, spin slowly, or do nothing. The capacitor is a small cylindrical component that fails from heat and age.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — Bearings dry out or windings fail. The motor may be hot, smell burnt, or spin roughly by hand (power off).
3. **Failed contactor** — The relay that sends power to the outdoor unit can pit, stick, or fail to close, cutting power to the fan and compressor.
4. **Tripped compressor/fan overload** — If the unit overheated, it may shut down until it cools. It may restart, then fail again if the root cause remains.
5. **Debris or ice physically blocking the fan** — Leaves, a stick, or a warped blade jamming the fan.
6. **Loss of 24V control signal or low-voltage wiring fault** — A control-board, thermostat, or transformer issue may prevent the contactor from energizing.
7. **Blown fuse or breaker to the outdoor unit** — No power reaching the condenser at all.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only steps a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser or touch the capacitor.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it uses them.
- **Air filter:** A badly clogged filter can cause the whole system to overheat and shut down. Replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor and outdoor breakers in your main panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Some homes have a pull-out disconnect box near the condenser. Confirm it's fully seated (but do not open the unit itself).
- **Visible blockage:** With the system OFF at the breaker, look through the top grille for leaves, sticks, or debris jamming the fan blade. Clear only what you can reach from outside the grille.
- **Indoor vents:** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked, which can contribute to overheating shutdowns.
- **Condensate:** A full condensate pan/float switch usually stops the indoor unit, not the outdoor fan — but check it if the system is dead overall.

If the fan still won't spin after these checks, turn the system off at the thermostat and call an HVAC technician. Running the compressor with a dead fan can wreck it.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will follow a logical sequence. Use this to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Confirm the call for cooling** and verify 24V control voltage reaching the contactor.
2. **Check the contactor** — measure whether it's pulling in and passing line voltage.
3. **Test the run capacitor** with a meter for microfarad (µF) rating against the label spec. A weak or dead capacitor is the usual culprit.
4. **Test the fan motor** — check windings for continuity/shorts and try to spin it by hand (power off) to feel for seized bearings.
5. **Inspect wiring and the fan blade** for damage, loose terminals, or burnt connections.
6. **Verify overload behavior** — confirm the compressor/motor isn't tripping on thermal overload from an underlying fault.

A capacitor swap is quick. A motor replacement takes longer and must match voltage, horsepower, RPM, and rotation direction.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None — shut system off | Test & replace capacitor |
| Fan dead, compressor humming | Bad capacitor or contactor | Shut off to protect compressor | Test capacitor/contactor, replace |
| No fan, no compressor, no hum | Tripped breaker, contactor, or 24V loss | Reset breaker once | Trace power, test contactor/transformer |
| Fan spins slow or wobbles | Weak capacitor or worn motor bearings | None | Replace capacitor or fan motor |
| Fan jammed, burnt smell | Seized/burned fan motor | Cut power, don't restart | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Fan blade physically stuck | Debris in the fan | Clear visible debris (power off) | Inspect blade & motor for damage |
| Runs a few minutes then quits | Overheating / thermal overload | Replace filter, clear coil area | Find and fix root cause |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US installed prices including a service call. Your area, company, and unit tonnage will move these.

- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80 – $180 (often applied to the repair)
- **Run capacitor (installed):** $150 – $400 — the part itself is only $15 – $40
- **Contactor (installed):** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor (installed):** $400 – $700+ depending on motor size and availability
- **Fan blade replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Low-voltage/transformer repair:** $150 – $400

If your Trane unit is still under the parts warranty, the component may be covered — you'd typically pay only labor. Ask the tech to check the serial number against Trane's warranty lookup.
