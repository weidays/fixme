## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code on American Standard equipment — it's the most common symptom homeowners describe. Your system may turn on, the indoor blower may run, and the outdoor unit may even hum, yet the air coming out of your vents stays warm or only mildly cool.

On newer American Standard systems with the AccuLink communicating platform, the thermostat or control board may display a specific alert or fault number when something is wrong. On older single-stage and conventional units, there's no display at all — the only "code" is that the house won't cool down. Because behavior varies widely by model (Platinum, Gold, Silver) and by whether the system is single-stage, two-stage, or variable-speed, this page covers the general causes rather than one fixed fault.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode/setting, or a miscommunication on AccuLink systems.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — the outdoor unit or air handler isn't getting power.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan can't start; you may hear humming or clicking.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — blocked by grass, leaves, or dirt, so the system can't reject heat.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; vents blow weak or warm air.
7. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit fails to close.
8. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — requires a certified tech to find, repair, and recharge.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — outdoor unit overheats and shuts down on internal protection.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the system runs but produces no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A dirty filter is the single most common cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the temperature is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Make sure the fan is on AUTO, not just ON.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker to the air handler or outdoor unit. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated resets can damage equipment.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Do **not** remove panels or touch internal components.
- **Condensate drain:** If you see standing water near the indoor unit or the unit shut off, the drain may be clogged. You can clear a visible drain line, but a tripped float switch points to a backed-up line that may need professional clearing.

Do not open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech should work through these steps so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint and check airflow** — inspect filter, blower, and registers.
2. **Measure voltage** at the contactor, capacitor, and motors to confirm power delivery.
3. **Test the capacitor** with a multimeter against its microfarad rating.
4. **Inspect the contactor** for pitting or failure to pull in.
5. **Check the coils** — clean condenser, inspect evaporator for ice or dirt.
6. **Take refrigerant readings** — connect gauges and measure superheat/subcooling to determine charge. Low readings mean a leak search, not just a recharge.
7. **Check compressor and fan motor** amperage and windings.
8. **Read any AccuLink/board fault history** on communicating systems.

A good tech explains *what* they measured and *why* before recommending a part. Be cautious of anyone who wants to "add a pound of Freon" without finding the leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Indoor unit runs, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker or bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor, check wiring |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on refrigerant lines or coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off AC, run fan to thaw, replace filter | Find airflow fault or leak, recharge |
| Vents barely cool, AC runs constantly | Dirty condenser or low refrigerant | Clean around outdoor unit | Clean coil, check charge |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries or no power | Replace batteries, check breaker | Check transformer, low-voltage fuse |
| AC stops, water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible line | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Runs but never gets cold | Failed compressor | None — call pro | Test compressor, quote replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 professional
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$300
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$1,800
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,000+ (often worth comparing to full unit replacement)

Costs vary by region, model series, and whether your system is under American Standard's manufacturer warranty — registered systems often carry 10-year parts coverage, which can dramatically reduce part costs (labor is usually still billed).

## Related codes

- **AC frozen / iced evaporator coil** — low airflow or refrigerant
- **Outdoor unit won't turn on** — contactor, capacitor, or breaker
- **AccuLink communication fault** — thermostat-to-board signal loss on communicating systems
- **Condensate float switch tripped** — drain clog shutting the system down
- **Compressor short cycling** — capacitor, overload, or refrigerant issue
