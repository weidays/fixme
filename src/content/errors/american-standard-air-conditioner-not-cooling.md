## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom your American Standard system is showing. Your air handler may be blowing air that isn't cold, the outdoor condenser may not be running, or the house simply never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Unlike a furnace flash code, "not cooling" can have many root causes spanning airflow, electrical, and refrigerant systems. If you have a communicating **AccuLink** thermostat, it may display a specific alert or fault number on screen — note that code, because it narrows the diagnosis considerably. Conventional thermostats give no code, so technicians work through the system step by step.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — chokes airflow across the indoor coil, which can ice it up and stop cooling.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — leaves, grass, and dust insulate the coil so it can't reject heat.
3. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "fan" not "cool"), or a setpoint above room temperature.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the condenser or air handler lost power.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common condenser part; the fan may spin but the compressor won't start.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak or no cooling and often a frozen coil. Requires a pro.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser is pitted or stuck.
8. **Frozen evaporator coil** — usually a downstream symptom of airflow or refrigerant problems.
9. **Failed compressor or fan motor** — the most expensive outcome, diagnosed last.
10. **Clogged condensate drain triggering a safety float switch** — cuts cooling to prevent water damage on some installs.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should perform. Stop and call a pro if anything is beyond this list.

- **Filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it and run the system 30 minutes.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is at least a few degrees below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for tripped breakers serving the indoor air handler and the outdoor unit. Reset one time only. If it trips again, stop — that signals an electrical fault.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Gently rinse visible coil fins with a garden hose (never a pressure washer) with power off.
- **Condensate drain:** If you see water pooling near the indoor unit or a clogged drain line, a tripped float switch may be the cause. A wet/dry vacuum on the drain line outlet can clear minor clogs.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **Off** and the fan to **On** for a few hours to thaw, then call a pro — ice usually means airflow or refrigerant trouble.

Do not open sealed refrigerant lines, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech should work in a logical order. Use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Read any AccuLink fault codes** and confirm the complaint.
2. **Verify airflow** — inspect filter, blower operation, and check temperature split across the indoor coil (typically 16–22°F).
3. **Check power and controls** — measure voltage at the contactor, test the capacitor with a meter, inspect the contactor for pitting.
4. **Measure refrigerant pressures** with gauges and check superheat/subcooling. Low charge points to a leak, which they should locate with an electronic detector or dye.
5. **Test the compressor and condenser fan motor** for amp draw and winding resistance.
6. **Inspect the condensate system** and float switch.

Be wary of any tech who wants to "just add a pound of Freon" without finding the leak — that's a temporary fix that wastes money.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air handler runs, air not cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil | Check refrigerant charge, airflow |
| Outdoor unit silent, no power | Tripped breaker | Reset breaker once | Diagnose short or failed component |
| Fan spins, compressor won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Warm air, hissing or oily residue | Refrigerant leak | None — call pro | Locate and repair leak, recharge |
| Outdoor unit hums then clicks off | Failed contactor / compressor | None — call pro | Test contactor and compressor |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low charge | Replace filter, thaw, then call | Diagnose airflow vs refrigerant |
| Water near indoor unit, no cooling | Clogged drain / float switch | Clear drain line | Clear blockage, verify float switch |
| House warm only on hot days | Undersized charge or dirty coil | Clean condenser coil | Verify charge, check superheat |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $0 DIY rinse – $150 professional
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing against full condenser or system replacement on older units)

Prices vary by region, refrigerant type (R-410A vs older R-22), and whether the system is under American Standard's limited warranty — registered units may have parts coverage that drops your out-of-pocket cost.
