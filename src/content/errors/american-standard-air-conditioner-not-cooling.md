## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific fault code — American Standard air conditioners rarely display lettered codes the way furnaces do. It means the system is calling for cooling but indoor air is not getting cold, or the outdoor unit isn't running properly.

The cause falls into a few buckets: airflow problems (filter, coil, blower), electrical faults (capacitor, contactor, breaker), refrigerant issues (leak, frozen coil), or a failed compressor. Some newer variable-speed American Standard units (Platinum series with AccuLink/Link communicating controls) will flash fault codes at the thermostat or outdoor board — check your manual, because those codes narrow the diagnosis considerably.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, can freeze the evaporator coil and stop cooling entirely.
2. **Thermostat settings or batteries** — set to the wrong mode, fan-only, or a dead battery in a non-powered stat.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power, so the indoor air just blows warm.
4. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks cooling until it thaws.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces capacity, often pairs with a frozen coil or hissing.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
9. **Failed compressor or fan motor** — the most expensive outcome, usually after other failures go ignored.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is hot, smells burnt, or keeps tripping.

- **Filter:** Pull the air filter. If it's gray or clogged, replace it. A blocked filter is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the temperature is below room temp, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace batteries if your model uses them.
- **Breaker:** Check the electrical panel for a tripped breaker (both the indoor air handler and outdoor condenser may have separate breakers). Reset **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, and clear leaves, grass clippings, or debris from around the condenser so it can breathe.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Check for standing water near the indoor unit and clear the visible drain line if you can do so safely.
- **If you see ice:** Turn the system to **Off** (fan **On** can help it thaw) and let it melt for a few hours before running again.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that won't stay running.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech follows a logical path so a quote should reflect real testing, not guesswork:

1. **Verifies the call for cooling** at the thermostat and checks 24V control voltage.
2. **Checks for power** at the disconnect, breaker, and contactor.
3. **Tests the capacitor** with a meter — a weak or failed cap is cheap and common.
4. **Inspects the contactor** for pitting and proper pull-in.
5. **Measures airflow** and checks the filter, blower, and evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
6. **Takes refrigerant pressures** (superheat/subcooling) to judge the charge and look for a leak.
7. **Reads board/thermostat fault codes** on communicating Platinum/Link systems.
8. **Tests the compressor and fan motor** for windings and amp draw if the basics check out.

A good tech identifies the *root cause* (e.g., why refrigerant is low) rather than just adding refrigerant to a leaking system.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter or frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw coil | Restore airflow, check charge |
| Outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, test circuit |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on lines or coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Off, thaw, new filter | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Hissing or oily residue outside | Refrigerant leak | None — call pro | Leak search and repair |
| Fan spins, no cold air | Compressor not running | None — call pro | Test compressor windings |
| Water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible drain line | Clear drain, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY).
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-454B on newer units can cost more).
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650 installed.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth weighing against a new unit if out of warranty).
- **Diagnostic/service call:** $80–$180, often credited toward the repair.

Prices vary by region and contractor. Many parts may be covered if your American Standard registered warranty is still active — ask before paying full price.
