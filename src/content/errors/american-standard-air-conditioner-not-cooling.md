## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific fault code — American Standard air conditioners don't display a text error for this the way a furnace shows numbered codes. Instead, your system is running (or trying to run) but the air coming from the vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

On variable-speed and communicating systems (Platinum series with a AccuLink / Link thermostat), the thermostat may show a fault or a communication message that points to a specific component. On single-stage and two-stage systems, there's usually no on-board display, so diagnosis relies on measured temperatures, pressures and electrical readings.

The root cause almost always falls into one of three buckets: **airflow problems**, **refrigerant problems**, or **electrical/compressor problems**.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow can freeze the evaporator coil, which stops cooling entirely.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure. The compressor or condenser fan hums but won't start, or trips the breaker.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — cooling gets progressively weaker; the outdoor coil may frost. Requires a licensed tech.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor unit can't reject heat, so cooling capacity drops, especially on hot days.
6. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the fan on top of the outdoor unit isn't spinning; the compressor overheats and shuts off.
7. **Bad contactor** — the electrical relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck and won't close.
8. **Thermostat or control/communication fault** — wrong settings, dead batteries, or (on Link systems) a communication error between the thermostat and boards.
9. **Compressor failure** — the least common but most expensive; the compressor is seized, shorted, or has lost its windings.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are genuinely safe for a homeowner. Stop at anything requiring you to open the sealed refrigerant system or the outdoor electrical panel.

- **Thermostat**: Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Set the fan to AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter**: Pull it and inspect it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Vents and registers**: Make sure supply and return vents are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Breaker**: Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Ice**: Look at the indoor coil and the refrigerant lines. If you see ice or frost, turn the system to OFF and set the fan to ON to help it thaw. Don't chip at the ice.
- **Condensate drain**: A clogged drain can trigger a float switch that shuts down cooling. Check for standing water near the indoor unit; some clogs can be cleared with a wet/dry vacuum on the outdoor drain line.
- **Outdoor unit**: Clear leaves, grass and debris from around the condenser. Make sure the fan on top is spinning when the system calls for cooling (look, don't reach in).

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the process helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** and confirm thermostat wiring/settings; on Link systems they'll read any communication faults.
2. **Measure temperature split** — the difference between return and supply air. A healthy split is roughly 16–22°F; low split points to airflow or refrigerant issues.
3. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower operation, evaporator coil condition, and check for ice.
4. **Check electrical components** — test the capacitor with a meter, inspect the contactor, and verify the condenser fan and compressor draw the correct amps.
5. **Attach gauges** (certified tech only) to read suction and head pressures and calculate superheat/subcooling — this confirms or rules out a refrigerant charge or leak problem.
6. **Leak search** if the charge is low — using electronic detectors or dye, because simply adding refrigerant without repairing the leak is against EPA best practice.
7. **Compressor test** if everything else checks out — winding resistance and megohm testing.

A good tech should show you readings, not just guess. Be cautious of anyone who wants to "top off the refrigerant" without checking for a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, turn off to thaw ice | Confirm airflow, check charge |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn system off, run fan to thaw | Find airflow/refrigerant cause |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Fan motor or capacitor | Clear debris, note the fault | Replace fan motor/capacitor |
| Weak cooling on hot days | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse fins gently with hose | Deep-clean coil, check charge |
| Cooling fades over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None — call a pro | Leak search and recharge |
| Nothing runs outdoors | Contactor or breaker | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor |
| Thermostat blank or error | Batteries / comm fault | Replace batteries | Diagnose control/Link fault |
| Loud unit, then shuts off | Compressor overheating | Turn off, call a pro | Compressor and electrical test |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter**: $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries**: $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain cleaning**: $75–$250
- **Condenser coil cleaning**: $100–$300
- **Run/start capacitor**: $150–$400
- **Contactor**: $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor**: $300–$650
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge**: $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A costs more than legacy R-22)
- **TXV or metering device**: $500–$900
- **Compressor replacement**: $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth weighing against a full system replacement on older units)

Prices vary by region, unit size and whether your system is still under American Standard's parts warranty — registered units often carry a 10-year parts warranty, which can cut component costs significantly. Keep your registration and installer paperwork handy.
