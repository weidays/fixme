## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — most American Standard split-system air conditioners don't display alphanumeric error codes the way furnaces do. Instead, "not cooling" describes a symptom: the system is calling for cooling but the air coming from your vents is warm, lukewarm, or the house simply never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

On American Standard's communicating systems (AccuLink / variable-speed Platinum models with a connected thermostat), you may see a specific message or fault on the thermostat screen. On older single-stage and two-stage units, there is no display — you diagnose by symptom. This page covers both, but notes where behavior depends on your model.

The underlying problem falls into one of three buckets: **airflow** (filter, coils, blower), **refrigerant** (low charge from a leak), or **electrical/mechanical** (capacitor, contactor, compressor, control board).

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter** — restricts airflow, ices the indoor coil, and kills cooling. The single most common cause.
2. **Tripped breaker or disconnect at the outdoor unit** — the outdoor condenser has no power, so only the indoor blower runs (warm air).
3. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a programming/communication fault on connected models.
4. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood; the unit can't reject heat.
5. **Frozen indoor evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; produces warm air and water around the indoor unit.
6. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
8. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, ice on the line, hissing. Requires a pro.
9. **Failing compressor** — the unit trips the breaker, hums and shuts off, or runs without cooling.
10. **Control board or communication fault** — more common on Platinum/variable-speed AccuComfort systems.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. If they don't restore cooling, call a licensed technician.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the temperature is below room temperature. Set the fan to AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it against the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause and a free fix.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers in your panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping means a real fault.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** There's a pull-out disconnect box near the condenser. Make sure it's fully seated.
- **Vents and registers:** Confirm supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain:** If you see water near the indoor unit or the unit shut off, the drain may be clogged and tripped a safety float switch. You can clear a visible clog and pour drain tablets in the pan.
- **Ice check:** If you see frost or ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to OFF (fan ON to help thaw) and let it fully thaw before running again — then call a pro if it refreezes.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or remove panels from the energized outdoor unit.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician follows a logical sequence. Knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirms the symptom** and checks thermostat call, mode, and (on communicating models) any fault messages.
2. **Measures temperature split** — the difference between return and supply air. A healthy split is roughly 16–22°F; a low split points to a charge or airflow problem.
3. **Inspects airflow** — filter, blower operation, evaporator coil cleanliness.
4. **Checks electrical components** — measures the capacitor's microfarads, inspects the contactor, verifies voltage at the compressor.
5. **Reads refrigerant pressures** with gauges on the service ports and compares to superheat/subcooling targets for the model.
6. **Leak search** if charge is low — electronic detector, UV dye, or bubble test. A reputable shop finds the leak rather than just topping off.
7. **Tests the compressor** — amp draw, windings, and whether it starts and runs.

Ask for a written diagnosis naming the failed part and its measured value (e.g., "capacitor reading 18µF, rated 45µF"). That's evidence, not a guess.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, indoor fan runs | Outdoor unit has no power | Check breaker & disconnect once | Diagnose contactor, capacitor, or wiring fault |
| Weak airflow, then warm air | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw with fan ON | Clean coil, find airflow restriction |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Gradual loss of cooling, hissing | Low refrigerant / leak | None — call a pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or electrical fault | Reset once only, then stop | Test compressor & circuit |
| Water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible clog, add tablets | Clear line, check float switch |
| Outdoor coil packed with debris | Dirty condenser | Gently rinse fins with hose | Deep clean, comb fins, check charge |
| Thermostat blank or odd message | Dead batteries / comm fault | Replace batteries | Diagnose board/communication |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included. Your area and unit size affect pricing.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0–$50 DIY; $100–$250 pro
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor:** $400–$900
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$2,000+ depending on leak location
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,800+ (often makes an older unit not worth repairing)
- **Control board (communicating models):** $400–$900
- **Service/diagnostic call:** $80–$200 (often credited toward repair)

If your unit is out of warranty and needs a compressor or major refrigerant repair, ask for a replacement quote to compare — sometimes a new system is the smarter spend.
