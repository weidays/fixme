## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single error code on American Standard equipment — it's a symptom. Your system is powered and may even be running, but the air coming from your vents is warm, lukewarm, or simply not cold enough to satisfy the thermostat.

On basic single-stage American Standard units (many Silver and Gold series), there's no on-board fault display — you diagnose by behavior and component testing. On higher-end variable-speed **AccuComfort Platinum** units, the system communicates with the thermostat and may show a specific fault or service message at the thermostat or via LED flash codes on the outdoor control board. **Check your specific model's manual** — flash-code meanings vary by board revision and we won't guess yours.

The job here is to figure out *which* of several common failures is causing the lack of cooling. Some you can rule out safely yourself; others need a licensed tech.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Dirty condenser coil** (outdoor unit) — caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, it can't reject heat.
3. **Failed dual run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan won't start or struggles.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, ice on the lines, or long run times.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; blocks all cooling until thawed.
6. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or wiring/calibration fault.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor** — unit runs but overheats and shuts down on the compressor.
9. **Contactor failure** — worn or stuck contacts prevent the outdoor unit from energizing.
10. **Compressor failure** — the worst case; the unit hums or trips but won't cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are genuinely safe for a homeowner. Stop at anything that requires opening the electrical compartment or touching refrigerant.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Lower the temperature a few degrees and listen for the outdoor unit to kick on.
- **Breaker:** Check the electrical panel for a tripped breaker to the air handler/furnace and the outdoor unit. Reset it **once**. If it trips again immediately, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting it.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit clearance:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris away from the condenser. Gently rinse the outside of the coil fins with a garden hose (water only, no pressure washer) if they're dirty.
- **Condensate:** A full drain pan or clogged condensate line can trip a safety float switch and shut the system down. Check for standing water near the indoor unit.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, **turn the system off at the thermostat** and let it thaw fully (a few hours) before running again. Running a frozen unit makes things worse.

If none of these restores cooling, it's time for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the diagnostic flow helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirm the complaint** and check thermostat call and 24V signal to the outdoor unit.
2. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower operation, and evaporator coil condition.
3. **Test the capacitor** with a meter; a weak or bulged cap is replaced on the spot.
4. **Check the contactor** for pitting and proper pull-in.
5. **Verify fan and compressor operation** — amperage draw against the nameplate ratings.
6. **Take refrigerant pressures** with gauges and measure superheat/subcooling to judge the charge. Low readings trigger a **leak search** (electronic detector or dye).
7. **On AccuComfort/Platinum units,** read communicating fault data from the board or thermostat to pinpoint module or sensor faults.

A good tech explains *what* they found and *why* it caused the symptom. Be wary of a "just add refrigerant" quote with no leak search — that's a temporary fix that masks a real problem.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, dirty filter | Clogged filter restricting airflow | Replace filter | Inspect coil if freezing recurred |
| Outdoor unit silent, breaker OK | Failed capacitor or contactor | None (electrical) | Test and replace capacitor/contactor |
| Outdoor fan runs, no cooling | Failed compressor or low charge | None | Pressure test, leak search, compressor check |
| Ice on lines/coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, replace filter, let thaw | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Weak cooling, long run times | Dirty condenser coil or low charge | Rinse outdoor coil, water only | Coil cleaning, refrigerant check |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries or no power | Replace batteries, check breaker | Check 24V transformer/wiring |
| Water near indoor unit, no cooling | Clogged condensate / tripped float | Clear visible water | Clear drain line, test float switch |
| Outdoor unit hums then trips | Capacitor or seized compressor | Stop resetting breaker | Diagnose start components/compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included unless noted:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$300
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $350–$700
- **Condenser coil cleaning (professional):** $150–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,300–$2,500+ (often worth weighing against full replacement on older units)

Capacitor and contactor failures are the most common *affordable* fixes. Refrigerant and compressor issues are where costs climb — and where getting a second opinion pays off.
