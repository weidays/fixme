## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Your American Standard air conditioner may be running, blowing air, or even short-cycling, but the indoor temperature isn't dropping the way it should. On communicating American Standard systems with a color thermostat (like the AccuLink platform), you may see an actual error message or alert on the screen; on older single-stage units there's no display, just warm air.

Cooling happens when refrigerant absorbs heat at the indoor evaporator coil and releases it at the outdoor condenser. Anything that breaks that loop — low charge, blocked airflow, a dead fan, a failed compressor, or a tripped safety — shows up as "not cooling."

Because the most common serious causes (refrigerant leaks, compressor faults, electrical failures) require gauges, certification, and tools most homeowners don't have, this is rated a **pro** problem. The safe checks below are worth doing first, though — they fix a surprising number of cases for free.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — chokes airflow, drops cooling output, and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Thermostat set wrong or failing** — set to "fan/on" instead of "cool," wrong temperature, or dead batteries on a battery-powered stat.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit (condenser) has its own breaker; if it trips, the indoor blower still runs but no cooling occurs.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, and debris insulate the coil so it can't dump heat.
6. **Failed run capacitor** — a common, inexpensive part; if it fails, the compressor and/or condenser fan won't start.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — almost always a leak, not normal "topping off." Requires a licensed tech.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the outdoor fan won't spin, the unit overheats and shuts down on a safety.
9. **Failed contactor or control board** — on communicating/variable-speed boards, faults may lock out cooling.
10. **Compressor failure** — the most expensive cause, typically last to be ruled out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. If they don't restore cooling, stop and call a licensed HVAC tech.

- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the fan is on **Auto**, and the target temperature is below the room temperature. Replace batteries if it's a battery-powered model.
- **Replace the air filter.** A clogged filter is the #1 cause. Slide in a clean filter of the correct size — this alone fixes many "not cooling" complaints and can prevent coil freezing.
- **Check both breakers.** Your system usually has two: one for the indoor air handler/furnace and one for the outdoor condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop — that's an electrical fault for a pro.
- **Look at the outdoor unit.** Clear grass, leaves, and debris away from the coil. Make sure the outdoor fan is spinning when the system calls for cooling. Never insert anything into a running unit.
- **Check the vents.** Make sure supply and return registers aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Inspect for ice or water.** If you see ice on the copper lines or coil, turn the thermostat to **Off** (or fan-only) and let it thaw for several hours, then replace the filter. Check that the condensate drain isn't clogged — a full drain pan can trip a safety float switch and stop cooling.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** and check thermostat settings and the call for cooling.
2. **Measure temperature split** (return air vs. supply air); a healthy system drops roughly 16–22°F.
3. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower, evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
4. **Check electrical** — capacitor reading against rated microfarads, contactor operation, voltage at the compressor and fan.
5. **Attach refrigerant gauges** and read pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge charge and find restrictions.
6. **Leak search** if charge is low — electronic detector or dye, never just "add refrigerant."
7. **Read board diagnostics** on communicating/AccuLink systems, where fault history can pinpoint a locked-out component.

If a tech wants to "just add a couple pounds of refrigerant" without searching for a leak, that's a red flag — low charge means a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blowing but warm | Low refrigerant or compressor fault | Replace filter, check breakers | Gauge pressures, leak search, repair charge |
| Indoor coil/lines iced over | Dirty filter or low airflow/charge | Turn off, thaw, new filter | Diagnose airflow vs. charge, fix root cause |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Failed capacitor or fan motor | Check outdoor breaker | Test/replace capacitor or condenser fan motor |
| Nothing runs outside | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check control board |
| Weak airflow, poor cooling | Clogged filter or dirty coil | Replace filter, clear vents | Clean evaporator/condenser coil |
| Water around indoor unit / no cooling | Clogged condensate drain, float switch tripped | Clear visible drain blockage | Clear drain line, test safety float |
| Short-cycling, error on screen | Board fault on communicating unit | Note the alert message | Read diagnostics, replace board if faulted |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined. Your price varies by region and unit accessibility.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat replacement:** $0 (batteries) to $150–$400 installed.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250.
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,800+, or more on variable-speed AccuComfort units. On older units, weigh this against full system replacement.
- **Control board (communicating systems):** $400–$900.

If your system is out of warranty and a major component (compressor or coil) has failed, get a quote for full replacement too — sometimes repair money is better spent on a new, more efficient unit.
