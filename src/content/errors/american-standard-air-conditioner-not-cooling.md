## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code — it's the symptom you'll describe to a technician. On most American Standard split systems, the outdoor condenser and indoor air handler run but the air coming from your vents isn't cold (or isn't cold enough). On communicating **AccuLink** systems using the 824 or 1050 control, you may also see a fault code on the thermostat screen; those codes vary by board revision, so read them alongside, not instead of, the symptoms below.

The root cause falls into one of three buckets: airflow problems, electrical/control problems, or refrigerant/compressor problems. The first you can often address yourself; the last two are pro-level work.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — restricted airflow drops coil temperature, ices the evaporator, and kills cooling output. Most common and most DIY-friendly.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "fan" not "cool"), or a setpoint above room temperature.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** to the condenser — the outdoor unit won't run at all.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the indoor coil or the line set.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start, or the outdoor fan won't spin.
6. **Burned or stuck contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser isn't closing.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, sometimes hissing or oily residue at fittings.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor** — unit runs hot and shuts down on high-pressure protection.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; outdoor unit hums and trips the breaker, or runs but produces no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner:

- **Air filter** — pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is well below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker panel** — check whether the AC breaker (or the outdoor disconnect) has tripped. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping means an electrical fault.
- **Supply and return vents** — make sure they're open and not blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Condensate drain** — if your air handler has a float switch that's tripped from a clogged drain, the system may stop cooling. Check the drain pan for standing water; a wet/dry vacuum on the drain line outlet can clear minor clogs.
- **Outdoor unit** — clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser so it can breathe. Do this with the power off.

Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser, touch the capacitor, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech should work through this sequence so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measure supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is typically 16–22°F).
2. **Check airflow** — inspect filter, blower, and evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
3. **Confirm power and controls** — voltage at the disconnect, thermostat call, contactor pull-in, and capacitor microfarad reading against the rated value.
4. **Read pressures** — connect gauges to measure suction and head pressure and calculate superheat/subcooling. This reveals undercharge (leak), overcharge, or a restriction.
5. **Leak search** — if charge is low, use an electronic detector or dye to locate the leak before adding refrigerant.
6. **Compressor test** — check windings and amp draw if the compressor hums, trips, or won't start.
7. **On AccuLink systems** — pull stored fault codes from the 824/1050 control to confirm a board-reported fault.

Be skeptical of any quote that adds refrigerant without first finding the leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower & coil if persists |
| Ice on indoor coil or line | Low airflow or low charge | Turn off AC, run fan to thaw, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or refrigerant leak |
| Outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / disconnect | Reset breaker once | Diagnose short, contactor, or wiring |
| Fan or compressor hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — do not open panel | Test & replace capacitor |
| Condenser silent on cooling call | Burned contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| Gradual loss of cooling, hissing | Refrigerant leak | None — EPA regulated | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Fan motor or capacitor | None | Replace fan motor/capacitor |
| Unit hums, trips breaker | Failed compressor | None | Compressor test / replacement |
| AC stops, water in pan | Clogged condensate / float switch | Clear drain line | Replace pump or float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 pro
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$600
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is much pricier)
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ — often the point where replacing the whole condenser or system makes more sense on units 10+ years old

Prices vary by region, system size, and refrigerant type. Get a written diagnosis before approving major work.

## Related codes

- **American Standard AC frozen evaporator coil** — the most common follow-on from low airflow or low charge.
- **American Standard AC tripping breaker** — points to a capacitor, contactor, or compressor fault.
- **American Standard AC outdoor fan not running** — fan motor or capacitor failure.
- **AccuLink 824/1050 fault codes** — communicating-system codes that vary by board revision; read them with the symptoms above.
