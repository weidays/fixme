## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's the symptom you notice when your American Standard air conditioner runs (or tries to run) but the air coming from your vents is warm, lukewarm, or barely cool. On AccuLink-enabled systems with a digital control board, you may also see a fault flagged at the thermostat or indoor unit, but many American Standard single-stage units have no display and simply fail to drop the temperature.

Because "not cooling" can stem from anything between a clogged filter and a dead compressor, this page walks you from the cheapest, most likely causes down to the expensive ones — so you can sanity-check a technician's quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter** restricting airflow across the indoor coil — the single most common cause.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** at the disconnect, so the outdoor condenser never starts.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the outdoor fan or compressor hums or won't start.
4. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, choking heat rejection.
5. **Frozen indoor (evaporator) coil** from low airflow or low refrigerant — you'll see ice.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — cooling fades, especially on hot afternoons.
7. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a wiring/communication fault.
8. **Failed contactor** in the condenser, so the compressor never gets power.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive outcome, often the end of the unit's life.
10. **Blocked condensate drain** tripping a safety float switch that shuts the system off.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are genuinely safe for a homeowner — no tools beyond your hands and no electrical work.

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling and frozen coils.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, fan to **AUTO**, and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it has them.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker (air conditioners usually have **two** — one for the indoor air handler, one for the outdoor unit). Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris away from the condenser. Gently rinse visible dirt off the fins with a garden hose (power off first).
- **Condensate drain:** If you see water near the indoor unit or a full drain pan, the safety float switch may have shut the system down. Drop in a drain tablet, but a fully clogged line needs a pro.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to **OFF** and run the fan **ON** to thaw it. Then replace the filter. If it re-freezes, you likely have a refrigerant or airflow problem requiring a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good American Standard tech follows a logical order so you can sanity-check the work:

1. **Confirms the complaint** and checks thermostat call, mode, and indoor airflow.
2. **Measures supply vs. return air temperature** (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
3. **Inspects the filter and coils** for restriction or ice.
4. **Checks electrical** — incoming voltage, the capacitor (with a meter), and the contactor.
5. **Attaches refrigerant gauges** and reads pressures plus **superheat/subcooling** to judge the charge. Low charge means a leak hunt — never a simple "top off."
6. **On AccuLink/communicating systems**, plugs into the control to read stored faults and live data.
7. **Tests the compressor** for proper amp draw, locked-rotor, or open windings if everything upstream checks out.

Be wary of any quote that adds refrigerant without explaining where the leak is.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents | Inspect ductwork, blower |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Tripped breaker / failed contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed run/start capacitor | None — call pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Off + fan to thaw, new filter | Find/repair leak, recharge |
| Cooling fades on hot days | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call pro | Leak detection & recharge |
| Outdoor coil filthy | Restricted heat rejection | Rinse fins, power off | Chemical coil cleaning |
| System off, water near unit | Clogged condensate / float switch | Drain tablet, clear standing water | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Nothing runs at all | Thermostat / power fault | Batteries, breaker, mode | Diagnose control & wiring |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Failing compressor | None — call pro | Compressor test/replace |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; your market and unit may vary):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY).
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$300.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ (depends on leak location and refrigerant type — older R-22 units cost far more).
- **Fan motor replacement:** $400–$700.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+. On an older unit, replacing the whole system is often the smarter spend.

A standard diagnostic service call typically runs **$80–$180**, often credited toward the repair.
