## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Your American Standard air conditioner is powered on and may even be running, but the air at your vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Unlike furnaces, most American Standard ACs don't flash a numbered error for "no cooling." On **communicating systems** (Platinum series with an AccuLink or a communicating thermostat), you may see a coded fault or alert on the thermostat screen pointing to airflow, refrigerant, or compressor issues. On **non-communicating Gold and Silver systems**, there's usually no display at all — you diagnose by behavior. We'll note where this varies below.

The root issue is almost always one of three things: airflow is blocked, the system can't move heat (refrigerant or compressor problem), or an electrical component that drives the fan/compressor has failed.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode (not set to COOL), or a setpoint above room temp.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power, so the indoor fan blows room-temperature air.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure. The compressor or condenser fan hums but won't start, or the fan won't spin.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer and airflow drops to a trickle.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity; often paired with a freeze-up. Refrigerant doesn't "get used up," so low charge means a leak.
7. **Dirty/blocked outdoor condenser coil** — can't reject heat; cooling fades on hot days.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that energizes the outdoor unit pits or sticks, so the compressor never starts.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — outdoor fan doesn't spin; the unit overheats and shuts down on high pressure.
10. **Compressor failure** — least common but most expensive; the heart of the system won't pump refrigerant.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are genuinely safe for any homeowner — no panels off the live electrical compartment, no refrigerant, no bypassing safety switches.

- **Check the air filter.** If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Check the thermostat.** Set mode to COOL, fan to AUTO, and the setpoint at least 5°F below current room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check the breaker.** Most systems have two: one for the indoor air handler/furnace and one for the outdoor condenser. If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Check vents and registers.** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Look for ice.** If the indoor coil or the copper lines at the outdoor unit are coated in frost/ice, turn the AC OFF and set the fan to ON to thaw it (can take a few hours). Running a frozen system makes things worse.
- **Check the condensate drain.** If the drain pan is full or a float switch has tripped from a clogged line, the system may refuse to cool. You can clear a visible clog at the drain line and add drain tablets.

If none of these restore cooling, it's time for a licensed technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the workflow helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** — confirms the thermostat is sending 24V to the outdoor unit (Y signal). On communicating systems they'll read fault codes from the thermostat/control board.
2. **Check power and the contactor** — measures voltage at the disconnect and tests whether the contactor is pulling in.
3. **Test the capacitor** — measures microfarads against the rating printed on it; a weak cap is a cheap, common find.
4. **Measure system pressures and temperatures** — gauges on the refrigerant lines plus a temperature split across the coil tell them if the charge is correct or there's a leak.
5. **Inspect the coils and airflow** — looks for a dirty condenser, a frozen evaporator, and a restricted filter or blower issue.
6. **Leak search** — if charge is low, a good tech finds the leak (electronic detector or dye) rather than just topping off refrigerant.
7. **Compressor test** — checks windings and amp draw if start components are good but the compressor won't run.

A reputable tech won't just add refrigerant and leave — low charge means a leak that needs to be found.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, vents barely blowing | Clogged filter or frozen coil | Replace filter; turn off to thaw ice | Find why it froze (airflow/charge) |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker or failed contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor, find fault |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed run/start capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Failed condenser fan motor or capacitor | None — call a pro | Replace motor/capacitor |
| Air blows but never cold, hot days worst | Dirty condenser coil or low refrigerant | Rinse exterior coil gently with hose (power off) | Clean coil, check charge, find leak |
| Ice on copper lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Off + fan ON to thaw, swap filter | Diagnose airflow vs. leak |
| System won't cool, water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate line/float switch | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear blockage, reset float switch |
| Thermostat blank or odd reading | Dead batteries or thermostat fault | Replace batteries | Replace/recommission thermostat |
| Nothing cools, compressor silent | Compressor failure | None — call a pro | Test windings, quote replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter:** $0–$30 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5–$15 (DIY)
- **Condensate line cleaning:** $0 DIY – $150 pro
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Coil cleaning (condenser/evaporator):** $150–$450
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,800; often a tipping point toward replacing the outdoor unit, especially on older systems

Note: systems using R-22 cost more to recharge since R-22 is phased out. Newer American Standard units use R-410A or R-454B; pricing varies by region and refrigerant.

## Related codes

- **AC freezing up / ice on coil** — closely linked to airflow and refrigerant issues above.
- **Outdoor unit won't turn on** — overlaps with breaker, contactor, and capacitor faults.
- **AC blowing warm air** — same diagnostic path as "not cooling."
- **Communicating thermostat fault/alert codes** — on Platinum/AccuLink systems, check the thermostat screen for specific airflow, refrigerant, or compressor alerts that narrow the cause.
