## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Your American Standard air conditioner is either running without producing cold air, or not running at all. On **AccuLink communicating systems** (paired with a Platinum thermostat), the system may post a specific fault code or message at the thermostat. On **older single-stage Silver and Gold units**, there is no on-board diagnostic display, so the "code" is simply the behavior you observe.

Because the root cause ranges from a $0 dirty filter to a failed compressor, this page walks you through the safe checks first, then explains how a technician narrows it down — so you can sanity-check any quote you're given.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, can freeze the indoor coil, and kills cooling output. The single most common cause.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "fan" not "cool"), or a setpoint above room temperature.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit or air handler has lost power.
4. **Dirty or blocked condenser coil** — the outdoor unit can't reject heat, so the system runs but barely cools.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — usually downstream of low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the lines.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling and long run times. Refrigerant doesn't get "used up"; if it's low, there's a leak.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit doesn't pull in.
9. **Failed condenser fan or blower motor** — no airflow over a coil means no usable cooling.
10. **Compressor failure** — the least common but most expensive; the system runs but produces no cooling at all.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the outdoor disconnect, touch the capacitor, or handle refrigerant lines.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. Run the system for an hour and recheck cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the fan is on **Auto**, and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Breaker:** Find the AC breakers in your panel (often a pair). If one is tripped, switch it fully OFF then ON — **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro; repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** With the system off, clear leaves, grass, and debris from around and inside the condenser fins. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Condensate:** Check the drain pan and line. A full pan or a tripped float switch can shut the system off. Clearing a visible clog at the outdoor drain end is safe; don't disassemble the air handler.
- **Frozen coil/lines:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system OFF and run the fan only to thaw it (can take a few hours), then retry. If it re-freezes, call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician follows a logical sequence — so a quote that skips straight to "you need a new compressor" without measurements deserves a second opinion.

1. **Verifies the complaint** and checks thermostat call, mode, and setpoint.
2. **Confirms power** at the disconnect, contactor, and capacitor.
3. **Measures the capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarads.
4. **Checks airflow** — filter, blower operation, and coil cleanliness.
5. **Reads the AccuLink fault codes** at the thermostat on communicating systems.
6. **Connects gauges** to read suction and head pressures, then calculates **superheat and subcooling** to judge the refrigerant charge.
7. **Inspects for leaks** with electronic detectors or dye if the charge is low.
8. **Tests the compressor and motors** for proper amp draw and windings.

Expect a diagnostic fee of **$75–$200**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Nothing runs at all | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Find cause of overload |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Runs constantly, barely cools | Dirty condenser or low refrigerant | Clean debris from outdoor unit | Check charge, find leak |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw with fan | Diagnose airflow/charge |
| Indoor fan runs, no cold air | Compressor or contactor fault | None — call pro | Test contactor, compressor |
| System shuts off then won't restart | Full condensate pan / float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Clear line, replace pump/switch |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries / power loss | Replace batteries | Check 24V transformer, wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** under $10 (DIY)
- **Condenser coil cleaning / tune-up:** $100–$250
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$700
- **Blower motor:** $400–$700 (ECM motors run higher)
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **TXV / metering device:** $300–$650
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ — often the point where replacing the condenser or whole system makes more sense, especially on older units

Note: pricing varies by region, refrigerant type (older R-410A vs. newer systems), and whether the unit is still under American Standard's parts warranty.

## Related codes

- **AccuLink communication fault** — thermostat loses contact with the indoor or outdoor unit
- **Open high-pressure / low-pressure switch** — refrigerant pressure outside safe limits
- **Outdoor unit lockout** — repeated faults trigger a protective shutdown; don't reset repeatedly
- **Frozen evaporator coil** — airflow or charge problem causing ice buildup
- **Condensate float switch trip** — full drain pan shutting the system down
