## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a specific fault code on American Standard air conditioners — it is a symptom. Standard single-stage and two-stage units (Silver and Gold series) usually have no display and give no code at all. Communicating systems using an AccuLink control (many Platinum models paired with a Nexia or AccuLink thermostat) may show a fault or "call for cool not met" message on the thermostat.

The condition means the system is either not running when it should be, or it is running but the air coming from your vents is warm, weak, or barely cooler than room temperature. The root causes range from a $10 filter to a failed compressor, so pinpointing which one matters before you approve any repair.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, can freeze the indoor coil, and is the single most common cause of weak cooling.
2. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "fan" or "heat"), or a loose wire so the outdoor unit never gets the call.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit is dead while the indoor blower still runs, so you get airflow but no cooling.
4. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood can't reject heat.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor hums or won't start.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks the coil until it thaws.
7. **Low refrigerant / leak** — causes gradual loss of cooling and often coil freeze-up. Requires a certified tech.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor or blower motor** — no airflow across a coil.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive outcome; the unit runs but produces no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is beyond them.

- **Check the air filter.** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of "weak cooling" calls.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to **Cool** and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check the breakers.** Look for a tripped breaker for both the indoor air handler and the outdoor condenser. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated trips signal an electrical fault.
- **Check your vents and registers.** Make sure supply and return vents are open and not blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed doors.
- **Check for ice.** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **Fan Only** for a few hours to let it thaw, then run cooling again. Recurring ice means a real problem.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Clear standing water from the pan if you can safely reach it.
- **Rinse the outdoor coil.** With the unit off at the breaker, gently rinse the outdoor coil from the outside with a garden hose (no pressure washer).

If cooling doesn't return after these, it's time for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical order — this helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirm the complaint** and measure supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 15–20°F).
2. **Verify the call for cooling** reaches the outdoor unit with a meter — checking the thermostat, low-voltage wiring, and control board.
3. **Test the capacitor** with a meter for correct microfarad (µF) rating.
4. **Test the contactor** for proper pull-in and clean contacts.
5. **Check the fan and compressor** for amp draw against nameplate specs.
6. **Attach refrigerant gauges** and read pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge the charge and look for a leak.
7. **On communicating systems**, pull fault history from the AccuLink control or thermostat.

Be cautious of any quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" or "replace the compressor" without these measurements.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Check duct/blower if it persists |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker or thermostat | Reset breaker once, check thermostat | Test contactor, wiring, control board |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Run fan to thaw, replace filter | Find leak, recharge, check airflow |
| Gradual loss of cooling | Low refrigerant / leak | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Failed fan motor or capacitor | None | Replace motor/capacitor |
| Runs constantly, no cooling | Dirty condenser or failed compressor | Rinse outdoor coil | Measure pressures, test compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$300
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Blower motor:** $400–$900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,200+ (often worth weighing against a full system replacement on older units)

Diagnostic fees of $80–$200 are typical and often credited toward the repair.
