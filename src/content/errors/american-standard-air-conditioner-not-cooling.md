## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a single fault code on American Standard equipment — it is a symptom. Your air conditioner may be running, blowing air, and showing no error at the thermostat, yet the air coming out of the vents is not cold. On systems with a communicating thermostat (like the AccuLink/Trane ComfortLink II platform American Standard shares), you may see a generic fault or "cooling demand not met" message, but most single- and two-stage systems give no specific code for this condition.

Because cooling depends on the indoor blower, the outdoor condenser, a sealed refrigerant charge, and electrical components all working together, "not cooling" can come from many places. The checks below help you rule out the simple causes before paying for a service call.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, can freeze the indoor coil, and is the single most common cause of weak or no cooling.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "fan" or "heat"), or a tripped/incorrect program schedule.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit has its own breaker; if it trips, the indoor fan still runs but no cold air is produced.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure that prevents the compressor or condenser fan from starting; the outdoor unit may hum or sit idle.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — caked dirt, grass, or cottonwood on the outdoor coil chokes heat rejection and kills cooling capacity.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling and can freeze the coil; requires EPA-certified repair.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit can pit or stick, leaving the condenser dead.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the compressor runs but overheats and shuts down without airflow over the coil.
9. **Frozen evaporator coil** — a result of low airflow or low charge; blocks cooling until thawed.
10. **Compressor failure** — least common but most expensive; the unit runs but never cools.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening the sealed system or bypassing a safety device.

- **Replace the air filter.** A dirty filter is the most common and cheapest fix. Use the correct size and a MERV rating your system supports.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to COOL, the temperature is below room temperature, and the fan is on AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check your breakers.** Look for a tripped breaker for both the indoor air handler/furnace and the outdoor condenser. Reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Look at the outdoor unit.** Make sure the fan is spinning and the coil isn't blocked by leaves, grass, or debris. Gently clear vegetation, keeping at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Inspect supply and return vents.** Open closed registers and remove furniture or rugs blocking returns.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A full drain pan or a clogged line can trigger a float switch that disables cooling. If the pan is overflowing, the line may need clearing.
- **Look for ice.** If you see frost or ice on the lines or indoor coil, turn the system OFF (set thermostat to fan only) and let it thaw for several hours, then replace the filter before restarting.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass safety switches, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified HVAC technician will typically:

- Confirm the thermostat is calling for cooling and the indoor blower is running.
- Measure voltage at the contactor and condenser to verify the outdoor unit is getting power.
- Test the run/start capacitor with a meter for its rated microfarads.
- Inspect the contactor for pitting and check that it pulls in on a cooling call.
- Connect refrigerant gauges to read suction and discharge pressures, calculating superheat and subcooling to determine if the charge is correct.
- Check temperature split across the indoor coil (typical target is roughly 16–22°F drop between return and supply air).
- Inspect the condenser fan motor, coil cleanliness, and compressor amp draw.
- On communicating systems, pull fault history from the AccuLink/ComfortLink thermostat.

If a quote jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without mentioning a leak search, ask questions — topping off a leaking system is a temporary fix, not a repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect ductwork and coil |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor |
| Outdoor unit hums, fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None (electrical) | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, change filter | Check charge, find leak |
| Outdoor coil caked in dirt | Dirty condenser coil | Clear vegetation, rinse gently | Professional coil cleaning |
| Compressor runs, never cools | Low refrigerant / leak | None | Leak detection and repair |
| Unit cycles on and off rapidly | Overheating, bad capacitor, low charge | Check filter | Diagnose electrical/refrigerant |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries or wiring | Replace batteries | Check thermostat and wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, including parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,200 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ (often worth weighing against system replacement on older units)

Note: R-410A pricing varies, and older systems using R-22 refrigerant are far more expensive to recharge due to phase-out. Costs vary by region and accessibility.

## Related codes

- **AC freezing up / frozen coil** — closely related; usually airflow or charge.
- **Outdoor unit not turning on** — contactor or capacitor failures.
- **AccuLink / ComfortLink communication errors** — on communicating thermostats, can interrupt cooling demand.
- **AC short cycling** — overlapping electrical and refrigerant causes.
