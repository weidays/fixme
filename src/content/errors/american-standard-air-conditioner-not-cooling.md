## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a stored fault code — American Standard air conditioners typically don't flash a specific blink code for "no cooling" the way a furnace board does. Instead, you notice the indoor air stays warm even though the system is set to cool.

The condenser (outdoor unit) and air handler/furnace blower must work together: the outdoor compressor and fan move heat outside, while the indoor blower pushes cooled air through your home. If any part of that loop fails — airflow, electrical, or refrigerant — you lose cooling.

On communicating American Standard systems with an **AccuLink / Platinum 1050 thermostat**, the thermostat *may* display a numbered fault or alert. The exact alert text varies by control board and firmware, so read what's actually on your screen rather than assuming.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, can freeze the indoor coil and cut cooling.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (heat vs cool), or wrong settings.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit, air handler, or both have lost power.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — common; the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start.
5. **Frozen indoor evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the lineset.
6. **Low refrigerant / leak** — cooling weak or absent; almost always indicates a leak (never just "low").
7. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — blocks heat rejection so the system can't cool effectively.
8. **Stuck or burned contactor** — the relay that powers the compressor fails to close.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — outdoor fan doesn't spin; unit overheats and shuts down.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; unit runs but moves no heat.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening a sealed electrical panel or touching refrigerant.

- **Filter:** Pull and inspect your air filter. If it's gray or clogged, replace it. This alone fixes many "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the temperature is below room temp. Replace the batteries if your model uses them.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for tripped breakers serving the AC and air handler. Reset **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate:** Check the condensate drain pan and float switch. A full pan or tripped float switch can shut down cooling. Clear standing water if accessible.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser so it can breathe. Look (don't touch) for ice on the copper lines — ice means you should shut the system off and let it thaw, then call a pro.

Do **not** open the outdoor disconnect, touch the capacitor, add refrigerant, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical path so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** — confirms the thermostat is sending the signal and 24V control voltage is present.
2. **Check airflow** — inspects filter, blower operation, and the evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
3. **Test electrical components** — measures the capacitor with a meter, checks the contactor, and confirms the compressor and fan motor draw correct amperage.
4. **Read refrigerant pressures** — connects gauges to check suction and head pressure and calculate superheat/subcooling.
5. **Leak search** — if refrigerant is low, they use an electronic detector or dye to find the leak before recharging.
6. **Inspect the condenser coil and fan** — confirms heat rejection is working.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search is a red flag — low refrigerant always means a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Check coil/blower if no improvement |
| Indoor unit blows, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker / contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor, check capacitor |
| Outdoor fan spins, no cooling | Bad capacitor or compressor | None | Test capacitor, check compressor windings |
| Ice on copper lines or coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, let thaw, replace filter | Find leak, recharge, verify airflow |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed capacitor | None | Replace dual run capacitor |
| Outdoor coil caked with dirt | Dirty condenser | Gently rinse fins (power off) | Deep coil cleaning, verify pressures |
| Water near indoor unit, system off | Full pan / float switch | Clear drain if accessible | Clear blockage, test float switch |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries / settings | Replace batteries, set to COOL | Replace thermostat, check wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10 – $40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150 – $400
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300 – $650
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150 – $400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $600 – $1,600 (varies with leak location and refrigerant type)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000 – $2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500 – $2,500+ (often makes replacing the unit worth considering)

Prices vary by region, refrigerant type (R-410A vs newer A2L blends), and whether parts are under American Standard's warranty. Always confirm warranty coverage before approving major repairs.
