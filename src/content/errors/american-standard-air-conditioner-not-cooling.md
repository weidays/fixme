## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code — it's the symptom you'll describe when your American Standard air conditioner runs but the house stays warm, or won't blow cold air at all. American Standard split systems generally don't display alphanumeric error codes at the condenser. On AccuLink-equipped systems with a communicating thermostat, you may see a system message or fault indication on the thermostat screen, but most single- and two-stage units give you no on-unit display.

Because "not cooling" can stem from airflow, electrical, refrigerant, or control problems, this page walks you through the safe checks you can do yourself and the diagnostics a technician should perform — so you can sanity-check any quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the condenser (outdoor unit) and air handler are often on separate circuits.
3. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "heat" or "fan only"), or a programming/scheduling problem.
4. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil packed with grass, dust, or cottonwood can't reject heat.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that sends power to the compressor; pitted or stuck contacts stop the unit.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, ice on the lines, and long run times.
8. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit blows warm while iced over.
9. **Clogged condensate drain / float switch tripped** — a safety switch cuts cooling to prevent water damage.
10. **Failed compressor or fan motor** — the most expensive outcome, usually after other faults go unaddressed.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is hot, smells burnt, or sparks.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, with the setpoint below room temperature and the fan on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for tripped breakers serving the **indoor air handler** and **outdoor condenser**. Reset once. If a breaker trips again immediately, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse external dirt off the outer fins with a garden hose (power off) — do not pressure-wash or bend the fins.
- **Condensate drain:** If you have a drain pan with standing water or a tripped float switch, clear visible clogs and pour cleaning tablets in the drain. A wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain line end can pull a clog.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to **OFF** and the fan to **ON** for an hour or two to thaw it before running cooling again. Persistent freezing means a real fault — call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech follows a logical sequence. Knowing it helps you judge a quote:

1. **Confirms the complaint** and checks thermostat call, mode, and wiring at the air handler.
2. **Measures airflow and inspects the indoor coil** for icing or dirt.
3. **Checks electrical** at the condenser: capacitor microfarad reading, contactor condition, voltage, and amp draw on the compressor and fan motor.
4. **Connects gauges** to read suction and head pressures, then calculates **superheat and subcooling** to judge the refrigerant charge — without venting refrigerant.
5. **If charge is low, performs leak detection** (electronic sniffer, dye, or nitrogen pressure test) rather than just adding refrigerant.
6. **On AccuLink/communicating systems,** retrieves fault history from the thermostat and verifies control communication between indoor and outdoor units.

A good tech explains *why* a part failed (e.g., a leak caused the freeze) — not just what to replace.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs but air is barely cool | Dirty filter / dirty condenser coil | Replace filter, rinse outdoor coil | Deep coil clean, airflow & charge check |
| Outdoor unit silent, no fan | Tripped breaker, bad capacitor or contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace capacitor or contactor |
| Compressor hums then stops | Failed run capacitor | None | Replace capacitor, verify amp draw |
| Ice on lines, blows warm | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Cools then quits, water near unit | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear line, test float switch |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries, wrong setting | Replace batteries, set to COOL/AUTO | Wiring/control board check |
| Long run times, high bills | Low charge, dirty coil, undersized airflow | Filter & coil cleaning | Charge verification, leak test |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Electrical fault or seized compressor | Stop resetting, call pro | Diagnose short, motor, or compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined. Your price varies by region and system size.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150–$300 pro
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$300
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Coil deep cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $600–$1,800 (depends on leak location)
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,800
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth comparing to a full system replacement)

If your unit is under American Standard's parts warranty (typically 10 years with registration), the part may be covered but labor usually isn't — confirm with your dealer.
