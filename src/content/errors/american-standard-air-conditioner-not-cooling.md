## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single blink code — it's the symptom you see when your American Standard air conditioner runs (or tries to run) but the air coming from your vents isn't cold, or the house just won't reach the setpoint. On older Silver and Gold series units this shows up as warm air despite a running outdoor unit, while Platinum variable-speed systems with an AccuLink/Link thermostat may display a specific fault or "system fault" message on the wall control.

Because cooling depends on a chain of components — thermostat, indoor blower, refrigerant charge, compressor, and clear coils — "not cooling" can have a cheap cause or a very expensive one. The checks below help you rule out the easy stuff before paying for a service call.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, reduces cooling, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a programming/wiring fault telling the system not to call for cooling.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser or indoor air handler has lost power.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — weak or no cooling, often with hissing or ice on the lines.
6. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan motor won't start; you may hear humming.
7. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor unit can't reject heat, so cooling drops, especially on hot days.
8. **Failed contactor or control board** — no signal reaching the compressor.
9. **Failing compressor** — the heart of the system; the most expensive failure.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are homeowner-safe. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or touch capacitors.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Set the fan to AUTO. If it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank, replace the batteries.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling and frozen coils.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for tripped breakers serving the indoor air handler and outdoor condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and returns:** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside fins with a garden hose (power off).
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system OFF and set the fan to ON to thaw it (can take several hours). Running an iced unit causes damage.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Check the drain pan for standing water; a wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain line can clear minor clogs.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good American Standard tech will work the cooling chain in order, and you can sanity-check their steps:

1. **Confirm the call for cooling** — verify thermostat signal reaches the air handler and condenser.
2. **Check airflow** — inspect filter, blower, and coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Measure electrical** — test the capacitor (microfarads), contactor, and incoming voltage.
4. **Take refrigerant readings** — connect gauges and measure superheat/subcooling to confirm proper charge. Low charge means a leak search.
5. **Inspect the compressor** — check amp draw and whether it starts and runs.
6. **On Platinum/variable-speed units** — pull fault history from the AccuLink/Link thermostat or control board.

If a tech quotes a compressor or refrigerant job without ever putting gauges on the system or checking the capacitor first, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, system running | Dirty filter / low charge | Replace filter; check vents | Measure charge, find leak |
| Outdoor unit silent, no air inside | Tripped breaker / lost power | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, board, wiring |
| Humming but fan/compressor won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Diagnose airflow/charge |
| Weak cooling on hot days | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse outdoor fins (power off) | Deep coil clean, charge check |
| Loud hum then breaker trips | Compressor/electrical fault | Leave off | Compressor & electrical test |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries / no power | Replace batteries | Check wiring/transformer |
| Standing water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain line | Clear blockage, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (regional and model variation applies):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Control board (common on Platinum/variable-speed):** $400–$900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,800+ (often makes replacing the unit worth considering on older systems)

A simple service call/diagnostic typically runs $80–$180, often credited toward the repair.

## Related codes

- **Outdoor unit ices over** — usually low refrigerant or airflow restriction.
- **AC blowing warm air** — overlaps heavily with this issue; same diagnostic path.
- **Condenser fan not spinning** — capacitor or motor fault.
- **AccuLink/Link "System Fault"** — pull the specific code from the thermostat on Platinum series units.
- **AC trips breaker** — often a failing compressor or electrical short.
