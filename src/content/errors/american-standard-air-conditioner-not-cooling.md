## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Your American Standard air conditioner may be running, blowing air, and showing no error, yet the air coming from your vents is warm or only mildly cool. On communicating systems with an AccuLink thermostat, you may instead see a fault flagged at the thermostat or a blinking LED on the outdoor or indoor control board.

The root issue almost always comes down to one of four things: **restricted airflow, low refrigerant, an electrical/component failure, or a control problem**. Because diagnosing low cooling capacity safely requires gauges and electrical testing, this is rated a **pro-level** repair once the basic homeowner checks are exhausted.

Exact LED blink patterns and fault behavior vary by model and control board, so always cross-reference the diagnostic label inside your unit's access panel.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked return** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops cooling output and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat and cooling collapses.
3. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer, so you get warm air despite the unit running.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, often with ice on the lines. Requires a pro to find and seal the leak, then recharge.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a cheap part whose failure stops the compressor or fan from starting, often with a humming or clicking sound.
6. **Failed contactor** — burned or pitted contacts prevent the outdoor unit from energizing.
7. **Thermostat or wiring fault** — wrong mode, dead batteries, miscalibration, or a broken low-voltage wire.
8. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power.
9. **Failing compressor** — the most expensive failure; the unit hums but won't cool, or trips the breaker.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is hot, smells burnt, or sparks.

- **Air filter** — pull it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Swap the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers** — check the panel for both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) circuits. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers** — make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit** — clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Gently rinse exterior fins with a garden hose (power off) if visibly dirty.
- **Condensate drain** — if your indoor unit has a float switch and the drain is clogged, the system may shut off cooling. Look for standing water in the drain pan and clear visible blockages.
- **Ice check** — if you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to **OFF** and run the fan on **ON** to thaw it (can take several hours), then call a pro — ice means a deeper problem.

Do **not** open sealed refrigerant lines, bypass safety switches, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measures supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Tests electrical** — measures voltage and amperage at the contactor and compressor; tests the capacitor with a meter (not by eye).
4. **Connects gauges** — reads suction and head pressures and superheat/subcooling to determine if refrigerant is correct, low (leak), or overcharged.
5. **Leak search** — if low, uses electronic detector or dye to locate the leak before any recharge.
6. **Checks controls** — on communicating systems, reads fault history from the AccuLink thermostat or board.
7. **Compressor test** — checks windings and start behavior if a compressor failure is suspected.

A trustworthy quote names the failed part and the test result that proves it — not just "needs refrigerant."

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked return | Replace filter, open vents | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Diagnose airflow/refrigerant cause |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Tripped breaker / contactor / capacitor | Reset breaker once | Test & replace contactor/capacitor |
| Humming, no start | Bad capacitor or seized compressor | None — call a pro | Test capacitor, then compressor |
| Gradual loss of cooling over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None — call a pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Dirty/clogged outdoor coil | Debris buildup | Rinse fins (power off) | Professional coil cleaning |
| No response, blank thermostat | Dead batteries / wiring | Replace batteries | Test low-voltage wiring & board |
| Water around indoor unit, AC shuts off | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible blockage | Clear line, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries** — $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing** — $0 DIY to $75–$200 pro
- **Capacitor replacement** — $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement** — $150–$350
- **Outdoor coil cleaning** — $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge** — $600–$1,800 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Evaporator coil replacement** — $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement** — $1,500–$2,800+ (often worth weighing against a new system on older units)

Note: systems using R-410A vs. newer low-GWP refrigerants will see different recharge costs; ask your tech which yours uses.
