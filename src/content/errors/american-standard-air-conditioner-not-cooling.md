## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's the symptom American Standard homeowners report most. Your air conditioner may be running, blowing air, or sitting silent, but the house isn't getting cooler. On units with a communicating thermostat (like the AccuLink/ComfortLink II platform), you may see an actual fault or status message; on older or single-stage systems, you'll just notice warm air.

Because American Standard's diagnostic behavior **varies by model and board** — variable-speed Platinum systems report far more detail than a basic single-stage Silver condenser — this page covers the underlying causes rather than one specific code. If your thermostat shows a numbered alert, note it down for your technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause. A clogged filter starves the system, ices the indoor coil, and stops cooling.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit (condenser) and indoor blower are often on separate circuits. One can run while the other is dead.
3. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "fan" not "cool"), or a too-high setpoint.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common condenser failure. The fan or compressor hums but won't start, or the outdoor unit is silent.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant line.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system runs constantly but barely cools. Requires a certified tech.
7. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, and debris block heat rejection, killing efficiency.
8. **Failed contactor or control board** — the relay that energizes the condenser fails to close.
9. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; the system runs but produces no cooling at all.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only steps a homeowner should attempt. If any of them don't resolve the issue, call a licensed HVAC technician.

- **Check the air filter.** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of "not cooling" calls.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace the batteries if it's a battery-powered model.
- **Check the breakers.** Find the breakers for the air handler/furnace and the outdoor condenser. If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again immediately, stop and call a pro.
- **Look at your vents and registers.** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Inspect the outdoor unit.** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around and on top of the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A full drain pan or a clogged line can trip a safety float switch that shuts the system off. You can clear visible standing water and pour drain tablets into the line — but don't disassemble the float switch.

**Do not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps shutting down.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician follows a logical sequence, and knowing it helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measures supply and return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 15–20°F).
2. **Checks airflow** — inspects the filter, blower, and evaporator coil for dirt or ice.
3. **Electrical testing** — uses a multimeter to test the capacitor (microfarad reading vs. its rating), the contactor, and the control board for proper signals.
4. **Reads system pressures** — connects gauges to the refrigerant ports to check for low charge, which points to a leak.
5. **Leak search** — if refrigerant is low, uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak rather than just refilling.
6. **Compressor check** — tests amperage draw and windings if the compressor is suspected.
7. **Communicating-system diagnostics** — on ComfortLink II/AccuLink systems, pulls fault history from the thermostat to confirm the board's reported errors.

If a tech quotes a compressor or refrigerant repair **without** gauge readings and a leak search, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter or low refrigerant | Replace filter, check vents | Test charge, find leak, repair |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Tripped breaker or failed capacitor | Reset breaker once | Replace capacitor/contactor |
| Ice on indoor coil or line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off AC, run fan to thaw, replace filter | Diagnose charge/airflow cause |
| System short-cycles or won't stay on | Clogged drain / tripped float switch | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear blockage, test switch |
| Outdoor fan won't spin | Failed capacitor or motor | None — leave it off | Replace capacitor or fan motor |
| Runs constantly, barely cools | Dirty condenser coil or low charge | Clean debris from unit | Coil clean or charge/leak repair |
| Nothing runs at all | Power, board, or thermostat | Check breaker & thermostat | Test board, transformer, wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges for American Standard repairs (parts + labor; regional and model variation applies):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Blower or condenser fan motor:** $300–$700
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Control board (communicating systems):** $400–$900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth weighing against full unit replacement on older systems)

Capacitors fail often and are inexpensive, so always rule that out before agreeing to a major repair.

## Related codes

- **American Standard AC frozen coil** — when low airflow or refrigerant ices the evaporator.
- **American Standard outdoor fan not spinning** — usually a capacitor or fan motor.
- **American Standard ComfortLink II communication error** — board-to-thermostat fault on communicating systems.
- **American Standard AC tripping breaker** — points to a short, hard-starting compressor, or failed component.
