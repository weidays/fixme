## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a fault code — American Standard air conditioners generally do not flash a single dedicated "not cooling" code. Instead, the system runs but the air coming from your vents is warm, weak, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

On communicating systems (variable-speed AccuComfort units with a matched American Standard thermostat or air handler control board), you may see a fault message or alert on the thermostat — for example a comfort sensor, communication, or compressor fault. On older single-stage and two-stage models, there is no display, so diagnosis relies on observed behavior and technician measurements.

Because "not cooling" can stem from airflow, electrical, control, or refrigerant problems, this is ultimately a **pro-level** diagnosis once the basic safe checks below are ruled out.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause; restricts airflow and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked with grass, dust, or pollen, so heat can't be rejected.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** to the outdoor unit, leaving the blower running but the compressor off.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — compressor or condenser fan won't start; very common on aging units.
5. **Burned or stuck contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit fails to close.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling and often a frozen coil. Always a leak, never normal "use."
7. **Frozen evaporator coil** — a result of low airflow or low refrigerant; blows warm until thawed.
8. **Thermostat problem** — dead batteries, wrong mode/setting, or a communicating thermostat fault.
9. **Clogged condensate drain** with a safety float switch tripped, shutting cooling off.
10. **Failed compressor** — least common but most expensive; usually accompanied by hard-start humming or breaker trips.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here and call a pro if cooling doesn't return.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it. If it's gray or clogged, replace it with the correct size and try the system again.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it uses them. On communicating thermostats, note any on-screen alert text to share with the tech.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC or air-handler breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop — that indicates an electrical fault for a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Make sure nothing is leaning against it. Do not spray it while powered or remove panels.
- **Condensate drain:** If you have a drain pan with standing water, the float switch may have shut cooling off. You can carefully empty an overflowing pan, but a clogged drain line needs a pro.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or outdoor lines, turn the thermostat to **OFF**, set the fan to **ON**, and let it thaw for several hours before running it again.

Do **not** open sealed refrigerant lines, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical path so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** and check thermostat settings, mode, and any communicating fault codes.
2. **Confirm airflow** — inspect filter, blower operation, and indoor coil for ice or dirt.
3. **Check electrical** — measure voltage at the contactor, test the capacitor with a meter, and inspect the contactor for pitting or chatter.
4. **Measure temperature split** — the difference between return and supply air (typically 15–22°F when working).
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** and read suction/head pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge the charge.
6. **Leak search** if refrigerant is low — with electronic detector or dye, since topping off without repair is improper.
7. **Compressor test** — check amp draw, windings, and start behavior if it won't run.

Ask the tech to show you readings (capacitor microfarads, subcooling, temperature split). A quote for "more refrigerant" without a leak search is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Outdoor unit silent, blower runs | Tripped breaker, bad contactor/capacitor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor or capacitor |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find leak, check charge, repair |
| Cooling cuts out, water in pan | Clogged drain, tripped float switch | Empty pan if overflowing | Clear drain line, test float |
| Outdoor fan hums but won't spin | Failed capacitor | None | Replace capacitor |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Low refrigerant or failing compressor | None | Pressure test, leak search, compressor test |
| No response at all | Thermostat or control board | Check batteries/mode | Test thermostat, control board, wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor. Local rates vary widely.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Service/diagnostic call:** $80–$200.
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250.
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$500 (communicating thermostats cost more).
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$2,000+ depending on leak location.
- **Outdoor fan motor:** $400–$700.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ — often the point where replacing the condenser or system is worth pricing.

If your unit is under American Standard's registered limited warranty, covered parts may reduce these costs significantly — ask the installer to verify warranty status by serial number.

## Related codes

- **Frozen evaporator coil** — closely tied to "not cooling"; usually airflow or charge.
- **Communicating system fault / comfort sensor alerts** — on AccuComfort variable-speed thermostats.
- **Compressor lockout / high-pressure trip** — protective shutdowns that present as no cooling.
- **Low refrigerant / charge fault** — on communicating boards that monitor system pressures.

Behavior and on-screen alerts differ between single-stage, two-stage, and variable-speed models — confirm your exact model and thermostat before assuming any specific code.
