## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a stored fault code on most American Standard air conditioners. Your system is powered and may be running, but the air coming from the vents isn't cold enough — or the home never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Standard single-stage and two-stage American Standard condensers (Platinum, Gold, Silver series) don't have a display screen, so there's no on-unit code to read. Variable-speed **AccuComfort** systems paired with a communicating control (like the AccuLink platform) *can* log fault codes on the indoor unit or thermostat — if you see a specific code there, look that code up rather than treating this as generic.

The job here is to figure out *why* cooling has dropped: airflow, refrigerant charge, electrical components, or the compressor itself.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops capacity and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass clippings, cottonwood, and dust insulate the coil so it can't reject heat.
3. **Frozen evaporator (indoor) coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant. You'll often see ice on the copper lines or no airflow at all.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common electrical failure that prevents the compressor or fan motor from starting.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — undercharged systems cool poorly and ice up. This is *always* a leak, never normal "loss."
6. **Tripped breaker or blown outdoor disconnect fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power while the indoor blower still runs.
7. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a wiring/communication fault (especially on AccuLink systems).
8. **Failed contactor or fan motor** — the outdoor fan or compressor won't engage.
9. **Compressor failure** — least common but most expensive; the unit hums or trips but won't cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here and call a pro for anything beyond this list.

- **Filter:** Pull and inspect the filter. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check the electrical panel for a tripped breaker. You may reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a technician — do not keep resetting it.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Gently rinse leaves, grass, and debris off the outside of the condenser coil with a garden hose (power off). Clear at least 2 feet of space around the unit.
- **Condensate drain:** If the unit shut off and you see water near the indoor coil, the drain may be clogged and tripped a float switch. You can clear a visible clog and add drain tablets.
- **Ice on the lines:** If you see ice, turn the system to **OFF** (fan ON is okay) and let it thaw fully before running again. Persistent icing means low refrigerant or airflow — call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence, not guesswork. Use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measures supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Confirms airflow** — checks filter, blower operation, and coil condition.
3. **Checks electrical** — tests the capacitor with a meter, inspects the contactor, and confirms the outdoor unit gets line voltage.
4. **Attaches gauges** — reads suction and liquid pressures and measures superheat/subcooling to judge the refrigerant charge.
5. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses electronic detector or dye to find the leak before adding refrigerant.
6. **Compressor test** — checks amp draw and windings if the compressor won't start.

⚠️ A tech who just "tops off the refrigerant" without finding the leak is selling you a temporary fix. Always ask where the leak is.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower & ductwork |
| Outdoor fan runs, no cold air | Failed capacitor or contactor | None — safe checks only | Test/replace capacitor or contactor |
| Ice on copper lines | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Indoor blower runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker/blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Diagnose electrical fault |
| Unit hums but won't start | Capacitor or compressor | None | Test compressor & start components |
| System shut off, water present | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear line, check float switch |
| Dirty/clogged outdoor coil | Debris buildup | Rinse coil with hose (power off) | Professional coil cleaning |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries or comms fault | Replace batteries | Check wiring / AccuLink board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 service call
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$300
- **Outdoor fan motor:** $300–$600
- **Coil cleaning (professional):** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing against a full system replacement on older units)

Diagnostic/service call fees typically run $80–$180 and are often credited toward the repair.

## Related codes

- **AC frozen / iced over** — overlapping causes (airflow and refrigerant).
- **Outdoor unit not turning on** — electrical and contactor faults.
- **AccuLink / AccuComfort fault codes** — if your communicating thermostat shows a numeric code, look up that specific code.
- **Condensate overflow / float switch trip** — when a clogged drain shuts the system down.
