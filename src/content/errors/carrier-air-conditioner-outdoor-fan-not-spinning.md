## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numeric fault code — it's a physical symptom you can see and hear at your Carrier condenser (the big outdoor unit). The compressor may be running or humming, but the large fan on top sits still. That fan pulls heat out of the refrigerant; without it, the system can't reject heat, head pressure climbs fast, and the compressor overheats.

Most Carrier residential condensers use a single-phase PSC (permanent split capacitor) fan motor driven by a dual run capacitor and switched by a contactor. On Infinity and Greenspeed variable-speed units, the fan is controlled by an ECM motor module instead, so the failure points differ — we note that where it matters.

This is rated **pro** severity: a non-spinning fan with a running compressor will damage the compressor if left on, and the repair involves high-voltage components and a charged capacitor that holds a dangerous shock even after power is off.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — The single most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan motor its starting torque. When it weakens, the fan won't start (you'll often hear humming) or starts only with a manual push.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — Worn bearings, overheating, or age cause the motor to lock up or fail open.
3. **Contactor not pulling in** — A pitted, stuck, or coil-failed contactor cuts power to both the fan and compressor, or chatters.
4. **Tripped or failed compressor/fan overload** — Thermal protection has shut the fan down due to overheating, often a symptom of another problem.
5. **Wiring/connection fault** — Loose, corroded, or burned spade connectors at the capacitor or motor.
6. **Debris or ice jamming the blade** — Sticks, grass, or a bent blade physically blocking rotation.
7. **ECM module failure (Infinity/Greenspeed only)** — Variable-speed fan motors fail differently and require a control-board or module diagnosis, not a capacitor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without opening the high-voltage compartment:

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature so there's an active call for cooling.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor disconnect/breaker. Reset a tripped breaker once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and odd behavior; replace if dirty.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Near the condenser is a pull-out disconnect box — make sure it's fully seated.
- **Visible blade obstruction:** With the system **off at the breaker**, look through the top grille for sticks, leaves, or ice on the blade. Remove loose debris by hand. Do **not** reach in with power on.
- **Indoor coil ice:** If the indoor coil or refrigerant lines are frosted, turn the system to FAN-only to let it thaw and see our Frozen Evaporator Coil guide.

Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser, touch the capacitor, or "push-start" the fan unless you understand capacitor discharge — the capacitor stores a lethal charge even with power off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Visual and mechanical check** — Spins the fan by hand (power off) to feel for seized bearings or a free-spinning blade.
2. **Capacitor test** — Discharges and measures the capacitor's microfarad (µF) rating against the nameplate. A reading more than ~10% low confirms a weak cap.
3. **Voltage checks** — Confirms 24V at the contactor coil during a cooling call and line voltage to the motor when the contactor pulls in.
4. **Contactor inspection** — Looks for pitted contacts, chatter, or a dead coil.
5. **Motor windings test** — Checks the motor for open windings, shorts, or a grounded winding with a meter.
6. **ECM diagnosis (variable-speed units)** — Pulls fault data from the control board and tests the ECM module per Carrier's procedure.

If a tech wants to replace the motor, they should first prove the capacitor is good — replacing only the motor while leaving a weak cap will burn out the new motor.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor humming, fan still | Failed run capacitor | Shut off at thermostat; do not run | Discharge & replace capacitor, verify µF |
| Fan starts only with a hand push | Weak capacitor | Turn off; schedule service | Replace dual run capacitor |
| No fan, no compressor, no hum | Contactor or power loss | Check breaker/disconnect once | Test/replace contactor, check 24V |
| Fan won't turn even by hand | Seized fan motor | Power off; don't force it | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Fan blade blocked by debris/ice | Obstruction | Remove loose debris (power off) | Clear/repair blade, find ice cause |
| Burning smell at unit | Overheated motor/wiring | Shut off immediately, call pro | Replace motor and damaged wiring |
| Variable-speed fan dead, board light blinking | ECM module fault | Note any board codes | Diagnose ECM/control board |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US installed prices including parts and labor; your area and unit may vary.

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $300 (part is cheap; most of the cost is the service call)
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC):** $300 – $650, usually with a new capacitor included
- **ECM fan module (Infinity/Greenspeed):** $500 – $1,200+ depending on part availability
- **Wiring/connector repair:** $100 – $250
- **Diagnostic/service call alone:** $90 – $180, often credited toward the repair

A DIY capacitor swap can cost under $30 in parts, but only attempt it if you know how to safely discharge a capacitor and de-energize the unit. The shock risk makes this a pro job for most homeowners.
