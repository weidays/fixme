## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is not a fault code — most Carrier air conditioners don't display a code for this. It's a physical symptom: the compressor may be humming or the unit may be running, but the fan blade on top of the outdoor condenser unit isn't turning.

The outdoor (condenser) fan pulls air across the condenser coil to reject the heat your system pulled out of the house. When it stops, refrigerant pressure and temperature spike within minutes. The compressor can overheat, trip on internal protection, or suffer permanent damage. This is a **stop-using-it** situation until it's fixed.

On **Infinity variable-speed systems**, the ECM fan is controlled and monitored by the Infinity control board, and a fan problem there may show a fault on the wall controller. On standard **Comfort and Performance** single-stage units, there's no diagnostic display outside — you diagnose by observation and measurement.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor.** The most common cause by far. The dual run capacitor provides starting torque to the fan motor (and compressor). When it weakens, the fan hums but won't start, or only spins after a manual push.
2. **Failed condenser fan motor.** Worn bearings, a burned winding, or an open thermal overload. The motor may buzz, run hot, or do nothing at all.
3. **Contactor not pulling in.** If the contactor doesn't close, the whole outdoor unit stays dead — no fan, no compressor. Pitted contacts or a failed coil are typical.
4. **Seized or obstructed fan blade.** Debris, a bent blade, or a rusted/seized bearing physically blocks rotation. The motor may hum against the jam.
5. **Loss of power / tripped breaker or disconnect.** The outdoor disconnect pulled or the breaker tripped, so nothing runs.
6. **Compressor running but fan circuit fault.** Wiring, a bad splice, or a rodent-chewed wire feeding only the fan motor.
7. **Control board or Infinity fault (variable-speed units only).** ECM module or communication fault on Infinity systems.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for a homeowner. Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser, touch the capacitor, or poke the fan while power is on.

- **Set the thermostat off, then wait.** Turn the system off at the thermostat and leave it off if the fan isn't spinning while the compressor runs — this protects the compressor.
- **Check the indoor filter.** A filthy filter causes a cascade of cooling problems; replace it if dirty.
- **Check the breaker.** Look at your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Check the outdoor disconnect.** The pull-out box on the wall near the condenser may be pulled or loose. Push it fully seated (power off at the breaker first if unsure).
- **Look, don't touch, at the fan blade.** With the system OFF, visually check for obvious debris, leaves, or a stick jamming the blade. Never reach in with power on.
- **Verify airflow at supply vents.** Confirm vents are open and unblocked.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A full drain pan or clogged line can trip a safety float switch and shut the system down.

If the fan still won't spin after these checks, it's an electrical repair — call a licensed HVAC technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the sequence helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirm call for cooling and power** at the contactor with a meter (24V control, line voltage).
2. **Test the run capacitor** with a capacitance meter, comparing the reading to the printed microfarad rating (e.g., 5 µF or 7.5 µF on the fan terminal). Out-of-spec = replace.
3. **Discharge the capacitor safely** before touching wiring.
4. **Check the contactor** — measure voltage across contacts, inspect for pitting, verify the coil energizes.
5. **Test the fan motor** — check windings for continuity/resistance, spin the blade by hand for bearing drag, feel for a tripped thermal overload.
6. **Inspect wiring** for burned terminals, chewed insulation, or loose connections.
7. **On Infinity systems**, read the fault log at the wall controller and check the ECM module.

A good tech isolates the failed part rather than swapping parts blindly. Be wary of a quote for a full motor when only a capacitor was tested.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't spin; spins if pushed | Weak/failed run capacitor | Shut unit off; don't run it | Test and replace capacitor |
| Compressor hums, fan dead, unit hot | Failed fan motor or bad cap | Shut off to protect compressor | Test motor windings; replace cap or motor |
| Whole outdoor unit dead, no hum | Contactor not closing or no power | Reset breaker once; seat disconnect | Test/replace contactor, trace power |
| Fan buzzes, blade jammed | Seized bearing or debris | Clear visible debris with power off | Replace motor if bearings seized |
| Breaker trips immediately | Shorted motor or wiring fault | Reset once only; then stop | Locate short, replace faulted component |
| Fault on Infinity wall control | ECM/board or comm fault | Note the fault message | Read fault log, test ECM/board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included. Prices vary by region and unit access.

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $300
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC):** $350 – $650, higher for premium or hard-to-source motors
- **Fan blade replacement:** $150 – $300
- **Infinity/ECM fan module or board:** $400 – $900+ (varies widely by model)
- **Diagnostic/service call:** $80 – $180, often applied to the repair

Capacitors and contactors are inexpensive wear items; if a tech recommends a full motor, ask them to show you the failed capacitor test first — a bad capacitor can mimic a bad motor.
