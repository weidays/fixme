## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numbered fault code — it's a physical symptom on your Carrier outdoor unit (the condenser). The large fan on top of the condenser is supposed to pull air through the coil to reject the heat your system pulled out of your house. When that fan stops turning, heat has nowhere to go.

On most Carrier split systems the compressor and the outdoor fan are powered through the same contactor, so the compressor may try to keep running while the fan is dead. That's dangerous: head pressure climbs quickly and the compressor can overheat. **If your fan isn't spinning, turn the system off at the thermostat now** and leave it off until it's fixed. This is a "pro" repair because most fixes involve a charged capacitor, line-voltage wiring, or a motor swap.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed or weak run capacitor** — The most common cause by far. The dual run capacitor gives the fan motor the jolt it needs to start. A swollen, leaking, or out-of-spec capacitor leaves the motor humming but not turning.
2. **Burned-out condenser fan motor** — Motors fail from age, heat, and bearing wear. A motor that's seized, open, or has a failed internal winding won't run even with a good capacitor.
3. **Failed contactor** — The contactor is the relay that sends power to the fan and compressor. Pitted, stuck, or chattering contacts can cut power to the fan.
4. **Tripped motor overload / overheated motor** — Some fan motors have a thermal cutout that opens when hot and resets when cool, causing intermittent stopping.
5. **Debris jamming the blade** — Sticks, ice, or a bent blade physically blocking rotation.
6. **Wiring/connection fault** — A burned wire, loose spade terminal, or corroded connection at the motor or capacitor.
7. **Low-voltage / control problem** — A failed control board (on Infinity/communicating systems) or thermostat issue not sending the call. Less common.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not open the electrical access panel on the condenser** — the capacitor stores a dangerous charge even with power off.

- **Thermostat**: Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace dead batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers**: Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor (AC) breaker, plus the outdoor disconnect box near the condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Air filter**: A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and odd behavior. Replace it if dirty.
- **Visible blade obstruction**: With the system OFF at the thermostat, look down through the top grille for leaves, a stick, or ice on the blade. Do not reach in or remove the grille.
- **Indoor vents**: Make sure supply registers and the return are open and unblocked.
- **Condensate**: A full condensate pan with a tripped float switch can shut the system down — check that the drain line isn't clogged and the pan isn't overflowing.

If the fan still won't spin after these, it's a service call.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Carrier tech will work in roughly this order, and you can sanity-check a quote against it:

1. **Confirm the call** — Verify the thermostat is sending a cooling demand and 24V is reaching the contactor coil.
2. **Check incoming power** — Measure line voltage at the contactor with the disconnect in.
3. **Test the run capacitor** — With power safely discharged, measure microfarad (µF) value against the rating printed on the cap. Out-of-spec = replace.
4. **Inspect the contactor** — Look for pitted/welded contacts and confirm it pulls in and passes voltage to the fan.
5. **Test the fan motor** — Check windings for resistance and shorts, spin the blade by hand to feel for seized bearings, and verify the thermal overload isn't open.
6. **Inspect wiring** — Look for burned terminals at the motor and capacitor.
7. **On Infinity/communicating systems** — Pull fault history from the control board and confirm no board-level fault.

A capacitor test takes minutes; a motor test takes longer. Be wary of anyone replacing the motor without first proving the capacitor and contactor are good.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but doesn't spin; spins if nudged | Failed run capacitor | Shut off at thermostat | Discharge & replace capacitor, verify µF |
| Fan dead, compressor humming/cycling | Bad capacitor or contactor | Turn system off immediately | Test cap, contactor; replace as needed |
| Fan and compressor both dead | Contactor, disconnect, or breaker | Check breaker/disconnect once | Test contactor coil & 24V control |
| Fan starts then stops after a while | Overheating motor / thermal overload | Turn off, let cool | Replace fan motor |
| Buzzing, no movement, burnt smell | Seized motor or burned wiring | Shut off at breaker, call pro | Replace motor and/or repair wiring |
| Blade visibly stuck/bent/iced | Debris or ice jam | Turn off; remove visible debris only | Clear jam, inspect blade & bearings |
| Newer Infinity unit, no fan, error on display | Control board / comms fault | Note the error shown | Pull board fault codes, diagnose board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges, parts plus labor. Prices vary by region, model, and brand-vs-universal parts.

- **Run capacitor replacement**: **$150 – $400**. The part is cheap ($15–$40), but most of the cost is the service call and diagnosis.
- **Contactor replacement**: **$150 – $350**.
- **Condenser fan motor replacement**: **$350 – $700+**. OEM Carrier motors and ECM motors cost more than universal PSC replacements; the capacitor is often replaced at the same time.
- **Fan blade replacement**: **$150 – $300** if the motor is fine.
- **Wiring/terminal repair**: **$150 – $300** depending on damage.
- **Control board (Infinity/communicating)**: **$400 – $900+**.
- **DIY capacitor swap**: parts only ($15–$40) — but only if you're qualified to safely discharge it. For most homeowners, this is a pro job.

A weak capacitor that goes unfixed can burn out the fan motor, turning a ~$200 repair into a ~$600 one — so don't delay.
