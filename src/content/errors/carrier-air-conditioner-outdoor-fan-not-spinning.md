## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a Carrier numeric fault code — it's a physical symptom of your outdoor condenser unit. The fan on top of the outdoor unit pulls air across the condenser coil to release the heat your system pulled out of your home. When it stops spinning while the compressor or unit is energized, the system can no longer reject heat.

On most Carrier split systems (Comfort, Performance, and many Infinity models), the outdoor fan is a single-phase PSC (permanent split capacitor) motor that relies on a run capacitor for the torque to start and keep turning. On **Infinity and Greenspeed variable-speed condensers**, the fan is driven electronically and a fault will usually show as a code or message on the Infinity wall control rather than a silent stalled fan — behavior there differs and is best read off the system control.

Because a stalled fan with a running compressor can spike head pressure and damage the compressor, this is a **pro-level** problem: shut the system off at the thermostat until it's diagnosed.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor.** The single most common cause. A weak or dead capacitor leaves the motor without start torque — it hums and may spin if nudged, but won't start on its own.
2. **Failed condenser fan motor.** Worn bearings, an open winding, or a tripped internal thermal overload. The motor may be hot, noisy, or completely dead.
3. **Failed or stuck contactor.** If the contactor isn't pulling in, neither the fan nor the compressor gets line voltage. Pitted or welded contacts also cause problems.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuses.** A loss of 240V at the disconnect or panel kills the whole outdoor unit.
5. **Obstructed or seized fan blade.** Debris, ice, or a bent blade jamming against the shroud.
6. **Control board or relay fault (Infinity/variable-speed units).** On electronically controlled fans, a board or module failure stops the fan; this reports through the Infinity control.
7. **Low/no 24V control signal.** A bad transformer or thermostat wiring issue means the contactor never gets the command to close.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — no panel opening, no touching capacitors, no bypassing anything.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it uses them.
- **Indoor air filter:** A badly clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and pressure faults that trip the outdoor unit. Replace if dirty.
- **Circuit breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor AC breakers in your panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** The pull-out box near the condenser should be fully seated. Don't open it or probe inside.
- **Visible obstructions:** Look (don't reach in) for leaves, sticks, or ice in the fan. Clear loose debris from around the unit with the power off.
- **Condensate drain:** A backed-up drain or float switch can shut the system down; clear a visibly clogged drain line.

If the fan still won't start, turn the system off and call a technician. **Do not repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out** — that risks compressor damage.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Carrier tech will work in a logical order, and you should hear them describe steps like these:

- Verify 240V line voltage at the disconnect and 24V control voltage at the contactor coil.
- Confirm the contactor is pulling in (closing) when there's a cooling call.
- **Test the run capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarads (e.g., the "5" side of a 45/5 dual cap feeds the fan). A reading well below spec confirms a bad cap.
- Check the fan motor for continuity, an open winding, a tripped thermal overload, and seized/noisy bearings.
- Inspect the fan blade for damage or obstruction and the motor mount for security.
- On Infinity/variable-speed units, read fault codes off the wall control and test the fan module or board per Carrier service literature.

Expect a diagnostic fee of roughly **$80–$150**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't start; spins if nudged | Dead/weak run capacitor | Turn unit off; don't touch the cap | Test & replace capacitor (it holds a charge) |
| Outdoor unit completely dead, no hum | Tripped breaker or blown fuses; bad transformer | Reset breaker once; check thermostat | Test line/control voltage, replace fuses/transformer |
| Compressor runs, fan doesn't | Capacitor or fan motor failure | Shut system off immediately | Test cap, then motor; replace failed part |
| Loud grinding/screeching from fan | Worn motor bearings | Turn off to prevent further damage | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Fan jammed / blade hits shroud | Debris or bent blade | Clear loose debris (power off) | Free/replace blade, check motor shaft |
| Buzzing contactor, intermittent fan | Pitted/sticking contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| Fan fault on Infinity wall control | Board/module fault (variable-speed) | Note the code shown | Test/replace fan module or control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Run capacitor:** $150–$350 installed (the part is cheap, the diagnosis and safe discharge are the value).
- **Contactor:** $150–$300 installed.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$650+ installed; OEM Carrier motors and matched blades sit at the high end.
- **Fan blade only:** $100–$250 installed.
- **Transformer / control wiring:** $150–$350.
- **Infinity fan module / control board:** $300–$700+, varies widely by model.
- **Diagnostic fee:** $80–$150, often applied to the repair.

A DIY-savvy owner with the right meter can buy a capacitor for $20–$40 — but a charged capacitor can deliver a dangerous shock and must be discharged correctly, so we list this as a pro repair.
