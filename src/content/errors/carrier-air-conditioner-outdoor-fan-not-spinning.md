## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is not a Carrier fault code — most Carrier air conditioners flash codes only at the furnace or air handler board, not the outdoor unit. Instead, this is a symptom: the fan blade on top of your outdoor condenser unit isn't turning while the system is calling for cooling.

The outdoor fan pulls air across the condenser coil to reject the heat your system pulled out of the house. When it stops, the refrigerant can't shed heat, pressure and temperature spike, and the compressor's internal safety will shut it down (or, worse, the compressor overheats). That's why this is a **pro-severity** problem: continuing to run the system risks the most expensive component in the unit.

If the compressor is humming but the fan is dead, or the whole outdoor unit is silent, this page covers what's happening and what a fair repair looks like.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor.** The most common cause by far. The dual run capacitor supplies the starting/running torque for the fan motor (and compressor). A weak or dead cap means the fan won't start, often paired with a humming sound. Capacitors are a wear item and fail more in hot weather.
2. **Failed condenser fan motor.** Worn bearings, burned windings, or an open thermal overload. The motor may be silent, hum, or spin slowly and stall.
3. **Debris or ice jamming the blade.** Sticks, cottonwood seed, or a bent blade physically blocks rotation. Motors can burn out trying to push against a jam.
4. **Bad contactor.** The contactor is the relay that sends 240V to the fan and compressor. Pitted or stuck contacts can drop power to the fan.
5. **Tripped breaker or blown fuses at the disconnect.** No power reaching the outdoor unit at all — usually the whole unit is silent.
6. **Loose or corroded wiring.** Spade connectors on the motor or capacitor back off or corrode, breaking the circuit.
7. **Control board / low-voltage issue (ECM models).** Some Infinity and higher-end units use electronically controlled motors; a fault can prevent fan operation and may log at the indoor board.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not open the outdoor unit's electrical panel — the capacitor stores a dangerous charge even with power off.**

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and lower the setpoint several degrees below room temperature so the system is actually calling.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause the whole system to short-cycle or freeze; replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check both the AC breaker in your main panel and the outdoor disconnect box near the unit. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Batteries:** For battery-powered thermostats, swap in fresh ones if the display is dim or blank.
- **Visible obstructions:** With the system **off at the breaker**, look through the top grille for leaves, sticks, or a bird's nest. Clear obvious debris from around and inside the top grille — do not force or hand-spin the blade.
- **Ice:** If the outdoor coil is iced over, turn the system OFF and set the fan to ON at the thermostat to help it thaw. See our Frozen Evaporator Coil guide.
- **Airflow around the unit:** Make sure at least 2 feet of clearance around the condenser is free of plants, fencing, or stored items.

If the fan still won't spin after these checks, it's a technician job.

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the sequence helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirm power and the call for cooling** — verify 240V at the disconnect and 24V control signal reaching the contactor coil.
2. **Test the capacitor** with the power off and the cap safely discharged, checking microfarad reading against the rating printed on it. A reading well below spec confirms replacement.
3. **Inspect the contactor** for pitted contacts and verify it pulls in and passes voltage to the fan.
4. **Check the fan motor** — measure winding resistance, test the thermal overload, and confirm the blade spins freely by hand (power off).
5. **Inspect wiring** at the motor, cap, and contactor for loose, burned, or corroded connections.
6. **On ECM/Infinity systems**, pull any stored fault codes at the indoor board and check the motor module and communications.

A good tech tests the cheap parts (capacitor, wiring) before condemning the motor.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan doesn't spin | Failed run capacitor | Shut system off; don't hand-spin | Discharge and replace capacitor |
| Fan spins if nudged then keeps going | Weak capacitor (low torque) | Turn off, don't touch blade | Test µF, replace capacitor |
| Fan hums but won't turn, or spins slow | Failing fan motor | Shut off to prevent burnout | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Blade physically blocked | Debris or bent blade | Power off, clear visible debris | Straighten/replace blade, check motor |
| Whole outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker / blown fuses / bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Test disconnect, contactor, low voltage |
| Fan intermittent, buzzing relay | Pitted/stuck contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| No fan on high-end unit, board code logged | ECM/control fault | Note any indoor code | Diagnose motor module / board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined (varies by region and model):

- **DIY breaker reset or debris removal:** $0
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor replacement:** $400–$650 (higher for ECM/variable-speed on Infinity units)
- **Wiring repair / connector replacement:** $100–$250
- **Diagnostic/service call:** $80–$180 (often credited toward the repair)

ECM and communicating (Infinity) systems cost more because the motor and control modules are proprietary and pricier than standard PSC motors.
