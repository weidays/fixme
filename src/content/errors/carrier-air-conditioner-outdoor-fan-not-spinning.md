## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a stored fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a symptom you observe at the condenser (the outdoor unit). When the thermostat calls for cooling, the compressor and the outdoor fan motor should both energize together. The fan pulls air across the outdoor coil to reject the heat your system pulled from inside the house.

If the fan stays still while the compressor runs (you'll hear a hum and feel vibration), the unit cannot shed heat. Pressures and temperatures climb fast, and a thermal or high-pressure safety will usually shut the compressor down — or, worse, the compressor overheats before it trips.

On variable-speed **Infinity and Greenspeed** systems, the outdoor fan is driven by an ECM control board and may report a specific fault at the wall control. On standard single-stage **Comfort and Performance** units, there's no display — you diagnose by observation and measurement.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run (dual) capacitor** — by far the most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan motor its starting torque. A bulged, leaking, or out-of-spec capacitor leaves the motor humming but motionless.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — worn bearings or failed windings. Often smells burnt; the shaft won't turn freely by hand when power is off.
3. **Failed contactor** — pitted or stuck contacts won't pass power cleanly to the fan and compressor.
4. **Debris jamming the fan blade** — leaves, twigs, or a warped blade physically blocking rotation.
5. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — partial power loss can stall one component.
6. **ECM fan board fault (Infinity/Greenspeed only)** — the variable-speed motor or its control board has failed.
7. **Loose, corroded, or chewed wiring** at the motor, capacitor, or contactor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not open the electrical access panel** — the capacitor stores a dangerous charge even with power off.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and a temperature below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and shutdowns that mimic this problem. Replace if dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor (disconnect) breaker. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor unit clearance:** Look through the top grille for obvious debris (leaves, sticks) sitting on the fan blade. Clear visible debris only with the unit powered off at the disconnect.
- **Condensate:** A full condensate pan/float switch can shut the system down — check that the drain isn't backed up.
- **Listen:** If you hear humming from the outdoor unit, turn the system OFF at the thermostat to protect the compressor, then call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical path, and knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirm the call for cooling** reaches the outdoor unit (24V at the contactor coil).
2. **Verify line voltage** across the contactor when energized.
3. **Discharge and test the capacitor** with a meter — comparing measured microfarads (µF) to the rating printed on the can. A reading more than ~6% off spec means replacement.
4. **Test the fan motor** — check windings for continuity/shorts and spin the shaft by hand (power off) to feel for a seized bearing.
5. **Inspect the contactor** contacts for pitting and the coil for proper pull-in.
6. **On Infinity/Greenspeed units,** read fault data at the wall control and test the ECM motor module/board per Carrier's procedure.

Expect a diagnostic fee of **$90–$180**, usually credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't spin; spins if nudged | Failed run capacitor | None — capacitor is dangerous to handle | Discharge & replace capacitor |
| No hum, no movement; compressor also silent | Tripped breaker / dead contactor / lost 24V | Reset breaker once; check thermostat | Test contactor, transformer, fuse |
| Fan blade physically blocked | Debris or warped blade | Power off, clear visible debris | Replace bent blade, balance |
| Burnt smell, fan dead, compressor runs | Burned-out fan motor | Shut system off to protect compressor | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Intermittent fan, then shutdown | Pitted contactor or weak capacitor | Note when it happens | Replace contactor/capacitor |
| Wall control shows fan fault (Infinity) | ECM board/motor fault | Note exact code shown | Test/replace ECM module or board |

## Repair costs

US ballpark ranges including parts and labor (regional pricing varies):

- **Diagnostic fee:** $90–$180 (often credited toward repair)
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $90–$250 installed
- **Contactor:** $120–$300 installed
- **Condenser fan motor (standard PSC):** $300–$650 installed
- **Fan blade:** $80–$200 installed
- **ECM fan motor/control board (Infinity/Greenspeed):** $400–$900+ — varies widely by model and parts availability

If the compressor was run hot for an extended period before shutdown, ask the tech to verify compressor health — that's a much larger repair if it was damaged.
