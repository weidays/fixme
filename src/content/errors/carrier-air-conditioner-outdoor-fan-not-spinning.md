## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is a symptom, not a stored fault code on most Carrier units. The outdoor (condenser) fan pulls air across the outdoor coil so the system can dump the heat it removed from your home. When it stops, the compressor often keeps trying to run, pressures and temperatures climb fast, and the unit will eventually trip on internal protection or fail outright.

On basic single-stage Carrier condensers there is no display, so you only notice the still fan plus poor or no cooling. On Infinity communicating systems, a related issue may post a fault at the thermostat or control board, but the bare "fan won't spin" condition itself is mechanical or electrical and diagnosed by inspection.

This is rated **pro** because the most common fixes involve a capacitor that can hold a dangerous charge and line-voltage wiring inside an energized unit.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed dual run capacitor** — by far the most common. The capacitor gives the fan motor (and compressor) the jolt they need to start. A bulged, leaking, or out-of-spec capacitor leaves the fan dead while you may hear humming.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — worn bearings, overheating, or age. The blade won't turn freely, or it spins freely but the motor never energizes.
3. **Failed contactor** — the relay that sends power to the outdoor unit. Pitted or stuck contacts can starve the fan of voltage.
4. **Tripped or open thermal overload in the fan motor** — the motor protects itself when overheated, often a sign of a failing motor or low airflow.
5. **Debris blocking the blade** — sticks, ice, or a bent blade physically jamming rotation.
6. **Wiring fault** — loose, corroded, or chewed connections at the capacitor, contactor, or motor.
7. **Control board fault (Infinity/communicating units)** — less common, but a board that won't command the fan can cause this on higher-end systems.
8. **Loss of high-voltage power** to the condenser (blown fuse at the disconnect or tripped breaker) — the whole unit is dead, not just the fan.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to Cool and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and protective shutdowns; replace it if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check the AC breaker in your electrical panel. If it's tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro—do not keep resetting.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Make sure the pull-out disconnect box near the condenser is fully seated.
- **Vents and registers:** Confirm supply and return vents inside are open and unblocked.
- **Visible debris:** With the unit OFF at the breaker, look down into the fan for obvious leaves or sticks—but do **not** reach in or touch internal components.
- **Condensate:** Check that the indoor drain pan isn't overflowing (some systems shut down on a full pan).

Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser or touch the capacitor—it can store a lethal charge even with power off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

- **Visual and mechanical check:** Spins the blade by hand (power off) to feel for a seized motor; inspects for debris and bent blades.
- **Capacitor test:** Measures microfarads (µF) against the rating printed on the cap; a reading well out of tolerance means replacement.
- **Voltage checks:** Confirms line voltage at the disconnect, contactor, and across to the fan motor while the unit calls for cooling.
- **Contactor inspection:** Looks for pitted contacts and tests that it pulls in and passes voltage.
- **Motor test:** Checks windings for an open or grounded coil and verifies the thermal overload isn't tripped.
- **Board diagnostics (Infinity):** Reads any posted faults and confirms the board is commanding fan output.

A good tech isolates whether it's the capacitor (cheap), the motor (moderate), or the board (expensive) before quoting—be wary of a flat "replace the whole unit" without that.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan still | Failed run capacitor | Turn AC off; don't run it | Test µF, replace capacitor |
| Blade won't turn by hand | Seized fan motor | Turn off, call pro | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Blade spins freely, no power to motor | Weak capacitor or open motor winding | Turn off | Test cap and motor, replace failed part |
| Whole unit dead | Blown disconnect fuse / tripped breaker | Reset breaker once | Find cause of trip, replace fuse |
| Fan stops after running a while | Motor thermal overload tripping | Turn off, let cool | Replace overheating motor |
| Blade jammed by debris | Sticks, ice, bent blade | Clear visible debris (power off) | Straighten/replace blade |
| No fan command (Infinity) | Control board fault | None | Diagnose and replace board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Dual run capacitor:** $150–$350 installed (part is cheap; you're paying for the diagnosis and trip).
- **Condenser fan motor:** $350–$650 installed, depending on HP and whether a new blade is needed.
- **Fan blade only:** $80–$200 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$300 installed.
- **Control board (Infinity/communicating):** $400–$700+ installed.
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80–$180, often credited toward the repair.

If the unit is more than ~12–15 years old and needs a motor plus other parts, ask whether replacement makes more sense than repeated repairs.
