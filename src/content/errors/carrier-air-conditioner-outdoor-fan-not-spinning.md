## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numeric fault code — it's a symptom you'll see at the condenser (the outdoor unit). When you call for cooling, the compressor may hum or run, but the large fan on top of the outdoor unit stays still or barely turns.

That fan's job is to pull air across the condenser coil to dump heat from the refrigerant. Without it, head pressure climbs fast, the compressor overheats, and the system either stops cooling, trips on a safety, or — worst case — damages the compressor. Because the most common fixes involve a charged capacitor and 240-volt wiring, this is a **pro-level** repair for most homeowners.

Note that Carrier Infinity and some Performance systems with ECM (variable-speed) outdoor fans behave differently and may log a communicating fault at the indoor control or thermostat. Standard single-stage units use a simpler PSC motor with a run capacitor and won't display a code at all.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor.** The single most common cause. The dual run capacitor provides the torque the fan motor needs to start. When it weakens or fails, the motor hums but won't spin on its own — or spins if nudged. Capacitors are the most frequent AC failure overall.
2. **Failed condenser fan motor.** Bearings seize, windings burn out, or the internal thermal overload trips permanently. The motor won't run even with a good capacitor.
3. **Contactor not pulling in or stuck.** The contactor is the relay that sends 240V to the compressor and fan. Pitted contacts, a failed coil, or insects/debris can interrupt power to the fan.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuses at the disconnect.** Loss of power to the outdoor unit stops both the fan and compressor.
5. **Obstructed or damaged fan blade.** Debris, ice, a bent blade, or something lodged in the unit can physically block rotation.
6. **Control board or relay fault (ECM/Infinity units).** On communicating systems, a bad outdoor control board or motor module can stop the fan and may log a fault.
7. **Loose, corroded, or chewed wiring.** Rodents and corrosion can break the circuit to the fan motor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. The condenser holds a **charged capacitor and 240-volt wiring** — do not open the electrical panel or touch internal components.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups that mimic or worsen fan problems. Replace if dirty.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor furnace/air handler breaker and the outdoor AC breaker in your main panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Make sure the pull-out disconnect box near the condenser is fully seated.
- **Visible obstructions:** With the unit OFF at the breaker, look through the top grille for leaves, sticks, or debris on the blade. Clear loose debris by hand — do not disassemble anything.
- **Ice:** If the indoor coil or outdoor unit is iced over, turn the system to OFF (fan ON) and let it thaw for a few hours, then call a pro to find the cause.
- **Listen:** If you hear a hum from the outdoor unit but no spinning, shut it off — that points to a capacitor or motor and needs a technician.

If the fan still won't spin after these checks, turn the system off and call an HVAC professional. Running it risks compressor damage.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will work methodically — use this to sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirm power** at the disconnect and contactor with a meter (should read ~240V).
2. **Test the capacitor** with a meter on the capacitance scale, comparing the reading to the rated microfarads (µF) printed on it. A weak or shorted cap is condemned.
3. **Check the contactor** — verify the coil pulls in and contacts pass voltage without excessive pitting.
4. **Test the fan motor** for continuity, resistance, and a tripped or open thermal overload. They may briefly nudge the blade to confirm a capacitor-only failure.
5. **Inspect wiring** for burnt, loose, or rodent-damaged connections at the motor and board.
6. **On Infinity/ECM units,** read fault history at the thermostat or control and test the motor module per Carrier's procedure.
7. **Verify the repair** by measuring fan amperage and system pressures after the fix.

Be cautious of a quote that jumps straight to a full motor or compressor replacement without first testing the inexpensive capacitor.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't spin; spins if nudged | Failed run capacitor | Shut unit off; don't run it | Test and replace capacitor |
| No hum, no fan, compressor silent | No power / tripped breaker / disconnect out | Reset breaker once; seat disconnect | Trace power loss, test contactor |
| Compressor runs, fan dead | Bad fan motor or capacitor | Turn system off to protect compressor | Test motor & cap, replace as needed |
| Fan blade physically stuck | Debris or bent blade | Clear loose debris (power off) | Replace blade, straighten or balance |
| Buzzing/chattering at contactor | Failing contactor coil/contacts | None | Replace contactor |
| Communicating fault on thermostat | ECM motor or control board (Infinity) | Note the code; don't repeat resets | Diagnose board/motor module |
| Fan runs but unit ices up | Airflow/refrigerant issue | Replace filter; thaw unit | Check charge, airflow, coil |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included. Rates vary by region and by Carrier model series:

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC):** $400 – $700+
- **ECM/variable-speed fan motor or module (Infinity):** $600 – $1,200+
- **Fan blade replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Wiring repair / rodent damage:** $150 – $500
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80 – $200 (often credited toward repair)

A capacitor is cheap, but it's frequently a symptom of an aging or overloaded motor — ask the tech to confirm the motor amps are within spec after replacing the cap.
