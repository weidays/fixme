## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numeric fault code — it's a symptom you can see and hear at your Carrier condenser (the unit outside). When the system calls for cooling, the compressor and the fan on top of the outdoor unit should both run. The fan's job is to pull air across the condenser coil and dump the heat your AC removed from inside the house.

When that fan stops, heat has nowhere to go. Pressures climb fast, and on most Carrier units the compressor will trip on its internal overload or a high-pressure switch within minutes — leaving you with warm air and a compressor that may be loudly straining.

Standard single-stage and two-stage Carrier condensers use a simple PSC fan motor driven by a run capacitor. **Variable-speed Infinity systems use an ECM fan motor** and will usually log a related fault at the indoor control board rather than just silently failing — if you have an Infinity system, check the thermostat for a displayed fault first.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — by far the most common cause. The fan motor shares a dual capacitor with the compressor (or has its own). A weak or dead capacitor leaves the motor with no starting torque, so it hums but won't spin.
2. **Seized or worn fan motor** — bearings fail, especially on older units, and the motor stalls or won't turn at all. May smell burnt.
3. **Debris jamming the blade** — leaves, sticks, or a bent blade physically stopping rotation.
4. **Failed contactor** — the relay that sends power to the outdoor unit isn't pulling in, so nothing outside runs (fan *and* compressor both dead is a clue).
5. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuses** — no power reaching the condenser at all.
6. **Bad motor capacitor wiring / loose connection** — corroded or vibrated-loose spade terminals.
7. **Open high-pressure or float safety** — on some systems an open safety stops the whole outdoor unit; this usually points to a separate underlying problem.
8. **ECM fan module failure (Infinity)** — variable-speed motor or its module has faulted.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools that expose you to high voltage or refrigerant:

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and several degrees below room temperature so the system actually calls for cooling. Confirm batteries are good if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups and odd behavior. Replace it if dirty — see our Frozen Evaporator Coil guide.
- **Breaker:** Check your main panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated trips mean a fault.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** The pull-out box on the wall near the unit should be fully seated.
- **Visible debris:** With the system OFF at the breaker, look down into the top of the unit for leaves or sticks against the blade. Clear only what you can reach safely without removing panels.
- **Indoor vents:** Make sure supply registers aren't all closed.
- **Condensate:** A full drain pan can trip a float switch on some installs — check that the pan isn't overflowing.

Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser, touch the capacitor, or spin the fan by hand with power on. Capacitors store a dangerous charge even with the breaker off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a predictable path. Knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirms the call for cooling** and verifies 240V is reaching the condenser at the disconnect.
2. **Checks the contactor** — looks for 24V at the coil and confirms the contacts pull in and pass line voltage.
3. **Tests the capacitor** with a meter — measures microfarads (µF) against the rating printed on the can. A reading well below spec confirms a bad cap.
4. **Tests the fan motor** — checks windings for resistance and shorts, spins it by hand for bearing drag, and tries it on a known-good capacitor.
5. **Inspects the blade and shaft** for binding or damage.
6. **Checks safeties** — high-pressure switch and any float switches in the circuit.
7. On **Infinity/ECM** units, pulls the fault code at the board and tests the motor module.

A capacitor test takes minutes; if they jump straight to "you need a new motor" without testing the capacitor first, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None — high voltage | Test & replace capacitor |
| Fan spins if pushed, then keeps running | Weak capacitor | None | Replace capacitor |
| Fan dead, compressor also dead | Contactor or power loss | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, voltage, fuses |
| Fan won't turn, grinding/burnt smell | Seized motor bearings | Shut off system | Replace fan motor |
| Blade visibly jammed | Debris or bent blade | Clear debris (power off) | Replace blade if bent |
| Nothing outside runs | Tripped breaker/blown disconnect | Reset breaker once | Find cause of trip, replace fuses |
| Fault on Infinity thermostat | ECM motor/module fault | Note code | Test/replace ECM module or motor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges, parts and labor:

- **Capacitor replacement:** $130 – $300. The part is cheap ($15–$40); you're paying for the service call and expertise. Often the entire fix.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350.
- **Condenser fan motor (PSC):** $300 – $650 installed, depending on horsepower and brand. Usually replaced with a fresh capacitor.
- **Fan blade:** $80 – $200.
- **Disconnect fuses:** $80 – $180.
- **ECM fan motor/module (Infinity):** $500 – $1,200 — variable-speed parts are pricey.

If a tech quotes a new motor, it should typically come with a new matching capacitor. Replacing a $25 capacitor first is always the smart, cheapest diagnostic step.
