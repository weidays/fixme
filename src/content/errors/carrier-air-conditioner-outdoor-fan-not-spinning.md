## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numeric fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a symptom you observe at the condenser (the outdoor unit). The fan on top of the unit should spin whenever the compressor is running to blow heat out of the condenser coil.

When that fan stalls but the compressor still tries to run, the unit cannot reject heat. Head pressure climbs fast, and the compressor overheats. This is why we rate it **pro severity**: continued operation risks a compressor failure that can cost more than the rest of the system.

On Carrier **Infinity** systems with communicating controls and ECM-driven fans, a fan fault may show as a message or fault number on the Infinity thermostat. On standard **Comfort** and **Performance** single-stage units, there's no code — you just see the fan sitting still while the unit hums.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — by far the most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan motor its starting torque. When it weakens, the fan won't start on its own (but may spin if nudged).
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — bearings fail or windings short. The motor won't turn even with a good capacitor, and may smell burnt or feel hot.
3. **Failed contactor** — the relay that sends power to the outdoor unit. Pitted or stuck contacts can starve the fan and compressor of voltage.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse / disconnect** — no power to the outdoor unit at all (compressor also won't run).
5. **Obstruction** — leaves, a stick, or ice physically blocking the fan blade.
6. **Loose or burnt wiring** at the capacitor, motor, or contactor terminals.
7. **Control board / ECM fault** (Infinity and some Performance units) — less common but possible on communicating systems.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Stop here if nothing resolves it.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's a battery-powered model.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause the indoor coil to freeze and trip safeties — check and replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Look at your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** The pull-out box near the condenser should be fully seated.
- **Visible obstruction:** With the system **off at the breaker**, look down through the top fan grille for leaves, sticks, or debris on the blade. Clear anything obvious by hand.
- **Indoor vents:** Make sure supply registers aren't all closed.
- **Condensate:** Check that the drain pan isn't overflowing and tripping a float switch.

Do **not** open the electrical panel of the condenser, touch the capacitor, or spin the fan with a stick to "help" it start. The capacitor stores a dangerous charge even with power off, and forcing a stalled fan can mask a problem that's destroying the compressor.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will work in this order — use it to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify power** at the disconnect and contactor with a meter (line voltage in, 24V at the contactor coil).
2. **Discharge and test the run capacitor** with a meter set to capacitance, comparing the reading to the microfarad rating printed on the cap.
3. **Test the fan motor** — check windings for continuity/shorts and confirm it spins freely by hand (power off).
4. **Inspect the contactor** for pitting, chatter, or a failed coil.
5. **Check wiring** for loose, melted, or rodent-chewed connections.
6. On **Infinity/communicating** units, pull fault history from the thermostat and check the ECM/board.

A capacitor diagnosis should take minutes. Beware a quote that jumps straight to "new motor" or "new compressor" without showing you a failed capacitor reading first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan still, compressor humming, spins if nudged | Failed run capacitor | Turn system off; do not nudge repeatedly | Test & replace capacitor |
| Fan won't turn even when nudged; burnt smell | Seized/burned fan motor | Shut off at breaker | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Whole outdoor unit dead, no hum | Contactor, breaker, or disconnect | Reset breaker once; check disconnect | Test/replace contactor or trace power loss |
| Fan blade visibly jammed | Debris or ice | Power off, clear visible debris | Inspect for cause if recurring |
| Fan starts then stops, breaker trips | Shorted motor or wiring | Stop resetting; call pro | Isolate short, replace component |
| Infinity thermostat shows fan fault | ECM or control board | Note the message | Pull fault data, test ECM/board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $350. The part is cheap ($15–$45); the rest is the service call and diagnosis.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $300.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $350 – $700+, depending on motor type. ECM motors on Infinity units run higher.
- **Wiring repair:** $150 – $400 depending on extent.
- **Control board / ECM module (Infinity):** $400 – $900+.
- **DIY:** $0 if it's just a tripped breaker or cleared debris.

If the compressor was run with the fan stalled for an extended period, factor in a possible compressor diagnosis on top — another reason to shut the unit off immediately when the fan won't spin.
