## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a flash code on a Carrier display — it's a physical symptom. The fan inside your outdoor condenser unit (the big box outside) should spin whenever the compressor runs, pulling air across the condenser coil to dump heat from your home.

When that fan stays still while the compressor hums or clicks, heat has nowhere to go. The system pressure climbs fast, and on most Carrier units the compressor will overheat and shut down on its internal overload — or trip a high-pressure safety. Continuing to run it this way is the single fastest way to ruin a compressor, which is why this is rated a pro-level problem.

On Infinity-series systems with a communicating control board, you may see a related fault logged at the user interface, but the underlying causes below are the same across all models.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed or weak run capacitor** — The most common cause by far. The dual run capacitor gives the fan motor its starting "kick." A bulged, leaking, or out-of-spec capacitor leaves the motor humming but not turning.
2. **Failed condenser fan motor** — Bearings seize or windings burn out, especially on older units or after running hot. The motor won't start even with a good capacitor.
3. **Bad contactor** — The contactor is the relay that sends 240V to the fan and compressor. Pitted, stuck, or chattering contacts can starve the fan of power.
4. **Obstructed or damaged fan blade** — Sticks, ice, a bird, or a bent blade physically jamming rotation. Sometimes the blade hub loosens on the shaft.
5. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — Often the whole unit is dead, not just the fan, but worth ruling out first.
6. **Control board or relay fault** — On ECM-motor or Infinity communicating systems, a board or motor module can fail. Less common, harder to diagnose.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks you can do safely without opening up the electrical panel of the unit:

- **Thermostat:** Set it to Cool and the temperature several degrees below room temp. Confirm it's actually calling for cooling.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor disconnect or breaker. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Disconnect:** Make sure the pull-out disconnect box on the wall by the outdoor unit is fully seated.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freeze-ups that mimic outdoor-side problems. Replace if dirty.
- **Visible debris:** With the power OFF at the disconnect, look down through the top fan grille for leaves, sticks, or ice on the blade. Remove obvious loose debris by hand.
- **Condensate:** A full condensate pan or clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut the system down — check that it's draining.

**Do not** reach into the unit's electrical compartment, touch the capacitor, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping. The capacitor holds a dangerous charge even with power off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech will work through this in order, so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirm the call:** Verify 24V is reaching the contactor and the contactor is pulling in.
2. **Check incoming power:** Measure 240V at the contactor's load side to confirm the fan circuit is energized.
3. **Test the capacitor:** Discharge it safely, then read microfarads with a meter against the rated value stamped on the can (e.g., 45/5 µF). Out-of-spec gets replaced.
4. **Test the motor:** With a known-good capacitor, check whether the motor starts. Measure winding resistance and check the bearings for binding.
5. **Inspect the contactor:** Look for pitted or welded contacts and verify the coil pulls in cleanly.
6. **Communicating systems:** On Infinity/ECM units, the tech checks the motor module and board faults at the control.

Expect a diagnostic fee of **$75–$200**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan hums but won't turn; spins if nudged | Weak/failed run capacitor | Shut off unit; don't force it | Discharge & replace capacitor |
| Fan dead, compressor hums then clicks off | Failed fan motor or bad capacitor | Turn off at thermostat | Test/replace capacitor or motor |
| Whole unit dead, no fan or compressor | Tripped breaker, blown fuse, bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace fuse/contactor, find cause |
| Fan blade visibly jammed | Debris or bent blade | Power off, clear loose debris | Straighten/replace blade & hub |
| Fan starts and stops randomly | Chattering contactor or overheating motor | Note the pattern | Replace contactor or motor |
| Infinity unit shows fan fault | Motor module/board issue | Cycle power once | Diagnose board/ECM module |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$350. The part is cheap ($15–$40); most of the cost is labor and the trip.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Condenser fan motor replacement:** $350–$700, depending on motor type. ECM/variable-speed motors on Infinity units run higher.
- **Fan blade replacement:** $150–$300.
- **Low-voltage fuse / minor wiring:** $90–$200.
- **Diagnostic fee:** $75–$200, often applied to the repair.

If a unit is more than 12–15 years old and needs a motor plus other parts, ask your tech whether replacement makes more sense than repair.
