## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" is a symptom, not a stored fault code on most Carrier equipment. The outdoor (condenser) fan pulls air across the outdoor coil so the system can reject the heat it absorbed indoors. When that fan stops, your Carrier AC can no longer cool, head pressure climbs fast, and the compressor is at risk of overheating and locking out.

On basic **Comfort and Performance** single-stage units there's no diagnostic display — you simply notice the top fan isn't turning. On **Infinity** systems with variable-speed ECM fan motors, the indoor control or wall thermostat may log a communication or fan fault. Either way, this is a **pro-level** condition: by the time the fan is dead, you should stop running the system to protect the compressor.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run/start capacitor** — The single most common cause. The capacitor gives the fan motor the jolt it needs to start. When it fails, the motor hums but won't spin.
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor** — Worn bearings or a shorted winding. Often smells burnt or the motor is hot to the touch.
3. **Failed contactor** — The relay that sends power to the outdoor unit. Pitted or stuck contacts can leave the fan unpowered.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown high-voltage fuses** — No power reaching the condenser at all.
5. **Debris jamming the fan blade** — Sticks, ice, or a cracked blade physically blocking rotation.
6. **Loose or corroded wiring** — A disconnected fan lead or corroded terminal inside the disconnect or control box.
7. **Control board or ECM module fault (Infinity)** — On variable-speed units, the fan module or board can fail and stop driving the motor.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks you can safely do without opening the condenser or touching high-voltage parts:

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and lower the temperature several degrees. Confirm it's calling for cooling and, for Infinity wall controls, has battery/power.
- **Air filter:** A clogged indoor filter can cause freeze-ups and odd behavior. Replace if dirty.
- **Breaker:** Check both the indoor furnace/air-handler breaker and the outdoor AC breaker. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Visible debris:** With the system OFF at the breaker, look down through the top grille for obvious leaves, sticks, or ice on the fan blade — but do not reach in or spin the blade with power on.
- **Condensate:** A full condensate pan with a safety float switch can shut the system down; check the drain and pan indoors.
- **Vents:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked.

If the fan still won't spin after a single breaker reset, shut the system off and call an HVAC technician. Repeatedly resetting a unit that hums but won't run risks the compressor.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A pro should work through this sequence so you can sanity-check the visit:

1. **Verify power** — Confirms 240V at the disconnect and 24V control signal at the contactor.
2. **Test the capacitor** — Measures microfarads (µF) against the rating printed on the can. A reading well below spec confirms a weak capacitor.
3. **Spin test (power off)** — Turns the fan blade by hand to feel for bearing drag or a seized motor.
4. **Check the contactor** — Inspects for pitted contacts and confirms it pulls in when energized.
5. **Motor windings** — Uses a meter to check the fan motor for an open or shorted winding and proper resistance.
6. **Inspect wiring** — Looks for loose, burnt, or corroded fan leads.
7. **Infinity diagnostics** — On variable-speed units, reads fault history at the wall control and tests the ECM fan module.

A good tech won't simply swap the motor before testing the cheap capacitor first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan still | Failed capacitor | Shut off system; don't reset repeatedly | Test µF and replace capacitor |
| Fan dead, motor hot/burnt smell | Seized/burned fan motor | Turn off at breaker | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Outdoor unit fully dead | Tripped breaker / blown fuses | Reset breaker once | Find cause, replace fuses if needed |
| Fan blade visibly blocked | Debris or cracked blade | Power off, note debris | Clear jam or replace blade |
| Intermittent fan, clicking | Failing contactor | None — call pro | Replace contactor |
| Infinity fan fault logged | Control/ECM module fault | Note the code shown | Test board/ECM, replace as needed |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $300
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor (single-stage):** $350 – $650
- **Fan blade replacement:** $150 – $300
- **High-voltage fuses / wiring repair:** $100 – $250
- **Infinity ECM fan module or control board:** $400 – $900+ (model-specific)
- **DIY capacitor (capable, power off and discharged):** $15 – $40 for the part alone

Capacitors are the cheapest and most common fix. Anyone quoting you a full motor or board replacement should be able to show you a failed capacitor test first. Costs run higher in high-labor-rate metros and for hard-to-source Infinity parts.
