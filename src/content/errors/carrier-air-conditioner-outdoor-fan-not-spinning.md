## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numbered fault code — it's a physical symptom on your Carrier outdoor condenser unit. The large fan on top of the outdoor cabinet is supposed to spin whenever the system is calling for cooling. Its job is to pull air across the condenser coil and reject the heat your AC removed from inside the house.

When that fan stalls but the rest of the system tries to run, heat has nowhere to go. Refrigerant pressure climbs fast, the compressor overheats, and the unit will either trip a safety or — if it keeps running — risk permanent compressor damage. On Infinity communicating systems you may also see a related fault on the wall control referencing high pressure or an outdoor motor fault.

Because nearly every cause involves 240V power or a charged capacitor, this is a **pro-level repair**. The safe DIY steps below are limited to verifying it isn't a simpler problem first.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor** — The most common cause by far. The capacitor gives the fan motor its starting torque. When it weakens, the fan won't start on its own (but may spin if nudged) or won't start at all.
2. **Failed condenser fan motor** — Worn bearings, burned windings, or an open thermal overload. The motor may be hot, seized, or completely dead.
3. **Burned or stuck contactor** — The 24V-controlled relay that sends power to the condenser. Pitted contacts can prevent power from reaching the fan and compressor.
4. **Tripped disconnect or breaker** — The outdoor disconnect pulled, or a tripped breaker, cuts power to the whole condenser (fan and compressor both dead).
5. **Debris or obstruction** — A stick, ice, or debris physically jamming the fan blade, or a bent/seized blade.
6. **Wiring fault or rodent damage** — Chewed or loose wiring at the motor, capacitor, or contactor.
7. **Control board / low-voltage fault** — On Infinity and some Performance boards, a control fault can withhold the fan command. Less common but real.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the electrical panel on the condenser.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. For Infinity controls, check the screen for any displayed fault message.
- **Air filter:** A clogged filter can cause freezing and pressure trips that mimic fan problems. Replace if dirty.
- **Indoor breaker(s):** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Look at the disconnect box on the wall near the condenser. If the pull-block is loose or removed, reseat it firmly. Do not open the unit's panels.
- **Visible obstruction:** With the system **off at the breaker and disconnect**, look down through the top grille for obvious debris (leaves, a stick, a ball) jamming the blade. Remove only what you can reach safely from outside the grille.
- **Condensate / vents:** Make sure indoor supply registers are open and the condensate drain isn't backed up, since freezing can shut the system down.

If the fan still won't spin after these checks, **turn the system off and call a technician** — don't keep running it.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a predictable order. Use this to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Confirms power** at the disconnect and contactor with a meter (verifies 240V is reaching the unit).
2. **Tests the run capacitor** with a capacitance meter against its rated microfarads (printed on the can). A reading well below spec means replacement.
3. **Checks the contactor** for pitting, proper 24V pull-in, and continuity across the contacts.
4. **Tests the fan motor** — checks windings for shorts/opens, spins the shaft for bearing drag, and verifies the thermal overload hasn't tripped.
5. **Inspects wiring** for loose, burned, or rodent-damaged connections at the motor and capacitor.
6. **On Infinity/communicating systems,** reads stored faults at the wall control and verifies the outdoor board is commanding the fan.

A capacitor test takes minutes. If a tech wants to replace the motor without first testing the capacitor, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan won't start but spins if nudged | Weak/dead run capacitor | Shut unit off; don't run it | Test & replace capacitor |
| Compressor hums, fan dead | Capacitor or motor failure | Turn off to protect compressor | Test capacitor, then motor |
| Whole condenser dead (no fan, no compressor) | Tripped breaker/disconnect or contactor | Reset breaker once; reseat disconnect | Test contactor & incoming power |
| Fan blade physically stuck | Debris or seized bearing | Remove visible debris with power off | Clear jam or replace motor |
| Buzzing at contactor, no fan | Stuck/pitted contactor | None — call pro | Replace contactor |
| Infinity control shows fault | Board or motor fault | Note the message; power off | Read fault, test outdoor board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor. Prices vary by region and unit age.

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $350. The single most common and least expensive fix.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350.
- **Condenser fan motor replacement:** $350 – $650 (more for ECM motors on higher-end units).
- **Wiring repair / rodent damage:** $150 – $400 depending on extent.
- **Outdoor control board (Infinity/communicating):** $400 – $700+.
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90 – $180, often credited toward the repair.

If the same motor and capacitor have failed together — common when one takes out the other — expect the higher end of the motor range. Always insist the capacitor be tested before any motor replacement is approved.
