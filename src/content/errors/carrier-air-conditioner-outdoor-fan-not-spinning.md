## What this code means

"Outdoor fan not spinning" isn't a numeric fault code on most Carrier air conditioners — it's a symptom you observe at the condenser (the outdoor unit). When cooling is calling, the outdoor unit should run two things: the **compressor** and the **condenser fan motor** on top. If the fan blade sits still while the unit hums, clicks, or stays dead, something in the fan's power path or the motor itself has failed.

This matters because the outdoor fan pulls air across the condenser coil to dump the heat your system pulls out of the house. With no airflow, refrigerant pressure and compressor temperature climb fast. Carrier compressors have internal overload protection that will shut the unit down, but repeated overheating shortens compressor life. **Turn the system off at the thermostat until it's fixed.**

Note: Carrier **Infinity variable-speed condensers** behave differently and may show a fault at the wall control instead of just a silent fan. The causes below focus on standard single-stage and two-stage Carrier condensers with a dual-run capacitor and 24V contactor.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Failed run capacitor.** The single most common cause. The dual-run capacitor provides the starting and running boost for both the fan motor and compressor. When the fan side fails, the fan won't spin (often you'll hear the compressor hum).
2. **Seized or burned-out condenser fan motor.** Bearings fail, windings open, or the motor overheats. The blade may spin freely by hand but won't start under power, or it's stiff/locked.
3. **Bad contactor.** The contactor is the relay that sends high-voltage power to the outdoor unit. Pitted, stuck, or non-pulling contacts can starve the fan (and compressor) of power.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown high-voltage fuses** at the disconnect box beside the unit.
5. **Debris jamming the fan blade** — sticks, ice, or a warped blade physically blocking rotation.
6. **Failed control board or relay** (more common on Infinity/Performance units with electronics) or a wiring/connection fault.
7. **Low-voltage / thermostat call problem** — if the contactor never pulls in, nothing outdoors runs.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not open the electrical access panel on the condenser** — the capacitor stores a lethal charge even with power off.

- **Thermostat:** Set to COOL and the temperature several degrees below room temp. Confirm the system is actually calling for cooling. Replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Disconnect by the unit:** Make sure the outdoor disconnect switch hasn't been pulled or left off.
- **Air filter & indoor vents:** A clogged filter or closed vents won't stop the outdoor fan, but they cause similar "not cooling" complaints — verify the filter is clean and supply vents are open.
- **Visible debris:** With power **off at the breaker and disconnect**, look down through the top grille for obvious sticks, leaves, or ice on the blade. Do not reach in or open panels.
- **Condensate:** Check that the indoor drain pan isn't overflowing (some systems shut down on a full pan).

If the fan still won't spin after these checks, it's a pro repair.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence — use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirms the cooling call** and verifies 24V is reaching the contactor coil.
2. **Checks the contactor** — looks for the contacts pulling in, measures voltage across them, inspects for pitting or welding.
3. **Tests the run capacitor** with a meter (microfarad reading vs. the rating printed on the can). A capacitor reading well below spec is the verdict.
4. **Tests the fan motor** — checks winding resistance, looks for an open or grounded winding, and whether the blade spins freely by hand. A motor that's good but won't start points back to the capacitor.
5. **Checks high-voltage fuses** at the disconnect and inspects wiring/connections for burns or loose terminals.
6. **On Infinity/Performance boards:** reads any stored fault and tests the board's fan relay output.

A capacitor and contactor test takes minutes. Be cautious of any quote that jumps straight to a full motor or board replacement without testing the capacitor first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compressor hums, fan blade still | Failed run capacitor | Shut unit off; don't run it | Test & replace dual-run capacitor |
| Fan blade spins freely by hand but won't start | Bad capacitor or weak motor | Shut off, call pro | Cap test; replace cap or motor |
| Blade is stiff/locked by hand | Seized fan motor or bearings | Shut off | Replace condenser fan motor |
| Nothing runs outdoors, no hum | Tripped breaker, bad contactor, no 24V | Reset breaker once; check disconnect | Test contactor, low-voltage, board |
| Breaker trips immediately on reset | Shorted motor, capacitor, or wiring | Stop resetting; call pro | Find & repair the short |
| Stick/ice jamming blade | Physical obstruction | Power off, clear visible debris | Inspect for blade/motor damage |
| Infinity unit shows fault at wall control | Board/motor fault | Note the code, call pro | Read fault, test board output |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical installed US prices; your area, model, and parts availability will shift them.

- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $350. The most common and least expensive fix.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $300.
- **Condenser fan motor replacement:** $300 – $650+, depending on motor type (OEM vs. universal) and whether a new blade is needed.
- **Fan blade only:** $100 – $250.
- **High-voltage fuse / disconnect repair:** $100 – $250.
- **Control board (Performance/Infinity):** $400 – $900+.
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90 – $180, often credited toward the repair.

A capacitor is sometimes a confident DIYer's job, but it stores a dangerous charge and lives behind high-voltage terminals — which is why this page is rated **pro**. If you're not trained to safely discharge it, don't open that panel.
