## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code on York equipment — it's a symptom. Your York air conditioner may be doing one of several things: running constantly without lowering the temperature, blowing room-temperature or warm air, short-cycling (turning on and off quickly), or not turning on at all.

York condensers and air handlers don't share one universal diagnostic display. Some newer **Affinity** series units have communicating controls with fault history, while many **LX** and **Latitude** series units rely on simpler control boards or have no fault display at all. Because of this, "not cooling" is diagnosed by symptom and measurement rather than a single error code — the exact indicators vary by model and board generation.

This page walks you through the likely causes, the few checks you can safely make yourself, and how a pro will confirm the real problem so you can sanity-check any quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode (not on COOL), dead batteries, bad programming, or a failing thermostat.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the condenser or air handler has lost power.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit runs but blows little or no cold air.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a common, age-related failure that prevents the compressor or fan motor from starting.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser fails to close, so the outdoor unit won't engage.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling and often freezes the coil. Requires an EPA-certified tech.
8. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil is caked with dirt/debris and can't reject heat.
9. **Clogged condensate drain / float switch tripped** — a safety switch may shut the system down to prevent water damage.
10. **Failed condenser fan motor or compressor** — less common, more expensive, and the most serious outcomes.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should perform. If none of them resolve it, stop and call a licensed technician.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone can stop cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the target temperature is below the room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Make sure the fan is set to AUTO or ON.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker labeled for the AC or air handler. You may reset it **once**. If it trips again, leave it off and call a pro — repeated trips signal a real fault.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unobstructed by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers. Blocked airflow can freeze the coil.
- **Condensate drain:** Check for standing water in the drain pan or near the air handler. If a float switch has tripped on a full pan, clearing a visible clog and emptying the pan may restore operation. Don't disable the switch.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to OFF (fan ON) and let it thaw fully — this can take several hours — before running it again.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent York technician should work through a logical sequence — knowing this helps you judge whether a quote is fair:

1. **Verify the complaint** and check thermostat settings and call for cooling.
2. **Confirm power** at the condenser disconnect, breaker, and low-voltage (24V) circuit.
3. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower operation, and coil condition.
4. **Measure the capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarads, and test the contactor for proper pull-in.
5. **Check temperature split** (return vs. supply air) to gauge actual cooling performance.
6. **Connect gauges** to read system pressures and superheat/subcooling — this reveals low refrigerant, overcharge, or restrictions. (Refrigerant access requires EPA certification.)
7. **If low on refrigerant, perform a leak search** — topping off without finding the leak is bad practice you should refuse to pay for.
8. **Test the compressor and condenser fan motor** under load if everything else checks out.

Ask for the measured values (capacitor reading, temperature split, pressures). A good tech will share them.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs constantly, weak airflow | Dirty filter or frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw coil with fan ON | Diagnose airflow/refrigerant cause |
| Blowing warm air, outdoor unit silent | Failed contactor or capacitor | Check breaker once | Test and replace contactor/capacitor |
| Outdoor unit humming, won't start | Failed run/start capacitor | None — call a pro | Replace capacitor, verify motor |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| System dead, no response | Tripped breaker or float switch | Reset breaker once; check drain pan | Find cause of trip; repair |
| Short-cycling on and off | Low refrigerant, electrical, or control fault | None — stop resetting | Measure pressures and controls |
| Weak cooling on hot days only | Dirty condenser coil or low charge | Rinse outdoor coil gently with hose | Pressure test, clean, recharge |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined. Your price varies by region and contractor.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** under $10 (DIY)
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$400
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$250
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ (depends on leak location and refrigerant type; R-22 systems cost more)
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ — on an older unit, replacing the whole system is often more cost-effective.

If your York unit is over 12–15 years old and needs a compressor or major refrigerant repair, ask your contractor to compare repair cost against a full system replacement.

## Related codes

- **York AC not turning on** — total no-power conditions (breaker, contactor, transformer).
- **York AC frozen coil / icing up** — airflow and refrigerant-related freezing.
- **York AC short-cycling** — rapid on/off, often electrical or charge-related.
- **York thermostat communication fault** (Affinity communicating systems) — control-board level issues that vary by model.

If your specific York model has a fault LED or display, note the blink pattern or message and reference your unit's installation manual, since indicators differ across the LX, Affinity, and Latitude lines.
