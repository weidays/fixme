## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code on most York systems — it's a symptom. Your York air conditioner may be running (the indoor blower and/or outdoor fan turn on) but the air coming from the vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Some newer York communicating systems (Affinity series with a touchscreen control or compatible thermostat) display diagnostic messages or fault codes that point to a specific cause. **Most standard York split systems do not have an on-unit fault display**, so "not cooling" is diagnosed by symptoms and measurements, not a readout. Where your model shows a code, write it down — it narrows the search considerably.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (Heat instead of Cool), or a setpoint above room temperature.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser has its own breaker that may have tripped.
4. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, it can't reject heat.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan won't start or hums.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the indoor coil or copper line.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling and is a top cause on older systems. Requires a licensed tech.
8. **Failed contactor or control board** — the outdoor unit doesn't energize.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit runs but produces no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is unclear.

- **Check the air filter.** If it's gray or clogged, replace it. This alone fixes many "not cooling" calls.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the fan is on **Auto**, and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check the breakers.** Look at both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Check supply and return vents.** Make sure none are closed, blocked by furniture, or covered by rugs.
- **Check for ice.** If you see ice on the indoor coil or the copper line at the outdoor unit, **turn the system off (fan on)** and let it thaw for a few hours. Running a frozen system can damage the compressor.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A full drain pan or a clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut cooling down. Clear visible standing water and check that the drain line isn't plugged.
- **Rinse the outdoor coil** gently with a garden hose (power off) if it's visibly dirty.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps shutting off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York technician follows a logical sequence. Use this to sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verifies the complaint** and checks thermostat settings and call for cooling.
2. **Measures airflow and temperature split** across the indoor coil (typically a 16–22°F drop on a healthy system).
3. **Reads any fault codes** on communicating Affinity boards or checks the blower/board for indicator lights.
4. **Tests the capacitor and contactor** with a meter — quick, cheap checks.
5. **Connects refrigerant gauges** and measures superheat/subcooling to determine if charge is correct. Low charge means a leak search.
6. **Inspects the compressor** — windings, amp draw, and whether it starts.
7. **Provides a written diagnosis** before major repairs.

Be cautious of any quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without mentioning a leak search — adding refrigerant to a leaking system is a temporary patch, not a repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Check ductwork, blower |
| No air at all from vents | Blower or board failure | Check breaker, reset once | Test board, blower motor |
| Outdoor fan off, indoor on | Tripped breaker / capacitor | Reset breaker once | Replace capacitor/contactor |
| Ice on coil or line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, change filter | Find leak, recharge |
| Unit hums but won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Replace capacitor |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Low refrigerant or bad compressor | None — call pro | Leak search / compressor test |
| Water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible clog | Clear/flush drain, check float |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries or no power | Replace batteries | Check transformer/wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; regional and model variation applies):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Condenser coil deep cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 (more for an evaporator coil)
- **Control board (communicating Affinity):** $400–$900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+
- **Full system replacement** (if compressor fails on an old unit): often more economical at $5,000–$12,000

Get a written diagnosis. On units more than 12–15 years old with a failed compressor or major leak, ask whether repair or replacement makes more sense.

## Related codes

- **York AC freezing up / ice on coil** — low airflow or low refrigerant
- **York condenser fan not spinning** — capacitor or fan motor
- **York Affinity communicating fault codes** — model-specific board messages
- **York AC short cycling** — capacitor, thermostat, or refrigerant issues
- **York heat pump not heating** — reversing valve or defrost faults

*If your York unit displays an actual fault code on a communicating control, note it exactly — codes vary by board and model year, and the on-screen message is the most reliable starting point.*
