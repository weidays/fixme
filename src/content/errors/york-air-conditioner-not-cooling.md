## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numeric fault code on most York units — it's a symptom homeowners report when the system runs but the house won't reach the thermostat setpoint, or the air from the vents feels warm. York condensers do not display the kind of LED blink codes that some furnaces use, so diagnosis relies on measured performance (temperature split, pressures, amp draw) rather than a code lookup.

Behavior varies by equipment. Single-stage York condensers either run or they don't. Two-stage Affinity-series units may run in low stage longer than expected, which can feel like weak cooling even when nothing is broken. If your system has a communicating control board, some fault information may appear at the thermostat — check your specific model's manual, because that messaging is not consistent across generations.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, and dryer lint block heat rejection, so the system can't dump heat outside.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — if only the indoor blower runs, the outdoor unit may have lost power.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes warm air, long run times, and sometimes icing on the line.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common, inexpensive failure that prevents the compressor or fan motor from starting.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit runs but no cold air moves.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit can stick or burn out.
8. **Clogged condensate drain triggering a float switch** — a full safety switch can shut down cooling on some systems.
9. **Thermostat fault or dead batteries** — wrong mode, wrong setpoint, or a failing thermostat.
10. **Failing compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit runs but produces little or no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. If a step doesn't fix it, stop and call a pro.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Set the fan to AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look. If it's gray with dust or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone can stop cooling.
- **Breaker:** Check the electrical panel. Both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) circuits matter. If a breaker is tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a technician — do not keep resetting it.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse visible dirt off the coil fins with a garden hose (power off). Keep at least two feet of clearance on all sides.
- **Condensate drain:** If you have a wet-switch or float and the unit recently shut off, a full drain pan or clogged drain line may be the cause. Empty a visibly full pan.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to OFF (fan ON) and let it thaw fully before running again.

Do not open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or remove electrical panels on the condenser.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York technician should:

1. **Measure the temperature split** — the difference between return and supply air should typically be around 15–22°F. A low split points to refrigerant or airflow issues.
2. **Check refrigerant pressures** with gauges to determine if the charge is correct — and if it's low, search for the leak rather than just topping it off.
3. **Test the run capacitor and contactor** with a meter; these are quick, cheap finds.
4. **Measure compressor and fan motor amp draw** to confirm the compressor is actually pumping.
5. **Inspect the coils** (indoor and outdoor) for dirt and the evaporator for ice.
6. **Verify airflow** — blower speed, filter, and ductwork.
7. **Check control voltage and the contactor coil** to confirm the outdoor unit is being commanded on.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without finding a leak, or "replace the compressor" without amp readings, deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker, contactor, or capacitor | Reset breaker once; check thermostat | Test contactor, capacitor, control voltage |
| Warm air from vents, unit runs constantly | Low refrigerant or dirty outdoor coil | Rinse coil, replace filter | Pressure test, leak search, recharge |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find airflow/refrigerant root cause |
| Humming outside, fan not spinning | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Replace capacitor, verify amps |
| Weak cooling on a two-stage unit | Stuck in low stage (may be normal) | None | Verify staging and control logic |
| Unit shut off, water near air handler | Clogged condensate drain/float switch | Clear pan, add drain tablets | Clear line, test float switch |
| Nothing runs at all | Thermostat, batteries, or power | Replace batteries, check breaker | Diagnose control circuit |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5–$15 (DIY)
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$250
- **Outdoor coil cleaning (professional):** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often makes replacing the unit worth considering)

Prices vary by region, system size, and whether your unit is still under York's parts warranty — check your registration, as many York systems carry a 10-year parts warranty.
