## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single flashing fault code on York equipment — it's a symptom. Most residential York split-system air conditioners don't have a digital display on the outdoor unit, so "not cooling" describes what you're experiencing: the system runs (or tries to) but the house won't reach the thermostat setpoint, or the air from the vents isn't cold.

On systems paired with a communicating York thermostat or control board, you may see a fault indication or a status code in the thermostat menu instead. Because board behavior and fault reporting vary significantly by model and model year, check your specific thermostat or air handler manual for any displayed codes — the steps below apply to the underlying mechanical and electrical causes regardless of how your unit reports them.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — Restricted airflow is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — Wrong mode (Heat instead of Cool), dead batteries, or wrong fan setting.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — Outdoor unit or air handler has no power.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — From low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — Compressor or condenser fan won't start; common on units 8+ years old.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — The outdoor coil caked with dirt/grass can't reject heat.
7. **Failed contactor** — The relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
8. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Causes weak cooling, ice, and eventual compressor strain.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — Outdoor fan doesn't spin; unit overheats and shuts down.
10. **Compressor failure** — The most expensive cause; usually the last suspect after others are ruled out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Stop here if the problem isn't resolved.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the single most common cause.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. If it's a battery model, install fresh batteries.
- **Breaker:** Check the breaker panel for the air handler and the outdoor unit. If a breaker is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure vents aren't closed, blocked by furniture, or covered. Too many closed vents starves airflow.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look (don't touch) for ice on the lines or coil, heavy debris, or grass clippings blocking the coil fins. Clear loose leaves and yard debris with the power off.
- **Condensate drain:** A full drain pan or clogged drain line can trip a safety float switch and shut the system off. Check for standing water near the indoor unit.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **Off** and run the **fan only** for a few hours to thaw it before calling a tech.

**Do not** open the sealed refrigerant system, remove the electrical panel on the outdoor unit, discharge a capacitor, or attempt to add refrigerant.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech will work through these steps so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — Confirm setpoint, mode, and measure supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Check airflow** — Inspect filter, blower, and coil for ice or restriction.
3. **Check power and controls** — Test the contactor, low-voltage transformer, fuse, and confirm the thermostat is calling for cooling.
4. **Test the capacitor** — Use a meter to check microfarad rating against the label; a weak cap is a cheap, common find.
5. **Measure refrigerant charge** — Connect gauges, read pressures, and check superheat/subcooling. Low charge means a **leak search** (electronic detector or dye), not just a "top-off."
6. **Inspect the compressor and condenser fan** — Confirm both start and draw correct amperage.

A good tech finds the *root cause* of low refrigerant — not just refills it. Be wary of any quote to add refrigerant without a leak search.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; run fan to thaw | Check airflow, charge, coil |
| Nothing runs at all | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once; check thermostat batteries | Test transformer, fuse, contactor |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit silent | Failed capacitor or contactor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor/contactor |
| Ice on refrigerant lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or refrigerant leak |
| Weak cooling, high bills | Low refrigerant / dirty condenser | Rinse loose debris off outdoor unit | Leak search, recharge, coil cleaning |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Bad fan motor or capacitor | None — call a pro | Replace fan motor or capacitor |
| Water near indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible standing water; add drain tablets | Clear drain line, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (varies by region and model):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 (depends on leak location; R-454B units on 2025+ models may cost more)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,000–$1,800+ (often worth weighing against full system replacement on older units)

If your unit is still under York's parts warranty, the part may be covered but **labor usually is not** — expect to pay the labor portion.
