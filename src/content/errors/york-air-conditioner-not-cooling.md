## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a specific York fault code — it's the symptom you notice when the system runs but the air coming from your vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint. On York units with diagnostic boards (common on **Affinity** series), a related fault may flash on the control board or a connected communicating thermostat, but many **LX** and **Acclaim** units have no on-board display, so you diagnose by symptom.

The root cause falls into one of three buckets: an **airflow problem** (filter, coil, blower), a **refrigerant problem** (leak or low charge), or an **electrical problem** (capacitor, contactor, compressor, control board). The checks below help you rule out the cheap, safe causes before paying for a service call.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause; restricts airflow and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Thermostat set wrong** — fan set to "ON" instead of "AUTO," or mode left on "FAN"/"HEAT" instead of "COOL."
3. **Tripped breaker or blown outdoor fuse** — the outdoor unit (condenser) has lost power while the indoor blower still runs.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common York failure; the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system runs constantly but barely cools; often shows a frozen coil or hissing.
6. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, it can't reject heat.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit; pitted or stuck contacts stop the condenser.
8. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat exchange.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — outdoor fan doesn't spin, causing the unit to overheat and shut down.
10. **Failed compressor or control board** — least common but most expensive.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Air filter:** Pull it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling and can freeze the coil.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm mode is **COOL**, fan is **AUTO**, and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. If it's a battery thermostat, swap in fresh batteries.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. There are usually two — one for the indoor air handler/furnace, one for the outdoor condenser. Reset **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure registers aren't closed, blocked by furniture, or covered. Closing too many vents starves airflow.
- **Outdoor unit clearance:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside of the coil fins with a garden hose (power off) if they look dirty — no pressure washer.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trigger a float switch that shuts the system off. If you see standing water in the drain pan, that's a clue to mention to your technician.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** and the fan to **ON** for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter. If it freezes again, you have an airflow or refrigerant problem that needs a pro.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps shutting off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent York technician will typically:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measure supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Check airflow** — inspect filter, blower wheel, and both coils for restriction or ice.
3. **Test electrical components** — measure the run capacitor's microfarads against the rated value, inspect the contactor for pitting, and confirm voltage at the compressor.
4. **Connect refrigerant gauges** — read suction and head pressures and calculate superheat/subcooling to determine if charge is correct. If low, they'll leak-search rather than just "top off."
5. **Test the compressor and fan motors** — check amp draw against the nameplate.
6. **Read the control board** — on Affinity and communicating systems, pull stored fault codes.

If a quote jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search, or "replace the compressor" without showing you readings, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower and ducting |
| Indoor unit runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Replace fuse, find electrical fault |
| Ice on coil or refrigerant lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Thaw, replace filter | Leak search and recharge |
| Outdoor fan won't spin, humming | Failed capacitor or fan motor | None — call pro | Test/replace capacitor or motor |
| Runs constantly, barely cools | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call pro | Leak repair and EPA-certified recharge |
| Outdoor unit hot, then shuts off | Dirty condenser coil or fan failure | Rinse coil, clear debris | Clean coil, test motor |
| Nothing happens at all | Contactor or control board | Check thermostat/breaker | Replace contactor or board |
| Loud hum, won't start at all | Failed compressor | None — call pro | Compressor test/replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and model):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$300
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Coil cleaning (professional):** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing against a new condenser if the unit is old or out of warranty)

Many York units carry a **10-year limited parts warranty** if registered — check your serial number before paying out of pocket for a compressor or coil.
