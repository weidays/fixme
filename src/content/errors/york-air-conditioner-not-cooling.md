## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific York fault code. Most York residential air conditioners are relatively simple split systems and do not display a digital error code on the condenser for cooling problems — the diagnosis comes from observing the system's behavior.

In practice "not cooling" falls into one of three buckets:

- **The system runs but the air isn't cold** — usually airflow, refrigerant, or coil issues.
- **The outdoor unit won't start** — usually electrical: capacitor, contactor, breaker, or control signal.
- **The indoor blower runs but the outdoor unit doesn't, or vice versa** — usually a control wiring or thermostat issue.

If your York system uses a communicating control board (some Affinity-series setups), the indoor furnace/air handler control may flash an LED fault code. Codes vary by board, so check the label inside the access panel rather than assuming.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a setpoint that isn't calling for cooling.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit or air handler has no power.
4. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil can't reject heat, so the house never cools.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — weak cooling, longer run times, ice on the lines, often a hissing leak.
7. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; blocks cooling entirely.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the fan won't spin, the unit overheats and shuts down.
10. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; the unit won't cool at all and may trip the breaker.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here and call a pro for anything involving refrigerant, opening sealed electrical components, or repeated breaker trips.

- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL, lower the setpoint 3–5°F below room temperature, and confirm the fan is on AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and check it against a light. If it's gray or you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the single most common cause.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for a tripped breaker for the AC and the air handler/furnace. Reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a technician.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many vents starves the system.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser so it can breathe. Don't poke anything into the fan or coil.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain with a full safety float switch can shut down cooling. If you see standing water near the indoor unit, clear visible blockages and check the float switch.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to OFF and the fan to ON for an hour to thaw it, then run cooling again. If it re-freezes, you have a deeper problem.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York technician should:

1. Confirm the thermostat is calling for cooling and that 24V reaches the contactor.
2. Verify power and check the capacitor with a meter — a weak capacitor reads below its rated microfarads.
3. Inspect the contactor for pitting and confirm it pulls in when the system calls.
4. Measure indoor airflow and inspect the evaporator coil and filter.
5. Connect refrigerant gauges to read suction and head pressures, then calculate **superheat and subcooling** to judge the charge.
6. Check temperature split (return air vs. supply air) — a healthy system runs roughly 16–22°F.
7. If refrigerant is low, perform a leak search rather than just topping off.
8. Test the compressor's windings and amp draw if startup fails.

If a tech quotes a refrigerant "recharge" without mentioning a leak search, ask why — refrigerant doesn't get used up, it leaks out.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air from vents isn't cold | Dirty filter, low refrigerant, dirty coil | Replace filter, clean around condenser | Check charge, find leak, clean coil |
| Outdoor unit won't start, hums | Failed run capacitor | None — capacitors hold charge | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit dead, no fan, no hum | No power, tripped breaker, bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Replace filter, thaw with fan only | Diagnose airflow or refrigerant cause |
| Fan motor hot, won't spin | Failed condenser fan motor or capacitor | Power off the unit | Replace motor and/or capacitor |
| Unit trips breaker repeatedly | Compressor or electrical short | Stop resetting, shut off | Test compressor and wiring |
| Weak cooling, long run times | Dirty condenser coil, low charge | Rinse coil exterior gently | Measure subcooling, deep coil clean |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY).
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$700 installed.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ — often worth comparing against full system replacement on older units.

If your York unit is 12–15+ years old and faces a compressor or coil failure, get a replacement quote alongside the repair. Older units may also use R-22 refrigerant, which is expensive and being phased out.

## Related codes

- **York furnace/air handler LED fault codes** — check the indoor control board if the blower behaves oddly.
- **Thermostat "Cool On" with no cold air** — points to outdoor unit or refrigerant issues.
- **Frozen evaporator coil** — overlaps heavily with low airflow and low refrigerant causes above.
- **Condensate float switch lockout** — a clogged drain can stop cooling without any electrical fault.
