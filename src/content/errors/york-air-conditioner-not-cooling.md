## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a fault code in the traditional sense — your York air conditioner doesn't always flash a number on a board for it. Instead, it's a symptom: the system is powered and may be running, but the air coming from your vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

On communicating York systems (Affinity series with a YXV/YXT control), you may see a fault or status code on the thermostat or indoor control board that points to a specific cause — low charge, a locked rotor, or a pressure-switch trip. On older or single-stage units (LX, Latitude), there's often no display at all, and diagnosis is done by measurement. **Behavior varies by model and whether your unit has a communicating board — check your thermostat and indoor control for any displayed codes before assuming.**

The root causes fall into a handful of buckets: airflow problems, refrigerant problems, electrical/component failures, and controls. The sections below rank them.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common reason. Restricted airflow reduces cooling and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant. Ice on the coil blocks cooling entirely.
3. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan motor won't start. You may hear a hum or clicking outside with no fan spin.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system runs constantly but blows cool-not-cold air. Refrigerant doesn't get "used up," so low charge means a leak.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil is caked with dirt/grass, so the system can't reject heat.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck, so the condenser never energizes.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a control signal not reaching the unit.
9. **Compressor failure** — least common but most expensive; the heart of the system has failed.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are all things a homeowner can do safely:

- **Air filter** — pull it and look. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker** — check the panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated trips mean a real fault.
- **Outdoor unit** — make sure it isn't smothered by leaves, grass clippings, or a privacy fence too close. Keep two feet of clearance on all sides.
- **Supply and return vents** — confirm they're open and not blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain** — if the system shut off and the drain pan is full, a safety float switch may have tripped. Clear the drain line and pan; standing water and slime are common.
- **Frozen coil** — if you see ice on the indoor coil or the outdoor lines, switch the system to **OFF** or **fan-only** for a few hours to thaw, then replace the filter and run again. If it refreezes, call a pro.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass safety switches, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps shutting down.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York tech follows a logical path so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measures supply vs. return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Check airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Reads any displayed codes** — on communicating Affinity boards, pulls the fault history.
4. **Tests electrical** — measures capacitor microfarads, checks the contactor, verifies voltage at the compressor and incoming line voltage.
5. **Gauges refrigerant** — connects manifold gauges, reads pressures, and calculates superheat/subcooling to determine if charge is low and whether there's a leak.
6. **Leak search** — if low, uses electronic detector or dye to find the leak point.
7. **Compressor test** — checks amp draw, windings, and start behavior if everything upstream is good.

Expect a diagnostic fee of **$75–$200**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower & ductwork |
| Ice on indoor coil or lines | Low airflow or low charge | Fan-only to thaw, new filter | Find airflow restriction or leak, recharge |
| Outdoor fan won't spin, humming | Failed run capacitor | None | Test & replace capacitor |
| Runs constantly, cool not cold | Low refrigerant (leak) | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor coil dirty/clogged | Dirty condenser | Gently rinse with hose (power off) | Deep coil cleaning |
| Outdoor unit dead, indoor runs | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| No response at all | Thermostat / low-voltage fuse | Check mode & batteries | Replace fuse, trace control wiring |
| Loud hum, no cooling, trips breaker | Compressor failure | None | Test & replace compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0 DIY – $150 pro
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,200 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs older systems)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing against a full system replacement if the unit is out of warranty)

If your York unit is within its 10-year parts warranty (typical when registered), the part may be covered — you'd pay labor only. Check your registration.
