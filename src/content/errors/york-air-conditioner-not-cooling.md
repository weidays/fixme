## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a specific York fault code — it is the symptom you see when your York central air conditioner runs but the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint, or the air at the vents isn't cold. Most York residential split systems don't display alphanumeric error codes at the outdoor unit; instead, the failure shows up as warm air, short cycling, or the outdoor unit not starting.

If you have a York system paired with a communicating thermostat or board (some Affinity systems), you may see a thermostat-side message instead. The underlying causes below are the same regardless of how it's reported.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter or blocked airflow** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so no cold air comes out.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or condenser fan hums or won't start.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity; never "tops off" without finding the leak.
5. **Dirty condenser coil (outdoor unit)** — caked with grass, dust or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or fails, so the compressor never engages.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — kills power to the outdoor unit or control board.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring issue** — bad batteries, wrong mode, or a broken control wire.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the compressor runs but overheats and shuts down on internal protection.
10. **Compressor failure** — least common but most expensive; the unit draws power but produces no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This is the #1 cause and a free fix.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Vents and returns:** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Breaker:** Check the panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated trips indicate an electrical fault.
- **Condensate drain:** If a float switch is full, the system may shut off. Check for standing water in the drain pan and clear a visibly clogged drain line if accessible.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for ice on the lines or coil, and clear leaves or grass from around the condenser. If you see ice, switch the system to FAN ONLY and let it thaw fully before running it again.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech should work through a logical sequence so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measure supply vs. return air temperature (the "delta-T," typically 16-22°F on a healthy system).
2. **Check airflow** — inspect filter, blower, and coil for ice or debris.
3. **Test electrical** — measure capacitor microfarads against the rating, check the contactor, and confirm voltage to the compressor and fan.
4. **Read refrigerant pressures** — connect gauges to check superheat and subcooling, which reveal undercharge, overcharge, or a leak.
5. **Leak search** — if low on refrigerant, use a leak detector or dye rather than just adding refrigerant.
6. **Inspect the compressor and fan motor** — test amperage draw and windings if those components are suspect.

A good tech explains the measured numbers, not just "it needs Freon." Be wary of a quote to add refrigerant with no mention of finding the leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, weak airflow | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Set to fan-only to thaw, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or leak, recharge |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — high voltage | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit totally dead | Tripped breaker or bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Compressor runs, no cold air | Low refrigerant from leak | None | Leak detection and repair, recharge |
| Outdoor fan not spinning | Failed fan motor or capacitor | None | Replace motor/capacitor |
| House warm, thermostat blank | Dead batteries or low-voltage fuse | Replace batteries | Replace fuse, trace short |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Undercharge or compressor fault | None | Pressure test, evaluate compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Your price varies by region and tonnage:

- **Air filter:** $10-$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries / minor reset:** $0-$15 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150-$450
- **Contactor:** $150-$350
- **Low-voltage fuse:** $75-$150
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400-$700
- **Coil cleaning / clearing frozen coil:** $150-$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $600-$2,000 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,000-$2,800
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200-$2,500+ (often worth comparing against a full system replacement)

If your system uses R-22 refrigerant (common on older York units), recharge costs are much higher due to phase-out — replacement may be the better value.
