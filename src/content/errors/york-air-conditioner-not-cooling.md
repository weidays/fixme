## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom your York air conditioner is running, partially running, or cycling but failing to lower your home's temperature. On York systems with diagnostic boards (common on Affinity and Acclimate communicating models), you may see a specific fault flash or a code on the thermostat. On older or single-stage units, there's often no code at all — just warm air from the vents.

Because the exact diagnostic indicators **vary by model and control board generation**, treat this page as a general guide to the underlying causes rather than a lookup for one specific blink pattern. If your indoor thermostat or outdoor board shows a numeric or lettered code, note it for your technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (Heat vs. Cool), or a setpoint that's too high.
3. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser has lost power while the indoor blower still runs.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan motor won't start, so no heat is rejected outdoors.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — caked dirt and debris outside prevent the system from shedding heat.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity and often causes coil icing.
8. **Failing contactor or control board** — the compressor never energizes.
9. **Compressor failure** — the most expensive cause; the unit hums or trips but won't cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner to perform:

- **Air filter** — pull it and check for dust buildup. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. This alone fixes many "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on Auto. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker** — check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker serving the AC. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — that's a sign of an electrical fault.
- **Supply and return vents** — make sure they're open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit** — clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser; keep at least two feet of clearance.
- **Frozen coil** — if you see ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant lines, turn the system to **Off** (run the fan only) for a few hours to let it thaw, then replace the filter. Recurrent icing means call a pro.
- **Condensate drain** — a clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut down cooling. Check for standing water at the indoor unit; a wet/dry vac on the drain line outlet can clear minor clogs.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York technician will work systematically, and knowing the steps helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measure supply and return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 16–22°F).
2. **Check airflow** — inspect filter, blower, and evaporator coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Read any board/thermostat codes** — interpret model-specific faults from the York control board.
4. **Test electrical components** — measure the capacitor, contactor, and confirm the compressor and condenser fan energize.
5. **Check refrigerant** — connect gauges, read pressures and superheat/subcooling. Low charge points to a leak, which they'll locate with electronic or dye leak detection.
6. **Inspect the compressor** — test windings and amp draw if it won't start.

Be cautious of any tech who tops off refrigerant without searching for a leak — that's a temporary fix, not a repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, indoor fan runs | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil | Clear airflow restriction, check charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor runs | Tripped breaker / contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor or repair wiring |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow / low refrigerant | Thaw, replace filter | Find/repair leak, recharge |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries / settings | Replace batteries, set to Cool | Replace thermostat if faulty |
| Standing water, system off | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain with wet/dry vac | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Weak cooling everywhere | Dirty condenser / low charge | Rinse condenser coil gently | Clean coil, check refrigerant |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Electrical/compressor fault | Stop resetting, call pro | Diagnose short, test compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts and labor; regional pricing varies):

- **Air filter replacement:** $0–$30 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries / settings:** $0–$10 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 (pro)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Control board:** $400–$900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing against a full condenser or system replacement on older units)

If your system is out of warranty and over 12–15 years old, a major repair like a compressor is a good moment to weigh replacement.
