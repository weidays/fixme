## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code on York equipment — it's the symptom you notice at the thermostat. Your York air conditioner may be running (the outdoor fan spins, air moves from the vents) but the air isn't cold, or the house never reaches the set temperature. Some York systems with communicating controls or smart thermostats will display a fault or lockout code, but many older or single-stage units simply underperform with no error at all.

Because "not cooling" covers everything from a $15 filter to a failed compressor, the goal here is to help you rule out the cheap, safe stuff yourself — then understand what a technician should be checking so you can sanity-check the quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat problem** — wrong mode (heat vs. cool), dead batteries, or a miswired/failing thermostat.
3. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
4. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — packed with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, so the unit can't reject heat.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor can't start or runs weakly.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling; refrigerant does not get "used up," so low charge means a leak.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown contactor** — outdoor unit gets no power, so only the indoor fan runs.
8. **Failing compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit runs but produces little or no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Air filter** — pull it out and look. If it's gray, clogged, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **COOL**, the fan is on **AUTO**, and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Breaker** — check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Supply and return vents** — make sure they're open and not blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Outdoor unit** — verify it isn't buried in leaves, grass clippings, or debris. Keep at least two feet of clearance around it.
- **Condensate drain** — a clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop the system. You can clear a visible clog or add drain tablets, but don't disassemble the unit.
- **Check for ice** — if you see frost or ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, **turn the system off** (fan can stay on) and let it thaw completely before running again.

Do not open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified York technician will typically:

- Confirm the symptom and check thermostat calls and control-board signals.
- Measure supply and return air temperature (the **temperature split** should be roughly 15–20°F on a healthy system).
- Inspect the air filter and evaporator coil for restriction or ice.
- Check the outdoor condenser coil and clean if needed.
- **Test the run/start capacitor** with a meter — a weak or failed capacitor is a fast, common find.
- Verify the contactor pulls in and the compressor and fan motor draw the correct amperage.
- Connect refrigerant gauges to read **superheat and subcooling**, confirming the charge and looking for signs of a leak.
- If a leak is suspected, perform a leak search (electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test).
- Assess the compressor's electrical health if all else checks out.

Ask for the measured temperature split and refrigerant readings — a good tech will share them.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty air filter | Replace the filter | Inspect coil and blower for restriction |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker or bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, test outdoor power |
| Ice on coil or copper lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, let thaw fully | Find airflow/refrigerant cause and repair |
| Unit hums but won't start | Failed run/start capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Cools poorly on hot days only | Low refrigerant or dirty condenser | Clear debris around unit | Check charge, find and fix leak |
| Wrong mode or blank display | Thermostat setting or dead battery | Set to COOL, replace batteries | Test/replace thermostat and wiring |
| Runs constantly, little cooling | Failing compressor | None — call a pro | Diagnose compressor, quote replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; regional and unit-size variation applies):

- **Air filter:** $10–$30 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5–$10 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0–$150
- **Run/start capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 (more if the coil must be replaced)
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often near the cost of replacing the unit)

If your York system is out of warranty and needs a compressor or coil, ask for a quote to replace the whole unit — sometimes it's the smarter spend.
