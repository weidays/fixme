## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's the symptom you see when your York air conditioner runs but the air coming out of your vents isn't cold (or isn't cold enough). York central systems don't have a universal display code for this; instead, the cause shows up as weak airflow, warm air, short cycling, or an outdoor unit that won't start.

Some newer York communicating systems (Affinity with the Hx3 or affinity touchscreen thermostat) will display fault codes on the thermostat. Older single-stage units have no display at all — you diagnose by symptom and by what each part is doing. Because the underlying causes range from a $20 filter to a $1,800 compressor, this is rated **pro**: the safe checks are simple, but the actual repair almost always needs a licensed HVAC technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause of weak or warm airflow. Restricts air across the indoor coil and can freeze it solid.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat and cooling output drops.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit (or indoor air handler) may have lost power while the thermostat still calls for cooling.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — common on the compressor or condenser fan; unit hums but won't start, or fan won't spin.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, ice on the lines, longer run times. Requires a tech and leak repair.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks and won't pull in.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor or blower motor** — no airflow over one of the coils.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit won't cool at all despite power.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should perform. **Do not** open the electrical panel of the outdoor unit, touch the capacitor, or add refrigerant.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone can cause warm air and a frozen coil.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank.
- **Breakers:** Check your panel for a tripped breaker for the AC (often a double-pole 240V breaker). Reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated trips signal a fault.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit airflow:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a float switch and shut cooling off. Check for standing water in the drain pan or a clogged drain line. Drain tablets help prevent recurrence.
- **If the coil is iced over:** Turn the system OFF (set thermostat to OFF, leave the fan ON) and let it thaw fully for a few hours before running again. Running a frozen unit can damage the compressor.

If cooling doesn't return after these checks, the next steps need a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the process helps you sanity-check any quote:

1. **Confirms the call:** Verifies the thermostat is calling for cooling and 24V signal reaches the outdoor unit.
2. **Checks power and capacitor:** Tests incoming voltage, the contactor, and the run capacitor with a meter — a quick, cheap fix if that's the culprit.
3. **Measures airflow and filter/coil state:** Inspects the evaporator and condenser coils for blockage or ice.
4. **Connects refrigerant gauges:** Reads suction and head pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge the refrigerant charge. Low readings point to a leak.
5. **Leak search if charge is low:** Uses an electronic detector or dye to find the leak — a recharge without finding the leak is a red flag.
6. **Tests motors and compressor:** Checks amp draw on the condenser fan, blower, and compressor windings to confirm a failed motor or compressor.

A good tech explains *why* a part failed, not just *that* it failed. Be cautious of any quote that jumps straight to "you need refrigerant" without a leak search, or "you need a new compressor" without showing you amp/winding readings.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warmish air | Dirty filter | Replace the filter | Confirm coil isn't iced |
| Warm air, outdoor unit running | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse debris from outside | Deep coil clean, charge check |
| No air from any vent | Blower motor / power loss | Check breaker, filter | Test blower motor & capacitor |
| Ice on coil or refrigerant lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Leak search & recharge |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Outdoor fan won't spin | Capacitor or fan motor | None — call a pro | Replace capacitor/fan motor |
| Unit clicks but won't power on | Failed contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor |
| Gradual loss of cooling over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None — call a pro | Locate leak, repair, recharge |
| No cooling at all, power present | Failed compressor | None — call a pro | Compressor test & replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included. Prices vary by region and contractor.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $0 DIY rinse – $150–$300 professional cleaning
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$300
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Blower motor:** $400–$900 (more for variable-speed/ECM)
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800 (often worth weighing against a new system on older units)

If your York unit is more than 12–15 years old and faces a compressor or major coil repair, ask for a replacement quote to compare — repair costs can approach the value of a new system.
