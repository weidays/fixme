## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a single fault code. Most modern York condensers don't flash a blink code for "no cooling" the way a furnace board does — instead, the system runs (or tries to run) while failing to lower the indoor temperature. On York variable-speed inverter models (Affinity YXV, some Acclimate units), the outdoor control board *can* log fault codes for things like low pressure, high pressure, or communication loss, which a technician reads with a service tool or on-board display.

Because behavior varies by model, treat "not cooling" as a starting point. The underlying cause is almost always one of: restricted airflow, a frozen coil, an electrical component failure, low refrigerant, or a thermostat/control issue.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the most common and most overlooked cause. Restricted airflow drops cooling capacity and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat problem** — wrong mode (HEAT vs COOL), dead batteries, bad programming, or a failed thermostat not calling for cooling.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit or air handler may have lost power on one circuit while the other still runs.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor hums or won't start, and cooling stops.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant line.
6. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, it can't reject heat, so the house won't cool.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces capacity and often freezes the coil. Requires a licensed tech.
8. **Failed contactor or compressor** — the contactor won't pull in, or the compressor has failed electrically or mechanically. This is the most expensive outcome.
9. **Clogged condensate drain (with float switch)** — a full pan trips a safety switch and shuts the system down on some installs.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only steps a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Check the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone can stop cooling and freeze the coil.
- **Verify the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Try lowering the setpoint 3–4°F and wait for the system to respond.
- **Check the breakers.** Look at your electrical panel for a tripped breaker for the AC/condenser and the air handler. Reset it once. If it trips again, stop — call a pro.
- **Look at the vents.** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Check for ice.** If you see frost or ice on the indoor coil or the copper refrigerant line, turn the system to OFF (fan ON if available) and let it thaw fully before running again — running it iced-over can damage the compressor.
- **Check the condensate drain/pan.** If the pan is full of water, the float switch may have shut the unit off. Clearing a visible clog can restore cooling, but recurring water means call a pro.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York technician should:

1. Confirm the thermostat is calling for cooling and verify 24V control voltage at the air handler and condenser.
2. Check incoming line voltage, breakers, and the contactor for pitting or failure to pull in.
3. **Test the capacitor** with a meter against its rated microfarads — a weak cap is a common, cheap fix.
4. Inspect the filter, blower, and evaporator coil for airflow restriction and ice.
5. Inspect and, if needed, clean the outdoor condenser coil.
6. **Attach gauges** to read suction and head pressures and check superheat/subcooling — this reveals low refrigerant, overcharge, or a restriction.
7. On variable-speed/inverter York models, read stored fault codes from the outdoor board for pressure or communication faults.
8. If refrigerant is low, perform a leak search (electronic detector or dye) rather than just recharging.

If a tech proposes a refrigerant recharge without searching for the leak, push back — that's a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Replace filter, clear vents | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Outdoor unit dead, indoor runs | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Find cause of overload, replace fuse |
| Compressor hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on coil or line | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, let thaw | Find airflow/refrigerant cause |
| Outdoor coil caked with debris | Dirty condenser | Gently rinse off debris (power off) | Deep coil cleaning |
| House won't cool, no clear fault | Low refrigerant leak | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Nothing runs, water in pan | Tripped float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Clear line, check pump |
| Unit clicks but won't engage | Bad contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| Repeated lockout / no cooling | Compressor failure | None | Diagnose and replace compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined (regional variation applies):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $0 (batteries) to $150–$400 installed
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$300
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (older R-22 systems cost far more)
- **Blower motor:** $400–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ — often the point to weigh replacing the whole condenser, especially on older units

Note: R-22 refrigerant is phased out and very expensive. If your York unit uses R-22 and needs a recharge, a system replacement is often the better value.

## Related codes

- **York high-pressure / low-pressure faults** — logged on variable-speed inverter boards; point to refrigerant or airflow issues.
- **York communication fault** — on Affinity/Acclimate communicating systems, loss of comms between thermostat, air handler, and outdoor unit can stop cooling.
- **Outdoor fan motor fault** — fan not spinning leads to high head pressure and no cooling.
- **Float switch / safe-T-switch trip** — condensate safety shutdown common across many brands, not just York.
