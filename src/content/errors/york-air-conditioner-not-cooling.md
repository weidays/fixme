## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's the symptom you notice when your York air conditioner runs but the house won't cool down, or the air from the vents isn't cold. On most York central systems there's no error display on the outdoor unit, so "not cooling" covers everything from a clogged filter to a dead compressor.

The underlying problem is almost always one of three things: **airflow is restricted**, **refrigerant is low** (which means a leak), or an **electrical component has failed** (capacitor, contactor, or compressor). This page helps you tell the safe, DIY-checkable causes from the ones that need a licensed HVAC pro — and sanity-check any quote you're given.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter** — the single most common cause. A clogged filter starves the system of airflow, which can freeze the evaporator coil and stop cooling entirely.
2. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — grass, dust, and cottonwood block heat rejection, so the system runs but can't cool.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a bulged or dead capacitor stops the compressor or fan motor from starting. Very common in hot-weather failures.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak or no cooling and often a frozen coil. Requires a certified tech; refrigerant never "gets used up."
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the coil turns to a block of ice and blocks all air.
6. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power while the indoor blower still runs.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit sticks or burns, so the compressor never engages.
8. **Thermostat problem or wiring fault** — a miscalibrated, dead-battery, or misconfigured thermostat never calls for cooling.
9. **Failed compressor or fan motor** — the most expensive outcome, usually after long-term stress from any of the above.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening a sealed unit or touching wiring.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker for the AC (often a double-pole 240V breaker). Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure furniture, rugs, or closed registers aren't blocking airflow. Open all supply vents.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for a smothered condenser — clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris away from the fins so it can breathe.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and stop cooling. Check for standing water in the drain pan; drain tablets help prevent future clogs.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** (leave the fan on) for several hours to let it thaw, then replace the filter before restarting.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass the float switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps stopping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York tech should work through this sequence — if a quote skips straight to "you need a new compressor," ask questions:

1. **Verify airflow and filter**, then confirm the thermostat is calling for cooling and 24V is reaching the outdoor unit.
2. **Measure electrical components** — test the capacitor with a meter, inspect the contactor for pitting, and check for correct voltage and amp draw at the compressor.
3. **Attach refrigerant gauges** and read pressures, superheat, and subcooling to judge whether the charge is correct. Low readings point to a leak.
4. **Leak search** if refrigerant is low — electronic detector, dye, or bubble test to find the source before adding refrigerant.
5. **Check the coils** for cleanliness and the evaporator for freezing.
6. **Test the compressor and fan motor** windings and start behavior if everything upstream checks out.

Ask the tech to show you the readings. A capacitor test or pressure reading is objective; "it's just old" is not a diagnosis.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect ductwork, blower |
| Ice on coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Leak search, recharge, clear coil |
| Outdoor fan runs, no cold air | Failed capacitor or low charge | Check breaker only | Test/replace capacitor, check charge |
| Outdoor unit dead, indoor blower runs | Tripped breaker or bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Buzzing/humming outside, no start | Failed capacitor or compressor | None — call a pro | Test capacitor, then compressor |
| Cools weakly on hot days only | Low refrigerant / dirty condenser | Rinse condenser fins gently | Pressure test, leak repair |
| Never turns on to cool | Thermostat or 24V fault | Set to COOL, new batteries | Test thermostat and control wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Prices vary by region, unit size, and refrigerant type (older R-22 systems cost much more to recharge).

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Coil cleaning (condenser/evaporator):** $100–$400
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 (higher for R-22 systems)
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500+ (often worth comparing against full system replacement on older units)
- **Full system replacement:** $5,000–$12,000+ for aging or failed units

If your York system is over 12–15 years old and needs a compressor or major refrigerant repair, ask for a replacement quote to compare — sinking money into an old unit often doesn't pay off.
