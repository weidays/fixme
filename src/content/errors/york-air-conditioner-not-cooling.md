## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a fault code York logs to a display. Most York split-system air conditioners don't show alphanumeric error codes at the condenser the way some furnaces or heat pumps do. Instead, you notice the house won't reach setpoint, the air from the vents feels warm or weak, or the system runs constantly without satisfying the thermostat.

The cause sits somewhere in the chain that moves heat out of your home: airflow (filter, blower, coils), the refrigerant circuit (charge, compressor, metering device), or the electrical components that start and run the compressor and fan (capacitor, contactor, control board). This page walks through the likely culprits and what's safe to check yourself before you pay for a service call.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked indoor coil** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system can't absorb heat; you'll often see ice on the lines or coil.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan won't start; you may hear a hum or clicking.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks, so the condenser never starts.
7. **Thermostat or wiring fault** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a broken low-voltage wire.
8. **Compressor failure** — least common but most expensive; the heart of the system has died.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are genuinely safe for a homeowner. If none of them fix the problem, call a technician.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the single most common cooling complaint.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check the breakers for both the indoor air handler/furnace and the outdoor condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated trips signal an electrical fault.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unblocked by furniture, rugs or closed doors.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings and debris from around and through the condenser fins. Keep at least two feet of clearance.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut the system down. Check for standing water in the pan and clear the drain line if you know where it terminates.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to **Off** and let it thaw fully (several hours) before running again. Running an iced unit can damage the compressor.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass the float switch or safety controls, or open the electrical panel on the condenser to poke at the capacitor — capacitors store a dangerous charge.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical path. Knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — measures supply and return air temperature; a healthy split is roughly 15-20°F.
2. **Check airflow** — inspects filter, blower and coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Inspect the condenser** — confirms the outdoor fan and compressor start, checks coil cleanliness.
4. **Test electrical components** — measures the capacitor's microfarads against its rating, checks the contactor for pitting, and verifies voltage and amp draw.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** — reads suction and head pressures and superheat/subcooling to judge the charge. Low pressure points to a leak.
6. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses electronic detector or dye to find the leak rather than just topping off.
7. **Compressor test** — checks windings and start behavior if the compressor won't run.

Be wary of any tech who recommends adding refrigerant without finding the leak, or quotes a compressor replacement without showing you failed-component readings.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm vents | Dirty filter / blocked coil | Replace filter, open vents | Clean coil, check blower |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Turn off, let thaw | Find leak, recharge, verify airflow |
| Outdoor fan runs, air still warm | Failed capacitor or low charge | None — call a pro | Test/replace capacitor, check charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Failed contactor or capacitor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor/capacitor |
| Humming at condenser, no start | Bad run capacitor | Shut off to avoid damage | Replace capacitor, test compressor |
| Standing water, system shut off | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain line | Clear blockage, test float switch |
| Nothing turns on at all | Tripped breaker / thermostat | Reset once, check batteries | Trace wiring, test control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $5–$25 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 pro
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$300
- **Coil cleaning (condenser or evaporator):** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500, depending on leak location and refrigerant type (older R-22 systems cost far more)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+, often making full system replacement worth considering on older units

Prices vary by region, refrigerant type and whether your unit is still under York's parts warranty — check your registration, as the part may be covered even if labor isn't.
