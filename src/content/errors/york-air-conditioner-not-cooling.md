## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single York fault code — it's the symptom you're seeing: the air conditioner runs (or tries to run) but your home isn't getting cold. York units don't display a universal cooling-failure code the way a furnace flashes an LED pattern. Instead, the cause shows up as low airflow, warm supply air, an outdoor unit that won't start, or a system that short-cycles.

Because York builds several platforms — from basic **Latitude** single-stage units to **Affinity** communicating systems — the way a problem presents depends heavily on your equipment and thermostat. A communicating Affinity system may post an actual fault code at the thermostat; a builder-grade single-stage condenser gives you no code at all. This page covers the realistic causes and what a fix should cost.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil. The most common and cheapest culprit.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, miswired or failed thermostat not calling for cooling.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — the outdoor unit or air handler isn't getting power.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start, or the outdoor fan doesn't spin.
5. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the lines and weak airflow.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — long cooling cycles, warm air, hissing, or ice. Requires EPA-certified repair.
8. **Failed contactor or relay** — the outdoor unit won't engage even when the thermostat calls.
9. **Failed compressor or fan motor** — the most expensive outcome, usually after other faults go ignored.
10. **Control board fault** (communicating Affinity systems) — board logs a code and won't run cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here — don't go further.

- **Check the air filter.** If it's gray, clogged, or you can't remember the last change, replace it. Run the system a bit; restored airflow often fixes weak cooling.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check the breakers.** Look at your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker (there are usually two: air handler and condenser). If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Look at the supply vents.** Make sure registers are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many vents starves airflow.
- **Check the condensate drain.** A full drain pan or clogged line can trigger a float switch that shuts cooling off. You can pour drain tablets or clear a visible clog, but don't disassemble the pan.
- **Look (don't touch) for ice.** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or coil, turn the system to **FAN ONLY** to thaw it, then call a technician — ice means low airflow or low refrigerant.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety or float switch, add refrigerant, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York tech follows a logical sequence, and knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** at the thermostat and confirm 24V signal reaches the contactor.
2. **Read any fault codes** — on communicating Affinity systems, the thermostat or board reports a stored code.
3. **Check incoming power and the capacitor** with a meter; a weak capacitor is a fast, cheap find.
4. **Inspect the contactor** for pitting or failure to pull in.
5. **Measure airflow and inspect coils** — dirty condenser, frozen evaporator, restricted return.
6. **Take refrigerant pressures and temperatures** (superheat/subcooling) to judge charge and look for a leak.
7. **Leak-search** with electronic detector or dye if charge is low — a proper diagnosis finds the leak, it doesn't just top off.
8. **Test the compressor and motors** for windings, amperage, and mechanical failure.

Be wary of any tech who jumps straight to "you need refrigerant" without showing pressures or finding the leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Check duct/blower if not resolved |
| Thermostat blank or won't call | Dead batteries / wiring | Replace batteries, verify COOL mode | Test wiring, replace thermostat |
| Outdoor unit dead, breaker tripped | Electrical fault | Reset breaker once | Find short, repair circuit |
| Outdoor fan won't spin, humming | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Outdoor coil caked in debris | Dirty condenser | Gently rinse with hose, power off | Professional coil cleaning |
| Ice on lines/coil | Low airflow or low charge | Set to FAN to thaw | Find restriction or leak |
| Long cycles, warm air, hissing | Low refrigerant / leak | None — call pro | Leak search & EPA-certified repair |
| Unit won't engage at all | Failed contactor/relay | None — call pro | Test & replace contactor |
| Loud, won't start, trips breaker | Failed compressor | None — call pro | Compressor diagnosis/replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined. Your price varies by region and contractor.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Programmable thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$300
- **Low-voltage fuse / minor wiring:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 (more if the leak is in the coil)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,000
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,200+ (often makes full replacement worth considering on older units)
- **Control board (Affinity/communicating):** $400–$900

If your system is more than 12–15 years old and facing a compressor or coil replacement, ask your contractor to compare repair cost against a new system.
