## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a single stored fault code. Your York system may be running, blowing room-temperature air, short-cycling, or not starting at all. Newer York outdoor units and communicating systems (Affinity series with the S1 control or YORK Connect) can flash diagnostic LED codes or show faults at the thermostat, but many older LX and packaged units give no code — you diagnose by behavior.

Because the indoor blower, the outdoor condenser, the refrigerant circuit, and the controls all have to work together to produce cold air, "not cooling" can originate in any of them. The checks below help you rule out the easy causes before paying for a service call.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode (FAN vs COOL), dead batteries, wrong setpoint, or wiring fault.
3. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; you'll see ice on the indoor coil or copper line.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit or air handler isn't powered.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — common on the compressor or condenser fan; unit hums but won't start.
6. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — blocked by grass, dust, or debris, killing heat rejection.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling capacity; requires a licensed tech.
8. **Failed contactor** — the outdoor relay won't pull in, so the compressor never starts.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — compressor runs hot and trips on overload.
10. **Compressor failure** — the most expensive cause; usually a last resort after others are ruled out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening sealed refrigerant lines or bypassing a safety switch.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for the air handler and outdoor unit. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated resets can wreck the compressor.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and not blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Confirm the disconnect near the unit is fully seated.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or the copper line, turn the system to OFF (fan ON is fine) and let it thaw fully for a few hours before restarting.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Clear standing water from the drain pan and clean the line with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor termination.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent York tech follows a logical path so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirm the complaint** — measures supply vs return air temperature (a healthy split is roughly 15–20°F).
2. **Check airflow** — inspects filter, blower, and coil for restriction or ice.
3. **Verify power and controls** — tests 24V at the contactor, checks the thermostat call, fuses, and any LED fault codes on the board.
4. **Test capacitors and contactor** — with a meter; weak capacitors are a fast, cheap find.
5. **Measure refrigerant performance** — connects gauges, checks superheat and subcooling against York charts. A low charge points to a leak, not a "top-off."
6. **Leak search** — electronic detector or dye if charge is low. A reputable tech finds and fixes the leak, not just adds gas.
7. **Compressor and amperage tests** — confirms windings and draw before condemning the compressor.

If a tech jumps straight to "you need refrigerant" or "you need a compressor" without measuring superheat/subcooling or testing the cheap components, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower & ductwork |
| Ice on indoor coil or line | Low airflow or low charge | Turn off, thaw fully, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or leak |
| Outdoor unit silent, no air | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Find cause of trip, replace fuse |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Compressor runs, no cold air | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor coil caked with debris | Dirty condenser | Rinse with garden hose (gentle) | Deep coil cleaning |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor dead | Failed contactor | None — call pro | Test 24V, replace contactor |
| Breaker keeps tripping | Compressor or motor fault | Stop resetting | Amperage test, replace part |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat replacement:** $50 DIY – $300 installed.
- **Capacitor:** $150–$300 replaced.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 replaced.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650.
- **Coil cleaning / drain clearing:** $100–$300.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and charge amount.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000+.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth comparing to full system replacement on older units).

Refrigerant prices vary sharply by type — older R-22 systems are far more expensive to recharge than R-410A, which can make a leaking older unit uneconomical to repair.

## Related codes

- **York short-cycling / frequent on-off** — often a frozen coil, dirty condenser, or control fault.
- **York frozen evaporator coil** — low airflow or low charge.
- **York outdoor fan not running** — capacitor, fan motor, or contactor.
- **York thermostat no power / blank** — blown 24V fuse, transformer, or float switch open.
- **York Affinity / communicating fault LEDs** — refer to the board's blink-code chart on the unit's service label, which varies by model.
