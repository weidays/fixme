## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code — it's the symptom most York air conditioner owners describe when the system runs but the house stays warm, or the air from the vents isn't cold. York central split systems (LX, Affinity, and older Diamond/Latitude lines) don't usually flash a dedicated "no cool" code on a display; instead, the indoor furnace or air-handler control board may store a fault, or the outdoor unit may simply fail to cool effectively.

Because the cause can be anything from a $5 filter to a failed compressor, this is rated a **pro** issue: a few safe checks are worth doing first, but most real fixes require a licensed HVAC technician with gauges and meters.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — chokes airflow, reduces cooling, and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a miswired/failing thermostat not calling for cooling.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser has lost power while the indoor blower still runs.
4. **Failed run/dual capacitor** — a very common outdoor failure; the fan or compressor won't start.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — caked outdoor coil can't reject heat, so cooling drops off, especially on hot days.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, warm air, and sometimes ice on the lines.
7. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; blocks cooling entirely.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor** — compressor overheats and shuts down on its safety.
9. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit doesn't engage.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit hums or trips but doesn't cool.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Filter:** Pull the air filter. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cooling killer.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the fan to **AUTO**, and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Look for leaves, grass clippings, or debris choking the outdoor coil. Gently rinse the outside of the coil with a garden hose (power off first).
- **Condensate drain:** If you see standing water near the indoor unit, the condensate line may be clogged and a safety float switch may have shut the system off. Clearing the drain may be within reach, but stubborn clogs need a tech.

If the indoor coil or refrigerant lines are coated in ice, **turn the system off** and run only the fan to let it thaw — don't chip at ice or keep running it.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician will sanity-check the same basics, then go deeper:

- Verify thermostat call and 24V signal reaching the outdoor contactor.
- Check the contactor, capacitor, and compressor/fan amperage with a meter.
- Inspect the condenser and evaporator coils for dirt and airflow restriction.
- Connect refrigerant gauges to read suction and liquid pressures, then calculate **superheat and subcooling** to confirm proper charge.
- If charge is low, **find the leak** (electronic detector or dye) rather than just topping off — adding refrigerant without a repair is a temporary band-aid.
- Test the compressor windings and capacitor if the unit hums but won't start.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" or "replace the compressor" without these measurements is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, run fan to thaw | Confirm airflow, check coil and charge |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / contactor | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, wiring, transformer |
| Outdoor fan spins, no cold air | Failed capacitor / compressor | None — call a pro | Test/replace capacitor, check compressor |
| Hot day, mild cooling | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse outside of coil | Deep coil clean, verify charge |
| Ice on lines, warm vents | Low refrigerant / low airflow | Off + fan to thaw | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Humming, no start, breaker trips | Failed compressor or hard start | Stop resetting, call pro | Compressor/electrical diagnosis |
| Water near indoor unit, no cooling | Clogged condensate, float switch open | Clear visible drain clog | Clear line, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor):

- **Air filter:** $5–$30 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5–$15 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $100–$350
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Condensate line clearing:** $100–$250
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$600
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,800+ (often makes replacing an older unit worth considering)

Prices vary by region, model, and how accessible the part is. On older York units, weigh major repairs against the age of the system.

## Related codes

- **York AC not turning on** — no power, dead thermostat, or tripped breaker.
- **York AC blowing warm air** — overlaps heavily with low refrigerant and capacitor failures.
- **York AC freezing up / coil icing** — airflow and refrigerant issues.
- **York furnace/air-handler blower not running** — affects cooling airflow on shared indoor units.
- **Condensate float switch / safety lockout** — common cause of a system that "won't cool" after a clogged drain.
