## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific York fault code. Your air conditioner is running—or trying to—but the air coming from your vents is warm, lukewarm, or the house simply never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

York systems vary widely. Some Affinity-series communicating units display fault codes on the control board or thermostat; many Latitude, LX, and older single-stage units have **no onboard display at all**, so you're diagnosing purely by behavior. Because this is a general performance problem rather than a hard lockout, the cause can range from a $5 air filter to a failed compressor.

This page covers the most common reasons a York AC stops cooling and helps you sanity-check any quote you receive.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (heat/fan vs. cool/auto), or a setpoint set too high.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser may have lost power while the indoor blower still runs, so you feel air but no cooling.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — ice from low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit blows warm and may drip water.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — outdoor unit caked with grass, dust, or debris can't reject heat.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, longer run times, ice on the line. Requires a pro.
7. **Failed run capacitor** — compressor or fan motor hums but won't start; very common in hot climates.
8. **Failed contactor or compressor** — outdoor unit silent or buzzing; the most expensive failure.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop at anything that requires opening sealed panels or touching wiring.

- **Air filter** — pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. Run the system 30–60 minutes and recheck.
- **Thermostat** — confirm it's set to **COOL** and **AUTO**, with the setpoint a few degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers** — check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker on both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) circuits. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers** — make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Outdoor unit** — clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Gently rinse the fins from outside with a garden hose (power off first).
- **Condensate drain** — a clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut the system down. Check for standing water at the indoor unit's drain pan.
- **Frozen coil** — if you see ice on the indoor coil or copper line, turn the system **OFF** and run the fan only to thaw it fully (can take several hours) before restarting.

Do **not** add refrigerant, open the sealed system, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York tech follows a logical path—use this to judge whether a quote makes sense:

1. **Verify the complaint** and check thermostat operation, mode, and call for cooling.
2. **Confirm power** to both indoor and outdoor units; test the contactor and capacitor.
3. **Read any fault codes** on the control board or communicating thermostat (Affinity-series), and note flash patterns.
4. **Measure airflow** and inspect the filter and evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** and measure superheat/subcooling to determine charge—then leak-search if low.
6. **Check the condenser coil** for cleanliness and the fan motor for proper operation.
7. **Test the compressor** for amp draw, windings, and start ability if it won't run.

Expect a diagnostic fee of **$75–$200**, often credited toward the repair. Be cautious of anyone who recommends adding refrigerant without checking for a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blowing warm air, indoor fan runs | Outdoor unit lost power; tripped breaker | Reset breaker once; check filter | Test contactor, capacitor, compressor |
| House won't reach setpoint, long runtimes | Low refrigerant / leak | Replace filter, clear condenser | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Ice on coil or copper line | Low airflow or low charge | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Confirm charge, find restriction |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed run capacitor | None—stop | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit totally silent | Contactor, control board, or compressor | Check breaker once | Diagnose and replace failed part |
| Weak airflow, some cooling | Dirty filter or coil; blower issue | Replace filter | Clean coils, test blower motor |
| Water pooling, system shuts off | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible clog | Clear drain, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included:

- **Air filter:** $5–$30 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** under $10 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 installed
- **Condenser/evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Blower motor:** $400–$800
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+ (often worth weighing against full system replacement on older units)

Units still under York's parts warranty may cut part costs significantly—labor is usually still your responsibility.

## Related codes

- **York AC freezing up** — restricted airflow or low refrigerant
- **York AC tripping breaker** — short, failing compressor, or electrical fault
- **York condenser fan not spinning** — capacitor or fan motor failure
- **York AC short cycling** — oversized system, low charge, or control issue
- **Affinity series control board fault codes** — refer to your unit's specific service label for flash-code meanings, as they vary by model and board revision
