## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a specific York fault code — it's a symptom. Your York air conditioner is powered on and the thermostat is calling for cooling, but the air coming from your vents is warm, weak, or not cold enough to satisfy the setpoint.

York condensers do not typically flash a numeric cooling-failure code at the homeowner the way a furnace board does. On communicating Affinity systems with a touchscreen thermostat, you may see a fault message or a flashing LED on the control board inside the air handler or condenser. On standard single- and two-stage units, there is usually **no display at all** — you diagnose by behavior and measurement. Because the exact indicators vary by model and board, treat any onscreen message as a clue, not a diagnosis.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Dirty condenser coil or blocked outdoor unit** — grass, leaves, and dirt insulate the coil so heat can't escape.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure; the compressor or fan motor won't start or hums and stalls.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power while the indoor blower still runs.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, possible ice on the lines, often a hissing leak.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; warm air until it thaws.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the compressor is pitted or stuck.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a loose C-wire.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — compressor overheats and shuts down on its internal protector.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit runs but produces no cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is hot, smells burnt, or sparks.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes many "not cooling" calls.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor air handler/furnace breaker and the separate outdoor condenser breaker. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear grass, leaves, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse visible dirt off the outside of the coil with a garden hose (no pressure washer).
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and not blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate:** Check the drain pan and overflow switch. A full pan or tripped float switch can shut the system off.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the copper lines or the indoor coil, turn the system to OFF (fan ON) and let it thaw fully before running again.

Do **not** open the condenser to touch the capacitor or contactor — those hold a dangerous charge. Do not add refrigerant or open sealed lines.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York technician should:

1. Verify the thermostat call and confirm 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. Measure the run capacitor's microfarads against the rating stamped on it.
3. Inspect the contactor for pitting and check the condenser fan and compressor amp draw.
4. Check airflow and inspect the evaporator coil and filter for icing or dirt.
5. **Attach gauges** to read suction and head pressures and calculate superheat/subcooling — this is how they confirm a refrigerant problem.
6. If refrigerant is low, perform a **leak search** (electronic detector or dye), not just a top-off.
7. On Affinity communicating systems, read board fault history through the thermostat.

If a tech proposes adding refrigerant without finding a leak, push back — that's a temporary fix that wastes money.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Clogged filter | Replace filter | Inspect ductwork/blower |
| Outdoor unit dead, indoor fan runs | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Find the short, replace fuse |
| Outdoor fan hums but won't spin | Failed capacitor | None — high voltage | Test & replace capacitor |
| Ice on copper lines/indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Thaw with fan ON | Find cause, check charge |
| Gradual loss of cooling over weeks | Refrigerant leak | None | Leak search & repair, recharge |
| Outdoor unit silent, no hum | Stuck/burned contactor | None | Replace contactor |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse exterior | Deep coil cleaning |
| Compressor runs, zero cooling | Failed compressor | None | Replace compressor/system |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Condenser coil deep cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $600–$1,500 (depends on leak location and refrigerant type — older R-22 units cost far more)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,800–$2,800+ — on an older unit, replacing the whole system is often the better value.

Costs vary by region, refrigerant type, and whether your unit is still under York's parts warranty (register your unit to confirm).
