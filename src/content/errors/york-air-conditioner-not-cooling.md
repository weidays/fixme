## What this code means

"Not cooling" is not a single York fault code — it's a symptom. Your York air conditioner may be running, the thermostat may show a call for cooling, but the indoor air never gets cold, or the house never reaches setpoint.

York systems rarely flash a dedicated "no cooling" code. Instead, communicating models in the **Affinity/YXV** line may log generic comfort or communication faults on the indoor control board, while older **LX** and **YCG** single-stage units have no diagnostics at all — they simply run, short-cycle, or do nothing.

Because "not cooling" covers everything from a dirty filter to a dead compressor, the goal of this page is to help you rule out the cheap, safe causes yourself and recognize when you need a licensed HVAC tech.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, freezes the evaporator coil, kills cooling output.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, bad programming, or failed thermostat.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — outdoor condenser has lost power while the indoor blower still runs.
4. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, and cottonwood block heat rejection.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks cooling entirely.
6. **Failed run capacitor** — compressor or condenser fan won't start; you may hear humming.
7. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser doesn't pull in.
8. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, longer run times, possible icing.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — unit overheats and trips on high pressure.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; often the last suspect.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, bypass any safety switch, or handle refrigerant.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of "not cooling."
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Lower the temp 5°F and listen for the outdoor unit to start.
- **Breaker:** Check the electrical panel for a tripped breaker labeled "AC" or "condenser." Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals a real fault.
- **Disconnect/fuses:** Make sure the outdoor disconnect box (the gray box near the condenser) is fully plugged in or switched on.
- **Supply vents:** Confirm registers throughout the house are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate:** A full condensate pan or clogged drain can trip a safety float switch and shut down cooling. Check for standing water at the indoor unit and clear the drain line if you're comfortable doing so.
- **Outdoor unit:** Make sure the condenser fan is spinning and the coil isn't buried in leaves or grass clippings. Turn the system off and gently rinse the coil with a garden hose from the inside out.

If the evaporator coil or refrigerant lines are coated in ice, **turn the system off** and let it thaw fully (a few hours), then run the fan only. Recurring ice means a deeper problem for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good York tech follows a logical sequence so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** — confirm airflow, thermostat call, and indoor/outdoor split temperature (a healthy system drops supply air 15–20°F below return air).
2. **Check power and controls** — measures 24V at the contactor, tests the contactor and capacitor with a meter.
3. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower operation, coil cleanliness, ductwork.
4. **Gauge the refrigerant** — connects manifold gauges, checks suction/liquid pressures and superheat/subcooling against York's charging chart for your model.
5. **Leak search** — if charge is low, uses electronic detector, soap bubbles, or UV dye to find the leak before adding refrigerant.
6. **Test the compressor** — checks amp draw, windings, and start components.

Be wary of any tech who simply "tops off" refrigerant without finding the leak — that's a temporary fix that masks the real problem.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor blower runs, no cold air | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil with fan only | Diagnose airflow or low charge |
| Outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once, check disconnect | Replace contactor, test 24V circuit |
| Humming at condenser, fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None — shut off and call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Cooling weak, long run times | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call pro | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Ice on lines or coil | Low airflow or low charge | Off, thaw, replace filter | Diagnose root cause |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Electrical fault / failing compressor | Stop resetting, call pro | Megohm test, replace component |
| Warm air, fan spins fine outside | Failed compressor | None — call pro | Compressor diagnosis/replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter:** $15 – $40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $120 – $350
- **Run capacitor:** $150 – $300
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300 – $600
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400 – $1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A pricing varies)
- **Coil replacement (evaporator or condenser):** $900 – $2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200 – $1,800+ — often the point where homeowners weigh repair vs. replacing the whole unit, especially on systems over 10 years old.

Diagnostic/service-call fees typically run $80 – $200 and are sometimes credited toward the repair.

## Related codes

- **York "Communicating fault" / loss of communication** — Affinity/YXV systems showing comfort or comm errors on the indoor board.
- **High-pressure lockout** — often from a dirty condenser coil or failed fan motor.
- **Low-pressure lockout** — typically signals low refrigerant from a leak.
- **Frozen evaporator coil** — overlaps heavily with no-cooling complaints.
- **AC short-cycling** — related symptom that can also point to capacitor, charge, or thermostat issues.
