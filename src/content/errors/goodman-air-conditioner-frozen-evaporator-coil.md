## What this code means

Goodman air conditioners don't usually flash a dedicated "frozen coil" fault code — most homeowners discover it by symptom: weak or no cold air, hissing, water pooling near the air handler, or visible ice on the copper lines and indoor coil. Newer Goodman communicating systems (ComfortBridge / CoolCloud-equipped models) may report a low-pressure or airflow fault, but the underlying problem is the same.

A frozen evaporator coil means the indoor coil has dropped below 32°F and condensation on it has turned to ice. The coil is *supposed* to be cold — but it stays cold enough to freeze only when something disrupts the balance between airflow across the coil and refrigerant flow through it. Either too little warm air is moving over the coil, or too little refrigerant is absorbing heat. Both end the same way: a block of ice that kills your cooling.

This is rated **pro** because while you can safely thaw the coil yourself, the most common root causes (refrigerant leaks, failing blower motors) require licensed work.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the #1 cause. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing. Often fixed for the price of a filter.
2. **Closed, blocked, or undersized return/supply vents** — too many closed registers or blocked returns starve the coil of warm air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and choke airflow, mimicking a filter problem.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — a low charge makes the remaining refrigerant expand colder, freezing the coil. Goodman R-410A (or R-32 on 2025+ models) charge must be diagnosed and repaired by a pro.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor** — a weak ECM/PSC blower or bad capacitor moves too little air across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below ~62°F outdoors lowers system pressures and can freeze the coil.
7. **Dirty/clogged condensate drain causing a float switch to cycle the blower oddly**, or thermostat fan set wrong, leaving the coil cold with no air movement.
8. **Metering device (TXV/piston) failure** — a stuck expansion valve overfeeds or starves the coil.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Turn the system OFF first.** Set the thermostat to OFF and let the coil fully thaw before doing anything else. Running it frozen risks compressor damage.
- **Set the fan to ON** (with cooling OFF) to speed thawing by blowing room-temperature air across the coil — 1–3 hours typically.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, furry, or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone fixes a large share of cases.
- **Open all supply registers and clear returns.** Don't run with more than a couple of registers closed. Make sure furniture, rugs, or curtains aren't blocking returns.
- **Check the breaker and thermostat.** Confirm the thermostat is set to COOL with a reasonable setpoint and replace thermostat batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Look at the condensate drain.** If you see water pooling or a wet floor, the drain may be clogged. You can clear a visible drain line with a wet/dry vacuum at the outdoor termination.
- **Don't run the AC if it's below ~62°F outside.**

If the coil refreezes within a day or two of a clean filter and open vents, stop and call a technician — you're likely dealing with refrigerant or blower issues.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Goodman tech will sanity-check the same airflow basics, then go deeper:

- **Confirm thaw and inspect the coil** for dirt, biofilm, and bent fins.
- **Measure static pressure** across the air handler to verify the duct system isn't starving the coil.
- **Check blower performance** — ECM/PSC motor amp draw, blower capacitor, and wheel cleanliness.
- **Connect gauges to read suction/head pressures and superheat/subcooling.** Abnormal readings point to undercharge (leak) or a faulty metering device.
- **Leak search** if the charge is low — electronic detector, UV dye, or nitrogen pressure test. They should *find and fix the leak*, not just "top off" the refrigerant.
- **Verify charge** against Goodman's specs for your exact model and refrigerant (R-410A or R-32).

A quote that says "added refrigerant" with no leak search is a red flag — the coil will refreeze.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter | Replace filter, thaw coil | Verify airflow, clean coil |
| Coil refreezes after clean filter | Dirty evaporator coil | Thaw, confirm filter clean | Chemically clean coil |
| Hissing + ice + warm air | Low refrigerant (leak) | Shut off, thaw | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Little air from vents, ice forms | Weak blower / bad capacitor | Thaw, check fan setting | Test ECM/PSC motor, capacitor |
| Water on floor near air handler | Clogged condensate drain | Vacuum visible drain line | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Freezes only in cool weather | Operating below ~62°F outdoors | Stop running AC | Verify pressures, low-ambient kit |
| Frost + erratic cooling | Faulty TXV/piston metering | Thaw, shut off | Replace metering device, recharge |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and contractor):

- **Air filter replacement:** $5–$40 (DIY).
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450.
- **Blower capacitor:** $150–$400.
- **Blower motor (ECM/PSC):** $400–$900.
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450 (sometimes credited toward the repair).
- **Refrigerant recharge (R-410A or R-32):** $200–$700+ depending on amount; R-32 pricing on newer Goodman units may differ.
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$1,800+; a leaking coil on an older R-410A system can push toward full replacement.
- **Compressor damage from running frozen:** $1,500–$2,800+, often making replacement the smarter choice.

The cheapest outcome — a filter and a thaw — costs almost nothing. The expensive ones come from ignoring a refreezing coil and burning up the compressor, which is exactly what makes this a "fix it now" problem.
