## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil is not a digital fault code on Goodman equipment — it's a physical condition you'll usually spot as ice or frost on the indoor coil, on the copper refrigerant lines near the air handler, or weak/no cold air at the registers. Some newer Goodman communicating systems (with ComfortNet or the DSXC/GSXC boards) may flag a low-suction or low-pressure fault, but most standard Goodman split systems give no code at all.

The coil freezes when something disrupts the balance between airflow across the coil and the amount of refrigerant in it. Either too little warm air is passing over the coil, or the refrigerant pressure is too low. Both drop the coil's surface below 32°F, and humidity condensing on it turns to ice. Once ice forms, it blocks airflow further and the problem snowballs.

**Bottom line:** running the AC with a frozen coil risks liquid refrigerant returning to the compressor (slugging), which can destroy it. Shut the cooling off, let it thaw, and resolve the root cause.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — furniture over registers or too many rooms closed off starves the system of return air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust caking the coil fins insulates them and chokes airflow even with a clean filter.
4. **Failing or slow blower motor (PSC or ECM)** — a weak motor or bad capacitor can't move enough air. ECM-equipped Goodman air handlers may run but at reduced output.
5. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — lower pressure lowers the coil's evaporating temperature. This causes repeat freezing and steadily worse cooling.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below roughly 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil; not a fault, just a misuse.
7. **Stuck or failing metering device (TXV or fixed orifice)** — feeds too little refrigerant to part of the coil. Less common, needs a pro.
8. **Blocked condensate drain backing up** — usually causes water/overflow, but a saturated coil and ice can be related.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **First, turn the thermostat cooling to OFF and the fan to ON** to thaw the coil before doing anything else.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. Check it monthly in cooling season.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's a battery model and the display is dim or blank.
- **Breaker:** Make sure the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor breakers are both ON. A tripped indoor breaker stops the blower and freezes the coil.
- **Vents and registers:** Open all supply vents and clear furniture, rugs, or boxes off return grilles. Don't run the system with most rooms closed off.
- **Condensate drain:** Look for water around the indoor unit. If the pan is full or the drain line is clogged, clear it or use drain tablets. A safety float switch may also be shutting the unit off.
- **Let it fully thaw:** Wait 1–3 hours with fan ON until all ice is gone before restarting cooling. Never chip ice off with a knife or screwdriver — you'll puncture the coil.

If the coil refreezes within a day or two of a clean filter and open vents, the cause is mechanical or refrigerant-related — call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech follows a logical airflow-first, refrigerant-second path. Use this to sanity-check a quote:

- **Confirms the coil is thawed** before taking any pressure readings — readings on an iced coil are meaningless.
- **Measures airflow / static pressure** across the coil and checks the blower motor and capacitor. On ECM Goodman air handlers they'll verify the motor is ramping correctly.
- **Inspects the evaporator coil and filter** for dirt buildup.
- **Connects refrigerant gauges** to read suction and head pressure, then calculates superheat and subcooling to judge the charge. (Only an EPA-certified tech should handle refrigerant.)
- **Performs a leak search** (electronic sniffer, UV dye, or bubble test) if the charge is low — recharging without finding the leak is a temporary fix.
- **Checks the metering device (TXV/orifice)** if pressures point to a feed problem.

Be cautious of any tech who immediately "tops off the refrigerant" without checking airflow or searching for a leak. Adding refrigerant to a system that froze from a dirty filter is a misdiagnosis.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty/clogged filter | Replace filter, thaw with fan ON | Verify airflow, clean coil if needed |
| Coil freezes, many vents closed | Restricted airflow | Open all vents, clear returns | Check static pressure and duct sizing |
| Ice returns with clean filter | Dirty evaporator coil | Thaw; schedule service | Chemically clean the coil |
| Weak air, blower hums or won't run | Blower motor / capacitor | Check indoor breaker only | Test/replace motor or capacitor |
| Repeat freezing, cooling worse over weeks | Refrigerant leak | Shut off cooling, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Freezing in cool weather (<60°F) | Running AC too cold out | Turn cooling off | Usually no repair needed |
| Water around unit + ice | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain, use tablets | Clear line, check float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, including typical labor. Your area and contractor will vary.

- **Air filter replacement:** $5–$30 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0–$30 DIY; $75–$200 if a pro clears the line.
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $200–$450.
- **Blower capacitor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Blower motor (PSC):** $300–$600 installed.
- **Blower motor (ECM module/motor):** $400–$700+ installed.
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$400.
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $600–$1,500+ depending on where the leak is and how much refrigerant is needed.
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ (coil and labor; more if matched to an older R-410A system).

Diagnostic/service-call fees of $80–$180 are common and often credited toward the repair.
