## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" is not a fault code on a display — it's a physical condition. The indoor coil (inside your air handler or furnace cabinet) drops below freezing and ice builds up on it. Goodman systems don't have a dedicated "coil frozen" sensor, so you usually discover it by symptoms: warm air from vents, a hissing or dripping sound, water on the floor, or visible frost on the copper refrigerant line at the outdoor unit.

The coil is designed to run cold, but not below freezing. When something limits airflow across the coil or lowers refrigerant pressure, the coil temperature crashes, condensation freezes, and the ice blocks airflow even further — a snowball effect. Once iced over, the system essentially stops cooling.

The root cause is almost always **airflow** or **refrigerant**. Finding which one matters, because a dirty filter is a $15 fix and a refrigerant leak is a several-hundred-dollar repair.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Starves the coil of the warm return air it needs to stay above freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — reduces total airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and restrict air passing through the fins.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — lower pressure means a colder coil. This is the most common *non-airflow* cause and requires a technician.
5. **Weak or failing blower motor / capacitor** — the fan can't move enough air across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — using cooling when it's below roughly 62°F outside pushes the coil below freezing.
7. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — feeds too much refrigerant into the coil, over-cooling it.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Start here — these are all safe and often solve the problem.

- **Turn cooling OFF, set the fan to ON.** This stops adding ice and lets the blower melt the existing ice faster. Wait 1–24 hours until the coil and outdoor line are fully thawed before you test again.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, swap it. Note the size printed on the frame.
- **Open all supply registers and clear return grilles.** Move furniture, rugs, or boxes away from vents. Don't run the system with more than a couple of registers closed.
- **Check the thermostat.** Make sure it's set to Cool and a reasonable temperature, and that it isn't calling for cooling when it's cold outside.
- **Check the breaker.** If the outdoor unit tripped but the indoor blower ran, uneven operation can encourage freezing. Reset once; don't repeatedly reset a breaker that keeps tripping.
- **Check the condensate drain.** Thawed ice produces a lot of water. Make sure the pan isn't overflowing and the drain line isn't clogged — a wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain outlet often clears it.

If the coil freezes again within a day or two of a clean filter and open vents, stop and call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Goodman tech should:

- **Confirm the coil is fully thawed** before measuring anything — readings taken on ice are meaningless.
- **Measure airflow** across the coil and check blower motor amperage, capacitor, and speed tap settings.
- **Inspect and, if needed, clean the evaporator coil** and blower wheel.
- **Connect refrigerant gauges** and check suction pressure, superheat, and subcooling against the Goodman charging chart for your model.
- **Check the metering device** (TXV or fixed orifice) for correct operation.
- **Leak-search** with electronic detector or dye if the charge is low — adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a temporary fix, not a repair.

Sanity check: if a tech says "you're low on refrigerant" and wants to just top it off without ever mentioning a leak search, get a second opinion. A sealed system doesn't consume refrigerant — if it's low, it's leaking somewhere.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air from vents, ice on indoor coil | Dirty filter / blocked airflow | Turn off, thaw, replace filter, open vents | Verify airflow, check blower speed |
| Frost on outdoor line at the AC | Low refrigerant / airflow restriction | Thaw and check filter first | Gauge readings, leak search, recharge |
| Freezes again days after new filter | Refrigerant leak or weak blower | None — call a pro | Leak test, blower/capacitor test |
| Water on floor near air handler | Melting ice + blocked drain | Vac out drain line, thaw coil | Clear drain, confirm root cause |
| Coils freeze only on cool nights | Running AC below ~62°F outside | Don't run cooling in cold weather | Confirm low-ambient limits |
| Weak airflow, no ice yet but cold coil | Dirty coil or failing blower | Replace filter, clean returns | Coil cleaning, blower motor check |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your price varies by region and contractor.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0 DIY with a shop vac; $75–$200 if a tech clears it.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450 depending on access.
- **Blower capacitor replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Blower motor (ECM or PSC) replacement:** $400–$900+.
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+, depending on leak location. A leak at the evaporator coil itself often means **coil replacement, $1,000–$2,500**.
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900.

If your system uses R-22 (older Goodman units), refrigerant is expensive and increasingly scarce — a major leak may make replacement more economical than repair.
