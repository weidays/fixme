## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" isn't a digital fault code — your Goodman air conditioner has no sensor that flashes a number for it. It's a physical condition: the indoor evaporator coil (inside your air handler or attached to your furnace) drops below freezing, and the moisture in your air turns to ice on it.

A coil should run cold but stay above 32°F. When something starves the coil of heat — either too little warm air moving across it, or too little refrigerant inside it — the surface temperature crashes and ice builds. Once a layer of ice forms, it insulates the coil, the unit cools even less, and the freeze accelerates.

You'll usually notice it as **warm air from the vents while the outdoor unit runs**, water around the indoor unit, or visible frost on the copper line outside (the larger insulated "suction" line).

Because the two main root causes — chronic airflow problems and refrigerant issues — both require diagnosis with tools and (for refrigerant) EPA certification, we rate this **pro**.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the #1 cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air. Cheap to fix.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too-few supply/return vents** — furniture over returns, closed registers, or undersized ductwork reduce airflow below what the coil needs.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil insulate it the same way ice does, dropping its temperature.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — a low charge lowers coil pressure and temperature until it freezes. This is the most common *repeating* cause after the filter is ruled out.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wrong fan speed** — a weak ECM or PSC motor, bad capacitor, or incorrect tap setting moves too little air.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — running cooling when it's below ~60°F outside can freeze the coil.
7. **Stuck or restricted metering device (TXV/piston)** — over-feeds or under-feeds the coil, causing localized freezing.
8. **Clogged condensate drain backing water onto the coil** — less common but contributes in some setups.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no panels off the sealed system, no refrigerant, no electrical work inside the unit.

- **Turn the thermostat to OFF (cool), FAN to ON.** This stops making ice and lets the blower thaw the coil. Do this first.
- **Wait for a full thaw** — 1 to 3 hours, sometimes more. Don't chip or scrape ice; you can puncture the coil.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, swap it. Use the correct size and a standard pleated filter — avoid the densest "HEPA-grade" 1-inch filters, which can over-restrict airflow.
- **Open all supply registers and clear returns.** Move rugs, furniture, and boxes off vents. Closing too many rooms starves the system.
- **Check the breaker.** If the indoor blower's breaker has tripped, the coil will freeze even though the outdoor unit runs. Reset once; if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Check the thermostat batteries** if it's a battery model and the display is dim.
- **Look at the condensate drain and pan.** Standing water or a full pan suggests a clogged drain line. A wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain stub, or a drain tablet, often clears it.

After thawing **and** fixing an obvious airflow problem, run the AC and watch. If it freezes again within a day or two, the cause is deeper — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech won't just add refrigerant. Expect them to:

- **Measure airflow / temperature split** across the coil (return vs. supply air temp) to confirm the blower is moving enough air.
- **Inspect the blower wheel, motor, and capacitor**, and verify the fan speed tap or ECM programming is correct for the system.
- **Inspect the evaporator coil** for dirt and check the condensate drain.
- **Connect gauges and read superheat/subcooling.** This tells them whether the charge is low and whether the metering device (TXV/piston) is working — without this, "adding a pound of Freon" is a guess.
- **Leak-search** if the charge is low (dye, electronic sniffer, or nitrogen pressure test). A low charge always means a leak somewhere — refrigerant isn't "used up."

If a quote jumps straight to a refrigerant top-off with no superheat/subcooling reading and no leak search, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, outdoor unit running, ice on indoor coil/line | Frozen coil from low airflow | Set to fan-only, thaw, replace filter | Confirm airflow, check blower/coil |
| Freezes again days after new filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | None safe — stop running it | Superheat/subcool, leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water around indoor unit | Thawed ice + clogged drain | Vac the drain line, add tablet | Clear drain, check pan/float switch |
| Weak airflow from all vents | Failing blower / wrong speed | Check filter & registers | Test motor, capacitor, fan tap/ECM |
| Coil freezes only on cool days | Running AC below ~60°F outside | Switch to fan or heat | Verify low-ambient limits |
| Some rooms hot, coil ices | Closed vents / undersized ducts | Open all registers | Evaluate duct sizing/static pressure |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor. Goodman-specific prices vary by region and contractor.

- **New air filter:** $8–$30 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY with a shop vac; $75–$200 if a tech does it.
- **Blower capacitor:** $150–$350.
- **Blower (ECM) motor or module:** $400–$900.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400.
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $100–$350 (often credited toward repair).
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (older R-410A vs. newer refrigerants affect price).
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000+ (coil often has a parts warranty if registered; you still pay labor).
- **Compressor damage from running frozen:** $1,500–$2,800+ — the reason you should never keep running an iced unit.
