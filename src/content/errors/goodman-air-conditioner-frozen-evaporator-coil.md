## What this code means

Goodman air conditioners don't display a specific "frozen evaporator coil" fault code — this is a **physical condition**, not an electronic error. You'll notice it as ice or frost on the copper lines and the indoor coil (inside the air handler or on top of the furnace), weak or no cold air, water pooling near the indoor unit, and a house that won't cool down.

A frozen coil happens when the evaporator's surface temperature drops below freezing. That's caused by either **too little warm air moving across the coil** or **too little refrigerant absorbing heat from it**. Ice then insulates the coil, cooling collapses entirely, and the compressor is left working against abnormal conditions — which is why this is rated a **pro** repair once the obvious airflow issues are ruled out.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the #1 cause. Restricted airflow starves the coil of warm air, and it ices over.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers reduce airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm buildup insulate the coil and choke airflow through the fins.
4. **Blower running too slow or failing** — a weak blower motor, wrong speed tap, or failing capacitor reduces air across the coil.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge drops coil pressure and temperature below freezing. This is a sealed-system repair.
6. **Clogged condensate drain** — a backed-up drain (and float switch) is often noticed alongside icing and water damage.
7. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below about 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil on many systems.
8. **Faulty TXV or piston/metering device** — a stuck metering device over- or under-feeds refrigerant. Varies by model.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Do these first — several fix the problem for free:

- **Turn cooling OFF and the fan to ON.** At the thermostat, switch the system from COOL to OFF but set the fan to ON. This thaws the coil without straining the compressor. Wait until all ice is gone before restarting (1–24 hours).
- **Replace the air filter.** A dirty filter is the single most common cause. Slide in a clean one of the correct size.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply and return registers are open and not covered by furniture or rugs. Never close more than a couple.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to COOL and a reasonable temperature — not set unusually low, which can encourage freezing.
- **Check the breaker.** If the outdoor unit or air handler tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Inspect the condensate drain.** Look for standing water in the drain pan and clear a visibly clogged drain line with a wet/dry vacuum at the outdoor drain exit.
- **Let it fully thaw, then run and observe.** After thawing with a clean filter, run cooling for an hour. If it re-freezes, the cause is deeper — call a technician.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or bend on the coil to force ice off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A pro sanity-checks the same airflow items, then measures the sealed system:

1. **Confirms and clears the ice**, then verifies the blower's actual airflow (CFM) and correct speed tap.
2. **Inspects the evaporator coil** for dirt, biofilm, and bent fins that restrict airflow.
3. **Tests the blower motor and capacitor** for correct amperage and speed.
4. **Gauges the refrigerant** — measures suction/head pressures, superheat, and subcooling to judge whether the charge is correct or low.
5. **Leak-searches** if the charge is low, using electronic detectors, UV dye, or nitrogen pressure testing — a leak must be found and repaired, not just "topped off."
6. **Checks the metering device (TXV or piston)** and the condensate drain and float switch.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search or airflow check is a red flag — refrigerant doesn't get used up; if it's low, it leaked.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw | Verify airflow, clean coil |
| House warm, some cold air earlier | Dirty evaporator coil | Thaw, replace filter | Deep-clean coil, check fins |
| Coil re-freezes after clean filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Turn off, call pro | Leak search + repair + recharge |
| Weak air even with clean filter | Slow/failing blower | Check breaker, thaw | Test motor, capacitor, speed tap |
| Water around indoor unit | Clogged condensate drain | Vacuum drain line | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Freezing in cool weather | Running AC below ~60°F | Stop cooling when cool out | Advise on low-ambient operation |
| Ice + short cycling | Faulty TXV / metering device | Turn off, call pro | Test/replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Simple airflow fixes are cheap; sealed-system work is where costs climb.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0 DIY – $150 professional.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400 depending on access and severity.
- **Blower motor capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900 installed (ECM motors cost more).
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450.
- **Leak repair + recharge (R-410A):** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant amount.
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $500–$900.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ (coil, labor, refrigerant).

Prices vary by region, refrigerant type (newer R-454B systems can cost more to service), and unit accessibility. Always get an itemized quote and a diagnosis before authorizing sealed-system work.
