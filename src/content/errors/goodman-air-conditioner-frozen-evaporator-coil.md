## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" is not a numeric fault code on Goodman air conditioners — most Goodman split systems and air handlers (GSX, GSXC, GSXH, DSXC condensers with ARUF/ASPT air handlers) don't flash a dedicated icing code. Instead, you'll notice the symptoms: weak or no cold air, ice on the copper lines or on the indoor coil cabinet, water pooling near the air handler, and the system running constantly without cooling the house.

The evaporator coil is the indoor coil that absorbs heat from your home's air. It freezes when either **airflow across it drops too low** or **refrigerant pressure drops too low** — both cause the coil surface to fall below 32°F, and condensation turns to ice. Once iced over, the coil can't transfer heat, so cooling stops entirely.

The key thing to understand: a frozen coil is a **symptom**, not a root cause. Thawing it gets you cooling back temporarily, but if you don't fix what caused the freeze, it will ice up again — sometimes within hours.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of airflow, and it ices over.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — restricting airflow has the same effect as a dirty filter.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil fins block airflow even with a clean filter.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge drops coil pressure and temperature below freezing. This is the most common *non*-airflow cause and requires a technician.
5. **Failing or undersized blower motor / wrong fan speed** — weak airflow from a dying ECM/PSC motor or a miswired speed tap.
6. **Clogged condensate drain backing up to the coil area** — and running in very cold outdoor or low-load conditions.
7. **Stuck or failed TXV / metering device** — improper refrigerant metering causes localized freezing.
8. **Running the AC when it's too cold outside** (below ~62°F) — low load conditions can drop coil temp under freezing.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Do these only after turning **cooling OFF** and setting the **fan to ON** so the coil can thaw and dry.

- **Turn off cooling, fan to ON.** This is step one — never run cooling on a frozen coil.
- **Replace the air filter.** Pull the old one; if it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, install a fresh one of the same size.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure all supply registers and return grilles are open and not covered by furniture or rugs. Closing too many vents is a common cause.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to COOL with a reasonable setpoint, and replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check the breaker.** If the air handler or condenser breaker is tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Inspect the condensate drain.** Look for water pooling around the air handler. If you can safely reach the drain line, clear standing water with a wet/dry vac and add drain tablets.
- **Let it fully thaw and dry** (1–24 hours) before turning cooling back on.

If the coil refreezes after a clean filter and open vents, the cause is internal — refrigerant, coil, or blower — and you need a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech won't just thaw it and leave. Expect them to:

- **Confirm the coil is fully thawed** before testing anything.
- **Measure airflow / static pressure** across the coil and check blower motor speed and amp draw.
- **Inspect the evaporator coil** for dirt, and check the filter and ductwork for restrictions.
- **Connect gauges and check superheat/subcooling** to verify refrigerant charge — low subcooling/charge points to a leak.
- **Perform a leak search** (electronic detector, UV dye, or nitrogen) if the charge is low, since simply "adding refrigerant" without finding the leak is bad practice.
- **Test the TXV/metering device** operation if charge and airflow check out.
- **Verify the condensate drain** flows freely.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search, or "replace the coil" without checking airflow first, is worth a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw | Measure static pressure, check ductwork |
| Refreezes after clean filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Ice on outdoor line set, poor cooling | Low charge or dirty coil | Thaw, replace filter | Gauges, coil cleaning, charge check |
| Water pooling near air handler | Condensate drain clog | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear line, flush pan, check pan slope |
| Coil ices but airflow seems fine | Failing blower / wrong fan speed | None | Test motor amps, correct speed tap |
| Frost only on part of the coil | Stuck/failed TXV | None | Test and replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and severity variation is wide):

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $5–$30
- **Service call / diagnostic:** $75–$200
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900 (ECM motors run higher)
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $350–$750
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000+ (more if matched coil or warranty parts involved)
- **Compressor damage from running frozen:** $1,300–$2,800+ — the expensive consequence of not shutting down

The cheapest outcome is a filter and a thaw. The most expensive is a compressor killed by repeatedly running an iced-up system — which is exactly why you turn cooling OFF first.
