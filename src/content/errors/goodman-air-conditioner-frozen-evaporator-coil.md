## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil is not a digital fault code — Goodman ACs don't flash a number for it. It's a physical condition: the indoor coil (inside your air handler or above your furnace) gets so cold that moisture in the air freezes on it, building a layer of frost or solid ice.

When the coil ices over, it stops absorbing heat from your home. The result is an AC that runs constantly but barely cools, often with weak airflow, hissing, or water pooling around the indoor unit once the ice melts.

The root cause is almost always one of two things: **not enough warm air moving across the coil** (airflow problem) or **not enough refrigerant** (a leak or undercharge). Both drop the coil temperature below freezing. The freeze is a symptom — fixing it permanently means finding which one applies.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter.** The most common cause by far. A restricted filter starves the coil of warm return air, dropping its temperature below freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents.** Furniture over registers, closed dampers, or undersized ductwork reduces airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil.** Dust and biofilm on the coil fins insulate it and block airflow, even with a clean filter.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak.** A leak drops system pressure, which lowers the coil's boiling temperature so it ices up. Common on older units and a frequent repeat-freeze cause.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wheel.** A weak ECM motor, failed capacitor, or dirt-caked blower wheel moves too little air across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather.** Operating cooling when outdoor temps are below roughly 60°F can freeze the coil; most Goodman units aren't designed to cool in those conditions.
7. **Stuck or failed expansion device (TXV/piston).** Less common; a metering issue overfeeds the coil and freezes it.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do not open the sealed refrigerant system or handle refrigerant — that's licensed work.

- **Turn cooling OFF, fan to ON.** At the thermostat, switch the system to **Off** but set the fan to **On**. This thaws the coil safely. Don't keep running cooling on a frozen unit.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** Pull it out — if you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of frozen coils.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and clear of rugs, furniture, and curtains. Aim for at least 80% of vents open.
- **Check the thermostat setting.** Make sure it isn't set unrealistically low (e.g. 62°F) on a hot day, which makes the AC run nonstop.
- **Look at the condensate drain.** As the ice melts, water must drain. Check that the drain pan isn't overflowing and the drain line isn't clogged. Drain tablets help prevent algae clogs.
- **Check the breaker.** If the system tripped while frozen, reset the breaker once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Wait for a full thaw.** Give it 1–3 hours (longer if heavily iced) before restarting cooling. Restart only after the filter is clean and vents are open.

If the coil freezes again within a day or two of a clean filter and open vents, the cause is likely low refrigerant or a blower problem — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good HVAC tech will confirm the real cause rather than just thawing it:

1. **Verify airflow first.** They'll check the filter, inspect the coil for dirt, and measure static pressure across the air handler to spot duct or blower restrictions.
2. **Check the blower motor and capacitor.** Confirming the ECM or PSC motor reaches rated airflow (CFM) rules out a weak blower.
3. **Take refrigerant readings.** With gauges on the service ports, they measure suction pressure, superheat, and subcooling to tell whether the charge is low. Low superheat/subcool with low pressure points to a leak or undercharge.
4. **Leak search if charge is low.** They'll use electronic sniffers, UV dye, or bubble solution to locate a refrigerant leak before adding refrigerant — topping off a leaking system is just throwing money away.
5. **Inspect the metering device.** A TXV or piston that's stuck or sized wrong gets checked if airflow and charge are both correct.

A trustworthy quote should name *which* cause they found. "It just needed refrigerant" with no leak search is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, ice on indoor coil/lines | Dirty filter or blocked vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw | Measure static pressure, clean coil |
| Coil refreezes days after clean filter | Low refrigerant from a leak | Thaw and monitor only | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| AC runs nonstop, little cooling, frosted lineset | Low charge or blower weakness | Set fan to On, replace filter | Gauge readings, blower/capacitor test |
| Water around indoor unit | Melting ice overflowing drain | Clear drain line, add tablets | Confirm drain slope, treat clog |
| Coil dirty/black, clean filter | Dirty evaporator coil | None safe | Professional coil cleaning |
| Freezing during cool/spring weather | Running AC below ~60°F outdoors | Turn off AC in cold weather | Confirm no other fault |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your area, unit age, and refrigerant type (R-410A vs newer R-32/R-454B) affect the total.

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40.
- **Service call / diagnosis:** $90–$200, often credited toward the repair.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450.
- **Blower capacitor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Blower motor (ECM) replacement:** $400–$900.
- **Refrigerant leak detection:** $150–$450.
- **Refrigerant recharge:** $200–$700+ depending on amount and refrigerant type; newer R-454B/R-32 charges can run higher.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000+ installed, more if still under parts warranty (you pay labor only).
- **TXV/metering device replacement:** $300–$650.

If your unit is old (12+ years) and needs both a coil and a major recharge, ask whether replacement makes more financial sense.
