## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" is not a fault code your Goodman displays — it's a physical condition you'll see or feel: a sheet of ice on the indoor coil (the A-shaped coil inside your furnace or air handler), frost on the copper refrigerant line, weak or warm air at the vents, and often water pooling under the indoor unit as the ice melts.

The root cause is always one of two things: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant in the system**. Either way, the coil surface drops below freezing and condensation turns to ice. The ice then blocks airflow, which makes the freeze worse — a runaway cycle that can end in a destroyed compressor if you keep running the unit.

Because most Goodman split systems share the same airflow-and-refrigerant physics, the causes below apply broadly across the GSX, GSXC, GSXN, and ASX lines. What varies is the metering device (fixed orifice vs. TXV) and whether you have a furnace blower or a dedicated air handler.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — The single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, so the coil temperature drops below freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — Furniture over registers, closed doors in too many rooms, or a collapsed return duct restricts airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Goodman systems are sealed; if charge is low, it's because of a leak, not "using up" refrigerant. Low charge lowers coil pressure and temperature below freezing.
4. **Dirty evaporator coil** — A coil caked in dust and biofilm insulates itself and reduces airflow, dropping its surface temperature.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wheel** — A weak ECM or PSC blower, or a dust-loaded blower wheel, moves too little air across the coil.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — Operating cooling when it's below ~62°F outside drives coil temperature too low. Goodman units don't all have low-ambient protection.
7. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV / orifice)** — On TXV-equipped models, a stuck valve over-feeds or under-feeds the coil and causes freezing.
8. **Restricted or kinked condensate drain backing up** — Less common as a freeze cause, but ice melt plus a clogged drain compounds water damage.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not open the sealed refrigerant system, do not handle refrigerant, and do not chip at the ice.**

- **Thaw it first.** Set the thermostat to **cooling OFF** and the **fan to ON**. Let the system run the fan for 1–3 hours to melt the ice fully before doing anything else. Running the compressor on a frozen coil can ruin it.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A dirty filter is the #1 cause. Slide it out — if you can't see light through it, replace it with the correct size.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and not blocked by furniture, rugs, or curtains. Keep most interior doors open.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to "Cool," that the temperature target is reasonable, and replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Avoid running cooling when it's cold outside.
- **Check the breaker.** A tripped breaker may mean the system short-cycled on ice. Reset once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Clear the condensate drain.** Look under the indoor unit for standing water or a full drain pan. A clogged condensate line or failed pump can overflow as the ice melts — but the freeze itself needs the airflow/refrigerant fix.

If a clean filter, open vents, and a full thaw don't keep the coil from refreezing, the cause is internal — refrigerant, coil, blower, or metering device — and it's a pro job.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed HVAC tech will work the airflow-vs-refrigerant logic, not just "add freon." Expect them to:

1. **Confirm and complete the thaw**, then inspect the filter, coil, and blower wheel for restriction.
2. **Measure airflow / temperature split** across the coil to confirm whether warm air is actually reaching it.
3. **Attach gauges and check superheat and subcooling** to determine if the system is undercharged — and by how much.
4. **Leak-search** with an electronic detector or dye if the charge is low. A reputable tech finds and fixes the leak before recharging; just topping off a leaking system is a temporary, costly band-aid.
5. **Test the blower motor and capacitor** under load to confirm it's moving rated airflow.
6. **Inspect the TXV or orifice** on the metering device for a stuck or restricted condition.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search or airflow check is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on coil, recently changed filter long ago | Dirty filter | Thaw, replace filter, monitor | None if it resolves |
| Weak airflow at vents, several closed | Blocked vents/ducts | Open registers and returns | Inspect ductwork if persists |
| Coil refreezes with clean filter | Low refrigerant / leak | Stop running, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Musty smell, visibly dirty coil | Fouled evaporator | Thaw; replace filter | Professional coil cleaning |
| Blower runs weak or noisy | Failing blower/capacitor | None | Test/replace motor or capacitor |
| Freezes only in cool weather | Low ambient operation | Don't run cooling under ~62°F | Verify low-ambient setup |
| Water pooling under unit | Drain clog + ice melt | Clear visible drain | Clear line, fix pan/pump |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor). Your area, system size, and refrigerant type (R-410A vs newer R-454B/R-32) affect pricing.

- **New air filter (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Service call / diagnosis:** $80–$200
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Blower capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor (ECM) replacement:** $450–$1,000
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$500
- **Leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant amount
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ (more if the coil is under warranty for parts but you still pay labor)
- **Compressor replacement (if run frozen too long):** $1,500–$3,000+ — the reason you should never run a frozen unit

Goodman's parts warranty (typically 10 years registered) may cover the coil or compressor part itself, but you'll still pay labor and refrigerant.
