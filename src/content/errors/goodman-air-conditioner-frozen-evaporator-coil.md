## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" isn't a fault code on Goodman air conditioners — there's no number on a display. It's a physical condition: the indoor evaporator coil (inside your air handler or furnace cabinet) has dropped below freezing and built up frost or solid ice. You'll often notice it as **weak or no cool air**, a **block of ice on the copper lines or coil**, or **water pooling around the indoor unit** once the ice starts melting.

A coil freezes for one core reason: not enough heat is reaching it relative to how cold the refrigerant is making it. That happens when **airflow is restricted** (so warm room air can't keep the coil above freezing) or when the **refrigerant charge or metering is wrong** (so the coil runs colder than designed). Both ends of that equation matter, which is why the fix isn't always obvious.

This is rated **pro** because the two most damaging causes — low refrigerant from a leak, and a struggling blower — require gauges, recovery equipment, or electrical diagnosis that homeowners shouldn't attempt.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air.
2. **Blocked or closed airflow** — too many closed registers, crushed flex duct, a closed damper, or a dirty blower wheel reduces airflow below what the coil needs.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil fins insulate it and reduce heat transfer, dropping coil temperature below freezing.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — less refrigerant means a lower boiling point and a colder coil. This is the most expensive cause and never "uses itself up" normally — low charge means a leak.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wrong fan speed** — a weak ECM or PSC blower, bad capacitor, or incorrect speed tap moves too little air.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — operating cooling below roughly 60°F outdoor temperature can ice the coil even on a healthy system.
7. **Faulty metering device (TXV or fixed orifice)** — a stuck or mis-sized expansion valve overfeeds or starves the coil.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Turn the cooling OFF first**, but set the thermostat fan to **ON** to help thaw the ice.

- **Filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it with the correct size. This alone fixes a large share of freeze-ups.
- **Thaw the ice fully:** Leave cooling off and the fan running for several hours (up to 24 if it's badly iced). Don't chip at the ice — you can puncture the coil.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL with a reasonable setpoint; verify any batteries are good on battery-powered models.
- **Registers and vents:** Walk the house and open all supply and return registers. Make sure furniture, rugs, or closed doors aren't choking return airflow.
- **Breaker:** If the indoor blower never runs, check that the air handler/furnace breaker and the disconnect switch are on.
- **Condensate:** After thawing, check that the drain pan and condensate line aren't overflowing. Clogged drains can trigger a float switch that shuts the unit down.

If the coil refreezes within a day of a clean filter and clear airflow, stop running it and call a technician — the cause is internal.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Goodman tech should:

1. **Confirm the system is fully thawed** before testing — readings on an iced coil are meaningless.
2. **Measure airflow** — check blower amp draw, fan speed setting, and static pressure across the coil to rule out duct and blower problems.
3. **Connect refrigerant gauges and measure superheat/subcooling** to determine whether the charge is correct. Low subcooling with high superheat points to undercharge/leak.
4. **Inspect the coil and blower wheel** for dirt that blocks heat transfer or airflow.
5. **Leak-search** if charge is low — electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test. Note your Goodman's refrigerant type (R-410A on most existing units, R-32 on some newer models); the tech should match recovery and charging accordingly.
6. **Verify the metering device (TXV)** is feeding correctly.

Be wary of any tech who simply "tops off" the refrigerant without finding the leak — that's a temporary patch on a problem that will return.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on coil, dirty filter | Restricted airflow | Replace filter, thaw, restart | None if it doesn't recur |
| Coil refreezes after clean filter | Dirty coil or weak blower | Open all vents, thaw fully | Clean coil/blower, check fan speed & capacitor |
| Hissing, weak cooling, ice returns | Low refrigerant (leak) | Shut off, thaw | Leak search, repair, evacuate & recharge |
| Water around indoor unit | Thawing ice + clogged drain | Clear drain line, vacuum trap | Clear drain, check float switch |
| Coil ices on cool nights | Running AC in cold weather | Stop cooling below ~60°F outside | Verify charge; recommend low-ambient kit |
| Blower runs slow or noisy | Failing motor/capacitor | None | Test/replace blower motor or capacitor |
| Ice despite good airflow & charge | Faulty TXV/orifice | None | Replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor:

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Thaw + restart (DIY):** $0
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$200
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Blower capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Blower motor (ECM/PSC):** $400–$900
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-32 systems can run higher)
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,500+

Pricing varies by region, refrigerant type, and access. Get an itemized quote, and insist on a leak diagnosis before any recharge.
