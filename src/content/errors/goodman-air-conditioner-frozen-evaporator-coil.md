## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" is not a Goodman fault code on the control board — it is a physical condition you'll see or feel: a sheet of ice on the indoor evaporator coil, the copper refrigerant line set, or both. Most Goodman air conditioners won't flash a specific code for this; instead you notice weak airflow, warm air from the vents, water on the floor near the air handler, or visible frost.

The coil freezes when the surface temperature drops below 32°F. That happens for two big-picture reasons: **not enough warm air moving across the coil**, or **not enough refrigerant pressure inside it**. Either way, condensation freezes instead of draining away, ice builds up, and cooling collapses.

Why this is rated "pro": thawing the coil is easy, but the underlying cause is usually a refrigerant leak, a failing blower, or a clogged coil — none of which are DIY fixes. Continuing to run a frozen system can damage the compressor, which is the single most expensive part in the unit.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the most common cause by far. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, dropping its temperature below freezing.
2. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — restricts airflow the same way a dirty filter does. Common after furniture rearranging or closing vents in unused rooms.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and block airflow. Builds up slowly over years, especially with no filter or a cheap filter.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — low pressure lowers coil temperature and causes freezing. Goodman systems do not "use up" refrigerant; if it's low, there is a leak that must be found and sealed.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / bad capacitor** — if the indoor fan spins slowly or not at all, airflow drops and the coil ices over.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — operating cooling below roughly 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil; not a fault, just misuse.
7. **Clogged condensate drain backing up** (less common as a freeze cause but often appears alongside it) — water can't escape and refreezes.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch.

- **First, thaw it.** Set the thermostat to **Off** and the fan to **On**. Let the blower run 1 to 3 hours to melt the ice. Place towels to catch dripping water.
- **Replace the air filter.** A dirty filter is the #1 cause. Use the correct size and a MERV the manual recommends — an overly dense filter can also restrict airflow.
- **Open and clear vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and not blocked by rugs, furniture, or boxes.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to Cool and not at an unreasonably low temperature. Set the fan to Auto for normal operation after thawing.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the AC and air-handler breakers are on. A tripped breaker can leave the blower off.
- **Check the condensate drain.** If you see standing water in the drain pan, the line may be clogged — gently clear the pan and verify the drain line isn't blocked.
- **After thawing, run the AC and watch.** If it freezes again within a day, stop running it and call a technician — you're likely dealing with refrigerant or blower issues.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech works the airflow side before touching refrigerant, because airflow problems are cheaper and more common. Use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirms the coil is thawed** and inspects for ice patterns (uniform vs. line-set-only).
2. **Measures airflow** — checks filter, blower amperage, and capacitor; inspects the evaporator coil for dirt buildup.
3. **Checks the blower motor** RPM and the run capacitor's microfarad rating against spec.
4. **Connects gauges** to read suction and head pressures and measures superheat/subcooling. Low suction pressure with normal airflow points to a refrigerant problem.
5. **Leak search** if refrigerant is low — electronic detector, UV dye, or soap test. A reputable tech finds and fixes the leak rather than just "topping off."
6. **Inspects the condensate drain and pan** for clogs and proper slope.

A tech who immediately wants to "add a pound of Freon" without checking airflow or searching for a leak is cutting corners — refrigerant doesn't disappear on its own.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter | Thaw, replace filter | Verify airflow, inspect coil |
| Warm air, vents closed in rooms | Blocked/closed vents | Open vents and registers | Check duct static pressure |
| Coil refreezes after filter swap | Dirty evaporator coil | Thaw, run fan | Professional coil cleaning |
| Ice mainly on line set, hissing | Low refrigerant (leak) | Shut off cooling, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Blower weak or not spinning | Failing motor / capacitor | Thaw, check breaker | Test/replace capacitor or motor |
| Water on floor, slow drip | Clogged condensate drain | Clear visible pan water | Clear drain line, add safety switch |
| Freezing on cool spring days | Running AC when too cold out | Don't run cooling <60°F | Confirm no other fault |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your price varies by region, system size, and refrigerant type.

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250.
- **Blower run capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Blower motor (ECM or PSC):** $400–$900 installed; ECM modules run higher.
- **Professional evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450.
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and amount of refrigerant. R-410A pricing has risen; newer R-32 units differ.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000+ installed, more if the coil is welded into a cased unit.
- **Compressor replacement (from running frozen too long):** $1,300–$2,800+ — this is exactly what you're avoiding by shutting the system down when it freezes.
