## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" is not a fault code on the screen — Goodman air conditioners and heat pumps do not flash a number for it. It's a physical condition: the indoor evaporator coil (the A-shaped coil sitting on top of or inside your furnace/air handler) drops below freezing and frost or solid ice builds up on it.

A coil freezes when one of two things goes wrong: **not enough warm indoor air is moving across the coil**, or **the refrigerant pressure is too low**. Both cause the coil surface temperature to fall below 32°F, so the moisture condensing on it turns to ice instead of draining away.

Once iced over, the system blows little or no cold air, the line outside may frost up, and water can overflow the drain pan as the ice melts. Running the compressor against a frozen coil can liquid-slug and destroy it — which is why this is a **pro-level** problem if a simple airflow fix doesn't solve it.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow starves the coil of warm air and it freezes.
2. **Blocked return/supply airflow** — closed registers, crushed flex duct, a closet door blocking a return, or a dirty blower wheel.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and reduce heat transfer over the years.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — low pressure lowers coil temperature. This is a sealed-system repair, not a "top-off."
5. **Failing or low-speed blower motor / wrong fan tap** — an ECM motor losing capacity or a PSC motor set to too low a speed moves too little air.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — operating cooling below roughly 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil; common with smart-thermostat schedules in spring/fall.
7. **Stuck or failed metering device (TXV/piston)** — meters too little refrigerant into the coil, dropping its temperature.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — no gauges, no refrigerant, no opening the sealed system.

- **Turn cooling OFF, fan ON.** Set the thermostat system switch to OFF and the fan to ON. This thaws the coil safely without running the compressor against ice. Allow 1–3 hours.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted filter is the #1 cause. Swap in the correct size and let air move freely.
- **Open all supply registers and clear returns.** Make sure no furniture, rugs, or closed doors are choking the return air.
- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it isn't calling for cooling when outdoor temps are below ~60°F.
- **Look at the condensate drain and pan.** Melting ice produces a lot of water. Make sure the drain line isn't clogged and the pan isn't overflowing; clear a visible clog and mop up standing water.
- **Check the breaker.** If the system was off due to a tripped breaker and you reset it, note whether it trips again — repeated tripping is a tech issue, not a reset loop.

If the coil refreezes within a day of a clean filter and open vents, stop running cooling and call a technician — you likely have a refrigerant or airflow hardware problem.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech should work the problem in this order, and a good quote will reflect it:

1. **Confirm the coil is fully thawed** before any pressure readings — measuring on an iced coil gives garbage numbers.
2. **Measure static pressure and airflow** across the coil and blower; verify the blower speed tap or ECM programming matches the system tonnage.
3. **Inspect the coil and blower wheel** for dirt, and the filter slot for restriction.
4. **Connect gauges and take superheat/subcooling readings** to judge the charge — low subcooling with low pressures points to a leak.
5. **Leak-search** if undercharged: electronic detector, UV dye, or nitrogen/bubble test. They should find the leak, not just add refrigerant.
6. **Check the metering device** (TXV bulb mounting, piston sizing) if airflow and charge look correct but the coil still ices.

Be wary of any tech who simply "adds a pound of Freon" without locating a leak — refrigerant doesn't get used up, so a low charge means it leaked out somewhere.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Replace filter, open registers, thaw on FAN | Measure airflow, clean blower wheel |
| Ice on outdoor lineset, low cooling | Low refrigerant from leak | Shut cooling off, thaw, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge to spec |
| Refreezes after clean filter | Dirty coil or weak blower | Visually inspect coil for dust | Clean coil, test/replace blower motor |
| Water overflowing near furnace | Melting ice + clogged drain | Clear drain line, mop water | Confirm drain slope, treat algae |
| Freezes only in cool weather | AC running below ~60°F outdoor | Avoid cooling on cold days | Verify low-ambient suitability |
| Hissing/bubbling near coil | TXV or metering issue | None — call pro | Test/replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Simple causes are cheap; sealed-system work is where it gets expensive.

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80–$200, often credited toward the repair.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400 depending on access.
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900 (PSC cheaper, ECM modules higher).
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $350–$700 including labor and recovery.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+, depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A systems run higher).
- **Full evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000+ if the coil itself is leaking and out of warranty.

Goodman registered units often carry a **10-year parts limited warranty**, so a coil or TXV part may be covered — you'd still pay labor and refrigerant. Verify registration before approving a major repair.
