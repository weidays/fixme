## What this code means

Goodman air conditioners don't display a literal "frozen evaporator coil" error code — there's no fault number for it on standard single- and two-stage Goodman systems. Instead, this is a **diagnosed condition**: the indoor evaporator coil (inside your air handler or above your furnace) drops below 32°F and the moisture in the air freezes onto it, building up a layer of ice.

Once ice forms, airflow across the coil collapses, the system stops cooling, and meltwater can overflow the drain pan. You'll often notice it as warm air from the vents, a block of ice or frost on the copper lines near the indoor unit, or water on the floor near the furnace/air handler.

The core problem is always one of two things: **the coil isn't getting enough warm air across it** (an airflow problem) or **there isn't enough refrigerant to keep pressures normal** (a refrigerant problem). Everything below sorts out which one you have.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow lets the coil get too cold and freeze.
2. **Blocked or closed return/supply vents** — closed registers, blocked returns, or shut interior doors starve the coil of airflow.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil fins act like the filter is clogged, even with a clean filter.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — a slow leak lowers pressure and coil temperature, causing freeze-ups that get worse over weeks.
5. **Failing blower motor or wrong fan speed** — a weak blower (or incorrect tap setting after a service) moves too little air.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — operating the system below roughly 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil.
7. **Clogged condensate drain** — backed-up water raises humidity around the coil and can contribute to icing.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **Turn the cooling OFF and set the fan to ON** for all of them — running the blower with cooling off is the fastest safe way to thaw a coil.

- **Switch to fan-only and let it thaw.** Set the thermostat to OFF (cool), fan to ON. This melts the ice and prevents compressor damage. Do not run cooling on a frozen coil.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, dusty, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of freeze-ups.
- **Open all supply registers and clear returns.** Make sure furniture, rugs, and closed doors aren't blocking airflow. Open closed registers throughout the house.
- **Check the condensate drain.** If water is pooling near the indoor unit, the drain may be clogged. Clearing the visible drain line with a shop vac (from the outdoor termination) is safe.
- **Confirm the thermostat setting and outdoor temperature.** Don't run the AC when it's below ~60°F outside.
- **Verify the breaker is on** for both the indoor and outdoor units after a thaw.

After thawing and replacing the filter, run cooling again. If the coil refreezes within a day, you likely have a refrigerant or coil/blower issue — call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech will sanity-check airflow first, then refrigerant. You can use this to judge a quote:

1. **Thaw and inspect** the coil, filter, and drain before measuring anything — readings on a frozen coil are useless.
2. **Measure airflow** across the coil and verify blower speed/tap matches the system tonnage.
3. **Inspect the evaporator coil** for dirt or biofilm restricting airflow.
4. **Connect gauges and take superheat/subcooling readings** to check the refrigerant charge. Low subcooling and high superheat point to undercharge/leak.
5. **Leak-search** (electronic detector, UV dye, or bubbles) if the charge is low — refilling without finding the leak is a temporary fix and a red flag.
6. **Check the metering device (TXV/piston)** if airflow and charge look correct but the coil still freezes.

Be cautious of any quote that adds refrigerant without a leak search, or replaces the coil before checking the filter, blower, and charge.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on copper lines, warm vents | Dirty filter / low airflow | Fan ON to thaw, replace filter | Verify blower speed, clean coil |
| Refreezes within a day of a clean filter | Low refrigerant / leak | Stop running cooling, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water on floor near furnace/air handler | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain with shop vac | Clear/clean drain, flush line |
| Weak airflow even with clean filter | Failing blower or dirty coil | Inspect for blockages | Test blower motor, clean coil |
| Freezes only in cool weather | Running AC below ~60°F out | Don't cool below 60°F | Confirm no low-ambient lockout needed |
| Gradual cooling loss over weeks | Slow refrigerant leak | Note pattern for tech | Pressure test, locate leak |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and system variation applies):

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Thaw + diagnostic service call:** $75–$200
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair:** $200–$1,000+ depending on leak location
- **Refrigerant recharge (after leak repair):** $150–$600 depending on refrigerant type and amount
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $800–$2,000+ if the coil itself is leaking

A simple filter-and-thaw is often $0–$40 DIY. The high end applies when a refrigerant leak forces a coil replacement, especially on older R-410A systems.
