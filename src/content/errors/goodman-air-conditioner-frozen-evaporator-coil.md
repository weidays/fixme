## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil isn't a fault code Goodman displays on a board — it's a physical condition you'll notice as **weak or no cooling, ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant lines, and water pooling around the air handler** as the ice melts. Some Goodman communicating systems (ComfortBridge / CoolCloud-enabled) may log a low-suction or airflow-related alert, but most standard split systems give no electronic warning at all.

The coil freezes when it gets **too cold relative to the airflow passing over it**. Either not enough warm indoor air is moving across the coil, or the refrigerant pressure inside the coil has dropped too low. Both push the coil surface below freezing, so humidity condensing on it turns to ice instead of draining away. Once a thin layer of ice forms, it insulates the coil, cooling stops, and the ice grows.

This is rated **pro** because the most common underlying causes after airflow — low refrigerant, leaks, or a failing blower — require gauges, leak detection, and EPA-certified refrigerant handling.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers or blocked returns starve the coil of warm air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil fins insulate it and reduce airflow, mimicking low airflow.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — low pressure means a colder-than-normal coil. Common on older systems; needs a tech.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wheel** — a weak ECM motor or dirt-caked blower wheel can't move enough air.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating the system below roughly 60°F outdoors can freeze the coil.
7. **Clogged condensate drain backing up** — water that can't drain re-freezes on the coil and grows the ice mass.
8. **Stuck or failing TXV/metering device** — over-feeding or under-feeding the coil. Pro diagnosis required.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Do these with the **system OFF** and the coil fully thawed:

- **Turn the system to fan-only or OFF first.** Let the coil thaw completely (1–24 hours) before testing anything. Never chip at ice.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted, or clogged filter is the #1 cause. Use the correct size and a medium-MERV pleated filter — an overly dense filter can also restrict airflow.
- **Open all supply registers and clear returns.** Make sure furniture, rugs, or closed doors aren't choking airflow.
- **Verify thermostat settings.** Set to COOL with the fan on AUTO; if you've been running it in cool outdoor temps, hold off.
- **Inspect the condensate drain and pan.** If water is pooling, pour drain tablets or use a wet/dry vac on the drain line to clear a clog.
- **Check the breaker.** If the air handler or condenser breaker has tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.

If the coil refreezes within a day after a clean filter and clear airflow, you almost certainly have a refrigerant or blower problem — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech will confirm the cause rather than just topping off refrigerant:

- **Measures static pressure and airflow (CFM)** to confirm whether the freeze is airflow-driven.
- **Inspects the blower wheel and motor amp draw** — a dirty wheel or weak ECM module reduces airflow.
- **Connects gauges to read suction and head pressure**, then calculates **superheat and subcooling** to determine if the charge is low or the metering device is off.
- **Performs a leak search** (electronic detector, UV dye, or nitrogen pressure test) if the charge is low — topping off without finding the leak is a red flag.
- **Inspects the evaporator coil surface** for dirt buildup and the TXV/piston operation.
- **Checks the condensate path** for backup contributing to ice.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" with no leak search or airflow check deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / closed vents | Replace filter, open vents, thaw coil | Verify CFM and static pressure |
| Coil refreezes after clean filter | Low refrigerant / leak | Shut off, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water around air handler | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain line, add tablets | Clear/replace drain, pan, float switch |
| Frost on outdoor line, low cooling | Low charge or stuck TXV | Shut off, thaw, call pro | Measure superheat/subcooling, replace TXV |
| Ice plus loud/weak blower | Failing blower motor or dirty wheel | Power off, call pro | Clean wheel or replace ECM motor/module |
| Freezing only in mild weather | Running AC below ~60°F outdoors | Avoid cooling in cool temps | Confirm no underlying charge issue |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and model):

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150–$250 professional
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $200–$450
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant recharge (R-410A):** $250–$700+ depending on amount; R-32 systems vary
- **Leak repair (minor braze):** $300–$900+
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900
- **Blower motor (ECM):** $450–$1,000+
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ (often with new line-set considerations)

If your system is older R-22 (pre-Goodman R-410A models), a coil or leak repair may not be cost-effective versus replacement — ask your tech for a repair-vs-replace breakdown.
