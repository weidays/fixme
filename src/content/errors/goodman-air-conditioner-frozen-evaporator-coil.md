## What this code means

"Frozen evaporator coil" isn't a flashing fault code on a Goodman — it's a physical condition you'll see or feel: ice or frost building up on the indoor evaporator coil (inside the air handler or above the furnace) and often on the copper suction line running outside.

The evaporator coil gets cold by design. It only freezes when something disturbs the balance between **how much heat is reaching the coil** and **how cold the refrigerant inside it is getting**. Either too little warm air is moving across the coil (an airflow problem) or there's too little refrigerant (a charge or leak problem). Both drop the coil below freezing, condensation turns to ice, and ice then blocks airflow even more — a runaway cycle.

The result for you: weak or no cold air, higher bills, water around the air handler when it thaws, and risk of compressor damage if you keep running it.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air.
2. **Restricted airflow elsewhere** — too many closed or blocked supply/return vents, a collapsed flex duct, or a very dirty blower wheel.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil fins insulate it and reduce heat pickup.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — under-charged systems run colder coil temperatures and frost up. This requires a tech.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wrong blower speed** — a weak ECM or PSC motor, or an incorrect tap/setting, moves too little air.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — operating cooling when outdoor temps are below ~60°F can freeze the coil on many systems.
7. **Faulty metering device (TXV/piston)** — a stuck or failing expansion device floods or starves the coil. Pro diagnosis.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop and call a pro for anything beyond this list.

- **Turn cooling OFF, fan ON.** At the thermostat, set the system to OFF (not cool) and the fan to ON. This thaws the coil with room-temperature air and prevents compressor damage. Let it run several hours.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes many frozen coils.
- **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles aren't closed, covered by rugs/furniture, or blocked. The system needs free airflow.
- **Check the thermostat setting.** Don't run cooling when it's cold outside (below ~60°F).
- **Check the breaker.** If the system tripped, leave it off while thawing; only reset once.
- **Check the condensate drain.** When the coil thaws it dumps water. Make sure the drain pan isn't overflowing and the drain line isn't clogged — a wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain stub or drain tablets can help clear it.

After the coil is fully thawed and you've replaced the filter and opened vents, you can try cooling again. If it freezes again within a day or two, the cause is deeper — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech won't just thaw it and leave. Expect them to:

1. Confirm the coil is fully thawed before testing (a frozen coil hides true readings).
2. Measure **airflow** — static pressure across the coil and blower, and verify the blower speed/tap is correct for the tonnage.
3. Inspect the **filter, coil, and blower wheel** for restriction or buildup.
4. Connect gauges to read **suction and head pressures**, and measure **superheat and subcooling** to judge the refrigerant charge.
5. If charge is low, **leak-search** (electronic detector or dye) rather than just topping off — a leak will refreeze the coil.
6. Test the **metering device (TXV/piston)** if pressures suggest a metering problem.

If a quote jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without checking airflow or searching for a leak, that's a red flag — ask them to verify the cause first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked vents | Thaw, replace filter, open vents | Verify static pressure, check ductwork |
| Coil refreezes after filter change | Dirty coil or weak blower | Thaw again; confirm filter is clean | Clean coil, test blower motor/speed |
| Ice on outdoor copper line, hissing | Low refrigerant / leak | Thaw only; do not add anything | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Freezes only on cool evenings | Running AC in cold weather | Don't cool below ~60°F outside | Confirm no other fault |
| Water around air handler | Coil thawing + clogged drain | Clear drain line, use shop vac | Clear/repair drain, check pan |
| Coil frosts unevenly, poor cooling | Failing TXV/metering device | None — call a pro | Test/replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your price varies by region and contractor.

- **Filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Clearing the condensate drain (DIY):** $0–$25 (tablets/shop vac)
- **Service call / diagnosis:** $90–$200
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor (ECM) replacement:** $400–$900
- **Blower capacitor or PSC motor:** $150–$500
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,200+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A pricing varies)
- **Evaporator coil replacement (if leaking internally):** $1,000–$2,500+

A simple filter-and-thaw is often free. The high end shows up only when there's a refrigerant leak, a failed coil, or a blower replacement.
