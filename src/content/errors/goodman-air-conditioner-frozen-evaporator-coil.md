## What this code means

Goodman air conditioners do not produce a dedicated "frozen evaporator coil" error code. Instead, this is a **symptom you diagnose by sight and behavior**: ice or frost forms on the indoor evaporator coil (inside the air handler or furnace cabinet) and often along the copper refrigerant lines outside.

A coil freezes when its surface drops below 32°F. That happens for two broad reasons: **not enough warm air moving across the coil** (an airflow problem) or **not enough refrigerant pressure** in the system (a refrigerant problem). Either way, the cold coil condenses moisture that then freezes into a growing block of ice.

On variable-speed or communicating Goodman air handlers, you may see an **airflow or low-static fault** logged, which can point indirectly at conditions that cause icing — but the ice itself is not a stored code. Behavior varies by model, so treat any logged fault as a clue, not a diagnosis.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow lets the coil get too cold.
2. **Blocked or dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm on the coil fins insulate it and reduce airflow.
3. **Closed, blocked, or too few supply/return vents** — furniture, rugs, or shut registers starve the coil of return air.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — drops pressure and coil temperature; requires a pro and is common on older systems.
5. **Failing or slow blower motor / wheel** — reduced CFM across the coil, often on older PSC blowers.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below ~60°F outdoor temp can ice the coil.
7. **Clogged condensate drain causing a safety float to cycle the system oddly** (model-dependent), or a stuck contactor running the compressor without the fan.
8. **Thermostat set too low or fan stuck on "auto" with very long run times** in humid conditions.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Do these only — they are all homeowner-safe:

- **Turn the system OFF and let the coil thaw.** Set the thermostat to **OFF**, then set the fan to **ON** to speed thawing. Never chip or scrape the ice.
- **Check and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted filter is the #1 cause. Swap in a clean one of the correct size.
- **Check the thermostat.** Make sure it isn't set unreasonably low, and replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Check the breaker.** If the system tripped, that's a clue — don't repeatedly reset it.
- **Walk every room and open all supply registers and return grilles.** Move furniture or rugs blocking them.
- **Check the condensate drain and pan.** A puddle near the air handler or a full pan suggests a clogged drain line — you can clear a visible clog with a wet/dry vac at the outdoor drain exit.

After thawing, run the system and watch: if it ices again within a day, you have an airflow or refrigerant issue that needs a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech will:

1. **Confirm the coil is fully thawed** before testing — readings are meaningless on an iced coil.
2. **Measure airflow / static pressure** across the coil and blower to check for restriction.
3. **Inspect the filter, coil, and blower wheel** for dirt and the blower motor for correct speed.
4. **Connect gauges and measure superheat and subcooling** to determine if the charge is correct. Low readings point to a leak.
5. **Perform an electronic leak search or dye test** if refrigerant is low, since simply recharging without fixing the leak is improper.
6. **Verify the condensate drain, float switch, and blower control board** operate correctly.

If a tech wants to "just add refrigerant" without searching for a leak, ask why — that's a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak/warm airflow | Dirty filter / low airflow | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Measure static pressure, clean coil |
| Ice on outdoor copper lines, AC runs constantly | Low refrigerant from leak | Turn off; call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water leaking near air handler | Melting ice / clogged drain | Thaw; clear drain with wet/dry vac | Clear drain, check float switch |
| Coil ices only in cool weather | Running AC below ~60°F | Turn AC off in cool weather | Confirm no other fault |
| Refreezes within hours of thawing | Blower or coil restriction | Replace filter, open vents | Test blower motor & CFM |
| Newer unit logs airflow/static fault | Restriction or blower issue | Check filter & vents | Diagnose per board fault log |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and contractor):

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Professional coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor replacement (PSC):** $400–$650
- **Blower motor replacement (ECM/variable-speed):** $600–$1,200
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 (more if the evaporator coil itself must be replaced)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,800

A simple frozen coil from a dirty filter can cost you **$0** beyond a new filter. The expensive cases are leaks and coil replacement.
