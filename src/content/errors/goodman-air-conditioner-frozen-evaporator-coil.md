## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil is not a fault code on Goodman equipment — it's a physical condition you'll see or feel: ice or frost on the indoor coil, the copper line set, or the air handler cabinet, paired with weak or warm airflow at the registers.

The evaporator coil is the indoor part of your AC where refrigerant absorbs heat from your home's air. It only stays above freezing because warm air is constantly blowing across it. The moment that heat exchange gets out of balance — too little air, or too little refrigerant pressure — the coil surface drops below 32°F, the moisture condensing on it freezes, and the ice grows until it chokes airflow completely.

Once ice forms, it snowballs: less airflow means a colder coil means more ice. That's why a frozen coil almost never fixes itself while the system keeps running.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. A restricted filter starves the coil of warm return air. (Easy DIY check.)
2. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — undercharged systems run colder coil temperatures and ice up. Goodman systems do not "use up" refrigerant, so low charge means a leak. (Pro only.)
3. **Blower problems** — a failing blower motor, slipping/failed capacitor, or wrong blower speed setting reduces airflow across the coil.
4. **Blocked airflow in the ducts** — too many closed registers, crushed flex duct, an undersized return, or a closed/dirty return grille.
5. **Dirty evaporator coil** — years of dust on the coil fins insulate it and reduce heat transfer, dropping coil temperature.
6. **Running AC in cool weather** — operating the system when outdoor temps are below roughly 60°F can drive the coil below freezing on many systems.
7. **Failed metering device (TXV/orifice)** — a stuck expansion valve overfeeds or underfeeds the coil, leading to icing. (Pro only.)

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Turn the system OFF at the thermostat** and switch the fan to **ON** (fan-only). This blows room-temperature air over the coil to thaw it without running the compressor. Let it run until all ice is gone — typically 1 to 3 hours.
- **Replace the air filter.** A clogged filter is the #1 cause. Use the correct size and don't choose an overly dense filter that chokes airflow.
- **Open and clear your vents.** Make sure supply registers are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs, and the return grille is clean.
- **Check the thermostat setting.** Avoid running cooling when it's cool outside. Confirm the setpoint isn't unrealistically low.
- **Check the breaker.** If the indoor blower tripped (so the fan never ran), the coil can freeze. Reset a tripped breaker **once**; if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Check the condensate drain and pan.** A frozen coil dumps a lot of water when it thaws. Make sure the drain line isn't clogged and the pan isn't overflowing — clear standing water and clean the line with a wet/dry vac if needed.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or remove the coil cover. Those are technician tasks.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech will confirm the coil has fully thawed first, then:

- **Measure airflow and static pressure** to rule out blower or duct restrictions.
- **Inspect and clean the evaporator coil** if it's dirty.
- **Connect gauges and take superheat/subcooling readings** to determine if the charge is correct. Low subcooling/high superheat points toward undercharge or a metering issue.
- **Leak-search** with electronic detectors or UV dye if the charge is low — they should find and repair the leak, not just "top it off."
- **Check the blower motor, capacitor, and speed taps** against the unit's spec.
- **Verify the metering device (TXV/orifice)** is feeding the coil properly.

Be wary of any quote that adds refrigerant without performing a leak search — that's treating the symptom, not the cause.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter / blocked airflow | Replace filter, open vents, run fan to thaw | Measure airflow, clean coil |
| Coil refreezes after clean filter | Low refrigerant (leak) | Run fan to thaw, then call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Warm air, water around air handler | Coil froze then thawed/overflowed | Clear drain, replace filter | Inspect pan, drain, blower |
| Blower not running at all | Blower motor/capacitor/breaker | Reset breaker once | Replace motor or capacitor |
| Frost on outdoor line set | Low charge or metering fault | Thaw, then call pro | Gauge readings, TXV check |
| Freezing only in mild weather | Running AC when too cool out | Avoid cooling below ~60°F outdoors | Confirm low-ambient limits |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and unit variation applies):

- **New air filter (DIY):** $10–$40.
- **Coil cleaning / professional service call:** $150–$400.
- **Blower capacitor replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Blower motor (ECM or PSC):** $400–$900 depending on type and access.
- **Refrigerant leak search + minor repair + recharge:** $400–$1,400, depending on leak location and refrigerant type.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,800+ (R-410A coils; cost rises sharply if a major leak requires full coil replacement).
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$700.

A simple filter-and-airflow fix can cost nothing but the filter. A refrigerant leak is where the bills climb — and why this issue is rated pro.
