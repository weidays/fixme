## What this code means

Goodman air conditioners do **not** flash a specific fault code for a frozen evaporator coil — there's no LED blink pattern or display number for icing. Instead, you discover it by symptom: weak or no cold air, ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, water on the floor as it melts, or the system running nonstop without cooling.

The evaporator coil sits inside your air handler or above your furnace. It's where refrigerant absorbs heat from your indoor air. When something throws off the balance between **airflow** and **refrigerant**, the coil surface drops below freezing and condensation turns to ice. Once ice builds up, it insulates the coil and blocks airflow, which makes the problem snowball fast.

A frozen coil is almost always a symptom of another problem. Thawing it is only step one — you must find and fix the root cause or it freezes again.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops coil temperature below freezing.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers, blocked returns, or furniture over a vent starves the coil of air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and cut airflow over time.
4. **Failing or weak blower motor / wrong fan speed** — low CFM means the coil gets too cold. Common on aging Goodman air handlers and miswired ECM motors.
5. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — less refrigerant means lower coil pressure and temperature. Often paired with a hissing sound or oily residue near fittings.
6. **Running the AC in cool weather** — operating below about 60°F outdoors pushes the coil too cold. Watch this on shoulder-season nights.
7. **Stuck-open expansion valve (TXV) or metering issue** — less common, but improper refrigerant flow chills the coil unevenly.
8. **Clogged condensate drain causing a float-switch shutoff** that masquerades as freezing — check this if you see standing water.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **First, turn the thermostat to OFF (cooling) and the fan to ON** to thaw the coil before doing anything else.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look. If it's gray, furry, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This fixes a large share of frozen coils.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL with a reasonable setpoint, not stuck calling for cooling at an impossible temperature. Replace batteries if it's a battery model.
- **Supply and return vents:** Walk the house. Open closed registers, move rugs/furniture off vents, and make sure return grilles aren't blocked. Aim to keep most vents open.
- **Breaker:** If the system is dead, check the breaker for the air handler and condenser. A tripped breaker that you reset once is fine; if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Condensate drain:** Check the drain pan and line for standing water or overflow. If you have a wet/dry vac and an accessible drain, you can clear a clog. A tripped float switch from a backed-up drain can mimic these symptoms.
- **Outdoor unit airflow:** Make sure the condenser isn't choked with leaves, grass, or debris.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or remove panels to poke at the coil while it's iced. Let it fully thaw — running a frozen system risks compressor damage.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Goodman tech should not jump straight to "you need refrigerant." Expect this sequence so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Confirm and document the icing** and let the system thaw if it hasn't.
2. **Measure airflow / static pressure** and inspect the filter and blower. Many "low refrigerant" calls are actually airflow problems.
3. **Inspect and clean the evaporator coil** if it's dirty.
4. **Check blower motor operation and fan speed tap** (or ECM programming) against the unit's spec.
5. **Attach gauges and take superheat/subcooling readings** to verify the refrigerant charge — only after airflow is confirmed good.
6. **Leak search** if the charge is low, using electronic detector or bubble/UV dye. A reputable tech finds the leak rather than just topping off.
7. **Test the TXV/metering device** if charge and airflow are correct but the coil still ices.

Be cautious of any quote that adds refrigerant without first checking airflow and searching for a leak — adding refrigerant to a leaking system is a temporary, EPA-disfavored fix.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, ice on indoor coil/lines | Dirty filter | Replace filter, thaw coil with fan ON | Verify static pressure if it recurs |
| Several rooms with no/low airflow | Closed or blocked vents | Open registers, clear obstructions | Balance airflow, check duct sizing |
| Ice returns after filter change | Dirty evaporator coil | Thaw, then call pro | Pull and clean coil professionally |
| Coil freezes, blower sounds weak/slow | Failing blower or wrong fan speed | Thaw, schedule service | Test/replace motor, set correct CFM |
| Ice plus hissing or oily residue | Refrigerant leak / low charge | Stop running unit, call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Freezes only on cool nights | Low outdoor temp + marginal charge/airflow | Avoid running AC below ~60°F | Verify charge and airflow |
| Standing water, system shut off | Clogged condensate drain/float switch | Clear drain line with wet/dry vac | Clear/replace drain, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your actual cost depends on tonnage, region, and indoor unit type.

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$30.
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0 DIY (tablets + wet/dry vac) to $75–$200 with a service call.
- **Evaporator coil cleaning (professional):** $150–$400.
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$900 for PSC, $600–$1,200 for ECM, parts and labor.
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800.
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $150–$400.
- **Leak repair + recharge (minor):** $400–$900.
- **Evaporator coil replacement (leaking coil):** $900–$1,500+, higher on R-410A systems or cased-coil swaps.
- **Refrigerant top-off only (not a real fix):** $200–$600 — expect it to freeze again if there's a leak.

A frozen coil from a dirty filter costs essentially nothing to fix. A leaking coil is the expensive end of the range, which is why proper diagnosis matters.
