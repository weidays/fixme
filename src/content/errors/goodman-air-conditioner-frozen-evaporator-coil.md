## What this code means

Goodman residential air conditioners don't display a specific "frozen evaporator coil" fault code — there's no blinking-light sequence for it on a standard GSX or GSXC condenser. Instead, this is a **physical symptom** you diagnose by sight: a layer of frost or solid ice on the indoor evaporator coil (inside the air handler or on top of the furnace), often with ice creeping out onto the copper refrigerant line.

The evaporator coil gets cold by design. It freezes when something throws off the balance between **heat coming in** (warm indoor air across the coil) and **refrigerant flow** (which absorbs that heat). When the coil gets too cold or doesn't get enough warm air flowing over it, condensation on the coil turns to frost, then ice — which blocks airflow even more, making the freeze worse in a runaway cycle.

The result: little or no cold air, higher humidity, water pooling around the air handler when it thaws, and eventually a compressor at risk. This page covers what causes it and which fixes you can do yourself.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — The most common cause by far. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, so the coil temperature drops below freezing.
2. **Blocked airflow elsewhere** — Closed or blocked supply registers, a collapsed/disconnected return duct, or a dirty blower wheel all reduce airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Less refrigerant means lower coil pressure and temperature, so it freezes even with good airflow. Goodman systems are sealed; low charge always means a leak, not "normal use."
4. **Failing or weak blower motor** — A slow or failing ECM/PSC blower can't push enough air across the coil. More common on older air handlers or after a capacitor weakens.
5. **Dirty evaporator coil** — Dust and biofilm insulate the coil and restrict airflow through the fins, dropping its temperature.
6. **Stuck or failing metering device (TXV or piston)** — Overfeeds or underfeeds refrigerant and disrupts the cold/warm balance.
7. **Running the AC in cool weather** — Operating the system when it's below about 60°F outside can drive the coil below freezing.
8. **Clogged condensate drain** — Less a freeze cause and more a symptom amplifier, but a backed-up drain plus a thawing coil floods the area.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **First: turn the thermostat to OFF for cooling, but set the fan to ON.** Running the blower with the compressor off helps melt the ice safely.

- **Filter:** Pull and inspect it. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone solves a large share of freeze-ups.
- **Vents and registers:** Walk the house and open every supply register. Make sure furniture, rugs, or curtains aren't covering them. Open return grilles fully too.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL with a reasonable setpoint (not 60°F). Replace batteries if it's a battery model and the screen is dim.
- **Breaker:** If the system is dead after you shut it off, check the breaker for the air handler/furnace and condenser. Reset once if tripped — but if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Condensate drain:** Look for water pooling at the air handler base or a full drain pan. You can clear a visible clogged drain line with a wet/dry vacuum at the outdoor termination point and drop in drain tablets.
- **Let it fully thaw:** Wait 1–3 hours (longer for heavy ice) before running cooling again. Restarting on an iced coil makes everything worse.

If the coil refreezes after a clean filter and open vents, the cause is deeper — call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent tech won't just "add Freon." Use this to sanity-check the visit:

- **Confirms airflow first:** Measures static pressure, inspects the blower wheel and coil, verifies filter and duct condition. Many freeze calls are airflow, not refrigerant.
- **Checks the blower motor:** Tests amp draw and capacitor (on PSC motors) or confirms ECM operation and proper CFM for the tonnage.
- **Reads refrigerant pressures and temperatures:** Connects gauges and measures **superheat and subcooling**. Low charge shows as abnormal superheat and a low suction line temperature.
- **Hunts for a leak (if charge is low):** Uses an electronic leak detector, UV dye, or bubble solution at the coil, line set, and flare/braze joints. A proper repair means **finding and fixing the leak**, then recharging to Goodman's spec.
- **Tests the metering device:** Checks whether the TXV or piston is feeding correctly.
- **Inspects the coil:** Looks for dirt buildup that needs cleaning.

Be cautious of any quote that adds refrigerant without leak-testing — that's a temporary band-aid that will refreeze.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on coil, weak airflow, filter dirty | Clogged filter | Replace filter, set fan ON to thaw | None needed if it stays fixed |
| Refreezes after new filter, good airflow | Low refrigerant / leak | None — sealed system | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Coil ices, blower seems weak | Failing blower or bad capacitor | Visual only | Test/replace motor or capacitor |
| Frost plus warm air, dirty fins visible | Dirty evaporator coil | None — coil is delicate | Professional coil cleaning |
| Water flooding air handler when thawing | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain with shop vac, add tablets | Clear line, check pan/float switch |
| Freezes when it's cool outside | Low outdoor temp operation | Don't run AC below ~60°F | Advise on low-ambient kit if needed |
| Ice plus uneven cooling, all vents open | Stuck TXV/piston | None | Test and replace metering device |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor; your tonnage and region shift these:

- **Air filter (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 pro
- **Blower capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor (PSC):** $400–$700
- **Blower motor (ECM):** $600–$1,100
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Refrigerant leak diagnosis:** $100–$350
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ (depends on leak location and refrigerant type)
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+

Older Goodman systems using R-22 refrigerant are far more expensive to recharge — at that point, weigh repair against replacement.
