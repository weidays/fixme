## What this code means

A "frozen evaporator coil" isn't a digital fault code — it's a physical condition. The evaporator coil is the indoor cold coil that sits in your furnace or air handler. When it gets too cold, the moisture condensing on it freezes into a sheet of ice instead of draining away.

On a Goodman system you'll usually notice it as **weak or no cold airflow**, ice or frost on the copper refrigerant lines near the indoor unit, water pooling around the air handler after the ice melts, or the system running constantly without cooling the house.

The freeze itself is a symptom. The coil freezes for one of two underlying reasons: **not enough warm air moving across it** (an airflow problem) or **not enough refrigerant in the system** (a charge or leak problem). Both lower the coil temperature below freezing.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops the coil temperature until condensation freezes.
2. **Blocked return air or closed supply vents** — too many closed registers, blocked returns, or furniture over a return starves the coil of warm air.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — dust and biofilm insulate the coil and choke airflow through the fins.
4. **Failing or slow blower motor** — a weak ECM or PSC blower, bad capacitor, or stuck blower wheel reduces airflow.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — low charge drops the coil's pressure and temperature. This is a repair, not a top-off — it must be leak-tested.
6. **Running the AC in cold weather** — cooling with outdoor temps below ~60°F can freeze the coil even on a healthy system.
7. **Stuck-open metering device (TXV or piston)** or a dirty/clogged metering device causing poor refrigerant flow.
8. **Oversized system or low fan speed setting** — chronic short-cycling or a mis-set blower tap can let the coil run too cold.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

Do these in order. They cover the most common cause and may fix it for free.

1. **Turn cooling OFF, fan to ON.** At the thermostat, switch the mode from COOL to OFF, then set the fan to ON. This stops more ice forming and pushes room air over the coil to thaw it. Allow 1–3 hours.
2. **Check and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted, or dust-caked filter is the usual culprit. Swap in a clean one of the correct size — don't over-restrict with a very high MERV filter the system wasn't designed for.
3. **Open and unblock vents.** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by rugs, furniture, or closed doors. Aim for as many open vents as possible.
4. **Look at the condensate drain.** After the ice melts, lots of water drains. Make sure the drain pan isn't overflowing and the drain line isn't clogged — a wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain end clears many clogs. Standing water can trip a float switch and shut the system down.
5. **Check the breaker.** If a float switch or breaker tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
6. **Once fully thawed and dry, restart cooling.** If the coil freezes again within a day, the root cause is deeper — stop running it and book a technician.

Do **not** chip or scrape ice off the coil — the fins and copper tubing bend and puncture easily. Do not add refrigerant yourself; handling refrigerant requires EPA certification.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech works the airflow side first, then the refrigerant side:

- **Confirms the coil is thawed and dry** before measuring anything.
- **Measures airflow / static pressure** across the coil and checks blower speed taps or ECM programming.
- **Inspects the coil and blower wheel** for dirt buildup.
- **Checks the blower capacitor and motor amp draw** if airflow is weak.
- **Connects gauges and takes superheat/subcooling readings** to judge refrigerant charge — low subcooling with high superheat points to a leak or undercharge.
- **Performs a leak search** (electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test) if charge is low — a reputable tech won't just "add a pound" without finding the leak.
- **Verifies the metering device (TXV/piston)** operation.

If a quote jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak test or any airflow check, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil/lines, weak airflow | Dirty filter or blocked vents | Turn cooling off, fan on, replace filter, open vents | Verify airflow, check static pressure |
| Coil refreezes after cleaning filter | Dirty coil or blower issue | Thaw fully, leave cooling off | Clean coil, test blower motor & capacitor |
| Hissing, low cold output, icing | Low refrigerant / leak | None — stop running it | Leak search, repair, evacuate & recharge |
| Water around air handler, no cooling | Frozen coil overflowing drain | Clear drain, thaw, dry pan | Clear/replace drain line, check float switch |
| Freezes only on cool nights | Running AC in low outdoor temps | Don't cool below ~60°F outdoor | Confirm no other fault present |
| Weak airflow, blower runs slow/noisy | Failing blower or bad capacitor | None | Replace capacitor or blower motor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges. Your price varies by region, system size, and refrigerant type.

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10–$40
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150–$250 pro
- **Evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower capacitor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Blower motor (ECM) replacement:** $450–$900+
- **Refrigerant leak search:** $150–$450
- **Leak repair + recharge (R-410A):** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and amount of refrigerant
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$800
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,500+ (parts often under warranty on registered Goodman systems; labor is not)

Tip: Goodman offers a limited parts warranty (often 10 years with registration), so a failed coil's *part* may be covered — confirm your registration and ask the contractor to file the claim.
