## What this code means

A frozen evaporator coil is **not a fault code** on Goodman air conditioners—there is no display or blink pattern for it. It is a physical condition you discover when the system stops cooling, you see ice or frost on the indoor coil or the copper refrigerant lines, or water pools around the air handler when the ice melts.

The evaporator coil is the indoor coil that absorbs heat from your home's air. It normally runs cold (around 40°F at the surface) but above freezing. When something drops the coil temperature below 32°F, condensation on the fins freezes instead of draining away. Ice builds up, blocks airflow, and the AC loses its ability to cool—often while the outdoor unit keeps running.

The two root causes are almost always **not enough warm air moving across the coil** (airflow problem) or **not enough refrigerant pressure** (a charge or leak problem). Both make the coil colder than it should be.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — The single most common cause. A choked filter starves the coil of warm return air, dropping its temperature below freezing.
2. **Blocked return or supply vents / closed registers** — Furniture, rugs, or too many closed registers reduce airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Dirty evaporator coil** — Dust and biofilm insulate the fins, lowering surface temperature and cutting airflow.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — Low charge lowers coil pressure and temperature. Common on older systems; the ice often forms first near the line connection.
5. **Weak or failing blower motor / wrong blower speed** — A slow or failing indoor fan (ECM or PSC) moves too little air. Aftermarket board or speed-tap setup errors cause this too.
6. **Running the AC when it is too cold outside** — Operating below ~60°F outdoor temperature can freeze the coil; common when people run AC on a cool night.
7. **Stuck or dirty TXV / metering device** — A metering valve that won't open enough overcools the coil.
8. **Clogged condensate drain backing up** — Less common as a freeze cause, but a flooded pan and high indoor humidity worsen icing.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **First, turn the thermostat to Off and set the fan to On**—this thaws the coil safely with no chipping or heat guns.

- **Air filter:** Pull it. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it with the correct size. This fixes a large share of freeze-ups.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to Cool and a reasonable temperature (not 60°F). Replace dead batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Vents and registers:** Open all supply registers and make sure return grilles aren't blocked by furniture, drapes, or filters stacked on top of each other.
- **Breaker:** If the unit tripped, you may reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Condensate drain:** Check the drain pan and line near the air handler. If water is pooling, the drain may be clogged—clear the visible line opening or add drain tablets.
- **Outdoor temperature:** If it's below about 60°F outside, don't run the AC. That alone can freeze the coil.

After the coil fully thaws (often 1–3 hours with the fan running), restart cooling. **If it refreezes within a day or two, the cause is internal—call a technician.** Do not keep cycling it.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech will sanity-check airflow before touching refrigerant—gauges on a frozen system read nothing useful until it thaws:

1. **Confirm the coil is thawed** and inspect the filter, coil, and blower wheel for dirt.
2. **Measure airflow / temperature split** across the coil to find a restriction or weak blower.
3. **Verify blower speed and motor operation** (ECM programming or PSC speed taps), especially after any prior board or motor swap.
4. **Connect refrigerant gauges** and check pressures and superheat/subcooling against Goodman's charging chart for that model.
5. **Leak-test** with electronic detector or dye if the charge is low—Goodman won't authorize a "top-off" without finding the leak under warranty terms.
6. **Inspect the TXV or metering device** if pressures suggest a restriction.

Be wary of any quote that adds refrigerant without first checking the filter, coil, and airflow, or that recharges without leak-testing.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ice on indoor coil, weak airflow | Dirty filter | Replace filter, thaw with fan On | — |
| Coil freezes, some vents closed | Restricted airflow | Open all registers/returns | Check duct static, blower speed |
| Ice near copper line connection | Low refrigerant / leak | Thaw, then call pro | Leak test, repair, recharge |
| Refreezes within a day of thawing | Internal restriction or low charge | Stop running it | Diagnose charge, TXV, coil |
| Frost + slow/weak blower | Failing blower motor | None | Test/replace ECM or PSC motor |
| Ice when it's cool outside | Running AC below ~60°F | Turn off until warmer | Confirm no other fault |
| Water around air handler | Drain clog + melting ice | Clear drain line, add tablets | Clear drain, check pan/float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and model):

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $5–$30
- **Coil cleaning by a tech:** $150–$400
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Blower motor (PSC):** $300–$600
- **Blower motor (ECM, common on Goodman air handlers):** $500–$1,100
- **TXV / metering device replacement:** $400–$900
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ (more if the coil is under warranty but labor isn't)

If your system uses older R-22 refrigerant, recharge costs run much higher and a full system replacement may make more financial sense.
