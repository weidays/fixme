## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific blink code or error number — it's the symptom you notice when a Trane air conditioner runs (or tries to run) but the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint. On systems with a communicating Trane thermostat (like the ComfortLink II line), you may instead see a fault or "Cooling Demand Not Met" type message, but most Trane ACs simply keep running while the temperature climbs.

The root cause falls into one of a few buckets: **restricted airflow**, **an electrical/control failure**, **low refrigerant**, or **a frozen coil**. Because refrigerant and most electrical work require an EPA-certified or licensed HVAC technician, this is rated a **pro** repair — but several safe homeowner checks below can solve it for free or tell you what to expect on a quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause. A clogged filter starves the coil, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator solid.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — the outdoor unit (or the whole system) has no power, so it can't cool.
3. **Failed dual-run capacitor** — a very common failure. The compressor or condenser fan won't start, often with a humming or clicking sound.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — causes weak cooling, ice on the lines, and long run times. Requires leak repair, not just a recharge.
5. **Dirty condenser coil (outdoor unit)** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, it can't reject heat, so cooling drops.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; the system blows warm air while ice melts.
7. **Failed contactor** — the electrical relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
8. **Thermostat problem or dead batteries** — wrong setting, dead batteries, or a wiring fault tells the system not to call for cooling.
9. **Clogged condensate drain with a float switch** — a full drain pan trips a safety that disables cooling.
10. **Failed compressor or fan motor** — less common but the most expensive outcome.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is hot, smells burnt, or makes loud noises.

- **Check the thermostat.** Set it to COOL and the setpoint at least 3°F below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Check the breakers.** Look for tripped breakers for both the indoor air handler/furnace and the outdoor condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — do not keep resetting it.
- **Look for ice.** If you see ice on the copper refrigerant line or the indoor coil, turn the system to OFF (fan ON is fine) and let it thaw for several hours, then run with a clean filter.
- **Inspect the outdoor unit.** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside of the coil fins with a garden hose (unit off) if they're caked with dirt.
- **Check vents and registers.** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Check the condensate drain.** If your system has a float/safety switch and the drain pan is full, a clogged drain can shut cooling down. Clear visible standing water and use drain tablets.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass any safety switch, or open electrical panels with the power live.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech follows a logical path — so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** at the thermostat and confirm 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. **Check incoming power** to the condenser and the air handler.
3. **Test the capacitor** with a meter (microfarad reading against the rated value) and inspect the contactor for pitting or sticking.
4. **Measure system pressures** with gauges and read superheat/subcooling to judge the refrigerant charge.
5. **Check temperature split** (return vs. supply air) — a healthy system is roughly 15-20°F.
6. **Inspect coils** (evaporator and condenser) for dirt and the blower for proper airflow.
7. **Leak search** if refrigerant is low — using electronic detectors, soap bubbles, or dye. A reputable tech finds and repairs the leak rather than just recharging.
8. **Test compressor and fan motor** windings and amp draw if a major component is suspect.

If a tech wants to "just add a couple pounds of refrigerant" without searching for a leak, ask why — that's a temporary fix on a leaking system.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor fan runs, air is warm | Dirty filter / low airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Check airflow, blower, charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, no fan | Tripped breaker or dead contactor | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, transformer, fuse |
| Outdoor fan hums but won't spin | Failed capacitor | None — call pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Ice on copper line or coil | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Turn off, thaw, new filter | Find leak, repair, recharge |
| Weak cooling, long run times | Low refrigerant or dirty coils | Rinse outdoor coil | Pressure test, clean, leak search |
| House warm, drain pan full | Clogged condensate / float switch | Clear drain, tablets | Clear line, test float switch |
| System short-cycles, warm air | Compressor or control fault | None — call pro | Test compressor, controls |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries / setting | Replace batteries, set COOL | Check thermostat wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, including parts and labor. Regional rates vary.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Capacitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Condenser/evaporator coil cleaning:** $150–$450
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ (depends on leak location; a leaking evaporator coil can be $1,000–$2,000)
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ (often worth comparing against full system replacement on older units)
- **Diagnostic/service call:** $80–$200, often credited toward the repair

If your Trane is under 5–10 years old, check warranty status — many Trane parts carry a 10-year registered limited warranty, which can cut parts cost significantly (labor usually isn't covered).
