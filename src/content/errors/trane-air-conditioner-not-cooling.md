## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a numbered fault code — it's the symptom you notice when a Trane air conditioner runs but your home never reaches the thermostat setpoint, or the air from the vents isn't cold. Some Trane systems with communicating controls (XV / TruComfort variable-speed) **do** log fault codes you can read at the thermostat or control board, but many single-stage and two-stage units give no code at all. They just underperform.

The job of the system is simple: the outdoor condenser and compressor move heat out of your house, and the indoor evaporator coil absorbs it. "Not cooling" means one link in that chain — airflow, refrigerant, or the compressor circuit — has broken down. Because diagnosis often requires gauges and electrical testing, this is rated a **pro** repair, though several common causes have safe homeowner checks first.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil into a block of ice.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer, so warm air comes out.
3. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, longer run times, sometimes ice on the line. Requires certified repair.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor and/or outdoor fan won't start; very common and relatively cheap to replace.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, or cottonwood fluff blanketing the coil traps heat outside.
6. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or fails, so the condenser never energizes.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown disconnect fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power at all.
8. **Thermostat or control wiring fault** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a broken low-voltage wire.
9. **Failed condenser fan motor** — compressor overheats and shuts down on internal protection.
10. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; little or no cooling and often the most expensive outcome.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here if you're unsure — don't open the sealed refrigerant system or the electrical compartments.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Swap the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor AC breakers in your panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris at least 2 feet around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside fins with a garden hose (power off).
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or copper lines, turn the system to **OFF** but set the fan to **ON** for 1–2 hours to thaw it, then run cooling again with a clean filter.
- **Condensate:** Check that the drain line isn't clogged and the drain pan isn't overflowing — a full safety float switch can shut cooling off.

## How a technician will diagnose it

Knowing the workflow helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Visual and airflow check** — filter, coil cleanliness, blower operation, and return airflow.
2. **Electrical testing** — measures voltage at the disconnect, tests the **capacitor** with a meter, and checks the **contactor** for pitting and proper pull-in.
3. **Refrigerant readings** — connects gauges to measure suction/liquid pressures and takes superheat/subcooling readings to judge the charge. Low readings point to a leak.
4. **Leak search** — if charge is low, they use electronic detectors or dye to find the leak rather than just adding refrigerant.
5. **Compressor and fan motor test** — checks amp draw, windings, and start components.
6. **Control diagnostics** — on communicating XV/TruComfort systems, they read stored fault codes at the thermostat or board; on basic models they rely on measurements.

A good tech tells you the **root cause**, not just "it needs Freon." Repeatedly adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Inspect blower, ductwork |
| Ice on indoor coil/lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Thaw with fan ON, change filter | Find airflow restriction or leak |
| Outdoor fan runs, no cooling | Bad run capacitor or contactor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor/contactor |
| Outdoor unit totally dead | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Check disconnect, wiring, fuses |
| Long run times, slowly warmer | Low refrigerant (leak) | None | Leak search, repair, recharge (certified) |
| Outdoor coil caked with dirt | Dirty condenser | Rinse fins, clear debris | Deep coil cleaning |
| Hums then shuts off outside | Failed compressor or start parts | None — call pro | Compressor/start component diagnosis |
| Cooling stops, water near unit | Clogged condensate / float switch | Clear drain line | Clear drain, test float switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor); your area and unit size will vary:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 DIY.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 service call.
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650 installed.
- **Coil deep cleaning:** $150–$500.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (older R-22 systems cost far more).
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $900–$2,500+.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,300–$2,800+ — at this point, weigh replacing the whole system, especially if it's older or uses R-22.

If your Trane is under 10 years old, check whether the **compressor or parts are still under Trane's limited warranty** — that can dramatically cut the bill (you typically still pay labor).
