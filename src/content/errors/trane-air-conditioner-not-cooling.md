## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a stored fault code on most Trane air conditioners. Single-stage Trane units (XR, XL series) don't display error codes for cooling problems — you diagnose them by observation and electrical testing. Variable-speed and communicating systems (XV series with ComfortLink II controls or the XL824/XL1050 thermostat) may show a system message or alert on the thermostat screen when a sensor, compressor, or communication fault is detected.

In all cases, "not cooling" means the system is failing to remove heat from your home. The air handler may be blowing room-temperature or warm air, the outdoor condenser may not run, or the unit runs constantly without lowering the indoor temperature. This page covers the full cooling chain so you can narrow down the cause before paying for a service call.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — restricted airflow is the #1 cause of weak or no cooling and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
3. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a miswired/failed thermostat (especially after a DIY swap).
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit or air handler has lost power.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure that stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting.
6. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity and freezes coils; requires EPA-certified repair.
8. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the outdoor fan won't spin, causing the compressor to overheat and shut down.
9. **Dirty condenser coil** — caked dirt/debris outside prevents heat rejection.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit hums or trips but won't pump refrigerant.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here and call a pro for anything electrical, refrigerant, or sealed-system related.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Set the fan to AUTO to confirm the system cycles.
- **Air filter:** Pull and inspect it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the most common cooling killer.
- **Supply and return vents:** Make sure they're open and unobstructed by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers. Closing too many rooms starves airflow.
- **Breaker:** Check the electrical panel for a tripped breaker to the air handler or outdoor unit. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping means a fault.
- **Frozen coil / outdoor unit:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system OFF (set thermostat to OFF, fan to ON) and let it thaw fully — this can take several hours. Do not run a frozen unit.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain or full pan can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Check that the drain line isn't blocked and the pan isn't overflowing.
- **Outdoor unit clearance:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the exterior coil fins with a garden hose (power off) if they're dirty.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A qualified tech follows a logical sequence — use this to sanity-check any quote:

1. **Verify the complaint and airflow** — confirms thermostat call, checks filter and blower operation, and measures temperature drop across the indoor coil (a healthy split is about 16–22°F).
2. **Check power and controls** — measures line and 24V control voltage at the air handler and outdoor unit, inspects the contactor and capacitor with a multimeter.
3. **Test electrical components** — capacitors are tested for microfarad rating; the contactor, fan motor, and compressor windings are checked for proper resistance and amp draw.
4. **Inspect the refrigerant circuit** — connects gauges to read suction and head pressures, measures superheat and subcooling to determine if the charge is correct or if there's a leak.
5. **Leak search if charge is low** — uses an electronic detector or dye; reputable techs find and repair the leak rather than just adding refrigerant.
6. **Communicating systems** — for XV/ComfortLink II units, the tech reads stored alerts and sensor data through the thermostat or service tool.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air from vents, fan running | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil with system off | Verify airflow, check charge & coil |
| Outdoor unit silent, no cooling | Tripped breaker / contactor | Reset breaker once | Test contactor, 24V control circuit |
| Outdoor unit hums, fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None — power off | Replace capacitor, test fan motor |
| Outdoor fan runs, weak/no cooling | Low refrigerant (leak) | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low charge | Filter swap, thaw fully | Diagnose airflow vs. refrigerant |
| Unit short-cycles or trips breaker | Compressor or electrical fault | Stop resetting, call pro | Amp-draw test, compressor check |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries / settings | Replace batteries, set to COOL | Check thermostat wiring & control board |
| Constant running, never cools | Dirty condenser / low charge | Rinse outdoor coil | Measure pressures, clean coil |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (vary by region and contractor):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Service call / diagnostic:** $75–$200, often credited toward repair.
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY).
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$650 installed.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is far costlier).
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,500.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$1,800+; often it's worth comparing against full system replacement on older units.
- **Thermostat replacement (communicating XL824/XL1050):** $300–$700.
