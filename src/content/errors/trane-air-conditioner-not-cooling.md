## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single Trane fault code — it's a symptom. Your Trane air conditioner may run but blow warm or weak air, short-cycle, or not start the outdoor unit at all. Depending on your system, the thermostat or the indoor control board may or may not display a specific fault.

Trane systems with **ComfortLink II** thermostats and communicating boards can display diagnostic messages and store fault history. Conventional Trane systems with a standard thermostat give you no error display — you have to diagnose by behavior. Because the exact codes and readouts vary by model and board, this page focuses on the symptom and the safe, well-documented causes rather than guessing at a number your unit may not show.

Cooling depends on three things working together: airflow across the indoor coil, a correct refrigerant charge, and electrical components (capacitor, contactor, compressor) energizing the outdoor unit. A failure in any one shows up as "not cooling."

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked return airflow** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow reduces cooling and can freeze the coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, scheduling, or a setpoint above room temperature.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser has its own breaker/disconnect; if it's off, the indoor fan blows uncooled air.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer until it thaws.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common electrical failure. The compressor or fan motor hums but won't start, or the outdoor unit won't run.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Trane systems are sealed; "low on refrigerant" means a leak, not normal use. Requires EPA-certified repair.
7. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — debris and grime reduce heat rejection and cooling capacity.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit; pitted or stuck contacts stop the compressor.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the outdoor unit may try to start and trip out.
10. **Clogged condensate drain** — on systems with a float safety switch, a full drain pan shuts down cooling to prevent water damage.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, handle refrigerant, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone fixes many "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers in your panel, plus the outdoor disconnect box. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many rooms can freeze the coil.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse visible dirt off the fins with a garden hose (power off).
- **Condensate drain:** If you see standing water in the drain pan or the unit shut off, the float switch may have tripped. Clear the drain line and add drain tablets.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to FAN ONLY for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter. If it refreezes, you have a refrigerant or airflow problem for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech follows a logical order — you can use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the complaint** and check thermostat call, mode, and wiring at the control board.
2. **Read stored faults** on ComfortLink II / communicating systems, or diagnose by behavior on conventional systems.
3. **Check airflow** — filter, blower operation, coil condition, and return ducting.
4. **Measure electrical** — line voltage, capacitor microfarads (against the rating printed on it), contactor operation, and amp draw on the compressor and fan.
5. **Measure refrigerant** — connect gauges, read suction and liquid pressures, and calculate superheat/subcooling to determine if the charge is correct. Low charge means a leak search (electronic detector or dye).
6. **Inspect the compressor** — windings resistance, locked-rotor amps, and start behavior.

A legitimate diagnosis ends with a specific failed component, not "it needs more refrigerant" without a leak explanation. If a tech wants to add refrigerant without finding the leak, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor fan runs, air not cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw on fan-only | Check airflow, charge, coil |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan on | Tripped breaker / disconnect off | Reset breaker once | Check contactor, capacitor, wiring |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed run/start capacitor | None — call pro | Test & replace capacitor |
| Ice on lines or coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Thaw, change filter, open vents | Verify charge, find leak |
| Weak cooling on hot days | Dirty condenser coil / low charge | Rinse outdoor coil | Clean coil, check refrigerant |
| Unit shut off, water in pan | Clogged condensate drain | Clear drain, add tablets | Clear line, check float switch |
| Outdoor unit starts then trips | Failing compressor / hard start | None — call pro | Test compressor, amps, hard-start kit |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries / settings | Replace batteries, set to COOL | Check thermostat wiring/power |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included where noted:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries / reset:** $0–$10 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 service call
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location (coil leaks cost the most)
- **TXV / metering device:** $400–$700
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,800+ (often worth comparing to a new system if out of warranty)

Many Trane parts carry a 10-year registered limited warranty — if your system is under 10 years old and was registered, the part may be covered and you pay labor only. Have your model and serial number ready.
