## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific Trane fault code — your air conditioner runs (or tries to) but the air coming from the vents is warm, lukewarm, or never reaches the thermostat setpoint. On communicating Trane systems (XV series with ComfortLink II or a Trane XL/XL824 thermostat) you may see an alert or fault icon on the thermostat screen. On older single-stage XR units there is no display, so you diagnose by symptom.

Because "not cooling" can stem from anything from a $20 filter to a failed compressor, this page is rated **pro**: the safe homeowner checks are quick, but most actual repairs involve refrigerant or electrical work that requires a licensed technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter** — restricts airflow over the indoor coil, drops capacity, and can freeze the coil solid.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so no cold air reaches the vents.
3. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode, dead batteries, or a miswired/failing thermostat not calling for cooling.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown outdoor disconnect fuse** — the outdoor condenser has no power, so only the indoor fan runs.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — the compressor or condenser fan motor won't start; you may hear a hum or clicking outside.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduced or no cooling, possible hissing or ice. Requires a licensed tech.
7. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood; heat can't escape.
8. **Failed contactor or relay** — the outdoor unit won't engage when the thermostat calls.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most serious and costly; the system runs but never cools.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should perform — none involve refrigerant, gas, or bypassing safety devices.

- **Filter:** Pull it and look at it against a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for tripped breakers. Trane systems usually have a separate breaker for the outdoor condenser. Reset a tripped breaker **once**; if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Confirm the pull-out disconnect box near the condenser is fully seated.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Condensate drain:** A full drain pan or clogged line can trip a float switch and shut off cooling. Clear visible standing water and check that the float switch isn't lifted.
- **Look for ice:** If the indoor coil or the refrigerant lines are frosted/iced, turn the system **OFF** (leave the fan running on ON) and let it thaw fully — several hours — before restarting. Persistent freezing means a pro is needed.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech follows a logical sequence — knowing it helps you sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** at the thermostat and confirm 24V signal reaching the contactor.
2. **Read any communicating fault codes** on ComfortLink II or XL824 thermostats (XV variable-speed units).
3. **Check airflow** — filter, blower speed, return restrictions.
4. **Inspect the coils** — indoor for ice, outdoor for dirt and debris.
5. **Measure electrical** — capacitor microfarads, contactor operation, compressor amp draw vs. nameplate.
6. **Take refrigerant readings** — gauge pressures plus superheat/subcooling to determine if the charge is correct or if there's a leak.
7. **Leak search** if undercharged — electronic detector, dye, or nitrogen pressure test.

Be cautious of any tech who proposes adding refrigerant without first finding the leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Check blower/coil if it persists |
| Ice on indoor coil or lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find cause; check charge/airflow |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Diagnose short, replace fuse/contactor |
| Humming outside, no fan/compressor | Failed capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Slow cooling, hissing, ice | Refrigerant leak | None — call a pro | Locate leak, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor coil caked with debris | Dirty condenser | Gently rinse with hose (power off) | Deep coil cleaning |
| No cooling, no thermostat response | Dead batteries / bad stat | Replace batteries | Replace/rewire thermostat |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Failed compressor | None — call a pro | Test and replace compressor |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; varies by region and model):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$200
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300–$600
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,800+ (often worth weighing against full system replacement on older units)

Note: R-410A pricing varies, and Trane units built from 2025 onward may use newer low-GWP refrigerants (R-454B), which can affect recharge cost — confirm with your tech.
