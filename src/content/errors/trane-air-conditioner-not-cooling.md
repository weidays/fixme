## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a stored fault code — Trane residential air conditioners generally don't display a numeric error for this. Your system may run and blow air that never gets cold, blow warm air, short-cycle, or the outdoor unit may sit silent while the indoor blower runs.

Because there's no single code, the cause could be anything from a $15 filter to a failing compressor. This page walks you through the likely culprits in order, the checks you can safely do yourself, and what a technician should charge so you can sanity-check any quote.

If your Trane system uses a communicating thermostat (ComfortLink II / XL850, XL1050), it may show a text alert or fault on the screen instead of staying silent. Note anything it displays before calling.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — Restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common and cheapest cause.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — Caused by low airflow (dirty filter, blocked vents, weak blower) or low refrigerant. Ice blocks heat transfer, so air won't cool.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — A very common electrical failure. A bad capacitor stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting; you may hear a hum or clicking outside.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Trane systems are sealed. If refrigerant is low, there's a leak. Symptoms include warm air, ice on the copper lines, and long run times.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — The outdoor unit can't reject heat when caked with grass, dust, or debris, so cooling drops.
6. **Failed contactor** — The relay that powers the outdoor unit. Pitted or stuck contacts stop the compressor and fan.
7. **Thermostat problem** — Dead batteries, wrong mode (fan vs. cool), miswiring, or a communicating-thermostat fault.
8. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — Outdoor unit dead while indoor blower runs.
9. **Failed compressor** — The most expensive outcome. Often the last diagnosis after other causes are ruled out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. If none fix it, call a pro.

- **Check the thermostat.** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the fan set to **AUTO**, and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it uses them.
- **Inspect and replace the air filter.** A gray, matted filter is a top cause. Swap in a clean one and see if cooling returns over the next few hours.
- **Look for ice.** Check the copper refrigerant lines and the indoor coil area. If you see ice, **turn the system off (set to fan-only)** and let it thaw fully — running a frozen unit can damage the compressor.
- **Check the breakers.** Reset a tripped breaker for the air handler and condenser **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — don't keep resetting.
- **Open supply and return vents.** Blocked or closed registers choke airflow and mimic a system failure.
- **Clear the condensate drain and pan.** A full pan can trip a float switch and shut cooling down. Empty standing water and check for clogs.
- **Clear the outdoor unit.** Remove leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser; gently rinse the exterior coil fins with a garden hose (power off).

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, remove electrical panels on live equipment, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Trane tech should:

1. Verify the thermostat call and confirm 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. Check the capacitor with a meter (microfarad reading vs. rating on the label).
3. Test the contactor and inspect for pitted contacts.
4. Measure temperature split across the indoor coil (typically ~14–22°F difference between return and supply).
5. Attach gauges to read **suction and head pressures and superheat/subcooling** — this reveals low refrigerant, restrictions, or overcharge.
6. If refrigerant is low, perform a **leak search** (electronic detector, dye, or soap) rather than just topping off.
7. Inspect the blower motor, coils, and drain system.
8. On communicating systems, read stored faults from the XL850/XL1050 or ComfortLink board.

Ask the tech to show you the capacitor reading or pressure numbers — a quote to "add refrigerant" without a leak search is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but never gets cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil (fan mode) | Check airflow, coil, refrigerant charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Bad capacitor, contactor, or breaker | Reset breaker once, check thermostat mode | Test/replace capacitor or contactor |
| Ice on copper lines or coil | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Leak search, recharge, airflow correction |
| Humming outside, fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | Confirm power on | Replace capacitor |
| Warm air, long run times | Low refrigerant leak | None safe | Locate leak, repair, evacuate & recharge |
| Weak cooling, hot outdoor unit | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse coil with hose (power off) | Deep coil clean, verify charge |
| Nothing runs at all | Tripped breaker / thermostat / control board | Reset breaker once, check batteries | Diagnose power path and controls |
| System short-cycles | Capacitor, low charge, or control fault | Replace filter, note pattern | Electrical and pressure diagnosis |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US pricing including parts and labor. Your area, model, and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22) affect the total.

- **Air filter:** $10–$30 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$200 (or DIY with tablets/wet-vac)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair + recharge:** $600–$1,600 (R-22 systems can run much higher)
- **Blower or condenser fan motor:** $400–$700
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 (communicating thermostats cost more)
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,900+ (often worth weighing against a full system replacement on older units)

Many companies charge a diagnostic/service fee of **$90–$180** that may be applied toward the repair.
