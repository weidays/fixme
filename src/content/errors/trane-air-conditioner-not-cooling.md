## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single Trane fault code — it's a symptom. Your system may be powered on, the thermostat calling for cool, and the indoor blower running, but the air coming out of the vents is room-temperature or warm. On systems with a communicating thermostat (Trane XL824, XL850, or ComfortLink II), you may see an alert message or a fault code on the outdoor board instead of a clean shutdown.

The root cause is almost always one of three things: **airflow is restricted**, the **outdoor unit (compressor) isn't running**, or the **refrigerant charge is low** from a leak. Each has very different costs, and only some are safe for a homeowner to address.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or frozen indoor coil** — The most common and cheapest cause. A clogged filter starves airflow, the coil ices over, and cooling stops.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — The outdoor unit has its own breaker. If it's tripped, the indoor blower still runs but no cooling happens.
3. **Failed run capacitor** — A common Trane outdoor failure. The fan or compressor hums but won't start, or the unit is silent. Often a sub-$30 part with labor.
4. **Bad contactor** — The relay that powers the compressor pits or sticks, so the outdoor unit never energizes.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — Causes weak cooling, ice on the line, or short-cycling. Requires a licensed tech — never a DIY top-off.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — A coil caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood can't reject heat, so cooling capacity drops sharply.
7. **Thermostat or wiring fault** — Dead batteries, miswired stages, or a failed thermostat that isn't sending the cool call.
8. **Failed compressor** — The worst case. The unit tries to start, trips, or hums and locks out. Expensive.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no tools beyond a screwdriver, no panels with line-voltage exposed.

- **Filter:** Pull and inspect it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of no cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Set it to COOL and at least 5°F below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered and the screen is dim or blank.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and the **separate outdoor AC breaker** in your panel. Reset a tripped one **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Visible vents:** Make sure supply and return registers aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain:** A full drain pan or clogged line can trip a safety float switch that disables cooling. Check for standing water and clear a visible clog at the drain outlet.
- **Frozen coil / iced line:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system OFF (set thermostat to OFF, fan to ON) and let it thaw for several hours before running again. Persistent freezing means low refrigerant or airflow problems — call a pro.

If none of these restore cooling, the problem is in the refrigerant or electrical system and needs a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech will work the system methodically — use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the call:** Confirm the thermostat is sending 24V to the contactor (Y signal).
2. **Check the contactor and capacitor:** Measure capacitor microfarads against the rated value stamped on it, and inspect contactor points.
3. **Measure amp draw:** On the compressor and fan motor to spot a seized compressor or weak motor.
4. **Take refrigerant readings:** Connect gauges to read suction and head pressures, then calculate superheat and subcooling to judge the charge and find a leak.
5. **Leak search:** If the charge is low, an electronic detector or UV dye locates the leak — adding refrigerant without finding the leak is a temporary, improper fix.
6. **Inspect coils and airflow:** Check the condenser coil cleanliness, blower operation, and static pressure.

Be wary of any tech who recommends "just adding a pound of refrigerant" without a leak search — that's treating the symptom, not the cause.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blower runs, warm air, outdoor unit silent | Tripped breaker, bad capacitor or contactor | Reset outdoor breaker once; replace filter | Test/replace capacitor or contactor |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Dirty filter or low refrigerant | Turn off to thaw; replace filter | Find/seal leak, recharge, check airflow |
| Weak cooling, runs constantly | Low refrigerant or dirty condenser coil | Rinse outdoor coil gently with hose | Leak search, recharge, coil cleaning |
| Outdoor fan spins, no cold air | Failed compressor or capacitor | None — call a pro | Amp-test compressor, replace if failed |
| Outdoor unit hums then trips | Stuck compressor or weak start capacitor | None — do not repeat resets | Test compressor, install hard-start kit |
| Nothing runs at all | Dead thermostat, blown fuse, no power | Replace batteries; check breakers | Replace 3A board fuse, trace wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor (varies by region and contractor):

- **Air filter:** $10–$30 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 service call
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A pricing varies)
- **Fan motor replacement:** $400–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,800+ — often the point where replacing the whole condenser or system makes more sense

If your Trane unit is under 10 years old, check whether the compressor or parts are still under the **Trane registered limited warranty** — covered parts can cut a compressor job to labor only.
