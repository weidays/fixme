## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific fault code on most Trane air conditioners — it's a symptom. Your system may be running while the air stays warm, short-cycling, or not turning on at all. Standard single-stage Trane units (XR14, XR16) don't display codes, so you diagnose by behavior. Variable-speed and communicating systems (XL18i, XV18, XV20i) with a ComfortLink II or compatible thermostat may show a specific alert or fault on the screen — check that display before assuming a generic problem.

Because "not cooling" covers everything from a clogged filter to a dead compressor, the goal here is to separate the cheap, safe fixes you can confirm yourself from the problems that legitimately need a licensed HVAC technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (set to "heat" or "fan"), or a programming/schedule conflict.
3. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so air blows warm.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser has its own breaker; if it's off, the indoor fan may still run with no cooling.
5. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a very common failure that stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — produces weak cooling, ice, and hissing; requires EPA-certified repair.
7. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with dirt or debris, the unit can't reject heat.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit can stick or burn out.
9. **Bad compressor** — the most expensive failure; the unit hums but won't cool, or trips the breaker.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Air filter:** Pull it out. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a surprising number of "not cooling" calls.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **AUTO**. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor air handler/furnace breaker and the separate outdoor condenser breaker. If one tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain / safety switch:** A full drain pan can trip a float switch and shut down cooling. If you see standing water in the pan, the drain is clogged — clear it or call for service.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, switch the system to **FAN ONLY** and let it thaw for several hours before running cooling again. Never chip at the ice.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bend coil fins aggressively, or repeatedly reset a breaker that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Trane technician will work through a logical sequence so you can sanity-check the visit:

1. **Verify the symptom** and read any fault codes on communicating thermostats.
2. **Check airflow** — filter, blower operation, and ductwork.
3. **Inspect the outdoor unit** — listen for the compressor and condenser fan, check the contactor and capacitor with a meter.
4. **Measure electrical values** — capacitor microfarads, voltage at the contactor, and amp draw on the compressor.
5. **Connect refrigerant gauges** to read suction and head pressures and calculate superheat/subcooling — this reveals undercharge, overcharge, or restriction.
6. **Inspect the coils** for cleanliness and the evaporator for ice or oil residue (a sign of a leak).
7. **Leak search** if refrigerant is low, using electronic detectors or dye.

A quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without a leak search, or "replace the compressor" without electrical testing, deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but warm | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, run fan to thaw | Diagnose airflow or refrigerant cause |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan runs | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor, capacitor |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low refrigerant or low airflow | Fan-only to thaw, replace filter | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Weak cooling on hot days | Dirty condenser coil | Gently rinse off debris (power off) | Deep coil cleaning, charge check |
| Unit trips breaker repeatedly | Compressor or wiring fault | Stop resetting, call pro | Electrical diagnosis, compressor test |
| Thermostat blank or wrong mode | Dead batteries / settings | Replace batteries, set to COOL | Replace thermostat, check wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor; prices vary by region and contractor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$25 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries:** under $10 (DIY).
- **Programmable thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is far costlier).
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,500.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$3,000+; on an older unit, full system replacement is often the smarter spend.

A diagnostic service call typically runs $90–$200 and is usually credited toward the repair.
