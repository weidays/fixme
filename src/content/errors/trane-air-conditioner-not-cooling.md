## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single blink code — it's a symptom. Your Trane air conditioner may be running (the fan spins, you hear the outdoor unit hum) but the air from your vents is room-temperature or warm, and the house never reaches setpoint. On variable-speed Trane systems (XV series with a communicating board), you may also see a fault or status code on the indoor control or thermostat. Single-stage XR and XL units typically give no code at all — you just notice the heat.

Because "not cooling" can stem from anything from a $20 filter to a failed compressor, this page walks you through the likely causes in order, the few checks that are genuinely safe, and what a technician should do so you can sanity-check the quote.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil into a block of ice. The single most common and cheapest cause.
2. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked with dust, grass, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat. Common after a season without cleaning.
3. **Failed run capacitor** — a bulged or dead capacitor stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting. Very common in hot climates.
4. **Low refrigerant charge from a leak** — system runs but can't cool; often shows ice on the copper lines or indoor coil. Always indicates a leak, not "topping off."
5. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks; the condenser won't energize.
6. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low charge; ice blocks heat transfer entirely.
7. **Tripped breaker or blown control fuse** — outdoor unit gets no power.
8. **Thermostat issue or wiring fault** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a broken low-voltage wire.
9. **Clogged condensate line tripping a float switch** — a full drain pan safety switch shuts cooling off to prevent water damage.
10. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; the unit hums or trips but won't pump refrigerant. Least common but most expensive.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. If none resolve it, call a pro.

- **Filter:** Pull it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Lower it 3°F and listen for the outdoor unit to start.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor AC breakers in your panel. If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop — call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Visually check that it's not buried in leaves, grass clippings, or debris. Hose off the *exterior* fins gently with the power off if they're dirty — never poke between them.
- **Condensate drain:** If you have an emergency drain pan with water in it, the float switch may have shut cooling off. Clearing a clogged drain line is a known fix, but if you're unsure, leave it to the tech.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the copper lines or the indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** (fan ON is okay) and let it thaw for a few hours before running again — then replace the filter.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, bypass safety switches, or repeatedly reset a breaker that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Trane tech should:

1. **Confirm the complaint** and check the thermostat, mode, and call for cooling.
2. **Measure airflow and the temperature split** across the indoor coil (typically 16–22°F drop). A low split points to airflow or charge problems.
3. **Inspect the filter and both coils** for restriction and ice.
4. **Test the capacitor and contactor** with a meter — fast, cheap parts to rule out first.
5. **Attach refrigerant gauges** and read pressures and superheat/subcooling. This tells them if the charge is low and hints at where.
6. **Leak-search** if refrigerant is low — electronic detector, soap bubbles, or dye. A pro should never just "top off" without finding the leak.
7. **On XV/communicating systems,** pull active and stored fault codes from the board to confirm electronic faults.
8. **Check the compressor** windings and amp draw if it won't start or trips.

Sanity check: if a tech quotes a compressor or refrigerant job *without* attaching gauges or checking the capacitor first, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, fan runs, dirty filter | Clogged filter / low airflow | Replace filter, clear vents | Verify split, check coil for ice |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Low charge or low airflow | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find leak, recharge, repair |
| Outdoor fan won't spin | Bad capacitor or contactor | None — power off | Test & replace capacitor/contactor |
| Outdoor unit dead, no hum | Tripped breaker / blown fuse | Reset breaker once | Find cause of trip, replace fuse |
| Hums then shuts off | Failed capacitor or compressor | None | Test compressor & start components |
| Cooling stops, water in pan | Clogged condensate / float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Flush line, test float switch |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Low refrigerant or undersized | None | Gauge pressures, leak search |
| Some rooms hot, others cool | Duct or airflow imbalance | Open all vents | Inspect ducts, dampers, airflow |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90 – $200
- **Air filter:** $10 – $40 (DIY)
- **Run capacitor:** $150 – $350
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150 – $400
- **Condensate line clearing / float switch:** $100 – $300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400 – $1,500 (depends on leak location)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $1,000 – $2,500
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500 – $2,500+ (often worth comparing to a new system if the unit is 10+ years old)
- **TXV / metering device:** $400 – $900

If your Trane unit still uses R-22 refrigerant, leak repairs get expensive fast — R-22 is phased out and costly. Replacing an aging R-22 system often makes more sense than chasing leaks.
