## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific blink code — it's the symptom most Trane air-conditioner owners search for when the thermostat calls for cool but the house stays warm. Depending on your model, the outdoor unit may be silent, humming, or running full-tilt while warm air comes from the vents.

On basic single-stage units (XR13, XR14) there's no display, so you diagnose by observing behavior: fan spinning, compressor humming, ice on the lines. On higher-end two-stage and variable-speed systems (XL16i, XV18, XV20i) the indoor control board or a compatible communicating thermostat may log a fault or show a status LED that narrows the cause. Because board behavior varies by model, check your unit's specific service literature rather than assuming a code means the same thing across the lineup.

A properly working system should drop supply-air temperature roughly 15–20°F below the return air. If it isn't, one of the causes below is at play.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the most common and cheapest cause. Restricted airflow drops capacity and can freeze the coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant. The unit runs but blows warm or weak air; you may see ice on the copper lines.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a bulging or dead dual capacitor stops the compressor or fan from starting. You'll often hear a hum or clicking.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity and can freeze the coil. Requires an EPA-certified tech; refrigerant does not "get used up."
5. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit can burn or stick, leaving the condenser dead or stuck running.
6. **Dirty condenser coil** — a caked outdoor coil can't reject heat, so cooling fades on hot days.
7. **Thermostat problem or miswiring** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a failed thermostat that never calls for cool.
8. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor disconnect or breaker has tripped.
9. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; the unit hums or trips the breaker and never cools. Common on older systems.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here and call a pro for anything electrical inside the unit or anything involving refrigerant.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If it's gray or clogged, replace it and give the system 30 minutes.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breaker:** Check the main panel and the outdoor disconnect for a tripped breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a tech — repeated resets can damage the compressor.
- **Vents and returns:** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or the indoor coil, turn the system to OFF (fan ON is fine) and let it thaw fully — several hours — before running it again.
- **Condensate:** A full drain pan or clogged condensate line can trip a float safety switch and stop cooling. Check for standing water and clear a visible clog if you're comfortable doing so.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser so it can breathe.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech should work methodically — a quote that skips straight to "you need refrigerant" without leak testing is a red flag.

1. **Verify the call for cool** and confirm 24V is reaching the contactor.
2. **Measure airflow and temperature split** across the coil to confirm the symptom.
3. **Test the capacitor** with a meter for correct microfarad rating.
4. **Check the contactor** for pitting, chattering, or stuck contacts.
5. **Inspect and clean the condenser coil** if it's fouled.
6. **Connect gauges** to read suction and head pressures, then calculate superheat/subcooling to judge the refrigerant charge.
7. **Leak-test** with electronic detector or dye if the charge is low — a proper repair fixes the leak, not just tops off.
8. **Check the compressor** windings and amp draw if it won't start or trips.
9. On variable-speed/communicating models, **read stored fault data** from the board or thermostat to confirm sensor and inverter status.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak/warm air, dirty filter | Restricted airflow | Replace filter, wait 30 min | Check blower and duct static |
| Ice on lines or coil | Frozen evaporator | Turn off, thaw fully, replace filter | Check charge and airflow |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed capacitor | None (electrical) | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit totally dead | Tripped breaker or contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Cooling fades on hot days | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse debris off exterior | Deep-clean coil, check charge |
| Runs constantly, never cold | Low refrigerant (leak) | None | Leak test, repair, recharge |
| No response at all | Thermostat/wiring | Check mode, batteries | Test thermostat and 24V circuit |
| Hums then trips breaker | Failed compressor | Stop; don't reset repeatedly | Test windings, quote replacement |

## Repair costs

Ranges are typical US installed prices; your region, unit age, and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22) affect the total.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat replacement:** $100–$350 depending on model and compatibility.
- **Run/dual capacitor:** $120–$300 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$300.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type. Older R-22 systems cost far more per pound.
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000 installed.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,300–$2,500+; on an older system, weigh this against full replacement.

If your system is 12–15+ years old and facing a compressor or coil, ask for a repair-vs-replace comparison before authorizing the work.
