## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code on Trane equipment — it's a symptom. Your thermostat is calling for cooling, the air handler may be blowing, but the air coming from the vents isn't cold (or isn't cold enough to bring the house to setpoint).

Trane communicating systems (with a ComfortLink II or similar control) may display a specific fault or status message at the thermostat. Non-communicating systems give you no code at all — you only notice the house staying warm. Because the cause spans airflow, electrical, and the sealed refrigerant system, the right fix depends entirely on which part of the system has failed.

This is a **pro-level** problem once the easy checks are ruled out: refrigerant work, electrical components inside the condenser, and compressor diagnosis all require a licensed technician and proper tools.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common reason a Trane AC "stops cooling."
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so the air feels cool but never cold.
3. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — grass, dust, or cottonwood blanketing the coil prevents the unit from rejecting heat.
4. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a bulging or dead capacitor stops the compressor or condenser fan from starting. Very common in hot climates.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — the system loses capacity gradually; you may hear hissing or see ice. Always indicates a leak.
6. **Bad contactor** — the electrical relay that powers the condenser fails to pull in, so the outdoor unit never starts.
7. **Failed condenser fan motor** — the compressor runs but the fan doesn't, causing high-pressure lockout and no cooling.
8. **Thermostat or control wiring fault** — a miswired, dead, or misconfigured thermostat (common after a DIY swap) never sends the cooling signal correctly.
9. **Failed compressor** — the costliest cause; the heart of the system won't pump refrigerant.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here and call a pro if these don't restore cooling.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it against a light. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause and a $15 fix.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Set the fan to AUTO.
- **Breakers:** Check the electrical panel for a tripped breaker for the air handler **and** the outdoor condenser (often a separate double-pole breaker). Reset **once**. If it trips again, leave it off and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** Make sure the pull-out disconnect near the condenser is fully seated.
- **Supply and return vents:** Confirm registers are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs, and the return grille isn't clogged.
- **Condensate drain / pan switch:** If the air handler has a full drain pan or float switch tripped (water near the unit), it can shut cooling off. Clear standing water and check the drain line outlet outside.
- **Look for ice:** If you see ice on the copper lines or coil, turn the system to **FAN ONLY** (not cool) for 1–2 hours to thaw it, then run cooling. If it ices again, you have a refrigerant or airflow problem — call a pro.

Do **not** open the condenser electrical panel, touch the capacitor, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A licensed tech should work through the system in order — use this to sanity-check the quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** — confirms 24V is reaching the contactor when the thermostat calls.
2. **Check airflow and filter** — measures temperature split across the indoor coil (typically ~16–22°F drop when healthy).
3. **Inspect the capacitor and contactor** — tests microfarads on the capacitor and checks the contactor pulls in.
4. **Read refrigerant pressures with gauges** — high and low side pressures plus superheat/subcooling tell them if the charge is correct or if there's a leak or restriction.
5. **Leak search** — if low on charge, electronic detector or dye to locate the leak before adding refrigerant.
6. **Check the condenser fan and compressor** — amperage draw and whether the compressor starts and pumps.
7. **On communicating Trane systems**, they'll pull fault history from the ComfortLink control.

Be wary of any tech who "just adds refrigerant" without finding a leak, or recommends a full system replacement before diagnosing the actual failed part.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter / low airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp split, check airflow |
| Ice on coil or copper lines | Frozen coil (low airflow or low charge) | Run fan-only to thaw, swap filter | Find airflow restriction or leak |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor blows | Bad capacitor or contactor | Check breaker once | Test/replace capacitor or contactor |
| Compressor hums, fan dead | Failed condenser fan motor | Shut unit off | Replace fan motor and capacitor |
| Hissing, gradual loss of cooling | Refrigerant leak | None — call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| House never cools after thermostat swap | Wiring / config error | Recheck wires vs. manual | Correct wiring, configure control |
| Breaker keeps tripping | Electrical fault / failing compressor | Reset once only | Diagnose short or compressor |
| Loud buzz, no start, warm air | Failed compressor | None — call pro | Compressor test / replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined. Costs vary by region and system size.

- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90 – $200
- **Air filter:** $0 – $30 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150 – $400
- **Contactor:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser fan motor:** $300 – $700
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100 – $250
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $500 – $1,800+ (depends on leak location and refrigerant type)
- **Coil cleaning (indoor or outdoor):** $150 – $400
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200 – $2,800 (often pushes toward full condenser or system replacement)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150 – $400

If your Trane is under 10 years old and still under parts warranty, the part may be covered — you'd pay labor only. Keep your model and serial numbers handy.
