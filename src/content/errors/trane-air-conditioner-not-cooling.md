## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a specific Trane fault code. Your Trane air conditioner may be running, blowing air, and drawing power — but the air coming from your vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Trane central air conditioners don't display a numeric "not cooling" code the way a furnace control board flashes a blink count. Instead, you diagnose the system by symptom: the outdoor condenser, the indoor blower, the refrigerant charge, and the airflow path all have to work together. If any one fails, you lose cooling.

On communicating systems (like the XV20i with a ComfortLink II thermostat), the thermostat may show an alert or fault message that points to a specific subsystem — but on most single-stage XR and XL units there's no display, so you work the symptom.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow can freeze the evaporator coil and kill cooling output.
2. **Dirty or blocked outdoor condenser coil** — leaves, grass, dryer lint, or cottonwood matting the coil prevent heat rejection.
3. **Thermostat set wrong or dead batteries** — set to "fan" instead of "cool," wrong setpoint, or a dead battery-powered thermostat.
4. **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse** — the outdoor unit gets no power, so only the indoor fan runs.
5. **Failed run capacitor** — a weak or bulged dual capacitor leaves the compressor and/or fan unable to start; you may hear a hum or click.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit is pitted or stuck and won't pass power.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — undercharge reduces cooling and can freeze the coil. Always a leak — never "just top it off."
8. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by #1, #2, or #7; the coil ices over and blocks airflow entirely.
9. **Clogged condensate drain with float switch** — a full drain pan trips a safety switch that shuts off cooling.
10. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive failure; the unit runs but produces no cold.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything requires opening the sealed refrigerant system or the electrical panel beyond the breaker.

- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it against a light. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. A dirty filter is the #1 cause of lost cooling and frozen coils.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **Cool**, the setpoint is below room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. Replace the batteries if it's a battery-powered model.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and the outdoor AC breaker in your electrical panel. If one is tripped, reset it once. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse the outside fins with a garden hose (power off) if they look dirty.
- **Condensate drain:** If you see water near the indoor unit or the system shuts off shortly after starting, the drain may be clogged and a float switch tripped. Clearing the drain line is homeowner-safe.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system **off** and run the fan only to thaw it (can take a few hours). Don't run cooling on a frozen coil.

Do not open the sealed system, add refrigerant, bypass the float switch, or repeatedly reset a breaker that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech follows a logical sequence — you can sanity-check a quote against this:

1. **Confirms the complaint** and checks the thermostat call and 24V control signal.
2. **Verifies power** to the outdoor unit, tests the contactor and the dual run capacitor with a meter.
3. **Inspects airflow** — filter, blower operation, and evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
4. **Cleans/inspects the condenser coil** and checks the outdoor fan motor.
5. **Connects refrigerant gauges** and measures suction/liquid pressures, superheat, and subcooling against Trane's charging chart for that model. This reveals undercharge (leak) or other refrigerant faults.
6. **Performs an electronic leak search** if charge is low — dye, electronic detector, or nitrogen pressure test.
7. **Tests the compressor** windings and amp draw if everything upstream checks out.

If a tech wants to "add a pound of refrigerant" without doing a leak search, that's a red flag — low charge always means a leak.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker, bad contactor, blown fuse | Reset breaker once; check thermostat | Test/replace contactor, fuse, or wiring |
| Warm air, outdoor unit humming, not starting | Failed run capacitor | None (high voltage) | Test and replace dual run capacitor |
| Ice on lines or indoor coil | Dirty filter, low refrigerant, airflow block | Replace filter, run fan to thaw | Find leak, recharge, check airflow |
| Weak cooling, outdoor unit dirty | Clogged condenser coil | Rinse fins with hose (power off) | Deep coil cleaning, fan motor check |
| Cools then shuts off, water near unit | Clogged condensate drain, float switch | Clear drain line, use tablets | Clear/repair drain, test float switch |
| Runs constantly, never cold enough | Low refrigerant (leak) | None — call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Outdoor unit runs but no cold air at all | Failed compressor | None | Compressor test/replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Your area, model, and SEER affect the final number.

- **Air filter replacement:** $0 (DIY) – $30
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 – $15 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 if a pro clears it
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150 – $400
- **Contactor replacement:** $150 – $350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100 – $400
- **Outdoor fan motor:** $300 – $650
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400 – $1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A pricing varies)
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900 – $2,000
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,000 – $1,800+ (often worth comparing against full system replacement on older units)

If your Trane is under its 10-year parts warranty (registered), the part may be covered — but labor usually isn't.
