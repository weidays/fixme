## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a blink-code fault like a furnace lockout — it's a symptom. Your Trane air conditioner may be running, blowing air, and drawing power, yet the air coming from the vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint.

Trane outdoor units vary widely in how much they tell you. Older XR-series condensers have no display at all, so "not cooling" is something you diagnose by behavior. Higher-end XV variable-speed and communicating systems (with a ComfortLink II thermostat) may show a fault or status message on the thermostat, but the underlying problem is still mechanical or electrical. Always note any message your thermostat shows before calling — it speeds diagnosis.

Because low refrigerant, compressor, and electrical faults are involved, this is generally a **pro-level** issue. The safe checks below can resolve the easy cases; everything else needs a licensed HVAC technician.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked airflow** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow drops cooling capacity and can freeze the coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant. Ice on the indoor coil or copper line stops cold air entirely.
3. **Tripped breaker or outdoor disconnect pulled** — the indoor blower runs on a separate circuit, so you get airflow but no cooling.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — a very common failure. The compressor and/or fan hums but won't start, or the outdoor fan won't spin.
5. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked with dust, grass, or cottonwood, the unit can't reject heat and cooling fades, especially on hot days.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — never "normal." A sealed system shouldn't lose charge. Symptoms include ice, hissing, or weak cooling.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit can pit or stick, leaving the condenser dead.
8. **Thermostat fault or wiring/control board issue** — incorrect settings, dead batteries, or a board that won't command cooling.
9. **Failed compressor** — the worst case. The unit won't cool at all and may trip the breaker.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop if anything is unclear.

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Set the fan to AUTO. If it's a battery model, replace the batteries.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and look at it against a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter alone causes most "not cooling" calls.
- **Breaker:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped AC breaker. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping means a real fault.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** The pull-out box near the condenser should be fully seated. Don't open or probe inside it.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor coil:** Gently rinse leaves and debris off the outside of the condenser fins with a garden hose (power off). Don't use a pressure washer or bend the fins.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Clear visible standing water and drop a cleaning tablet in the pan.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or copper line, turn the system to OFF and the fan to ON to thaw it (can take a few hours). If it refreezes after thawing, you have low airflow or low refrigerant — call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence — you can use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verifies the complaint** and checks thermostat call for cooling (24V signal).
2. **Measures temperature split** — the difference between return and supply air. A healthy split is roughly 16–22°F; low or high split points to specific faults.
3. **Inspects airflow** — filter, blower, evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
4. **Checks electrical components** — tests the run capacitor with a meter, inspects the contactor for pitting, confirms the compressor and fan motor draw correct amperage.
5. **Connects refrigerant gauges** — reads suction and head pressures, calculates superheat/subcooling to determine if charge is correct, low, or overcharged.
6. **Leak search** — if refrigerant is low, they should find the leak (electronic detector or dye), not just top it off.
7. **Reads any control-board or communicating-thermostat faults** on equipped models.

Be cautious of any quote that jumps straight to "add refrigerant" without explaining where it went, or "replace the compressor" without electrical testing first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter / low airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Check coil, blower, charge |
| Ice on coil or copper line | Frozen evaporator | Turn OFF, run fan to thaw | Diagnose airflow vs. low charge |
| Indoor air blows, outdoor unit dead | Breaker, disconnect, contactor | Reset breaker once, seat disconnect | Test contactor, control voltage |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Weak cooling on hot days | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse coil exterior | Deep coil clean, check charge |
| Hissing, oily residue, weak cooling | Refrigerant leak | None | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or electrical fault | Stop resetting | Full electrical + compressor test |
| Thermostat blank or unresponsive | Dead batteries / wiring | Replace batteries | Check wiring, board, transformer |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor. Regional rates and model complexity (especially variable-speed/communicating systems) push these higher.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries:** under $10 (DIY).
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 pro.
- **Run capacitor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Contactor replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Condenser coil deep cleaning:** $150–$400.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs. older R-22 is far costlier).
- **Outdoor fan motor:** $400–$700.
- **Control board (communicating/variable models):** $400–$900.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+; on an older unit, full system replacement often makes more sense.

If your system is out of warranty and over 12–15 years old, ask your tech to compare a major repair against replacement before committing.
