## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific blink code — it's the symptom most Trane homeowners search for when the thermostat calls for cool but the house stays warm. Your Trane air conditioner depends on three things working together: enough **airflow** across the coils, the right **refrigerant charge**, and healthy **electrical components** (capacitor, contactor, compressor).

When any one fails, you'll see warm or weak air, longer run times, ice on the lines, or an outdoor unit that hums but won't start. Some Trane systems with communicating thermostats (XV-series, Trane Link) will display a fault or alert on the thermostat screen instead of a board blink code — note the exact wording, as it speeds up diagnosis.

This page covers the no-cooling symptom broadly. Behavior varies by model, so where it matters we say so rather than guess.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the indoor coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor unit, indoor blower, or both can lose power independently.
3. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode, or a miswired/failing communicating thermostat.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the unit runs but blows little or no cold air.
5. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, ice on the suction line, longer run times.
6. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — blocked airflow outside reduces heat rejection and cooling capacity.
7. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start; common in summer heat.
8. **Bad contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks, so the unit won't energize.
9. **Failing compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit may trip on overload or fail to start.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, touch the capacitor, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

- **Filter:** Pull it and look toward a light. If it's gray and clogged, replace it. Run the system after — restricted airflow is the top cause.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. For communicating thermostats, note any on-screen alert text.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for the AC and air handler/furnace breakers. Reset one that's tripped **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Make sure the disconnect near the unit is fully seated.
- **Condensate drain:** A clogged drain can trip a float switch and stop cooling. Clear visible standing water in the pan and add drain tablets. Don't disassemble the float switch.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn the system to OFF (fan ON is fine) and let it thaw fully before running again.

If cooling doesn't return after a fresh filter, correct thermostat settings, and a thawed coil, it's time for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech follows a logical path — you can use this to sanity-check a quote:

1. **Confirms the complaint** and checks thermostat call, mode, and any communicating-system fault codes.
2. **Verifies airflow** — filter, blower operation, coil cleanliness, and duct issues.
3. **Checks electricals at the condenser** — incoming voltage, contactor pull-in, and capacitor microfarads against the rating on the part.
4. **Measures refrigerant performance** — gauges on the service ports, superheat and subcooling readings against Trane's target for your model. Low charge prompts a **leak search** (electronic detector or dye), not just a top-off.
5. **Tests the compressor** — amp draw, start behavior, and windings if it won't run.
6. **Inspects the condenser coil and fan motor** for restriction or failure.

Be cautious of any quote to "add refrigerant" without a leak diagnosis — that's a temporary fix that violates good practice and EPA expectations.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak airflow, warm air | Dirty filter | Replace filter | Check blower, ductwork |
| Ice on lines/coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw coil, replace filter | Find leak, recharge, verify charge |
| Outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker or disconnect | Reset breaker once, seat disconnect | Test contactor, transformer, wiring |
| Unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — do not open | Test and replace capacitor |
| Long runs, never cools | Low refrigerant / dirty condenser | Clear debris from outdoor unit | Leak search, recharge, clean coil |
| Indoor air warm, fan blows | Stuck contactor, low charge | Check thermostat mode | Test contactor, measure charge |
| No display / alert shown | Thermostat / control fault | Replace batteries, note alert | Diagnose communicating control |
| Trips breaker repeatedly | Failing compressor or short | Stop resetting; call pro | Compressor amp/winding test |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor. Your area, model, and warranty status will move these.

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $150 service call
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$300
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak search + repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,200+ (often worth weighing against a new system on older units)

A diagnostic service call typically runs **$90–$200**, usually credited toward the repair. If your Trane is under its registered warranty, covered parts can sharply reduce these figures — keep your model and serial numbers handy.
