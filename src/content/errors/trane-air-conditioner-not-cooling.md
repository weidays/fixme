## What this code means

"Not cooling" is a symptom, not a stored fault code — your Trane air conditioner is running (or trying to) but the air coming from your vents isn't cold, or the house never reaches the thermostat setpoint. Trane systems with **ComfortLink II** or a communicating thermostat may show a specific alert, but most single-stage XR-series units give no code at all; you simply notice warm air.

Cooling failures fall into two buckets: **airflow problems** (the system makes cold refrigerant but can't move air across it) and **refrigeration problems** (the system can't make cold refrigerant). The checks below help you tell them apart and avoid paying for a diagnosis you could have skipped.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, can freeze the indoor coil into a block of ice, and stops cold air entirely.
2. **Thermostat issue** — wrong mode (FAN vs COOL/AUTO), dead batteries, or setpoint above room temperature.
3. **Tripped breaker or pulled disconnect** — the outdoor condenser has its own breaker; if it's off, the indoor fan blows room-temperature air.
4. **Dirty outdoor (condenser) coil** — caked with grass, cottonwood, or dust, the unit can't reject heat and cooling capacity collapses.
5. **Frozen evaporator coil** — from low airflow or low refrigerant; you may see ice on the copper lines near the indoor unit.
6. **Failed run capacitor** — common cause of a compressor or fan that hums but won't start; outdoor fan may not spin.
7. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the condenser sticks or burns; the outdoor unit stays silent.
8. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — never "uses up" normally; low charge means a leak somewhere in the system.
9. **Failed compressor** — the most expensive cause; the unit may trip the breaker, hum, or run without cooling.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Air filter:** Pull it out. If you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 no-cool cause and a $15 fix.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to COOL, the setpoint is at least a few degrees below room temperature, and the fan is on AUTO. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and the outdoor condenser breakers in your panel. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Outdoor disconnect:** The pull-out box near the condenser should be fully seated.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Condensate drain:** A full drain pan or clogged line can trip a safety float switch and shut cooling off. Clearing a visible clog or emptying the pan is safe; tablets help prevent regrowth.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant lines, **turn the system to OFF and run the fan only** to thaw it (can take several hours), then replace the filter. Persistent freezing points to low refrigerant or an airflow problem — call a pro.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Trane tech will:

1. Confirm the complaint and check thermostat call/wiring and control board status (or ComfortLink II alerts on communicating systems).
2. Measure airflow and inspect the filter, blower, and evaporator coil for ice or dirt.
3. Verify line voltage, the contactor, and capacitor microfarad readings at the condenser.
4. Connect gauges to read suction/discharge pressures and take **superheat and subcooling** measurements to judge refrigerant charge.
5. If charge is low, perform a leak search (electronic detector, UV dye, or nitrogen pressure test) **before** adding refrigerant.
6. Test compressor windings and amperage draw if cooling capacity is absent.

If a tech proposes "just adding a pound of refrigerant" without looking for a leak, push back — that's a temporary fix that violates good practice.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warm air, indoor fan runs | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter; run fan to thaw | Diagnose airflow or low charge |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan on | Tripped breaker / bad contactor | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, test control circuit |
| Outdoor unit hums, fan won't spin | Failed run capacitor | None (capacitor holds charge) | Test and replace capacitor |
| Ice on copper lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Filter + thaw with fan | Leak search, charge correction |
| House never reaches setpoint | Dirty condenser coil / low charge | Rinse visible debris (power off) | Coil cleaning, pressure/charge check |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or wiring fault | Stop resetting; call pro | Test compressor windings, amperage |
| Thermostat blank | Dead batteries / tripped float | Replace batteries, clear drain | Diagnose 24V transformer/wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and contractor rates vary):

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 DIY.
- **Thermostat batteries / settings:** $0–$10 DIY.
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Contactor:** $150–$300 installed.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $250 pro.
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300.
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location and refrigerant type (R-410A vs older R-22 is far costlier).
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+.
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,800+; on an older unit, replacing the whole condenser or system is often the better value.

If your Trane is under its registered 10-year parts limited warranty, the part may be covered — but labor usually isn't. Have the model and serial number ready when you call.
