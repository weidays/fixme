## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a blink-code on Trane equipment — it's a symptom. Your Trane air conditioner may be running, blowing air that isn't cold, short-cycling, or doing nothing at all. The underlying cause can be anything from a clogged filter to a failed compressor.

Trane variable-speed systems (XV series with communicating controls) can store fault codes on the indoor control board or thermostat that point to the real issue. Single-stage XR and XL units generally don't — diagnosis is done by measuring temperatures, pressures, and electrical values. This page covers the full range so you can sanity-check what a technician tells you.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter** — restricts airflow, drops cooling output, and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (not on COOL), or a setpoint above room temperature.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser has its own disconnect and breaker.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — extremely common; the fan or compressor hums but won't start.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — caked with grass, dust, or cottonwood, rejecting little heat.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — weak cooling, ice on the lines, hissing. Always a sealed-system leak, never "used up."
7. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; blocks cooling entirely.
8. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks and won't engage.
9. **Clogged condensate drain / float switch** — a tripped safety switch shuts cooling off to prevent water damage.
10. **Failed compressor or control board** — the costliest cause; usually a last conclusion after others are ruled out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Stop if anything requires opening a sealed unit or touching wiring.

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This is the single most common cause.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is several degrees below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor (air handler/furnace) and outdoor (condenser) breakers. Reset a tripped breaker **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Gently rinse caked dirt off the fins with a garden hose (power off).
- **Condensate drain:** If you see water near the indoor unit or the system stopped on a float switch, clear the drain line. A wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain end often clears the clog.
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the copper lines, switch the system to **FAN ONLY** for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter before running cooling again.

Do **not** add refrigerant, open the sealed system, bypass a float or safety switch, or repeatedly reset a unit that keeps tripping.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Trane tech follows a logical sequence — use this to judge whether a quote is fair:

1. **Confirms the complaint** and checks thermostat call for cooling at the indoor board.
2. **Measures temperature split** across the evaporator (typically ~16-22°F difference between return and supply air).
3. **Checks the run capacitor** with a meter — a weak or failed cap is a fast, cheap find.
4. **Inspects the contactor** for pitting and verifies 24V control voltage pulling it in.
5. **Reads refrigerant pressures** with gauges and compares to target superheat/subcooling. Low readings point to a leak — which they should then **locate**, not just "top off."
6. **Verifies airflow** — filter, blower speed, coil cleanliness.
7. **On XV/communicating systems**, pulls stored fault codes from the board or thermostat.
8. **Tests the compressor** windings and amp draw if everything upstream checks out.

If a tech jumps straight to "you need a new compressor" or "needs more Freon" without measuring pressures and checking the capacitor and airflow, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weak/warm air, dirty filter | Restricted airflow | Replace filter | Check blower speed, clean coil |
| Indoor fan runs, no cold air, outdoor unit silent | Failed capacitor or contactor | Reset breaker once | Test and replace capacitor/contactor |
| Ice on copper lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Fan-only to thaw, new filter | Find leak, recharge, verify charge |
| Hissing, weak cooling over days | Refrigerant leak | None — call pro | Leak search, repair, recharge |
| Water near indoor unit, system off | Clogged drain / float switch | Clear drain line | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Outdoor breaker keeps tripping | Electrical fault or seized compressor | Reset once only | Diagnose short, test compressor |
| Hot dirty outdoor coil | Coil rejecting little heat | Rinse fins, power off | Deep coil clean, pressure check |
| Nothing runs at all | No power, board, or thermostat | Check breakers/batteries | Diagnose board, transformer, wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor. Regional rates and brand markup vary.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$450
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$250
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $200–$1,500 (depends on leak location and refrigerant type — older R-22 systems cost far more)
- **Float switch replacement:** $75–$200
- **Control board (variable-speed XV):** $400–$900
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$3,000+ — at this point, weigh repair against replacing the system, especially if the unit is 10+ years old.

A diagnostic/service-call fee of **$80–$180** is typical and often applied toward the repair.
