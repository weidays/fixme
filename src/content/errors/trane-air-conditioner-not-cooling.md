## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a blink code — it's the symptom most Trane air-conditioner owners search for. Your system may run but blow warm or room-temperature air, short-cycle, or never reach the thermostat setpoint. On systems with a communicating control (TruComfort variable-speed and some XV models), the indoor board or thermostat may store an actual fault code; on older single-stage XR/XL units there's often no display at all, so you diagnose by behavior.

Because "not cooling" can stem from anything between a $15 filter and a failed compressor, this page ranks the likely causes and tells you what's safe to check yourself versus what needs a licensed HVAC tech.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked return** — restricts airflow, drops cooling capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **Thermostat issue** — dead batteries, wrong mode (fan "on" vs "auto"), or a failed thermostat not calling for cool.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** — the outdoor condenser has its own breaker; if it's off, the indoor fan blows uncooled air.
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer until it thaws.
5. **Dirty outdoor condenser coil** — debris, grass, or cottonwood blocks heat rejection.
6. **Failed run capacitor or contactor** — common on aging outdoor units; the compressor and/or fan won't start.
7. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — never normal; refrigerant doesn't get "used up," so this means a leak.
8. **Clogged condensate drain** — a safety float switch can shut the system off to prevent overflow.
9. **Failed compressor or fan motor** — the most expensive failure, usually last on the list.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only steps a homeowner should do — no tools, no panels, no refrigerant:

- **Filter:** Pull and inspect it. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause of weak cooling.
- **Thermostat:** Set mode to **Cool**, fan to **Auto**, and the setpoint several degrees below room temp. Replace batteries if the screen is dim or blank.
- **Breakers:** Check both the indoor air handler/furnace breaker and the outdoor condenser breaker/disconnect. If one is tripped, reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — don't keep resetting.
- **Vents and returns:** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Visually clear leaves, grass, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Don't remove panels.
- **Condensate:** If you see water near the indoor unit or the system shut off after running, the drain may be clogged. A wet/dry vac on the outdoor drain end can clear a simple clog.
- **Ice check:** If you see frost or ice on the indoor coil or the copper lines, turn the system to **Off** and run the fan only to thaw it (can take several hours). Running a frozen system causes damage.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech will work methodically, and knowing the sequence helps you sanity-check a quote:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** at the thermostat and confirm 24V signal reaches the outdoor contactor.
2. **Measure electrical** — check the run capacitor (microfarad reading), contactor, and amperage draw of the compressor and fan motor.
3. **Inspect airflow** — filter, blower operation, and coil condition; a frozen coil points to airflow or refrigerant problems.
4. **Connect refrigerant gauges** and measure superheat/subcooling to determine if the charge is correct — and if low, perform a leak search rather than just topping off.
5. **Read stored codes** on communicating boards (TruComfort/XV) for compressor, sensor, or communication faults.
6. **Check the condenser coil** temperature and cleanliness for heat-rejection problems.

Ask the tech to show you the readings (capacitor µF, subcooling, amp draw). A reputable pro will explain the numbers before recommending a part.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fan runs, air is warm | Outdoor unit off / tripped breaker | Reset breaker once; check disconnect | Diagnose capacitor, contactor, or compressor |
| Weak airflow, weak cooling | Dirty filter or frozen coil | Replace filter; thaw coil with fan-only | Check refrigerant charge and blower |
| Ice on coil or copper lines | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, run fan to thaw | Leak search, recharge, airflow correction |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor/contactor |
| System shuts off, water present | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear drain with wet/dry vac | Clear line, test float switch, check pan |
| Outdoor coil filthy | Blocked heat rejection | Rinse gently with hose (power off) | Deep coil clean, verify pressures |
| No cooling, thermostat blank | Dead batteries / control failure | Replace batteries | Replace thermostat or repair wiring |
| Runs constantly, never cools | Low refrigerant or failing compressor | None — call a pro | Charge/leak repair or compressor replacement |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and unit-type variation applies):

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5–$15 (DIY)
- **Thermostat replacement:** $120–$400
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $100–$300
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500+ depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$700
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,800+ (often worth weighing against full system replacement on older units)

If your unit is more than 12–15 years old and facing a compressor or major refrigerant repair, ask for a replacement quote to compare. Units using older R-22 refrigerant are especially expensive to recharge.
