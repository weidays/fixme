## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a single fault code — it's a symptom. Your Trane air conditioner may run, blow air, and even cycle on and off, but the air coming from your vents isn't getting cold (or isn't cold enough to keep up with the thermostat setpoint).

On older Trane systems there's often no diagnostic display at all, so you're working from symptoms. On newer communicating systems (XV-series with a ComfortLink II thermostat), the thermostat may show a specific alert or function code that points to the actual fault. Because the underlying causes range from a $5 filter to a $2,500 compressor, this page walks you from the cheapest, safest checks up to what a technician should be testing.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil. The single most common cause.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; the coil ices over and stops absorbing heat.
3. **Failed run/start capacitor** — a common electrical failure that prevents the compressor or fan motor from starting properly.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — reduces cooling capacity and eventually freezes the coil. Always a leak, never "normal" loss.
5. **Dirty condenser coil** — the outdoor coil caked with dirt, grass, or cottonwood can't reject heat.
6. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks, so the compressor doesn't engage.
7. **Tripped condensate float switch** — a clogged drain trips a safety switch that shuts cooling off.
8. **Bad capacitor on the blower or failed blower motor** — indoor air won't move across the coil.
9. **Failed compressor** — the worst case; the unit hums or trips the breaker but won't cool.
10. **Thermostat or control fault** — bad batteries, wrong mode, or a communication error on ComfortLink systems.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL**, the fan is on **AUTO**, and the setpoint is at least 3–4°F below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered.
- **Air filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause and a $5 fix.
- **Breakers:** Check the panel for a tripped breaker for both the indoor air handler/furnace and the outdoor condenser. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro — repeated tripping signals an electrical fault.
- **Vents and registers:** Make sure supply and return vents aren't blocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and weeds at least 2 feet around the condenser so it can breathe. Gently rinse loose debris off the coil fins with a garden hose (power off).
- **Frozen coil:** If you see ice on the refrigerant line or indoor coil, turn the system to **OFF** and run the **FAN ON** for a few hours to thaw it, then replace the filter before restarting.
- **Condensate drain:** If your indoor unit has a float switch and the drain pan is full or the line is clogged, the safety will stop cooling. Clearing a visible clog can restore operation.

**Do not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Trane tech follows a logical sequence — you can sanity-check the quote against these steps:

1. **Verifies the complaint** and checks thermostat mode, setpoint, and (on communicating systems) reads any function/alert codes.
2. **Measures temperature split** — the difference between return and supply air. A healthy split is roughly 16–22°F; low or high points to specific faults.
3. **Inspects airflow** — filter, blower operation, and coil condition. A frozen coil is thawed before further testing.
4. **Tests electrical components** — checks the capacitor with a meter, inspects the contactor for pitting, and confirms the compressor and fan motor draw correct amperage.
5. **Connects refrigerant gauges** and measures subcooling/superheat to determine if the charge is correct. Low readings trigger a **leak search** with an electronic detector or dye.
6. **Isolates the failure** — distinguishes a failed capacitor (cheap) from a failed compressor (expensive) before quoting.

Be cautious of any quote that recommends "adding refrigerant" without finding and fixing a leak — that's a temporary patch, not a repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil | Replace filter, thaw coil with fan on | Check airflow, charge, leak search |
| Ice on refrigerant line or coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, thaw, replace filter | Find airflow restriction or leak |
| Outdoor unit hums, won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — call a pro | Test and replace capacitor |
| Outdoor unit dead, indoor runs | Failed contactor or breaker | Reset breaker once | Replace contactor, check wiring |
| Cooling stops, drain pan full | Tripped condensate float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Clear line, test float switch |
| Indoor fan won't run | Blower capacitor or motor | Check breaker and filter | Test/replace blower motor or cap |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or wiring fault | Stop resetting; call a pro | Diagnose compressor and circuit |
| Weak cooling on hot days | Dirty condenser coil | Rinse fins with hose | Deep coil clean, verify charge |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (your area and system size will vary):

- **Air filter:** $5–$30 (DIY)
- **Coil thaw / drain clearing:** $0 DIY – $250 if a pro clears the line
- **Run/start capacitor:** $150–$400
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil deep cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Blower motor (indoor):** $400–$800
- **Condenser fan motor:** $400–$700
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $400–$1,500 depending on leak location
- **Evaporator or condenser coil replacement:** $1,200–$2,800
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,500–$2,500 (often worth comparing against a new system if out of warranty)

Trane registered systems often carry a 10-year parts warranty — confirm your registration, since covered parts can dramatically cut the cost of a compressor or coil.
