## What this code means

"Not cooling" isn't a specific blink-code or fault number — it's the symptom homeowners report when a Trane air conditioner runs but the house won't reach temperature, or the air from the vents isn't cold. Trane split systems don't typically flash a dedicated "not cooling" code; instead the cause shows up as poor performance, ice on the lines, a humming outdoor unit, or in communicating **XV** systems, a fault message on the thermostat.

Because the root cause can range from a $20 filter to a failed compressor, this page walks you through what's safe to check yourself and how a technician narrows it down — so you can sanity-check any quote.

> If you smell a burning electrical odor, see smoke, or hear loud grinding from the outdoor unit, shut the system off at the breaker and call a licensed HVAC pro. Don't keep cycling it.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or blocked return airflow** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow chokes the system and can freeze the indoor coil.
2. **Frozen evaporator coil** — caused by low airflow or low refrigerant; ice blocks heat transfer so air blows warm or weak.
3. **Tripped breaker or blown fuse** on the outdoor (condenser) circuit — indoor blower runs but outdoor unit is dead.
4. **Failed run capacitor** — the compressor or fan motor hums but won't start; very common on aging units.
5. **Failed contactor** — the relay that powers the outdoor unit pits or sticks, so the condenser never energizes.
6. **Low refrigerant from a leak** — gradual loss of cooling, ice on the larger copper line, hissing. Always indicates a leak.
7. **Dirty condenser coil** — outdoor coil caked with dirt/grass can't reject heat, so cooling fades on hot days.
8. **Thermostat fault or dead batteries** — system never gets the call for cooling.
9. **Clogged condensate drain** — a full safety float switch shuts cooling off to prevent water damage.
10. **Failing compressor** — the worst case; outdoor unit hums, trips the breaker, or won't start at all.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are all homeowner-safe. Do them in order:

- **Filter:** Pull the air filter. If it's gray or you can't see light through it, replace it. A clogged filter is the #1 cause and the cheapest fix.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to **COOL** and the setpoint is below room temperature. Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Try lowering it 3–4 degrees and listen for the system to respond.
- **Breakers:** Check your electrical panel for a tripped breaker — Trane systems usually have a separate breaker for the outdoor unit. Reset it **once**. If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Vents and returns:** Make sure supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Outdoor unit:** Clear leaves, grass clippings, and debris from around the condenser. Keep at least 2 feet of clearance. Gently rinse the outdoor coil fins with a garden hose (water only, low pressure) with the unit powered off.
- **Ice check:** If you see ice on the copper lines or indoor coil, turn the system **OFF** (set fan to ON to help thaw) and let it melt fully — several hours — before retesting.
- **Condensate drain:** If your indoor unit has a full drain pan or you find a tripped float switch, the unit may shut off cooling. Clearing a visibly clogged drain line is reasonable; persistent clogs need a pro.

Do **not** open the sealed refrigerant system, add refrigerant, or bypass any safety switch.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent Trane tech will:

1. **Verify the call for cooling** — confirm the thermostat is sending 24V to the contactor and the indoor blower is running.
2. **Measure airflow and temperature split** — a healthy system shows roughly 15–20°F drop between return and supply air.
3. **Check electrical components** — test the run capacitor (microfarad reading), the contactor, and the compressor windings.
4. **Read refrigerant pressures** — connect gauges to measure suction and head pressure and calculate superheat/subcooling. This reveals undercharge (leak), overcharge, or restriction.
5. **Inspect for ice and airflow restriction** — coil condition, blower wheel, ductwork.
6. **Leak search** — if refrigerant is low, use electronic detector or dye to find the leak. A quote that just "tops off" refrigerant without finding the leak is a red flag.
7. **On XV/communicating systems** — pull fault history from the thermostat and check the communicating control board.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air blows but isn't cold | Dirty filter / frozen coil / low refrigerant | Replace filter, thaw coil, check vents | Measure pressures, find leak, repair |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit dead | Tripped breaker / failed contactor or capacitor | Reset breaker once | Test/replace contactor or capacitor |
| Outdoor unit hums but won't start | Failed run capacitor | None — pro repair | Test µF, replace capacitor |
| Ice on copper lines or indoor coil | Low airflow or low refrigerant | Turn off, replace filter, thaw | Diagnose airflow vs charge, repair leak |
| Cooling fades on very hot days | Dirty condenser coil / undercharge | Rinse outdoor coil (power off) | Deep coil clean, verify charge |
| System shuts off with water in pan | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Flush drain, replace float switch |
| Breaker trips repeatedly | Compressor or wiring fault | Stop — call pro | Test compressor, wiring, hard-start |
| No response at all | Thermostat dead / no power | Replace batteries, check breaker | Diagnose control wiring/board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (varies by region and unit size):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries / reset:** $0–$10 (DIY)
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $0 DIY – $150–$300 pro
- **Run capacitor:** $150–$350
- **Contactor:** $150–$350
- **Condenser coil cleaning:** $150–$400
- **Refrigerant leak repair + recharge:** $300–$1,500 depending on leak location (a coil leak is far costlier than a fitting)
- **TXV / metering device:** $400–$900
- **Evaporator coil replacement:** $900–$2,000+
- **Compressor replacement:** $1,200–$2,500+ — on an older unit, often worth comparing to a full system replacement

Get a written diagnosis. If a tech recommends a compressor or coil on an out-of-warranty unit over 10–12 years old, get a second quote and weigh replacement.
