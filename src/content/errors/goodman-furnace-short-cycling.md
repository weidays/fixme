## What this code means

"Short cycling" isn't a single Goodman flash code — it's a pattern of behavior. Your furnace ignites, runs for a short time (often under a few minutes), shuts off, then restarts again soon after. On Goodman furnaces with an integrated furnace control (IFC) board, the diagnostic LED may flash codes for an open limit switch, flame sensing fault, or pressure switch issue while this is happening.

Short cycling almost always means the furnace is shutting itself down early for a reason: it's overheating, it can't confirm flame, it can't confirm airflow, or the thermostat is telling it to stop too soon. It is rarely random. The goal of diagnosis is to find which protective control is interrupting the cycle.

Because some causes (a cracked heat exchanger or a chronically tripping limit) carry safety risk and require gas-trained diagnosis, we rate this **pro**.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty air filter or restricted airflow.** The most common cause. Restricted return air overheats the heat exchanger, tripping the high-limit switch, which cuts the burners. The blower keeps running, the furnace cools, then it tries again.
2. **Dirty flame sensor.** A coated flame sensor can't confirm flame, so the IFC closes the gas valve seconds after ignition, then retries — a classic short-cycle pattern.
3. **Blocked supply registers or closed dampers.** Like a dirty filter, this restricts airflow and overheats the cabinet, tripping the limit.
4. **Thermostat problems.** Poor location (near a supply vent or heat source), loose wiring, low batteries, or a failing thermostat can satisfy the heat call prematurely.
5. **Overheating from a dirty or weak blower.** A dirty blower wheel or failing blower motor moves too little air, causing limit trips.
6. **Pressure switch / venting issues.** A partially blocked flue, condensate backup, or failing inducer can make the pressure switch open mid-cycle.
7. **Flue or condensate blockage (90%+ furnaces).** Clogged condensate trap or drain can trip the pressure switch on high-efficiency Goodman models.
8. **Failing high-limit switch.** A weak or out-of-spec limit can open at normal temperatures.
9. **Oversized furnace.** If the unit is too large for the home, it heats the thermostat area fast and shuts off before the rest of the house warms — short cycling by design. This is an installation problem, not a part failure.
10. **Control board (IFC) fault.** Less common, but a failing board can misread sensors and cut cycles short.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do **not** open the gas valve, bypass any switch, or repeatedly reset a locked-out furnace.

- **Filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. This fixes a large share of short-cycling cases.
- **Thermostat:** Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Make sure it's set to **Heat** and the setpoint is above room temperature. Check that it isn't mounted near a vent, lamp, or sunny window.
- **Supply and return vents:** Walk the house and confirm registers are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many vents restricts airflow.
- **Breaker:** If the furnace lost power, confirm the breaker is fully on (off, then on). Don't reset repeatedly.
- **Condensate (90%+ furnaces):** Look for water pooling near the furnace or a full condensate pan. A clogged drain can trip the pressure switch. Clearing standing water and checking the drain line is safe to inspect.
- **Listen and watch one cycle:** Note how long it runs and whether the burners light, the blower starts, or it stops before either. This helps your technician.

If a new filter and good airflow don't solve it, call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good Goodman tech follows the sequence of operation and reads the IFC diagnostic LED:

1. **Read the flash code** on the control board through the sight glass and note it against the legend on the furnace door.
2. **Measure temperature rise** across the furnace and compare to the rating plate. Excessive rise points to airflow restriction and limit tripping.
3. **Check the flame sensor microamps** with a meter. Low signal means a dirty or failing sensor; they'll clean or replace it.
4. **Inspect blower wheel and motor** for dirt buildup or a weak motor.
5. **Test the high-limit and pressure switches** for correct operation and continuity.
6. **Inspect venting, inducer, and condensate path** on high-efficiency models for blockage.
7. **Inspect the heat exchanger** for cracks if overheating is chronic — a safety-critical check.
8. **Evaluate sizing and ductwork** if the furnace is mechanically fine but still cycles fast.

A quote that jumps straight to a control board or heat exchanger without checking airflow and the flame sensor first deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Burners light, run a few minutes, shut off, blower keeps running | Overheating / dirty filter or airflow | Replace filter, open all vents | Measure temp rise, clean blower, test limit |
| Burners light then go out in seconds, retries | Dirty flame sensor | None safe beyond filter | Clean or replace flame sensor, check microamps |
| Furnace starts and stops on its own timing | Thermostat location or fault | New batteries, check placement | Replace/relocate thermostat, check wiring |
| Inducer runs, burners never light, then resets | Pressure switch / flue or condensate block | Clear standing condensate water | Test pressure switch, clear flue, check inducer |
| Heats one room fast, shuts off, house stays cold | Oversized furnace | None | Verify sizing / duct correction recommendation |
| Random shutdowns, board flashes odd codes | Failing IFC control board | None | Diagnose and replace control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined unless noted:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Flame sensor cleaning:** $80–$180 service call; **replacement** $150–$300.
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$350 depending on model.
- **Blower wheel cleaning:** $150–$350. **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$700.
- **High-limit switch:** $150–$350.
- **Pressure switch:** $150–$350.
- **Inducer motor:** $300–$600.
- **Condensate / drain clearing:** $100–$250.
- **Integrated furnace control board:** $350–$600+.
- **Heat exchanger replacement:** $1,000–$2,500+ (often the deciding factor in repair vs. replace).
- **Furnace replacement (oversizing/age):** $4,000–$8,000+.

Many short-cycling cases are resolved for the cost of a filter or a flame-sensor cleaning. The expensive outcomes are board, heat exchanger, or sizing-related.
