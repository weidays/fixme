## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman error code — it's a behavior. Your furnace ignites, runs for a short time (often 30 seconds to a few minutes), shuts off, then restarts again and again without satisfying the thermostat. Some Goodman boards will flash a diagnostic code (such as an open-limit or flame-loss pattern) on the LED, while others simply keep retrying until they hit a lockout.

Short cycling almost always means something is interrupting the heat call early: the furnace is overheating, losing its flame signal, getting a confused thermostat signal, or shutting down on a safety switch. The furnace is doing its job by stopping — the goal is to find *why* it keeps stopping.

If your furnace has stopped completely after several failed cycles, that is a different condition — see **Goodman Furnace Lockout** in Related codes.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter.** The most common cause. Restricted airflow lets the heat exchanger overheat, tripping the high-limit switch.
2. **Dirty flame sensor.** A carbon-coated sensor loses its ability to "see" the flame, so the board drops the gas valve about a minute after ignition, then retries.
3. **Blocked or undersized return/supply airflow.** Closed registers, crushed ducts, or a too-restrictive filter cause the same overheating as a dirty filter.
4. **Failing high-limit switch.** A weak or out-of-spec limit switch trips early even when temperatures are normal.
5. **Thermostat issues.** Loose wiring, low batteries, a poor location near a vent, or short-cycle (anticipator) settings cause false satisfied signals.
6. **Flue/exhaust or pressure-switch problems.** A partially blocked flue or a sticking pressure switch can cause early shutdowns on some models.
7. **Control board fault.** Less common, but a failing ignition control board can mis-time cycles or drop the call early.
8. **Oversized furnace.** A furnace too large for the home heats fast and shuts off quickly — a design issue, not a defect.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no panels with live gas or high-voltage work:

- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray or you can't see light through it, swap it. This resolves a large share of short-cycling cases.
- **Check the thermostat.** Replace the batteries, confirm it's set to Heat, and make sure it isn't mounted near a supply vent, lamp, or sunny window that fools it.
- **Walk the house and open vents.** Make sure supply and return registers are open and unblocked by furniture or rugs. Restricting airflow causes overheating.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on; a marginal connection can interrupt cycles.
- **Look at the area around the furnace.** Keep boxes, laundry, and stored items clear of the return airflow path.
- **Check the condensate drain (high-efficiency models).** A clogged drain or full trap can trip a float switch and cut cycles short.

If short cycling continues after a clean filter and good thermostat, it's time for a technician. Do **not** bypass any safety switch or repeatedly reset a furnace that keeps locking out.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent tech should:

1. **Verify the symptom** — watch a full ignition cycle and note exactly when shutdown happens (ignition, flame-prove, or after several minutes of run).
2. **Measure flame-sensor microamps** to confirm a weak or failing flame signal, and clean or replace the sensor.
3. **Check temperature rise** across the heat exchanger against the data-plate spec to identify airflow restriction or a slipping blower.
4. **Test the high-limit switch** for continuity and trip point.
5. **Read the board's LED diagnostic flash code** and check the pressure switch and inducer operation.
6. **Inspect the flue/venting** for blockage and confirm proper draft.
7. **Confirm blower speed and CFM** match the furnace tonnage and ductwork.

Ask which test pointed to the failed part. A quote that jumps straight to a control board without flame-sensor or limit testing deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs a few minutes, shuts off, restarts | Overheating from dirty filter / blocked airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temperature rise, check blower & limit |
| Ignites, runs ~1 min, then shuts off | Dirty/weak flame sensor | Replace filter first | Clean/replace sensor, test microamps |
| Rapid on/off near setpoint | Thermostat batteries/location/anticipator | Replace batteries, relocate test | Verify wiring & cycle settings |
| Trips off, LED shows limit/flame code | High-limit switch or flame loss | Note flash code | Test and replace limit switch or sensor |
| Stops shortly after inducer starts | Pressure switch / flue blockage | Check outside vent termination | Test pressure switch, clear flue |
| Erratic cycles, no clear pattern | Failing control board | None | Diagnose and replace board |
| Heats fast, short runs, no faults | Oversized furnace | None | Recommend airflow/sizing review |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 DIY.
- **Flame sensor clean or replace:** $80–$250 (often a service-call minimum if that's all it needs).
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 depending on model.
- **High-limit switch:** $150–$300.
- **Pressure switch:** $150–$350.
- **Flue cleaning / blockage removal:** $150–$400.
- **Control board:** $350–$600.
- **Blower motor (if airflow is the cause):** $400–$700.

Many short-cycling calls end with just a clean filter and a flame-sensor cleaning, so don't assume the worst until a tech has actually tested the parts.
