## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman error code — it is a behavior. Your furnace fires up, runs for a short time (often under two or three minutes), shuts off, then restarts again a short while later, repeating far more often than it should. On boards with a diagnostic LED, you may see flash codes for a flame-sense fault, an open limit switch, or a pressure-switch issue rather than a dedicated "short cycle" message.

The pattern matters because it tells you *why* the furnace is stopping early: it is either being told to stop by a safety switch (overheating, weak flame signal, pressure problem) or by the thermostat cycling too fast. A furnace that cycles too frequently wears out its ignitor, blower motor and control board prematurely and burns more fuel than needed.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the most common cause. Restricted airflow overheats the heat exchanger, tripping the high-limit switch, which cuts the burners early.
2. **Weak or dirty flame sensor** — the furnace lights, but the flame-rectification signal is too low, so the board shuts the gas valve within seconds as a safety measure.
3. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers act like a dirty filter, causing overheating and limit trips.
4. **Failing or misadjusted high-limit switch** — an aging limit switch can open at too low a temperature, stopping the burners prematurely.
5. **Thermostat problems** — poor location (near a supply vent), low batteries, loose wiring, or too-narrow cycle settings can cause rapid on/off signals.
6. **Pressure switch / flue or intake blockage** — a partially blocked intake, exhaust or condensate line can cause the pressure switch to open mid-cycle.
7. **Oversized furnace** — a unit too large for the home heats the space fast, satisfies the thermostat quickly, then cycles again. This is a design issue, not a repair.
8. **Failing control board or flame-sense circuit** — less common, but a degraded board can misread signals and shut down early.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are homeowner-safe checks. Do **not** bypass safety switches, open the gas valve, or repeatedly reset a furnace that keeps locking out.

- **Check the air filter.** If it is gray, clogged, or more than 60–90 days old, replace it with the correct size. This alone fixes many short-cycling cases.
- **Check the thermostat.** Replace the batteries if it uses them. Make sure it is set to Heat and not mounted in direct sun or near a supply vent. Try widening the temperature swing/cycle setting if your model allows.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on and not tripped or partly reset.
- **Check your vents and registers.** Open any closed supply and return registers, and clear rugs or furniture blocking airflow.
- **Check the condensate drain (high-efficiency models).** A clogged condensate line or full trap can trip the pressure switch. If you can see standing water backing up, clear the visible drain — but do not disassemble internal components.

If the furnace still short cycles after a clean filter, good batteries and open vents, it is time for a licensed technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will not just clean the flame sensor and leave. Expect them to:

- **Read the board's diagnostic LED** to see the last stored fault code (flame loss, open limit, pressure switch, etc.).
- **Measure the flame-rectification signal** in microamps with a meter; a low reading points to a dirty or failing flame sensor or a grounding/gas issue.
- **Measure temperature rise** across the heat exchanger and compare it to the rating-plate spec. High rise confirms an airflow/overheating problem.
- **Test the high-limit and pressure switches** for proper opening/closing.
- **Inspect the blower, motor amp draw and ductwork** for airflow restriction.
- **Check the flue, intake and condensate** on high-efficiency units for blockage.
- **Evaluate sizing** if no fault is found — an oversized furnace may be the real culprit.

If a quote jumps straight to a control board or heat exchanger without measuring temperature rise or flame signal, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Burners light, run ~1 min, shut off, repeat | Dirty flame sensor | Replace filter, check vents | Clean/replace flame sensor, verify microamp signal |
| Furnace runs a few minutes then blower keeps running | High-limit trip from overheating | Replace clogged filter, open registers | Measure temp rise, test/replace limit switch, check blower |
| Rapid on/off with no long runs | Thermostat fault or bad location | Replace batteries, check settings | Rewire or relocate thermostat, adjust cycle rate |
| Cycles with LED pressure-switch code | Blocked flue/intake or condensate | Clear visible condensate drain | Clear flue/intake, test pressure switch and hose |
| Heats fast, satisfies stat quickly, repeats | Oversized furnace | None | Load calculation, staging or replacement advice |
| Random shutdowns, board flashes odd codes | Failing control board | None | Diagnose and replace control board |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined (varies by region and model):

- **Air filter replacement:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Flame sensor cleaning:** $80–$150 service call; sensor part alone is often $10–$30.
- **Flame sensor replacement:** $150–$300.
- **High-limit switch replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Pressure switch replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400 depending on model.
- **Blower motor issues:** $300–$700+ if a motor or capacitor is involved.
- **Control board replacement:** $350–$700+.
- **Oversized furnace:** no cheap fix; correcting it may mean a staged unit or replacement (thousands).

Most short-cycling calls resolve at the filter, flame sensor, or limit-switch level — far cheaper than the worst case.
