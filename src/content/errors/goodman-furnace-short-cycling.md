## What this code means

Short cycling means your Goodman furnace fires up, runs for only a short time (often under 3-4 minutes), shuts off, then restarts again soon after — repeating this pattern without satisfying the thermostat or completing a normal heating cycle.

Short cycling is usually a **symptom, not a stored flash code**. Some Goodman integrated control boards will eventually log a limit-switch fault, ignition-failure, or lockout code on the diagnostic LED if the cycling is caused by overheating or failed ignition — but many cases produce no code at all. Because the exact flash patterns and lockout timing vary by board revision, **check the legend printed inside your furnace's blower-door panel** to interpret any LED you see.

The most important thing to understand: a furnace that overheats and shuts off repeatedly is stressing its heat exchanger, igniter, and blower. It won't blow up tomorrow, but left unaddressed it shortens the life of expensive parts — which is why this is rated a **pro-level** issue.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — The #1 cause. Restricted airflow lets the heat exchanger overheat, tripping the high-limit switch, which kills the burners early.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — Too many closed registers or blocked returns starve airflow the same way a dirty filter does.
3. **Thermostat problems** — Dead batteries, a thermostat in direct sunlight or near a heat source, loose wiring, or a too-tight "cycle rate" / anticipator setting.
4. **Flame sensor fouling** — A dirty flame sensor causes the board to think the flame went out, shutting down the gas valve seconds after ignition (a fast on/off pattern).
5. **Faulty high-limit or rollout switch** — A failing limit switch trips prematurely even with adequate airflow.
6. **Dirty or failing flame/ignition components** — Weak igniter or pressure-switch flutter can cause repeated start attempts.
7. **Cracked heat exchanger** — Can trip safeties; this is a safety concern requiring replacement.
8. **Oversized furnace** — An incorrectly sized unit heats the space too fast and satisfies the thermostat in minutes. Common after a botched replacement.
9. **Control board fault** — Less common; a failing integrated board mis-times cycles.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do — none involve gas, refrigerant, or bypassing safety switches.

- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or older than 1-3 months, swap it. This fixes a large share of short-cycling cases.
- **Check your thermostat.** Replace the batteries (if battery-powered), make sure it's set to **Heat**, and confirm it isn't in direct sunlight, above a vent, or near a lamp.
- **Walk your vents.** Open any closed supply registers and clear furniture, rugs, or boxes off return-air grilles. Aim to keep the vast majority of registers open.
- **Check the breaker.** A tripped or flickering breaker can cause restarts. Reset it **once**; if it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Check the furnace door panel.** The blower door must be fully closed — a loose panel can trip the door switch and cause cycling.
- **Look at the condensate drain (high-efficiency models).** A clogged drain or full condensate trap can trip the pressure switch. Check for standing water or a backed-up drain line.

Do **not** repeatedly reset a furnace that has locked out, jump out safety switches, or open the gas valve. If you smell gas, leave the house and call your gas utility's emergency line or 911.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will work the same logic top-to-bottom so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Read the LED diagnostics** on the control board and review fault history.
2. **Measure temperature rise** across the heat exchanger and compare it to the rating-plate spec — high rise points to airflow restriction.
3. **Check static pressure** to confirm whether airflow (ducting, filter, coil, blower) is the bottleneck.
4. **Inspect and clean the flame sensor**, then measure microamp signal.
5. **Test the high-limit and rollout switches** for continuity and trip point.
6. **Check the pressure switch and inducer** for proper operation, and the condensate trap/drain on condensing units.
7. **Inspect the heat exchanger** with a camera or mirror for cracks.
8. **Verify furnace sizing and gas pressure** if no mechanical fault is found.

Be skeptical of a quote that jumps straight to "new control board" or "new furnace" without airflow measurements and a flame-sensor check first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs a few minutes, shuts off, blower keeps running | Overheating / dirty filter | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp rise & static pressure |
| Fires then shuts off within seconds | Dirty flame sensor | Replace filter first | Clean/replace flame sensor, test microamps |
| Random restarts, breaker flickers | Electrical/board fault | Reset breaker once | Test wiring & control board |
| Cycles fast, thermostat acts odd | Thermostat fault/location | Replace batteries, relocate sensor | Recalibrate or replace thermostat |
| Gurgling water, condensing model cycles | Clogged condensate / pressure switch | Check drain for backup | Clear trap, test pressure switch |
| Heats fast, satisfies in minutes | Oversized furnace | None | Load calculation, staging adjustment |
| Cycling with burning smell or soot | Possible cracked heat exchanger | Shut off, call pro | Camera inspect, replace exchanger |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges (parts + labor; regional and model variation applies):

- **Air filter replacement (DIY):** $10-$40
- **Thermostat batteries / settings:** $0-$15
- **Flame sensor clean or replace:** $80-$250
- **High-limit or rollout switch:** $150-$400
- **Pressure switch / condensate clearing:** $150-$400
- **Inducer motor (if implicated):** $300-$600
- **Control board replacement:** $400-$700
- **Heat exchanger replacement:** $1,000-$2,500+ (often makes replacement worth considering on older units)
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90-$180, often credited toward repair

If a tech finds a cracked heat exchanger, weigh repair against full furnace replacement, especially on units over ~15 years old.
