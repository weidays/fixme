## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman fault code — it is a behavior. Your furnace lights, runs briefly, then shuts off, and repeats this far more often than normal. On Goodman boards with diagnostic LEDs (most PCBKF and PCBBF series), you may see a steady or flashing code that points to the underlying cause: an **open limit/temperature switch**, a **flame-sense fault**, or a **pressure-switch issue**.

Short cycling almost always means the furnace is protecting itself. Something is making it overheat, lose flame, or lose its safety circuit, so the control shuts the burners down early. The goal of diagnosis is to find *why* it keeps tripping — not to silence the safety that is doing its job.

Behavior varies by model. Two-stage units (GMVC, GMEC) modulate firing rate and may cycle differently than single-stage GMSS/GCSS furnaces. Read your specific LED code against the legend on the inside of the furnace door.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or restricted air filter** — Starved airflow overheats the heat exchanger, tripping the high-limit switch and ending the cycle early. By far the most common cause.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return airflow** — Too many closed registers, an undersized return, or a collapsed duct cause the same overheating as a dirty filter.
3. **Flame sensor dirty or failing** — A weak flame signal makes the board cut gas seconds after ignition, then retry. Common on units more than a few years old.
4. **Faulty or tripping high-limit switch** — A failing limit can open prematurely even with adequate airflow.
5. **Pressure switch / venting issues** — Partial vent blockage or a marginal inducer trips the pressure switch mid-cycle.
6. **Oversized furnace / poor thermostat location** — An oversized unit satisfies the thermostat too fast; a thermostat in a draft or near a vent reads temperature wrong.
7. **Failing control board (PCBKF/PCBBF)** — Less common, but a degraded board can misread sensors and cut cycles short.
8. **Condensate backup (high-efficiency models)** — A full condensate trap or tripped float switch interrupts operation.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone fixes a large share of short-cycling calls.
- **Open your vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles are unblocked by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers. Keep at least 80% of registers open.
- **Check the thermostat.** Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Confirm it's set to **Heat** and not near a heat source, sunny window, or supply vent.
- **Check the breaker.** A furnace on a tripped or marginal breaker can behave erratically. Reset it once; if it trips again, stop.
- **Inspect the condensate drain (high-efficiency units).** Look for standing water in the trap or a full drain line. Clearing a visible clog is fine; do not disassemble the burner area.
- **Look at the LED code** through the sight glass and note the flash pattern against the door legend.

Do **not** bypass the limit or pressure switch, do not repeatedly reset a locked-out unit, and do not open the gas valve or burner compartment. If you smell gas or burning, shut the furnace off and call a professional.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech won't just swap parts. Expect them to:

- Measure **temperature rise** across the furnace and compare it to the rating-plate spec — high rise confirms an airflow restriction.
- Check **static pressure** in the ductwork to find restrictive returns or ducts.
- Clean and test the **flame sensor**, measuring microamp signal during the flame cycle.
- Test the **high-limit and rollout switches** for continuity and trip point.
- Verify **inducer operation and pressure-switch closure**, and inspect the vent for blockage.
- Inspect the **heat exchanger** for cracks, since chronic overheating is a known cause of failure.
- Check **board diagnostics and voltage** before condemning the PCBKF/PCBBF control.

If a tech wants to replace the control board or gas valve without first ruling out airflow and the flame sensor, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs 3–5 min, shuts off, blower keeps running | Overheating / limit trip from dirty filter | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp rise, test limit, check ducts |
| Lights then dies in seconds, retries | Dirty/failing flame sensor | Note LED code | Clean or replace flame sensor, check microamps |
| Inducer runs, burners cut out early | Pressure switch / partial vent block | Check vent termination for blockage | Test pressure switch, inspect inducer & vent |
| Cycles fast even with clean filter | Oversized unit or bad thermostat location | Verify thermostat placement | Evaluate sizing, recommend staging/relocation |
| Short cycles + water near unit (90%+ furnace) | Condensate backup / float switch | Clear visible drain clog | Clear trap, test float, fix drainage |
| Erratic cycling, odd LED codes | Failing control board | Record flash code | Diagnose & replace PCBKF/PCBBF board |

| Repair | Typical US cost |
|---|---|
| Air filter (DIY) | $10 – $40 |
| Professional cleaning / flame sensor clean | $90 – $180 |
| Flame sensor replacement | $120 – $250 |
| High-limit switch replacement | $150 – $300 |

| Condensate float switch / drain service | $100 – $250 |

Diagnostic/service-call fees of $80–$150 are common and often credited toward the repair. A cracked heat exchanger found during diagnosis is a major repair and may favor furnace replacement.
