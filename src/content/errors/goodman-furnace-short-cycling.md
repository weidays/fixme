## What this code means

"Short cycling" isn't a single error code on the diagnostic LED — it's a behavior. Your Goodman furnace starts a heating cycle, runs briefly, then shuts off well before the thermostat is satisfied, and repeats. On many Goodman boards you'll see a normal flash code between cycles, or a fault code that points to the actual trip (for example a flame loss or limit-related flash).

Because the meaning of each LED flash pattern varies by control board, **check the wiring/diagnostic label inside your furnace door** for your specific model. The list below covers the documented mechanical and electrical causes that produce rapid cycling across the common Goodman gas furnace lines.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter / restricted airflow.** The most common cause. Reduced airflow lets the heat exchanger overheat, the high-limit switch trips, and the furnace shuts down — then restarts once it cools.
2. **Dirty flame sensor.** The furnace ignites, then loses flame detection a few seconds later and shuts down for safety, retrying repeatedly. Extremely common on older Goodman units.
3. **Overheating / high-limit switch tripping.** Beyond filters, this can come from closed vents, a blocked return, a failing blower motor, or a dirty blower wheel.
4. **Clogged condensate drain (high-efficiency models).** On condensing furnaces, a full drain or tripped float switch interrupts the cycle.
5. **Flue / pressure switch issues.** A partially blocked flue or weak inducer causes the pressure switch to open mid-cycle, shutting the burner down.
6. **Oversized furnace.** If the unit is too large for the home, it heats fast and cycles often — a design issue, not a fault.
7. **Thermostat problems.** Bad location (near a vent), wiring issues, or low batteries can cause erratic cycling.
8. **Failing control board or gas valve.** Less common, but a flaky board or valve can interrupt cycles unpredictably.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do **not** open the gas valve manually, bypass any limit or pressure switch, or repeatedly reset a furnace that has locked out.

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to the light. If you can't see through it, replace it — this alone fixes many short-cycling cases.
- **Thermostat:** Confirm it's set to Heat and the temperature is above room temp. If it's battery-powered, install fresh batteries. Make sure it isn't mounted in direct sun or near a supply vent.
- **Breaker:** Check that the furnace breaker is fully on; a tripped breaker won't cause cycling but a partial trip can cause odd behavior.
- **Vents and returns:** Walk the house and open any closed supply registers, and make sure return grilles aren't blocked by furniture or rugs. Closing too many vents starves airflow and trips the limit.
- **Condensate (high-efficiency units):** Look for water around the furnace base or a clogged drain line. You can clear a visibly clogged line and empty a full pan; condensate tablets help prevent algae buildup.

If the furnace still short cycles after these checks, it's a pro job.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech should:

1. **Read the LED flash code** on the control board and note the pattern between cycles.
2. **Measure temperature rise** across the heat exchanger and compare to the rating-plate range — high rise confirms an airflow or overheating problem.
3. **Check the flame sensor microamp signal** with a meter; a weak signal points to a dirty or failing sensor.
4. **Test the high-limit and rollout switches** for proper operation and check blower amp draw.
5. **Verify the pressure switch and inducer**, and inspect the flue for blockage.
6. **Inspect the condensate path and float switch** on condensing models.
7. **Test the control board and gas valve** only after ruling out the cheaper causes above.

If a quote jumps straight to a control board or gas valve without checking the filter, flame sensor and limit first, ask why.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs 1-3 min then shuts off, restarts | Dirty filter / restricted airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp rise, check blower |
| Ignites, flame lights, dies after seconds | Dirty/failing flame sensor | None (verify filter first) | Clean or replace flame sensor |
| Shuts off, blower keeps running, then retries | High-limit tripping from overheat | Replace filter, clear returns | Test limit, clean blower wheel |
| Water near furnace, cycles stop/start | Clogged condensate / float switch | Clear drain, empty pan | Clear drain, test float switch |
| Inducer runs but burner won't stay lit | Pressure switch / flue blockage | Check flue cap for obstruction | Test pressure switch, inspect flue |
| Heats fast, cycles often, no fault | Oversized unit or thermostat location | Relocate thermostat sensing issues | Load calc, staging adjustment |
| Random shutoffs, no clear pattern | Control board or gas valve | None | Test/replace board or valve |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY, $0 labor).
- **Flame sensor clean or replace:** $150–$350.
- **High-limit switch replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Pressure switch replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Inducer motor:** $300–$600+.
- **Condensate drain cleaning / float switch:** $100–$250.
- **Control board:** $400–$700.
- **Gas valve:** $300–$600+.
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $90–$180, often credited toward the repair.

A correctly sized but oversized furnace can't be cheaply "fixed" — staging adjustments help slightly, but replacement sizing is the real solution.
