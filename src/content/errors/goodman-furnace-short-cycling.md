## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single flash code — it's a behavior. Your Goodman furnace turns on, runs for only a short period (seconds to a few minutes), shuts off, then restarts soon after, never completing a full heating cycle or satisfying the thermostat.

Most Goodman integrated control boards do not display a dedicated "short cycle" fault. Instead the board may flash a related code — a flame-sense failure, an open limit/rollout switch, or a pressure switch fault — depending on *why* the cycle is being cut short. The pattern you see (light then die vs. run then quit) is your biggest clue.

Two distinct failure patterns hide under "short cycling":

- **Burner lights, then drops out in seconds** → flame can't be proven (flame sensor, ground, weak flame).
- **Burner runs several minutes, then quits** → the furnace is overheating and the high-limit switch is protecting the heat exchanger (almost always an airflow problem).

We mark this **pro** because the wrong assumption (e.g. ignoring overheating) can crack a heat exchanger over time.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, heat exchanger overheats, high-limit opens and cuts the burners. By far the most common.
2. **Dirty flame sensor** — carbon buildup keeps the board from "seeing" flame, so it shuts the gas off seconds after ignition for safety.
3. **Blocked/closed supply or return vents** — too many closed registers or blocked returns starve airflow, same overheating result as a dirty filter.
4. **Failing flame sensor or weak burner ground** — even after cleaning, an aged sensor or poor ground can't reliably prove flame.
5. **Plugged condensate drain (96% units)** — a full drain trips the pressure switch, cutting the cycle on high-efficiency condensing furnaces.
6. **Dirty or failing blower wheel / motor** — reduced airflow leads to overheating limit trips.
7. **High-limit switch failing** — opens too early even with adequate airflow.
8. **Pressure switch / inducer issue** — intermittent venting faults cut cycles short.
9. **Oversized furnace** — a unit too large for the home heats the thermostat area fast and shuts off; chronic since install.
10. **Control board fault** — least common, diagnosed only after the above are ruled out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. **Do not** bypass any safety switch, open the gas valve, or repeatedly reset a locked-out furnace.

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes many short-cycling cases.
- **Thermostat:** Set it to Heat, a few degrees above room temp. Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. A flaky or miswired stat can rapid-cycle the call for heat.
- **Vents and returns:** Walk the house. Open closed supply registers and clear furniture, rugs, or boxes off return grilles. Closed-off rooms starve airflow.
- **Breaker:** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on (flip fully off then on once if tripped). If it trips again, stop and call a pro.
- **Condensate (96% high-efficiency units):** Look for water pooling near the furnace base or a full drain pan. A clogged drain can trip the pressure switch. Clearing a visible clog or emptying a pan is safe; opening sealed combustion components is not.

If short cycling continues after a fresh filter and open vents, it's time for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows the pattern, not a guess:

1. **Watches a full cycle** and notes timing — does the burner die in seconds (flame proving) or after minutes (overheating)?
2. **Reads the control-board flash code** through the inspection window to see whether the board logged a flame, limit, or pressure-switch fault.
3. **Checks temperature rise** across the supply and return with a thermometer — high rise confirms an airflow/overheating problem.
4. **Measures flame-sense microamps** with a meter; low microamps = dirty or failing sensor or bad ground.
5. **Inspects the blower wheel, filter and coil** for restriction; tests the inducer and pressure switch.
6. **Tests limit and rollout switches** for continuity and proper trip behavior.
7. **Verifies gas pressure and igniter** before condemning the board.

You can sanity-check a quote: if a tech wants to sell you a control board or gas valve *before* checking the filter, temperature rise, and flame-sense reading, get a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Burner lights, dies after a few seconds | Dirty flame sensor / weak ground | Replace filter; otherwise call | Clean or replace flame sensor, fix ground, test microamps |
| Runs several minutes then shuts off | Overheating from low airflow | Replace filter, open all vents/returns | Check temp rise, clean blower/coil, test limit switch |
| Cycles rapidly, no clear pattern | Thermostat fault or oversizing | Replace stat batteries, verify settings | Check stat wiring/anticipator, evaluate sizing |
| Water near furnace, cycle cuts short (96%) | Plugged condensate drain | Clear visible clog, empty pan | Clear drain trap, test pressure switch |
| Igniter glows but no sustained flame | Failing igniter or gas/valve issue | None — call | Test igniter resistance, gas pressure, valve |
| Won't complete cycle, board flashes limit code | High-limit switch tripping/failing | Replace filter first | Test/replace limit switch, find airflow cause |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Flame sensor cleaning:** $80–$150 service call; **replacement** $150–$300.
- **Hot surface igniter:** $150–$350.
- **High-limit switch:** $150–$300.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $100–$250.
- **Pressure switch:** $150–$350.
- **Blower motor cleaning vs. replacement:** $150 (clean) to $450–$700 (ECM motor).
- **Control board:** $400–$600.
- **Gas valve:** $400–$600.

Most short-cycling calls end at the cheap end — filter, flame sensor, or drain. Board and gas-valve replacements are the exception, not the rule.
