## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman fault code — it's a behavior. Your furnace fires up, runs for a short time (often less than a few minutes), shuts down, then restarts again soon after, repeating far more often than normal. Depending on your model, the control board may or may not flash a diagnostic LED code while this happens.

Short cycling almost always means the furnace is being shut down early by a safety device — most commonly the **high-limit switch** sensing the heat exchanger getting too hot — or it's losing flame and dropping out before a full heat call is satisfied. On Goodman units with an integrated ignition control, you may see LED flash patterns pointing to "open limit," "flame loss," or "pressure switch" issues. The exact code map is printed on the inside of the burner-compartment door and varies by model, so read the legend on *your* unit rather than assuming.

Because some causes (a cracked heat exchanger, a failing gas valve) are safety-critical, we rate this **pro**: do the safe homeowner checks below, but plan on a technician if the simple fixes don't stop it.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the most common cause by far. Restricted airflow overheats the heat exchanger, tripping the high-limit switch and shutting the burners off early.
2. **Dirty flame sensor** — a sooted or oxidized flame rod stops "seeing" the flame, so the board drops the burners within seconds as a safety response, then retries.
3. **Blocked or restricted airflow** — closed/blocked supply registers, undersized or crushed return ducts, or a dirty blower wheel cause the same overheating as a dirty filter.
4. **Thermostat problems** — bad location (near a vent or in sun), dead batteries, loose wiring, or "cycles per hour" / anticipator settings set wrong.
5. **Faulty high-limit switch** — a weak or failing limit switch can trip prematurely even when airflow is fine.
6. **Pressure switch / venting issues** — partially blocked flue or condensate line, or a marginal inducer, can cause the pressure switch to open mid-cycle.
7. **Flame sensor wiring or grounding fault** — poor ground at the burner assembly causes intermittent flame-loss shutdowns.
8. **Cracked heat exchanger** — less common but serious; can cause erratic flame behavior and limit trips, and is a carbon-monoxide concern. Requires a pro.
9. **Oversized furnace** — if the furnace was overspecified for the home, it can satisfy the thermostat too fast and cycle frequently even when nothing is broken.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or training:

- **Filter:** Pull the filter. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it with the correct size (printed on the old filter's edge). This alone fixes a large share of short-cycling cases.
- **Thermostat:** Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Make sure it's set to **Heat**, the setpoint is above room temperature, and the fan is on **Auto**. If it sits near a supply vent or in direct sun, that can cause false short cycles.
- **Supply and return vents:** Walk the house and open any closed registers; remove rugs, furniture, or boxes blocking supply vents and especially **return grilles**. Restricted airflow is a top cause.
- **Breaker:** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on (flip it firmly off then on once if it looks tripped — once only).
- **Condensate (high-efficiency units):** If your furnace has a condensate drain line and trap, check that the drain isn't clogged or backed up, which can trip the pressure switch. Clear visible standing water if you can do so safely.

Do **not** bypass the high-limit or pressure switch, do not jump out safety devices, and do not keep resetting a furnace that locks out repeatedly — that defeats protections that exist for a reason. If you smell gas or a burning odor, stop and call your gas utility's emergency line.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will work the cycle in roughly this order, so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Read the LED flash code** at the control board and check it against the door legend for limit, flame, or pressure faults.
2. **Check airflow / temperature rise** — measure the supply-vs-return temperature rise against the rating-plate spec. A rise above spec confirms an airflow restriction (filter, ducts, blower).
3. **Inspect and test the flame sensor** — measure microamp signal; clean or replace the flame rod and verify burner ground.
4. **Test the high-limit switch** — confirm it's opening at the correct temperature and not prematurely.
5. **Check the pressure switch and venting** — inspect the inducer, flue, and condensate drain for restriction.
6. **Inspect the heat exchanger** — visual and sometimes combustion-analysis check for cracks, which is a safety-critical, replace-or-replace-furnace decision.
7. **Verify thermostat wiring and settings** — anticipator/cycle-rate config and location.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs a few minutes, shuts off, repeats; filter is dirty | Overheating / high-limit trip from airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Verify temperature rise, clean blower |
| Burners light then go out in 3–7 seconds, retries | Dirty flame sensor / bad ground | None safe beyond filter check | Clean or replace flame sensor, fix ground |
| Short cycles with thermostat near vent or dead batteries | Thermostat issue | Replace batteries, check location/settings | Rewire, set cycle rate, relocate stat |
| Cycles with whistling or weak airflow | Blocked ducts / closed registers / dirty blower | Open registers, clear returns | Inspect ductwork, clean blower wheel |
| Trips off even with clean filter and open vents | Failing high-limit switch | None | Test and replace limit switch |
| High-efficiency unit, water near furnace, cycles | Clogged condensate / pressure switch | Clear visible drain blockage | Clear trap, test pressure switch/inducer |
| Erratic flame, soot, or CO alarm | Possible cracked heat exchanger | Shut down, ventilate | Combustion test, replace exchanger/furnace |

## Repair costs

Honest US ballpark ranges (parts + labor; regional and model variation applies):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 DIY.
- **Flame sensor clean or replace:** $80–$250.
- **High-limit switch replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Pressure switch replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Inducer motor replacement:** $300–$650.
- **Blower motor / wheel cleaning or replacement:** $300–$900.
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Duct repair / return correction:** $200–$1,000+ depending on scope.
- **Cracked heat exchanger:** $1,200+, and often makes full furnace replacement the better value, especially on older units.

If a tech quotes a major repair, ask which specific component they confirmed and what reading proved it (temperature rise, microamps, limit-trip temp) — those numbers separate a real diagnosis from a guess.
