## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman fault code — it is a behavior. Your furnace turns on, runs for a short time (anywhere from a few seconds to a minute or two), shuts off, and then restarts soon after. On most Goodman gas furnaces the integrated control board will flash a diagnostic LED code through a sight glass on the lower door; the specific blink pattern varies by board generation, so check the wiring/diagnostic label inside your furnace door for your model's legend.

Short cycling is a symptom that something is interrupting the heat call early. The cause ranges from a harmless dirty filter to a failing limit switch, pressure switch, flame sensor or control board. Because it can mask an overheating condition, persistent short cycling should be diagnosed rather than ignored.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Clogged air filter or blocked airflow.** Restricted return air overheats the heat exchanger, tripping the high-limit switch and cutting the burners early. This is the single most common cause.
2. **Dirty or failing flame sensor.** The burners light, but the sensor doesn't confirm flame, so the board shuts gas off within a few seconds and retries.
3. **Overheating / high-limit trip.** Beyond the filter, this includes closed registers, undersized ductwork, a dirty blower wheel, or a weak blower motor that can't move enough air.
4. **Pressure switch / venting issues.** A partially blocked flue or condensate drain (on 90%+ models), or a marginal inducer, can open the pressure switch mid-cycle and stop the burners.
5. **Thermostat problems.** A bad thermostat, wrong location (near a register or heat source), low batteries, or loose wiring can cause rapid on/off calls.
6. **Flue or condensate blockage on high-efficiency models.** Water backing up in the condensate trap trips the pressure switch repeatedly.
7. **Oversized furnace.** A unit too large for the home satisfies the thermostat quickly, then restarts often. This is a design issue, not a failure.
8. **Failing control board or limit switch.** Less common, but a degraded board or limit switch can cut cycles short or read false faults.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **Do not** bypass any limit, pressure or rollout switch, open the gas valve, or repeatedly reset a furnace that is locking out.

- **Air filter.** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes many short-cycling cases.
- **Thermostat.** Replace the batteries if it uses them. Confirm it's set to **Heat** and that the setpoint is above room temperature. Make sure it isn't mounted in direct sun or near a supply register.
- **Supply and return vents.** Walk the house and open any closed registers; clear furniture or rugs blocking returns. Restricting airflow causes overheating trips.
- **Breaker.** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on. A breaker that's tripping points to an electrical fault — call a pro, don't keep resetting it.
- **Condensate (90%+ models).** Check the condensate drain line and trap for clogs or standing water. A backed-up trap can trip the pressure switch. Clearing a visible clog is safe.
- **Read the LED code.** Note the blink pattern through the door sight glass and match it to the label inside the door. Have this ready if you call a technician.

If short cycling continues after a clean filter, fresh batteries and open vents, it's time for a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician should:

- Read the control board's LED diagnostic code and check fault history.
- Measure temperature rise across the heat exchanger and compare it to the rating-plate spec — high rise points to an airflow problem.
- Test the high-limit switch, flame sensor microamp signal, and pressure switch operation while the unit runs.
- Inspect the inducer motor, flue and (on 90%+ units) the condensate trap for blockages.
- Check the blower wheel and motor for proper airflow.
- Verify thermostat wiring and operation, and confirm correct furnace sizing for the home.

Ask the tech to show you the LED code and any out-of-spec readings. A quote to replace a control board or limit switch should come with a measured reason, not a guess.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Burners run a few minutes then quit, blower keeps running | Overheating / high-limit trip from dirty filter | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp rise, test limit switch, clean blower |
| Burners light then drop out in 3–7 seconds | Dirty or failing flame sensor | Replace filter first | Clean or replace flame sensor, check microamps |
| Furnace cycles rapidly, LED shows pressure-switch code | Blocked flue, condensate backup, weak inducer | Clear visible condensate clog | Test pressure switch, inspect flue/inducer |
| On/off every minute regardless of temperature | Thermostat fault, low batteries, bad wiring | Replace batteries, check setpoint | Test/replace thermostat, repair wiring |
| Frequent short cycles, otherwise normal heat | Oversized furnace | None | Confirm sizing; suggest staging/airflow adjustment |
| Cycling plus tripping breaker | Electrical/board fault | Don't keep resetting | Diagnose control board and wiring |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges including parts and labor (varies by region and contractor):

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 DIY.
- **Thermostat batteries:** Under $10 DIY.
- **Flame sensor clean:** Often included in a service call; replacement part $10–$30, or $90–$200 with labor.
- **Pressure switch:** $90–$300 installed.
- **High-limit switch:** $100–$300 installed.
- **Inducer motor:** $250–$500 installed.
- **Control / ignition board:** $300–$600 installed, depending on board.
- **Diagnostic / service call:** $80–$180, often credited toward the repair.
- **Oversized furnace:** No simple fix; mitigation or replacement is a larger conversation with your HVAC contractor.
