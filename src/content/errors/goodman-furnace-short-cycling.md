## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman error code — it is a symptom where your furnace turns on and off far more often than it should, usually running only a few minutes (or seconds) per cycle. A properly sized, healthy furnace typically runs longer cycles and cycles a handful of times per hour in cold weather.

On most Goodman boards, short cycling is the furnace's safety logic doing its job: a limit switch, flame sensor, pressure switch, or control board is detecting a problem and shutting down the burners early to protect the equipment. Depending on the board and ignition type (single-stage, two-stage, or modulating), you may or may not see a diagnostic LED flash code on the control board. If you do, note the flash pattern and match it to the legend printed on the blower-door label — behavior varies by model.

Short cycling is rated **pro** because the common causes range from a $20 filter to a failing control board or improperly sized equipment, and chasing the wrong one wastes money.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — Restricted airflow overheats the heat exchanger; the limit switch trips and cuts the burners early. Most common and cheapest cause.
2. **Dirty flame sensor** — A coated sensor can't confirm flame, so the board shuts the gas valve seconds after ignition, then retries. Classic "fires up, dies in 5–10 seconds" pattern.
3. **Blocked supply/return vents or undersized ductwork** — Closed registers, crushed flex duct, or too few returns starve airflow and trip the high-limit switch.
4. **Thermostat issues** — Bad placement (near a heat source or supply vent), wiring problems, dead batteries, or anticipator/cycle-rate settings that cycle too fast.
5. **Faulty or tripping high-limit switch** — Either responding to a real overheat or failing prematurely.
6. **Pressure switch / flue or condensate blockage** — On 90%+ condensing models, a blocked condensate drain or flue can open the pressure switch and abort cycles.
7. **Cracked heat exchanger or flame rollout events** — Safety devices shut things down. (See related Flame Rollout page.)
8. **Failing control board or gas valve** — Less common; intermittent faults cause erratic cycling.
9. **Oversized furnace** — Heats the home too fast and satisfies the thermostat quickly. Common after a mismatched replacement; only fixable by proper sizing.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner — no panels held open, no safety switches bypassed, no gas valves touched.

- **Filter:** Pull and inspect the air filter. If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, replace it. Re-test.
- **Thermostat:** Replace the batteries if applicable. Confirm it's set to **Heat** and the temperature is above room temp. Make sure it isn't mounted in direct sun or near a supply vent.
- **Registers and returns:** Walk the house and open any closed supply registers; clear furniture, rugs, or boxes blocking return grilles. Most rooms should stay open for proper airflow.
- **Breaker:** Check the furnace breaker isn't half-tripped. Reset it **once** if needed — do not repeatedly reset a furnace that keeps shutting down.
- **Condensate (90%+ models):** Look for a full or overflowing condensate trap/drain line. A clogged drain can trip a float switch. Clearing a visibly blocked external drain line is fine; internal work is a pro job.
- **Visible vents:** Confirm the outdoor intake/exhaust pipes (on condensing units) aren't blocked by snow, leaves, or a nest.

If short cycling continues after these checks, stop and call a licensed HVAC technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech follows a logical sequence so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Reads the LED flash code** on the control board and reviews the cycle history if the board logs faults.
2. **Measures temperature rise** across the heat exchanger and compares it to the rating-plate spec to spot airflow restriction or overfiring.
3. **Checks static pressure** in the ductwork to confirm airflow problems vs. equipment problems.
4. **Tests the flame sensor microamps** — a healthy signal is typically a few microamps; a weak reading means cleaning or replacement.
5. **Verifies the limit switch and pressure switch** operate at the correct setpoints with a meter and manometer.
6. **Inspects the igniter, gas valve, and heat exchanger** for cracks, hot spots, or flame rollout evidence.
7. **Evaluates sizing** — if everything checks out mechanically, a load calculation (Manual J) may reveal an oversized unit.

Expect a diagnostic fee of roughly **$80–$180**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs a few minutes, shuts off, repeats | Dirty filter / restricted airflow | Replace filter, open vents/returns | Measure temp rise & static pressure |
| Fires up, dies in 5–10 seconds | Dirty/failing flame sensor | None safe beyond filter check | Clean or replace sensor, test microamps |
| Cycles fast in mild weather | Thermostat placement or cycle rate | Move heat sources, replace batteries | Adjust cycle rate/anticipator, check wiring |
| Shuts off on overheat (limit) | Limit switch tripping on heat | Replace filter, clear vents | Test/replace limit switch, find airflow cause |
| Inducer runs but no ignition cycle | Pressure switch / flue / condensate block | Clear visible drain or vent blockage | Inspect flue, trap, pressure switch |
| Erratic, no clear pattern | Control board or gas valve fault | None | Diagnose and replace board/valve |
| New furnace heats too fast | Oversized equipment | None | Manual J load calc, staging adjustment |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter:** $15–$40 (DIY)
- **Flame sensor clean or replace:** $80–$250
- **Hot surface igniter:** $150–$350
- **High-limit switch:** $150–$400
- **Pressure switch:** $150–$400
- **Condensate drain cleaning:** $100–$250
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$400
- **Control board:** $300–$700
- **Gas valve:** $300–$600
- **Heat exchanger (if cracked):** $1,000–$2,500+ — often makes replacement worth considering on older units
- **Correcting oversizing:** varies widely; may involve duct modifications or, in severe cases, replacement

Always get the LED flash code and a measured reason (microamps, temp rise, static pressure) before approving major parts.
