## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman error code — it's a behavior. Your furnace
fires up, runs for a short period (often under 3–5 minutes), shuts down, then
restarts a short time later. On Goodman models with a diagnostic LED or display,
short cycling often shows up alongside a stored code for an **open limit switch**,
**flame sensed but lost**, or **pressure switch fault**. The exact code text and
flash pattern varies by control board, so check the legend on the inner blower-door
label for your specific unit.

The furnace is usually protecting itself. A safety switch — most often the
high-temperature limit — is opening because the unit is overheating or losing flame,
and the control is shutting things down before damage occurs. Short cycling is a
symptom that something upstream is wrong, not the root problem.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — The single most common cause. Restricted return
   air lets the heat exchanger overheat, tripping the high-limit switch.
2. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — Too few open registers or closed
   dampers starve airflow and cause the same overheating trip.
3. **Dirty flame sensor** — A carbon-coated flame rod fails to confirm flame, so the
   board shuts the gas valve seconds after ignition, then retries.
4. **Thermostat problems** — Dead batteries, poor placement near a supply vent, or
   wiring issues can command rapid on/off cycles.
5. **Oversized furnace** — A furnace too large for the home heats the air fast and
   satisfies the thermostat quickly, then cycles repeatedly. Common in homes with
   replacement units sized by rule-of-thumb.
6. **Failing high-limit or rollout switch** — A weak or intermittent limit can open
   prematurely even with adequate airflow.
7. **Pressure switch / inducer / venting issues** — A partially blocked flue, weak
   inducer, or marginal pressure switch can interrupt the cycle.
8. **Control board fault** — Less common; a board with bad relays or sensing logic
   can mis-cycle the furnace.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Do not bypass safety switches, open the gas valve
beyond its normal position, or repeatedly reset a furnace that keeps locking out.

- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray, clogged, or older than a month, swap it.
  This resolves a large share of short-cycling cases.
- **Open your vents.** Make sure supply registers and return grilles aren't blocked
  by furniture, rugs, or closed dampers. Keep most registers open.
- **Check the thermostat.** Install fresh batteries if it's battery-powered. Make
  sure it's set to Heat and not located in direct sun or near a supply vent.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on; a tripped or
  half-tripped breaker can cause odd cycling.
- **Look at the condensate drain** (high-efficiency models). A clogged drain or full
  trap can trip a pressure or float switch. Clear visible blockage only.
- **Listen and count.** Note how long it runs and what shuts it off (blower keeps
  running? burners cut out first?). This helps a tech diagnose faster.

If the furnace still short cycles after a clean filter and these checks, stop
resetting it and call a licensed HVAC technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech won't just throw parts at it. Expect them to:

- Read stored fault codes from the control board's diagnostic LED.
- Measure **temperature rise** across the furnace and compare it to the rating-plate
  spec — high rise confirms an airflow/overheating problem.
- Inspect and clean or test the **flame sensor** with a microamp reading.
- Test the **high-limit and rollout switches** for resistance and trip behavior.
- Check **inducer operation and pressure switch** closure, and inspect the flue for
  blockage.
- Verify **blower speed and CFM**, and check for a dirty blower wheel or evaporator
  coil restricting airflow.
- For oversized-unit suspicion, perform or review a **Manual J** load calculation.

You can sanity-check a quote by asking what the temperature rise reading was and
which safety device was tripping. A blanket "it needs a new board" without those
readings is a red flag.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs 3–5 min, blower keeps running after burners stop | Overheating, limit trips | Replace filter, open vents | Check temp rise, clean blower/coil, test limit |
| Lights, then shuts off within seconds, retries | Dirty flame sensor | None (located near burners) | Clean or replace flame sensor, check microamps |
| Rapid on/off every minute or two | Thermostat fault or placement | Fresh batteries, check location | Test thermostat wiring, relocate if needed |
| Heats fast, satisfies quickly, cycles often | Oversized furnace | None | Manual J load calc, staging/airflow adjustment |
| Inducer runs, no ignition, repeats | Pressure switch / flue blockage | Clear visible drain/vent debris | Test pressure switch, inspect flue, inducer |
| Intermittent shutdowns with no pattern | Failing limit or control board | Confirm filter is clean | Test limit switches, board relays |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus labor. Your area and exact Goodman model affect price.

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Thermostat batteries / minor thermostat fix:** $0–$30 DIY; $150–$350 for a new
  thermostat installed.
- **Flame sensor clean or replace:** $80–$250 installed; the part itself is cheap.
- **High-limit or rollout switch:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Pressure switch:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Inducer motor:** $300–$600 installed.
- **Control board:** $400–$700+ installed, depending on model.
- **Oversized-furnace correction:** varies widely; airflow tuning is modest, but
  resizing/replacement runs into the thousands.

If a tech finds a **cracked heat exchanger** during diagnosis, that's a separate
serious safety issue and the furnace should be shut down — see the related pages
below.
