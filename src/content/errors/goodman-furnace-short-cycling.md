## What this code means

"Short cycling" isn't a single Goodman fault code — it's a behavior. Your furnace starts a heat cycle, then shuts down before the thermostat is satisfied, often restarting again minutes later. Some Goodman boards will flash a diagnostic LED (for example an open limit or a weak flame-sense code) when this happens; others simply run, drop out, and retry without a stored code. **Because the exact LED pattern varies by control board, always read the legend printed inside your furnace's blower-door panel rather than assuming.**

The furnace is protecting itself. Most short cycling traces back to one of two things: the unit is **overheating** (so a limit switch shuts off the burners), or the **flame is being lost** (so the board drops the gas valve for safety). Both are usually caused by something upstream — restricted airflow or a dirty sensor — not a failed major component.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the #1 cause. Restricted airflow lets the heat exchanger overheat, tripping the high-limit switch within a couple of minutes.
2. **Dirty flame sensor** — a carbon-coated sensor reads a weak flame signal, so the board shuts the gas valve as a safety measure, then retries.
3. **Thermostat issues** — bad placement (near a supply vent or in sunlight), low batteries, or a miswired/aggressive cycle setting causes rapid on/off.
4. **Restricted airflow elsewhere** — closed or blocked vents, undersized return ducting, or a dirty blower wheel that mimics a dirty filter.
5. **Tripping high-limit switch** — from overheating, or occasionally a failing limit switch itself.
6. **Blocked condensate drain (high-efficiency models)** — a tripped pressure or float switch from a clogged drain can interrupt the cycle.
7. **Failing inducer motor or pressure switch** — causes the board to abort the sequence before or during ignition.
8. **Oversized furnace** — if it's been short cycling since install, the unit may simply be too large for the home and heats the thermostat too fast.
9. **Control board or gas valve fault** — least common, but a failing board can mis-time or abort cycles.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. **Do not** bypass safety switches, open the gas valve, or repeatedly reset a furnace that has locked out.

- **Check the filter.** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see light through it, replace it. This alone fixes a large share of short-cycling calls.
- **Check the thermostat.** Replace the batteries if it uses them. Confirm it isn't in direct sun, near a register, or set to an unusual cycle rate. Make sure it's in "Heat" and the setpoint is above room temperature.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on (flip it firmly off, then on once).
- **Check supply and return vents.** Open closed registers and remove rugs or furniture blocking returns — choked airflow overheats the unit.
- **Check the condensate drain (high-efficiency 90%+ units).** If you see standing water in the drain pan or a clogged drain line, clearing it may release a tripped float switch.

If short cycling continues after a clean filter, good batteries, and clear vents, it's time for a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech works in a logical order so you can sanity-check the quote:

1. **Watches a full cycle** and reads the control-board LED code through the sight glass.
2. **Measures temperature rise** across the furnace and compares it to the rating-plate range — high rise confirms an airflow/overheating problem.
3. **Checks the flame-sense microamp signal** with a meter; a low reading points to a dirty or failing flame sensor.
4. **Tests the high-limit switch** for continuity and verifies it isn't tripping prematurely.
5. **Inspects the blower wheel, ducting and static pressure** if the filter is clean but airflow is still poor.
6. **Tests the pressure switch and inducer** and inspects the condensate path on high-efficiency models.
7. **Checks the control board timing and gas valve** only after ruling out the cheaper, more common causes above.

Be cautious of a quote that jumps straight to a control board or gas valve without measuring temperature rise or flame signal first.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Burners light, run 2-5 min, shut off, blower keeps running | Overheating from dirty filter / blocked airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp rise, check limit switch & blower |
| Burners light, flame drops out within seconds, retries | Dirty/failing flame sensor | None safe beyond filter check | Clean or replace flame sensor, test microamps |
| Furnace cycles every few minutes regardless of run | Thermostat placement or low batteries | Replace batteries, relocate concerns | Verify wiring, recalibrate or relocate stat |
| Cycles with standing water near unit (HE models) | Clogged condensate drain / float switch | Clear visible drain blockage | Clear drain, test float/pressure switch |
| Never gets to ignition, inducer hums then stops | Pressure switch or inducer fault | None | Test inducer, pressure switch & tubing |
| Short cycling since day one, comfort uneven | Oversized furnace | None | Load calc, staging/airflow adjustment |
| Random aborts, odd LED codes | Control board / gas valve | None | Test board timing and gas valve, replace if confirmed |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor included unless noted:

- **Air filter** — $10–$45 DIY. The cheapest possible fix.
- **Flame sensor clean or replace** — $100–$300; the part itself is often $15–$40.
- **Thermostat repair/relocation or replacement** — $100–$400 depending on model.
- **High-limit switch** — $150–$350.
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch** — $100–$300.
- **Pressure switch** — $150–$350.
- **Inducer motor** — $300–$600+.
- **Control board** — $400–$650.
- **Gas valve** — $400–$700.
- **Duct/airflow correction or addressing an oversized furnace** — highly variable; can run from a few hundred dollars to a system redesign.

Always get the temperature rise and flame-sense readings as part of the diagnosis — they justify which of these parts (if any) you're actually paying for.
