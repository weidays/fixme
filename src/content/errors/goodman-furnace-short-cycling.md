## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman fault code — it is a behavior: the furnace fires up, runs for a short period (often under a few minutes), then shuts off, and repeats. On Goodman furnaces with diagnostic LEDs, short cycling often shows up alongside a stored fault such as an open limit, a flame-sense failure, or a pressure-switch issue, depending on your board and model.

The pattern matters. A furnace that lights and runs only a minute or two before the blower shuts the burners down is usually **overheating** (airflow problem). A furnace that lights, then drops out after a few seconds and re-tries is usually a **flame-sensing or ignition** problem. A furnace that cycles on the thermostat without ever building real heat is often a **thermostat or oversizing** issue.

Because the cause can be airflow, gas, or controls, and several of those require combustion measurements, this is rated **pro** unless the fix is a filter or thermostat battery.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow overheats the heat exchanger and trips the high-limit switch, cutting the burners early.
2. **Dirty flame sensor** — a fouled flame rod can't confirm flame, so the board drops the gas valve a few seconds after ignition, then retries.
3. **Blocked or closed supply/return airflow** — too many closed registers, blocked returns, undersized ductwork, or a dirty blower wheel all overheat the unit.
4. **Faulty or tripping high-limit switch** — a failing limit may open prematurely even with adequate airflow.
5. **Thermostat issues** — bad location (near a supply vent), low batteries, or a too-tight anticipator/cycle-rate setting can cause rapid cycling.
6. **Plugged condensate drain (high-efficiency models)** — a blocked drain trips the pressure switch / float switch and interrupts the cycle. Applies to condensing GMVC/GMEC/GMES-type units.
7. **Pressure switch or inducer/venting fault** — partial vent blockage or a weak inducer can cause intermittent trips and retries.
8. **Oversized furnace** — a unit too large for the home heats the air fast, satisfies the thermostat quickly, and cycles frequently. This is a design problem, not a broken part.
9. **Control board fault** — less common; a failing ignition control board can misread inputs and cycle the unit.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for a homeowner. Do **not** open the gas valve manually, bypass any limit or safety switch, or repeatedly reset a locked-out furnace.

- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone resolves a large share of short-cycling calls.
- **Open closed registers.** Make sure most supply registers are open and return grilles aren't blocked by furniture or rugs.
- **Check the thermostat.** Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Confirm it's set to Heat and not located right next to a vent or heat source. If it has a "cycle rate" or "cycles per hour" setting, make sure it isn't set abnormally high.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on and not partially tripped. Don't repeatedly flip it to force restarts.
- **Look at visible vents.** Verify outdoor intake/exhaust pipes (high-efficiency units) aren't blocked by snow, leaves, or a nest.
- **Check the condensate drain (high-efficiency units).** If there's standing water near the furnace or the trap looks plugged, the float switch may be interrupting cycles. You can clear a visibly clogged drain line, but leave electrical/safety switches alone.

If short cycling continues after a fresh filter and clear vents, stop and call a pro.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good technician should:

- **Watch a full cycle** and note exactly when and why it shuts down (limit trip vs flame loss vs thermostat).
- **Measure temperature rise** across the furnace and compare it to the rating-plate range — high rise confirms an airflow/overheating problem.
- **Check static pressure** to find duct restriction, dirty blower wheel, or undersized return.
- **Read the diagnostic LED / fault history** on the board for stored codes (limit open, flame fail, pressure switch).
- **Test the flame sensor** microamp signal and clean or replace it if weak.
- **Inspect the high-limit switch, pressure switch, inducer, and condensate path** for intermittent faults.
- **Verify thermostat wiring and settings**, and assess whether the furnace is oversized for the home.

Ask which specific fault they observed and what measurement supports the repair. "Replace the board" without a stored board fault or process of elimination deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs 1–3 min then blower stays on, burners off | Overheating from dirty filter / blocked airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp rise & static pressure, clean blower |
| Lights then drops out after a few seconds, retries | Dirty/failing flame sensor | None safe beyond filter | Test flame-sense microamps, clean/replace sensor |
| Cycles rapidly without building heat | Thermostat fault/location/cycle rate | Replace batteries, check settings/location | Verify wiring, adjust cycle rate, relocate stat |
| Standing water near furnace, intermittent shutdowns | Plugged condensate drain (HE models) | Clear visible drain clog | Clear trap/float switch, verify drainage |
| Stops with limit/pressure fault in LED history | Limit switch, pressure switch, or inducer | None | Replace limit/pressure switch, inspect inducer/vent |
| Frequent short cycles in cold weather, even when clean | Oversized furnace / design issue | None | Load calc, staging adjustment, equipment recommendation |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY).
- **Flame sensor clean or replace:** $80–$250.
- **High-limit switch replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Pressure switch replacement:** $150–$400.
- **Condensate drain clearing / float switch:** $100–$300.
- **Blower wheel cleaning:** $150–$400.
- **Thermostat replacement:** $100–$350 (basic) to $250–$500+ (smart).
- **Inducer motor replacement:** $300–$650.
- **Integrated control board replacement:** $400–$700+.
- **Ductwork / oversizing remediation:** highly variable; get a load calculation first.

Diagnostic/service call fees ($90–$180) are often credited toward the repair. Always get the specific failed component named on the invoice.
