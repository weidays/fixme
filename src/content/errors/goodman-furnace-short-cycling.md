## What this code means

"Short cycling" isn't a single blink code — it's a symptom. Your Goodman furnace turns the burners on, runs for only a short time (often under 3 minutes), shuts off, then restarts a few minutes later. Most Goodman and Amana furnaces don't flash a dedicated "short cycling" fault; instead the control board may show a limit-open code, flame-sense fault, or nothing at all while the pattern repeats.

The furnace is doing this to protect itself. Its integrated ignition control board watches the flame sensor, high-limit switch, pressure switch, and thermostat call. When one of those signals drops out mid-cycle, the board shuts the burners off and tries again. That repeated stop/start is the short cycling you're hearing.

Because the actual cause ranges from a $20 filter to a failing control board, and because it involves the gas burners and safety limits, we've flagged this **pro** for anything beyond basic homeowner checks.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — The most common cause by far. Restricted airflow makes the heat exchanger overheat, tripping the high-limit switch and shutting the burners off. Once it cools, it fires again.
2. **Dirty or failing flame sensor** — A weak flame-sense signal makes the board think flame was lost. It shuts the gas valve within seconds and retries, causing rapid cycling. Very common on older Goodman units.
3. **Blocked airflow beyond the filter** — Closed/blocked supply registers, a dirty blower wheel, undersized ductwork, or a collapsed flex duct cause the same overheating as a dirty filter.
4. **Thermostat problems** — Wrong location (near a supply vent or heat source), loose wiring, dead batteries, or a short between the R and W wires can make the furnace start and stop erratically.
5. **Flue/pressure switch issues** — A partially blocked flue, cracked inducer hose, or weak inducer motor can cause the pressure switch to open mid-cycle and shut the furnace down.
6. **Clogged condensate drain (high-efficiency models)** — On condensing Goodman furnaces (GMVC/GMEC/GMES 90%+), a plugged drain trips a safety and interrupts the cycle.
7. **Failing high-limit switch** — A limit switch that opens too early, even with normal airflow, will nuisance-trip the burners.
8. **Oversized furnace** — A furnace too large for the home heats the space fast, satisfies the thermostat, and repeats. Not a broken part, but a real cause.
9. **Control board or gas valve fault** — Least common but possible; intermittent board or valve failures cause erratic operation.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop here — do not open the burner compartment, bypass any safety switch, or touch the gas valve.

- **Check and replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or you can't see light through it, swap it. This alone fixes a large share of short-cycling calls.
- **Check the thermostat.** Replace the batteries if it's battery-powered. Set it to "Heat" and a few degrees above room temp. Make sure it isn't in a drafty spot or right under a supply vent.
- **Check the breaker.** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on. A tripped or half-tripped breaker can cause odd behavior — reset it once.
- **Check supply and return vents.** Walk the house and open any closed registers. Make sure furniture, rugs, or vent covers aren't blocking return-air grilles. Restricted return air overheats the furnace.
- **Check the condensate drain (90%+ furnaces).** If you see standing water near the furnace or a full drain line, the safety switch may be tripping. Clearing a visible clog is fine; anything internal is a pro job.

If the furnace still short cycles after a clean filter, open vents, and good batteries, it's time to call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech won't just guess. Here's the typical sequence so you can sanity-check the visit:

- **Confirm the pattern** — how long it runs, when it shuts off (during ignition, mid-run, or at limit).
- **Read the board's flash code** with the door interlock and pull stored fault history if the board supports it.
- **Measure temperature rise** across the furnace and compare it to the nameplate range. High rise points to airflow restriction; low rise points to other issues.
- **Test the flame sensor** microamp signal (typically expecting a few microamps of clean DC). A weak reading means clean or replace the sensor.
- **Inspect the blower wheel, ductwork, and static pressure** to rule out airflow beyond the filter.
- **Test the high-limit and pressure switches** for nuisance tripping and check the inducer, flue, and hoses.
- **Check thermostat wiring** for shorts or loose connections.
- **Evaluate sizing** if no component fault is found — a load calculation may show the unit is oversized.

Expect a diagnostic fee of roughly **$90–$180**, often credited toward the repair.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs a few minutes, shuts off, restarts | Dirty filter / overheating | Replace filter, open vents | Test temp rise, check blower & static pressure |
| Flame lights then dies within seconds | Dirty/failing flame sensor | None (pro) | Clean or replace flame sensor, test microamps |
| Erratic on/off, no clear pattern | Thermostat wiring or batteries | Replace batteries, check location | Inspect/repair thermostat wiring |
| Standing water near furnace | Clogged condensate drain (90%+) | Clear visible external clog | Clear internal trap, test safety switch |
| Shuts down after inducer runs | Pressure switch / blocked flue | Check outside flue for blockage | Test pressure switch, inducer, clear flue |
| Trips even with clean filter | Failing high-limit switch | None (pro) | Test and replace limit switch |
| Heats fast then repeats all day | Oversized furnace | None | Load calculation, staging/airflow adjustments |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined:

- **Air filter** — $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Flame sensor clean** — $0 DIY / $90–$180 with a service call
- **Flame sensor replacement** — $90–$250
- **Thermostat replacement** — $130–$400 depending on model
- **Condensate drain clearing** — $90–$250
- **Pressure switch** — $150–$350
- **High-limit switch** — $150–$400
- **Inducer motor** — $300–$650
- **Control board** — $300–$650
- **Gas valve** — $300–$600
- **Oversized furnace remedy** — variable; airflow tuning is cheap, replacement is a major cost

Many short-cycling calls end at the filter or flame sensor, so don't assume the worst until a tech has measured temperature rise and tested the sensor.
