## What this code means

Short cycling isn't a single Goodman fault code — it's a *behavior*. Your furnace starts a heat call, runs for a short time (often under 3–5 minutes), shuts off before the thermostat is satisfied, then restarts a few minutes later. Some Goodman boards will flash a diagnostic LED (commonly an "open limit" or "flame loss" pattern), while others cycle without flagging anything.

The pattern matters. **Overheat-driven** short cycling means the furnace lights, gets too hot because air isn't moving, and a safety limit shuts the burners down. **Flame-loss** short cycling means the burners light but the flame sensor stops "seeing" flame, so the board drops the gas valve. **Thermostat-driven** cycling means the furnace is actually being told to stop too soon. Each has different fixes and costs.

Because the underlying cause ranges from a $5 filter to a failing heat exchanger, this is rated **pro** — DIY a few safe checks, but bring in a technician if those don't resolve it.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — the single most common cause. Restricted airflow lets the heat exchanger overheat and trips the high-limit switch.
2. **Dirty flame sensor** — a carbon- or oxide-coated flame rod stops detecting flame, so the board shuts the gas valve within seconds and retries.
3. **Blocked or closed supply/return vents** — too many closed registers or blocked returns starve airflow, mimicking a dirty filter.
4. **Thermostat issues** — dead batteries, a thermostat in a bad location (near a vent or in direct sun), or loose wiring causing intermittent calls.
5. **Failing high-limit switch** — a limit that opens early or is faulty trips before the furnace is actually overheating.
6. **Restricted exhaust/intake (pressure switch)** — a partially blocked flue, intake, or condensate-clogged drain can cycle the pressure switch on two-pipe units.
7. **Oversized furnace** — a furnace too large for the home heats the air fast, satisfies the thermostat quickly, and cycles often. Common in mismatched installs.
8. **Flame rollout or cracked heat exchanger** — less common but serious; safeties may shut the unit down repeatedly. See the rollout page below.
9. **Failing control board or gas valve** — least likely, but a degraded board or valve can cause erratic on/off behavior.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are safe for any homeowner. Stop at anything that requires opening sealed compartments or touching gas/electrical components.

- **Replace the air filter.** If it's gray, matted, or older than 90 days, swap it. This fixes a large share of short-cycling calls. Run the furnace afterward and see if cycles lengthen.
- **Check the thermostat.** Replace batteries if it's battery-powered. Make sure it's set to **Heat** and the setpoint is above room temperature. Confirm it isn't mounted in direct sun or above a register.
- **Walk the house for blocked vents.** Open closed supply registers and clear furniture, rugs, or boxes from return grilles. Closing too many vents starves airflow.
- **Check the breaker.** A furnace cycling on a tripping breaker should be left off and inspected by a pro — don't keep resetting it.
- **Check the condensate drain** (high-efficiency two-pipe units). A clogged drain or full trap can trip the pressure switch. Clearing a visible clog at the trap is fine; don't disassemble internal tubing.
- **Look at the exterior intake/exhaust pipes** (PVC pipes on the outside wall). Clear snow, leaves, or nests blocking them.

If none of these lengthen the run time, it's time for a technician. **Do not repeatedly reset a furnace that keeps locking out** — that can mask a real safety problem.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech works the airflow-then-flame-then-controls path so you can sanity-check the quote:

- **Verify the symptom and read the LED.** They'll count the diagnostic flash code on the control board to see if it logs an open limit, flame loss, or pressure switch fault.
- **Measure temperature rise.** They compare supply and return air temperature against the rating-plate range. A high rise points to airflow restriction; a low rise points elsewhere.
- **Check static pressure and blower.** High static pressure confirms an airflow problem — dirty filter, coil, undersized ducts, or a weak blower motor.
- **Test the flame sensor.** They measure microamp signal; a weak reading means a dirty or failing flame rod. Cleaning is often the first step.
- **Test safeties.** They check the high-limit switch and pressure switch for proper operation rather than just bypassing them.
- **Inspect the heat exchanger and burners** for rollout, cracks, or carbon buildup.
- **Check gas pressure and valve operation** last, since these are less common.

Be cautious of a quote that jumps straight to a control board or gas valve without first confirming the filter, flame sensor, and airflow are good.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs 3–5 min then shuts off, restarts | Dirty filter / blocked airflow | Replace filter, open vents | Measure temp rise, check static pressure, clean coil |
| Burners light then drop out in seconds | Dirty/failing flame sensor | None safe (internal) | Clean or replace flame sensor, verify microamps |
| Cycles with no clear pattern | Thermostat batteries/location/wiring | Replace batteries, relocate or recheck setpoint | Test thermostat wiring and anticipator settings |
| Heats fast, cuts off quickly, even airflow | Oversized furnace | None | Confirm sizing, adjust staging or fan speed |
| Cycling on two-pipe high-eff unit | Pressure switch / blocked flue or drain | Clear exterior pipes, clear drain clog | Test pressure switch, inspect flue and inducer |
| Repeated shutdowns + burning smell | Possible rollout / overheating | Shut off, call pro | Inspect rollout switch and heat exchanger |
| Limit trips even with clean filter | Failing high-limit switch | None | Test and replace limit switch |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor combined where a pro is involved:

- **Air filter:** $5–$25 DIY.
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5–$10 DIY.
- **Flame sensor cleaning:** $80–$160 on a service call.
- **Flame sensor replacement:** $120–$250.
- **High-limit switch replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Pressure switch replacement:** $150–$350.
- **Condensate drain clearing:** $75–$200.
- **Blower motor (if airflow is the root cause):** $400–$800.
- **Control board replacement:** $350–$600.
- **Gas valve replacement:** $400–$600.
- **Heat exchanger:** often $1,000+; on older units, weigh against replacement.

A basic diagnostic service call runs roughly **$80–$150**, often credited toward the repair. Many short-cycling calls end up being a filter and a flame-sensor cleaning — well under $250 total.
