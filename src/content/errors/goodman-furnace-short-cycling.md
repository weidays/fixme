## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a numbered Goodman fault code — it's a behavior. Your furnace lights its burners, runs for a short time, shuts off, then re-fires shortly after, over and over without satisfying the thermostat. On boards with a diagnostic LED, you may or may not see a flashing code depending on *why* it's cycling: an overheat trip or flame-sense problem often logs a code, while thermostat or oversizing issues usually log nothing.

A healthy Goodman gas furnace runs roughly 7–15 minutes per heat cycle. Cycles under about 5 minutes that repeat are abnormal and almost always point to overheating, a flame-sensing fault, an airflow restriction, or a thermostat issue.

Because some causes (heat exchanger overheating, control faults) carry safety and cost implications, we rate this **pro**: do the safe checks below, but plan on a technician if a filter and thermostat swap don't fix it.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — restricts airflow, furnace overheats, the high-limit switch cuts the burners early. By far the most common and a free DIY fix.
2. **Dirty or failing flame sensor** — the board can't confirm flame, so it shuts the gas valve seconds after ignition, then retries. Common on units 5+ years old.
3. **Blocked supply/return airflow** — closed registers, crushed flex duct, a dirty blower wheel, or undersized ductwork cause the same overheating limit trips as a dirty filter.
4. **Faulty high-limit / rollout limit switch** — a weak or out-of-spec limit opens too early and shuts the furnace down prematurely.
5. **Thermostat problems** — bad location (near a vent), short anticipator setting, loose wiring, or dying batteries cause rapid on/off signals.
6. **Cracked or failing flame sensor wiring / poor ground** — intermittent flame signal causes erratic cycling.
7. **Oversized furnace** — an over-large unit heats the air fast and satisfies the thermostat too quickly. Common after a poor replacement install; not a "repair," it's a design issue.
8. **Failing inducer or pressure switch** — intermittent pressure-switch closure can stop and restart the sequence.
9. **Control board fault** — relays or sensing circuits misbehave. Diagnosed last, after cheaper causes are ruled out.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do. Do **not** bypass limit switches, open the gas valve, or repeatedly reset a furnace that has locked out.

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see light through it, replace it. Re-check the cycling.
- **Thermostat:** Set it to Heat, several degrees above room temperature. If it's a battery model, install fresh batteries.
- **Thermostat location:** Make sure it isn't mounted next to a supply vent, lamp, or in direct sun — false readings cause rapid cycling.
- **Vents and registers:** Open all supply registers and clear return grilles. Don't close off rooms to "save" heat; that restricts airflow.
- **Breaker:** If the furnace shut down entirely, check the breaker and the service switch (looks like a light switch near the unit) are on. Reset a breaker **once** only.
- **Condensate (high-efficiency / 90%+ models):** Check the drain line and trap aren't clogged. A backed-up trap can trip the pressure switch and interrupt cycles.
- **Listen and smell:** If you smell burning or see scorching, shut the furnace off and call a pro — see our flame rollout guide.

If a fresh filter and good thermostat don't restore normal cycles, stop and call a technician.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A competent tech should work the cheap-to-expensive path, not jump to a control board:

1. **Confirm the symptom** and read any stored LED flash codes on the board.
2. **Check airflow** — filter, blower wheel cleanliness, duct static pressure (a manometer reading) to find overheating from restriction.
3. **Measure temperature rise** across the furnace and compare it to the rating-plate range; a high rise confirms airflow-driven overheating.
4. **Test the flame sensor** with a microamp meter; clean or replace it and verify a stable signal.
5. **Check the high-limit and rollout switches** for proper open/close at spec.
6. **Inspect the pressure switch and inducer** for intermittent operation.
7. **Verify thermostat wiring and heat anticipator/cycle settings.**
8. **Evaluate sizing** if everything tests good — an oversized unit can't be "repaired," only managed.

Ask which test pointed to the failed part. A quote that replaces a control board without a microamp or static-pressure reading deserves a second opinion.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs 1-3 min, shuts off, restarts | Dirty filter / restricted airflow | Replace filter, open all vents | Measure static pressure & temp rise, clean blower |
| Burners light then die in ~5-7 sec, retries | Dirty/failing flame sensor | Replace filter first | Clean or replace flame sensor, check ground |
| Cycles with rapid clicking at thermostat | Thermostat / dead batteries | Fresh batteries, check location | Test wiring, set heat anticipator |
| Shuts off on overheat repeatedly | Weak high-limit switch | Confirm filter is clean | Test and replace limit switch |
| Stops mid-cycle, inducer noise | Pressure switch / inducer | Check condensate drain (90%+) | Test pressure switch, clear trap, replace inducer |
| Heats fast, satisfies stat too quickly | Oversized furnace | None | Confirm sizing, recommend staging/zoning |
| Random cycling, no pattern | Control board fault | None | Replace board after other causes ruled out |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts and labor where noted. Your area and contractor rates vary.

- **Air filter:** $8–$40 — DIY, the cheapest fix.
- **Flame sensor:** $80–$220 installed; the part is $10–$30 DIY if you're comfortable.
- **High-limit / rollout limit switch:** $120–$300 installed.
- **Pressure switch:** $150–$350 installed.
- **Blower motor cleaning / capacitor:** $150–$450.
- **Inducer motor:** $300–$600 installed.
- **Control board:** $350–$650 installed.
- **Duct modification (chronic airflow restriction):** $400–$2,000+ depending on scope.
- **Oversizing remedy:** no cheap fix — managing it or, worst case, right-sizing on the next replacement.

A simple service call (diagnostic + flame-sensor clean) often lands around **$100–$250** and resolves a large share of short-cycling complaints.
