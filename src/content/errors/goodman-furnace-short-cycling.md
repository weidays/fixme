## What this code means

"Short cycling" is not a single Goodman fault code — it's a behavior. Your furnace lights, runs for a short period (often under three minutes), then shuts off, only to restart again soon after. On Goodman boards you may or may not see a diagnostic LED flash code, depending on what's triggering the shutdown.

If the furnace cycles because a **safety limit is tripping**, you'll often see a limit or flame-related LED flash pattern on the control board. If it cycles purely because the **thermostat is satisfied too quickly** (oversizing or thermostat issues), there's usually no error code at all — the furnace thinks everything is working correctly.

The key distinction: a furnace that short cycles *and locks out* is a different problem — see our Goodman Furnace Lockout page. This page covers furnaces that keep restarting on their own.

## Common causes, ranked by probability

1. **Dirty or clogged air filter** — The most common cause. Restricted airflow makes the heat exchanger overheat, tripping the high-limit switch and shutting the burners off early.
2. **Thermostat issues** — Dead batteries, poor location (near a supply register or heat source), or a failing thermostat that mis-reads temperature and cuts the cycle short.
3. **Blocked or restricted airflow** — Closed/blocked supply vents, crushed ductwork, or a dirty blower wheel — same overheating effect as a dirty filter.
4. **Dirty flame sensor** — A weak flame signal can cause the burners to drop out shortly after ignition, then re-light, producing a cycling pattern.
5. **Tripping high-limit switch** — From overheating (airflow) or a genuinely failing limit switch that opens at too low a temperature.
6. **Blocked condensate drain (high-efficiency models)** — A full or clogged condensate trap can trip a pressure/float switch and interrupt the cycle on GMVC/GMEC/GMES condensing furnaces.
7. **Flue/intake blockage or failing pressure switch** — A restricted vent or weak inducer trips the pressure switch mid-cycle.
8. **Oversized furnace** — Common after a botched replacement. The unit heats too fast, satisfies the thermostat, and shuts off. No code, normal-looking operation, just very short runs.
9. **Failing control board** — Less common, but a degraded board can mis-time cycles or drop signals.

## Safe checks before you call anyone

These are the only checks a homeowner should do without tools or risk:

- **Filter:** Pull it and hold it to a light. If you can't see through it, replace it. This alone resolves a large share of short-cycling calls.
- **Thermostat batteries:** Replace them if your thermostat takes batteries. A low battery causes erratic cycling.
- **Thermostat location & settings:** Make sure it's not in direct sun or near a heat source, and that it's set to "Heat" with a setpoint above room temperature.
- **Breaker:** Confirm the furnace breaker is fully on (not tripped or half-thrown).
- **Supply and return vents:** Walk the house and open every supply register and clear any blocked returns. Restricted airflow overheats the furnace.
- **Condensate drain (high-efficiency units):** If you see standing water near the furnace or a backed-up drain line, a clogged condensate path can trip a switch. You can gently clear a visible, accessible drain line — do not disassemble switches.

Do **not** bypass any safety switch, open the gas valve, or repeatedly reset a furnace that keeps locking out. Repeated resets can be dangerous and mask a real fault.

## How a technician will diagnose it

A good tech will work from cheapest/most-common to least:

1. Inspect the filter and verify airflow, including checking the blower wheel for dirt buildup.
2. Read the control board LED flash code and check it against the Goodman label inside the blower door.
3. Measure temperature rise across the heat exchanger and compare it to the rating-plate spec — too high a rise points to airflow restriction.
4. Test the high-limit switch and flame sensor (microamp reading on the flame sensor).
5. On condensing models, inspect the condensate trap, drain, and pressure/float switches; verify the inducer and flue/intake are clear.
6. If everything tests good but the furnace still runs very short cycles, evaluate sizing — a Manual J load calc versus the installed BTU capacity to confirm or rule out oversizing.

Ask any tech to show you the flash code and the temperature-rise reading. Those two numbers explain most short-cycling diagnoses.

## Symptom, cause and what to do

| Symptom | Likely cause | DIY action | Technician job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runs 1–3 min, shuts off, restarts; dirty filter | Overheating from airflow restriction | Replace filter, open all vents | Check blower wheel, measure temp rise |
| Erratic cycling, thermostat acts odd | Dead batteries or bad thermostat | Replace batteries, check location | Test/replace thermostat, check wiring |
| Burners light then drop out quickly | Dirty/weak flame sensor | None safe | Clean or replace flame sensor, test microamps |
| Cycles with limit-related LED flash | High-limit tripping on heat | Replace filter, clear vents | Test limit switch, verify temp rise |
| Standing water, high-efficiency unit cycles | Clogged condensate/float switch | Clear visible drain line | Service trap, test float/pressure switch |
| Cycles, no code, heats fast | Oversized furnace | None | Manual J load review, staging/airflow adjustment |
| Cycles with pressure-switch code | Flue/intake blockage or weak inducer | Check for visible blockage outside | Test inducer, pressure switch, clear vent |

## Repair costs

Honest US ranges, parts plus typical labor:

- **Air filter:** $10–$40 (DIY)
- **Thermostat batteries:** $5 (DIY)
- **Flame sensor cleaning/replacement:** $80–$250
- **High-limit switch replacement:** $150–$400
- **Condensate trap/drain service:** $100–$250
- **Pressure switch replacement:** $150–$400
- **Inducer motor replacement:** $300–$600+
- **Control board replacement:** $400–$700+
- **Oversizing fix:** Varies widely — from airflow/staging adjustments ($150–$400) up to replacing a mis-sized furnace (several thousand dollars). Get a Manual J before committing.

Prices vary by region, model, and contractor. A diagnostic fee of $80–$180 is typical and is often applied toward the repair.
